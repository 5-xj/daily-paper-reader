Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are increas-ingly deployed as autonomous agents in sci-entific tasks. Yet whether these systems can effectively engage in forms of inductive rea-soning relevant to scientific discovery remains an open question. In this work, we introduce FALSIFY BENCH , an evaluation framework for hypothesis-driven reasoning inspired by the classic Wason 2-4-6 task, in which agents must discover hidden semantic properties by itera-tively proposing examples and receiving feed-back. This task captures key elements of sci-entific reasoning: hypothesis generation, evi-dence gathering, and belief revision in response to both confirming and disconfirming evidence. Our evaluation of 12 LLMs across model fam-ilies and scales shows that reasoning models are generally stronger scientific reasoners than instruction-tuned models, although no model comes close to optimal performance. The pri-mary driver of success is the capacity for nega-tive testing: models that actively seek to falsify their hypotheses consistently outperform those that primarily seek confirmation. Moreover, a fine-grained turn-level analysis, neglected in previous work, reveals that failure is tied to identifiable patterns in how models navigate the hypothesis space. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are becoming in-creasingly capable across domains associated with scientific work, including literature review (Mialon et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2025), software engineering and programming (Jimenez et al., 2024; Jain et al., 2025), mathematics (Mirzadeh et al., 2025; Glazer et al., 2025), and expert-level knowledge (Wang et al., 2024; Rein et al., 2024; Center for AI Safety et al., 2026). These advances have motivated a growing body of work on autonomous agents capa-ble of performing scientific research in an end-to-end manner (Lu et al., 2024; Gottweis et al., 2025). As these systems become increasingly integrated into scientific workflows, a fundamental question arises: can they carry out the key forms of inductive reasoning required for scientific research? Central among these is the ability to generate, evaluate, and revise hypotheses in light of new evidence. A critical component of this process is falsification ,whereby a hypothesis is rejected in favor of an al-ternative when it is contradicted by the evidence. One of the best-known and most widely used paradigms for studying this process in humans is the Wason 2-4-6 task (Wason, 1960). Partici-pants are shown a triple of numbers (e.g., “2, 4, 6”) and asked to discover the hidden rule underlying it, denoted here as R. They do so by proposing new triples and receiving feedback from the exper-imenter on whether each triple fits the rule. Once sufficiently confident that they have discovered R,participants state their hypothesis (denoted here as 

H). When the rule to be discovered is more general than the one suggested by the initial example (as in Wason’s original task, where R is “Any increasing sequence of three numbers”), success rates are low. This is because participants tend to form overly spe-cific hypotheses (e.g., “Even numbers increasing by two”). In such cases, generating triples con-

Confirmation bias and has traditionally been inter-preted as an irrational tendency that runs counter to scientific reasoning. Later work by Klayman and Ha (1987) has made a more nuanced distinction between positive test-ing , in which one seeks evidence expected to con-firm the current hypothesis, and negative testing , in which one seeks evidence expected to disconfirm it. Crucially, they showed that positive testing can also lead to the falsification of a hypothesis and that the effectiveness of these two strategies depends on the relation between H and R. In this work, we introduce a generalized version of the Wason task in which LLMs must discover hidden semantic properties rather than numerical rules. In this semantic version, we exploit taxo-nomic relations to build games emphasizing the 

H ⊂ R configuration, where negative testing is the optimal strategy and which corresponds to Wason’s original case. This configuration is particularly interesting because it mirrors a recurring pattern in the history of science, where accepted theories later turn out to be special cases of broader ones (e.g., Newtonian mechanics as a limiting case of relativity). This parallel motivates our focus on the falsification and revision of overly narrow hypothe-ses through negative testing as a key component of scientific reasoning, and one worth testing in sys-tems such as LLMs. At the same time, following Klayman and Ha (1987), we do not assume that negative testing is universally superior. Instead, we examine how LLMs handle hypothesis testing across different relations between H and R and apply the most appropriate normative analysis to each case, as described below. 2 Related Work 

Inductive reasoning is central to a wide range of human activities, including empirical scientific re-search, and has therefore become a key focus in evaluations of reasoning in LLMs. Early studies have tested this ability in sequence-to-sequence models using synthetic benchmarks. Chollet (2019) introduced the Abstraction and Rea-soning Corpus (ARC), which tests models on grid transformation tasks, in which they must infer ab-stract transformations from a few example input-output pairs and learn to apply the correct rule to unseen grids. Lake and Baroni (2018) take a similar approach, testing the capability to learn mappings from commands to action strings, and testing their application to novel, unseen compositions of se-quences. Recent work has increasingly focused on under-standing how LLMs perform inductive reasoning beyond task performance. Jin et al. (2026) showed that chain-of-thought reasoning (Wei et al., 2022) does not universally improve induction and can even reduce performance. Li et al. (2025) intro-duced a framework for evaluating and explaining inductive reasoning in LLMs by disentangling in-duction and deduction steps. Louapre (2026) exam-ined how LLMs behave in an inductive game, ex-plicitly mapping models’ strategies to human-like traits that influence performance, and connecting their setting to scientific discovery. Within this line of research, our work is closely related to studies that also draw inspiration from Wason’s 2-4-6 task. Banatt et al. (2024) intro-duce WILT, a multi-turn inductive logic benchmark

Neither study explicitly takes into account the normative framework introduced by Klayman and Ha (1987): they neither sample triples from a rule that is a strict subset of the general target rule nor track how the hypotheses formed by mod-els at each turn relate to the target rule. In our experiment, we instead address this limitation by sampling triples of terms from nodes that are strict taxonomic descendants of R, thereby making neg-ative testing the better strategy for identifying it. 

3 Method 

3.1 Experiment Overview 

In our experiment, each LLM served in two roles: as the oracle, which answered queries, and as the player, which attempted to discover the hidden rule. In direct analogy with Wason’s original ex-periment, the oracle provided the player with an initial triple of terms sampled from a subordinate semantic category (e.g., “vertebrate”), which was itself included in a more general target category (e.g., “animal”). The more general target category served as the target rule R. By iteratively proposing new examples and receiving feedback, the player accumulated evidence, testing its current hypoth-esis either positively or negatively, until it could identify R. An example of a real game is shown in Figure 1. More specifically, on each turn, the player proposed three items along with a hypothe-sis and a rationale for why those items were chosen. The oracle responded with the string “Conform” or “Do not conform” depending on whether the set of items satisfied the target rule R. When sufficiently confident, the player submitted a final answer, and the oracle responded with either “Correct” or “In-correct”. Thus, the player was prompted to use 

Test actions to gather information and refine its hypotheses, and to use Guess actions once enough information had been collected. When the player submitted an incorrect guess, it learned that its cur-rent hypothesis was wrong and was prompted to perform new actions to gather additional evidence until it was confident enough to make a new guess. A game ended when either the player submitted a correct guess or the maximum turn limit was reached (we set the maximum number of turns to 20). We chose semantic categories, rather than numer-ical sequences, as the domain of our experiment for two reasons: LLMs are very likely to have en-countered the classic 2-4-6 task during pretraining, and semantic taxonomies allow us to define a rich set of candidate concepts with increasingly spe-cific subconcepts. The categories were extracted from WordNet (Miller, 1995), which enables the construction of a large number of such games by selecting general concepts and sampling from their descendants. From this large space, we curated a set of 100 representative games spanning different taxonomic depths and semantic domains. Each game was defined by two semantic cate-gories: the first was the general category that served Player Oracle

4 Performance on F ALSIFY BENCH 

Our evaluation across 12 models revealed substan-tial variation in player and oracle performance, which we analyze below. 

Player Performance and Confirmation Bias. 

Reasoning models generally achieved higher suc-cess rates than their instruction-tuned counterparts. The best instruction-tuned model, DeepSeek-V3.1, reached only 41% success rate, while top reason-ing models achieved 75% (GPT-5.2-Chat) and 68% (GPT-OSS-120B), with the remaining instruction-tuned models clustering between 33% and 35%. Notably, success rates remained far from saturated even for the best model, leaving substantial room for improvement. The advantage of reasoning mod-els did not uniformly extend to the smallest ones: GPT-5-Nano (12%) and Qwen3.5-9B (21%) per-formed worse than all instruction-tuned models, suggesting that extended thinking alone was insuf-ficient at smaller scales. Additionally, we found a strong correlation between success rates and Con-firmation bias: models that predominantly used positive testing consistently underperformed those that actively tested outside their current hypothesis (Spearman ρ = −0.779 , p = 0 .003 ). This pat-tern held across both model families and scales, with stronger reasoning models maintaining sub-stantially lower Confirmation bias ratios (GPT-5-Mini: 39.0%, GPT-5.2-Chat: 27.3%, GPT-OSS-20B: 24.2%) compared to instruction-tuned mod-els, which clustered between 66% and 82%. 

Oracle Quality. In terms of oracle performance with respect to human annotation, raw accuracy was moderately high across all models, with most LLMs clustering between 85% and 95%. Cohen’s κ revealed more meaningful variation, spanning from 0.34 (Mistral-Small-24B, Guess turns) to 0.98 (GPT-5.2-Chat, both conditions), indicating that some models provided less reliable taxonomic judg-ments than others. However, models with compa-rable oracle performance showed substantially dif-ferent success rates: for instance, MiniMax-M2.5, GLM-5, and GPT-5-Nano achieved broadly similar oracle κ scores (0.67–0.91, 0.78–0.76, and 0.81– 0.72, respectively) yet succeeded on 35%, 58%, and 12% of games respectively. This dissociation suggested that oracle quality was not sufficient for player success. We analyzed this further below. 

Disentangling Player Reasoning from Oracle Quality. We aimed to better assess whether player strategy or oracle quality were determinant for the player’s success. To this end, we fit a Bayesian mixed-effects logistic regression at the game level ( n = 1 ,200 ), with Confirmation bias and oracle error rate as fixed-effect predictors and LLM identity as a random effect. 5 Confirmation bias was used in the model as a continuous predic-tor in [0 , 1] , representing the proportion of positive tests within a game. 5 In-depth Turn Level Analysis 

The Confirmation bias reported in Section 4 mea-sures the intent behind a model’s testing strategy, implicitly assuming that positive testing is a less optimal behavior than negative testing. In order to determine the effectiveness of a given strategy in rejecting a wrong hypothesis, it is necessary to understand the relationship between each cur-rent hypothesis H and the target rule R in games (see Table 1). Indeed, it is possible that the player would form hypotheses for which negative testing is suboptimal and positive testing should instead be favored. For this reason, we employed one of the models tested on F ALSIFY BENCH as an offline LLM annotator, which annotates all Test turns of each game across all models with the set relation between H and R. To balance speed and anno-tation cost, we selected GPT-5-Mini as annotator. The exact prompt used to perform this annotation can be seen in Appendix C.

The strength of this correlation supports the idea that testing negatively (i.e., having lower Confir-mation bias) was a reliable proxy for reaching a conclusive falsification of wrong hypothesis in our framework.

7 Conclusion 

We introduced F ALSIFY BENCH , a benchmark for evaluating hypothesis-driven reasoning in LLMs through a semantic generalization of the Wason 2-4-6 task. Our evaluation of 12 models showed that reasoning models are generally stronger scientific thinkers than instruction-tuned models. However, no model came close to optimal performance, high-lighting substantial room for improvement in the inductive reasoning capabilities of current LLMs. The primary driver of success was found to be the capacity for negative testing: models that actively seek to falsify their hypotheses consistently out-perform those that seek to confirm them, and our turn-level analysis showed that disconfirmatory be-havior is a reliable proxy for progress toward the correct hypothesis. Finally, we showed that failure is also tied to how models explored the hypothesis space. In failed games, models tended to drift away from the target, forming hypotheses that were either disjoint from or only partially overlapping with the target rule, and were significantly more likely to propose rules based on surface-level properties of words rather than semantic categories. 

8 Limitations 

Our analysis uses WordNet as a proxy for seman-tic taxonomic knowledge. Although WordNet is a well-established lexical resource created by expert lexicographers, it has several limitations. For ex-ample, its hierarchy reflects specific lexicographic decisions, it is English-centric, and it may contain basic semantic inconsistencies. We sought to over-come these limitations by focusing on concrete and fairly unambiguous superordinate categories. Additionally, we collected human annotations for only one Test turn and one Guess turn per game. While this sampling strategy kept annotation effort manageable across 1,200 games, it forced us to operationalize oracle error as a binary variable. We acknowledge that this is a coarse approxima-tion. Nevertheless, the high raw accuracy observed across models suggests that the probability of a randomly selected turn resulting in an incorrect or-acle judgment is low. This means that our binary estimate is likely to have overestimated rather than underestimated the true oracle error rate, making our regression result conservative. Despite this, the effect of oracle error on player success remains negligible. Finally, our analysis was inspired by the his-tory of scientific discoveries, in which previously accepted hypotheses have been replaced by more comprehensive ones. We acknowledge, however, that this is only one aspect of the scientific process, and leave further exploration of this topic to future research.

A When Can a Hypothesis be Falsified? 

We provide a detailed analysis of when a test yields a falsification depending on the relation between the current hypothesis H and the target rule R.Following Klayman and Ha (1987), we classify any test x along two binary dimensions: whether 

x ∈ H (the test is positive with respect to the hypothesis) and whether x ∈ R (the test is true 

with respect to the target). A test within H is a 

positive test; a test outside H is a negative test. Whether the oracle confirms or rejects it determines whether the test is true or false . This yields four cases, summarized in Table 2, each with a distinct informational value for hypothesis revision.

In the following sections, we discuss in each possible relation between H and R which of the four quadrants yield a conclusive falsification and which do not. 

Case 1: H = R (Identical) 

When H is identical to R, both false positives and false negatives are impossible. Every member of 

H is in R (so no test within H can receive “Do not conform”), and no member outside H is in R (so no test outside H can receive “Conform”). Trivially, holding the correct hypothesis means that it cannot be falsified and only a Guess action would end the game. 

Case 2: H ⊂ R (H too narrow) 

When H is a strict subset of R, every member of 

H is automatically in R, making false positives im-possible: tests within H always receive “Conform”. The only route to falsification is a false negative – testing outside H and receiving “Conform”, reveal-ing that R extends beyond H and forcing upward revision. Crucially, not every negative test achieves this: testing outside H and receiving “Do not con-form” (true negative) is equally consistent with H

and provides no new information. A player relying exclusively on positive tests is therefore confined to true positives and can never falsify H. This is the dominant relation in F ALSIFY BENCH .

Case 3: H ⊃ R (H too broad) 

When H is strictly broader than R, every member of R is inside H, making false negatives impos-sible: no test outside H can receive “Conform”. The only route to conclusive falsification is a false positive – testing within H and receiving “Do not conform”, revealing that H must be narrowed. No-tably, a positive test strategy is the correct approach in this configuration: testing within H is the only way to a falsification. Crucially, not every positive test achieves this: testing within H and receiving “Conform” (true positive) is equally consistent with 

H and provides no reason to discard it. 

Case 4: H and R partially overlap 

When H and R partially overlap but neither con-tains the other, both false positives and false neg-atives are accessible. A test within H but outside 

R yields “Do not conform” (false positive, falsi-fication): only a subset of H is compatible with 

R, revealing that H must be revised to exclude that region. A test within R but outside H yields “Conform” (false negative, falsification): R extends beyond H, revealing that H must be revised to in-clude that region. Thus, two ways of falsification are available in this configuration. However, two non-conclusive outcomes are also possible: test-ing within both H and R yields “Conform” (true positive, non-conclusive), and testing outside both 

H and R yields “Do not conform” (true negative, non-conclusive). In both cases the feedback is con-sistent with H and provides no pressure to revise it in either direction. 

Case 5: H and R disjoint 

When H and R share no members, every test within 

H yields “Do not conform” (false positive, falsi-fication): H is entirely incompatible with R and must be discarded. Every test within R but outside 

H yields “Conform” (false negative, falsification): 

R extends into a region entirely outside H, and H

needs to be revised. The only non-conclusive out-come is testing outside both H and R, which yields “Do not conform” (true negative, non-conclusive): the feedback is consistent with H and provides no information about how to revise it. Finally, true positives are impossible in this configuration, since 

H and R share no members. 

B Game Construction Details 

We build game instances using the WordNet tax-onomy, where each node is a semantic category and its descendants represent increasingly specific subcategories. As we explained in Section 3.1, we started with seven high-level WordNet categories representing concrete concepts as candidate target rules: “animal”, “artifact”, “body part”, “food”, “location”, “plant”, and “worker”. However, many of the possible descendants of these categories in-volve low-frequency vocabulary. Hence, we man-ually filtered the subcategory pool, selecting only five target categories: “animal”, “artifact”, “body part”, “food” and “plant”. Since subcategories of these target categories can come from different depths in the taxonomy (e.g. “feline” is deeper than “mammal”), we first divided the subcategories into two groups based on their taxonomic distance from the targets. The 

close group has a distance of at most four edges, and the deep group exceeds four edges. To ensure diversity in the sampling categories for each game, we sampled triples from the close and deep cate-gories in equal proportions. C.2 Agent Roles and Action Space 

Each game began with exactly three positive exam-ples presented to the player. The player was never shown the target rule R or the sampling category S

directly; it observed only the three example items and the system prompt explaining the rules of the game (see Table 5). The interaction proceeded as a sequence of turns between the player and the oracle. The player was responsible for proposing actions aimed at discover-ing the hidden property, while the oracle evaluated these actions and provided feedback according to the player’s last action. On each turn, the player performed exactly one of two actions: • Test: propose a triple of items together with a hypothesis string and a reasoning trace. The action must be well-formed JSON matching the schema specified in the system prompt; malformed or schema-invalid submissions trigger a retry (see Appendix C.3) and are not recorded as turns. • Guess: submit a single property string as a final answer. A correct guess returns “Correct” and ends the game; an incorrect guess returns “Incorrect”, after which the player can con-tinue with additional Test or Guess actions. After receiving a valid player action, the oracle performed the following actions: • Feedback generation : for a Test action, de-termine whether the proposed items satisfy the target rule R and return the string “Conform” if they do and “Do not conform” otherwise; for a Guess action, determine whether the pro-posed property is semantically equivalent to 

R by returning the string “Correct” if it does and “Incorrect” otherwise. • Strategy classification : after every Test ac-tion, classify the player’s testing strategy as 

positive if the player tests examples expected to satisfy its current hypothesis, and negative 

if it tests examples outside the hypothesis in order to challenge it and explore broader alter-natives. This step was performed only for later analysis and did not affect gameplay directly. A game turn was defined as one accepted player action together with the oracle’s response. Invalid submissions and retry attempts did not increment the turn counter. 

C.3 Retry Mechanism 

The game engine employed two independent retry mechanisms to handle failures in producing valid JSON outputs, and none of these retry attempts were recorded as game turns. For player actions, if the engine received malformed JSON or an ac-tion that violated the schema, it re-prompted within the same turn requesting strict JSON output; after the first failure, up to three additional retries were permitted. For oracle judgments, each method was allowed up to two attempts: if the first response could not be parsed as JSON, the engine re-issued the prompt with an appended instruction (“IMPOR-TANT: Return ONLY a JSON object”) and then attempted to parse the second response. This nume-ber of retries was sufficient to complete all games with all tested models. 

C.4 Memory and statefulness. 

During the game interactions, the player was state-ful : its conversation history accumulated all prior oracle feedback and its own previous actions. In contrast, the oracle was stateless : each call to the oracle was performed using a newly initialized prompt that provided only the elements necessary to collect the current judgement, with no access to information from earlier turns.
