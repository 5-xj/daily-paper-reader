Abstract 

Interpreting gene function in specific biological contexts is essential for biomedical research, yet manual literature review is labor-intensive. We developed GeneKnow, a source-grounded framework that uses generative AI models within a controlled hybrid workflow to produce reliable, traceable literature synthesis supported by authentic citations. Through systematic benchmarking, we showed that GeneKnow outperforms mainstream web-interface AI tools in generating trustworthy context-specific gene function syntheses without fabricated citations and minimizing hallucinations.

Introduction 

Understanding gene function is a fundamental goal of molecular biology. In various scenarios, researchers need to determine what role a specific gene plays in a defined biological context, such as a cell type, disease state, or experimental perturbation, because gene function can be highly context-dependent 1–3 . Conventional approaches such as gene set or gene functional enrichment analysis provide a broad view of gene ontology terms or pathways associated with a gene list, but they offer limited insight into the roles of individual genes in a specific context 4–6 . Researchers therefore often rely on labor-intensive manual literature review to interpret gene-context relationships. The same burden also appears during computational method development, where published studies are frequently used as ground-truth evidence for training, validating, or benchmarking machine-learning models, which further increases the need for efficient and reliable literature synthesis. While generative artificial intelligence (AI) with large language models (LLMs) can make automated literature analysis increasingly feasible, factual reliability and accountability remain a major obstacle. Most current LLMs are prone to hallucination and often generate plausible but unverified claims and fabricated references 7,8 . Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and related systems can reduce hallucination by grounding generation in retrieved text, but many implementations, including OpenScholar 9, depend on pre-indexed corpora and do not guarantee the transparency and reproducibility of retrieval 10,11 . Recent agentic AI systems with web-browsing capabilities have improved generation faithfulness, but their search depth and retrieval behavior can vary across runs, and their retrieval and citation

Recent works have improved scientific literature synthesis through adaptive retrieval, citation-aware reasoning 14 , and structured integration of biological databases 15 . However, these tools are generally optimized for open-ended explorations or factual database queries rather than for extracting and synthesizing gene-function evidence across online publications. This leaves an unmet need for a method that prioritizes exact gene-context matching, reproducible retrieval, deterministic citation construction, and traceable evidence integration, which are particularly important in biomedical research. To address this need, we developed GeneKnow, a software tool for automated, source-grounded literature synthesis in gene-context analysis. In this paper, we describe GeneKnow’s retrieval and synthesis workflow and evaluate its factual consistency, claim yield, and citation fidelity in gene-context literature synthesis, compared with several state-of-the-arts generative AI tools including ChatGPT 5.2 Thinking, Claude Opus 4.6, and Gemini 3 Thinking.

Results 

We designed the GeneKnow framework to mimic how researchers review the literature while keeping key operations reproducible and transparent. GeneKnow adopts a controlled hybrid framework composed of deterministic steps and generative steps (Fig. 1). Deterministic steps are used for operations that require exactness and reproducibility, including paper retrieval, evidence selection, and bibliography construction. Generative steps are used for tasks that require language understanding, including summarization, self-verification, and cross-paper synthesis. By default, GeneKnow runs its generative steps through the ChatGPT API using gpt-5-mini, while its modular design allows other LLM APIs to be substituted. We implemented two modes for GeneKnow: “Discover” and “Inspect”. Both modes take a gene name and a context term as input. In Discover mode, GeneKnow retrieves candidate papers through a controlled academic search of the gene-context query from sources such as PubMed, then applies deterministic procedures for evidence selection and bibliography construction. Rather than relying on a single vector-search step over pre-indexed text chunks, GeneKnow first identifies relevant papers and then selects paragraph-level evidence based on joint gene-context relevance, which improves exact entity grounding and reproducibility. Instead of paper retrieval, the Inspect mode starts from a user-specified paper and applies the same subsequent evidence-selection and synthesis steps. Generative steps then proceed through hierarchical summarization from passage-level,

Together with this controlled summarization process, GeneKnow preserves traceability by storing retrieved passages, verified article summaries, and final synopses separately, allowing users to investigate how final claims are connected to passage-level evidence. Discussion 

GeneKnow demonstrates how generative AI can be made reliable for biomedical literature synthesis through a controlled, gene-context-centered workflow. In its hybrid design, deterministic steps handle operations where accuracy and reproducibility are critical, such as retrieval, evidence selection, and bibliography construction, while generative steps handle language-understanding tasks, including summarization, claim verification, and synthesis. By separating these roles, GeneKnow limits unconstrained generation while preserving the flexibility needed to interpret complex biomedical text. This design converts scattered literature into inspectable gene-context synopses that are better suited to biological interpretation and literature-supported ground-truth collection. In our evaluations, we show that GeneKnow supports accurate single-paper extraction, source-grounded cross-paper synthesis, and reliable citation construction, substantially reducing hallucinations and avoiding fabricated citations. These results suggest that controlled workflow design can make generative AI more trustworthy and accountable for research use. The current findings also suggest limitations and directions for future work. Because GeneKnow compresses evidence through hierarchical summarization, some source-specific details may be lost during intermediate steps, which may lead to over-generalization or uncertainty in the final synopsis. We attenuated this issue by applying a cross-stage prompting strategy in which prompts consistently followed a narrative backbone: role in context, mechanism, and outcome. Although this strategy substantially reduced the problem, the small fraction of uncertain claims remaining in the Discover benchmark suggests that

some residual information loss or distortion may still occur. Future improvements could further address this limitation by enhancing claim-level tracking across summarization stages. GeneKnow can potentially be extended to support more complex literature queries, including functional relationships among multiple genes and nested biological contexts, such as a cell type within a disease or treatment setting. It was suggested recently that gene embeddings derived from explicit functional descriptions preserve biological structure 21 , and context-enriched gene summaries can improve tissue-specific prediction tasks 22 . Thus, another future direction is to use verified GeneKnow summaries as input text for context-conditioned gene embeddings. Overall, GeneKnow provides a practical framework for trustworthy, reproducible, and source-grounded gene-context literature synthesis for biomedical research. 

Methods GeneKnow framework 

GeneKnow is a software framework for source-grounded gene-context literature synthesis. The system operates in two modes. In Discover mode, GeneKnow retrieves and synthesizes evidence across multiple papers for a specified gene-context query. In Inspect mode, it analyzes a designated paper to summarize the same relationship in a single-paper setting. All internal LLM steps in the reported runs used gpt-5-mini through the OpenAI API with

Query construction, paper retrieval, and full-text inclusion 

GeneKnow constructs queries from the exact gene symbol and user-supplied context aliases. The query template combines a gene group and a context group with Boolean AND: GeneKnow quotes the gene symbol exactly, joins optional gene aliases with OR when enabled, and joins context aliases with OR when enabled. A typical query therefore takes the form (GENE OR GENE_ALIAS) AND (CONTEXT OR CONTEXT_ALIAS). GeneKnow supports an optional automatic gene alias expansion through NCBI Gene. We use PubMed as the default search engine, with Europe PMC and Scopus also supported. In Discover mode, GeneKnow offers customizable search-depth parameters: --search-limit sets the maximum number of candidate papers retrieved from the search engine, and --max-papers sets the maximum number of PMCID-accessible full-text papers to review. In the reported runs, we set --search-limit to 25 and --max-papers to 5. GeneKnow excludes candidate papers without a PMCID from Discover review. GeneKnow downloads full-text XML from PMC when available, using Europe PMC as a fallback if PMC retrieval fails. In

Paper preprocessing and evidence passage selection 

GeneKnow converts full-text XML into paragraph-level units before LLM summarization. XML parsing was implemented using beautifulsoup4 (v4.14.2) with the lxml (v6.0.2) parser. GeneKnow extracts narrative sections such as abstract, introduction, results, discussion, and conclusions for further analysis, and excludes figure-container text to avoid extracting isolated panel labels or captions without sufficient narrative context. Paragraphs with word count between 20 and 500 are retained directly, while longer paragraphs are split into sentence-based chunks that remain within the maximum size limit. For a downloaded paper, each valid paragraph is then tokenized, wherein all the gene and context mentions are processed exclusively. Specifically, all the mentions of the query gene and its aliases are converted to a special token 𝑡 !"#" , and all the mentions of the query context and its aliases are converted to a special token 𝑡 $%#&"'& . Prompt-guided hierarchical summarization 

GeneKnow uses a hierarchical summarization workflow consisting of passage-level summaries, article-level summaries, and a final cross-paper synopsis. GeneKnow first summarizes each retained passage independently with a prompt-constrained summary of at most 100 words. This step also filters passages that primarily contain methods text, references, or figure-only content, or that lacks a direct mention of the gene-context relationship. GeneKnow then merges the remaining passage summaries into a single article-level summary of at most 150 words. A central prompt-engineering feature of GeneKnow is the use of a consistent role in context → mechanism → outcome narrative backbone across hierarchical summarization. When the source text supports a gene-centered narrative, prompts instruct the model to preserve that structure and to retain the original relationship type, including whether statements are causal, mechanistic, associative, or parallel. When the text does not support such a narrative, the prompts instead restrict the output to direct gene-context statements without imposing a mechanistic storyline. Across stages, the prompts explicitly discourag over-generalization, unsupported causal strengthening, loss of experimental conditions, and restructuring of the original evidence chain.

Iterative self-verification and revision 

GeneKnow then passes article-level summaries through a self-verification step to verify their faithfulness. GeneKnow first extracts atomic claims from the draft summary, then checks those claims against all the evidence passages of that paper iteratively using a verification model. The verification model assigns a verification flag that can be “yes”, “no” or “partial” to each claim. A claim will only be labeled as “yes” if it retains the correct relationship (e.g. causal, hierarchical, parallel, associative), predicate, and the original context (e.g. species, condition, treatment) of the source passage. A claim will be labeled as “no” if it conflicts with evidence, or if the evidence does not contain any relevant phrases of the claim. Any inaccurate but fixable claim, for example, claims lacking necessary context, will be labeled as “partial”, and revised to better align with the source passage. As a result, a verification report that contains a set of verification flags for all the claims and a list of revisions are generated for each evidence passage. GeneKnow then integrates all the verification reports for an article-level summary to make a revision plan. Claims are classified into three mutually exclusive categories, those to be kept, those to be deleted, and those requiring revision, based on their verification flags across reports. A claim is assigned to the “keep” set if at least one passage returns a “yes” flag; to the “deletion” set if every passage returns a “no” flag; and to the “revision” set if it receives at least one “partial” flag and no “yes” flag.

For the claims in the “revision” set, the system synthesizes a corrected formulation by aggregating candidate revisions proffered across all verification reports. In cases when multiple revisions are proffered, GeneKnow employs Jaccard similarity as a selection criterion to identify the candidate revision that maximizes lexical overlap with the original claim. This heuristic assumes that the most faithful revision is the one that preserves the highest proportion of the original terminological content while rectifying the identified inaccuracy. The revision plan is applied to the summary through a final editorial pass. The model is directed to excise claims marked for deletion, replace original phrasing with the selected revisions where provided, and preserve narrative fluency by inserting brief transitional language. All major intermediate outputs are stored separately, including evidence-passage files, article-level summaries, final reports, and token-usage logs. 

Final synthesis and bibliography construction 

In Discover mode, GeneKnow further compresses each revised article-level summary into a short, structured brief not exceeding forty words. The prompts also encourage the role in context → mechanism → outcome narrative backbone when applicable. These briefs, tagged with source identifiers, are subsequently cast into a unified synopsis via a final synthesis step. The model first extracts core gene roles recurrent across multiple briefs, then presents context-specific narratives sequentially without collapsing mechanistic or relational distinctions across sources. Benchmark of Inspect mode 

We evaluated GeneKnow Inspect with 20 pre-specified gene-context test cases that we also used for the Discover benchmark. We selected cases that cover both gene-cell type and gene-disease queries and were not used during prompt development. The full case list, including target papers for Inspect, is provided in Supplementary Material. We compared three approaches: GeneKnow Inspect, which used passage retrieval, hierarchical summarization, and self-verification; Full-paper LLM, which summarized the extracted full-paper text directly without staged passage selection or verification; and Hierarchical LLM, which applies multi-stage retrieval and hierarchical summarization as GeneKnow Inspect but skipped the self-verification step. An AI-driven evaluation framework was implemented following an asking-and-answering-questions approach. For alignment, atomic claims were first extracted from each system summary and converted into yes/no questions. Each question was then answered by the question-answering model using both the matched evidence text and the original claim from which the question was generated, with answer options restricted to “Yes,” “No,” and “Idk.” “Idk” was assigned only when the evaluation prompt determined that the available evidence or summary did not provide enough information to support a yes or no answer.
