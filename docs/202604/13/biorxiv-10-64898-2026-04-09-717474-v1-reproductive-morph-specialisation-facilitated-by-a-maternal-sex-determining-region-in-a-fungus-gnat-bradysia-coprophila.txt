# Abstract 9

Sexual reproduction is a ubiquitous process in eukaryotes, yet mechanisms of sex determination are 10 

strikingly diverse. One unique and understudied system is maternal genetic sex determination ( mat -GSD), 11 

in which the genotype of the mother determines offspring sex. In some species, mothers further specialise 12 

into g enetically determined reproductive morphs: gynogenic females that produce all -female broods, and 13 

androgenic females that produce all -male broods. Although this partition echoes the evolution of separate, 14 

dimorphic sexes, it remains unclear whether the morp hs diverge in traits beyond offspring sex 15 

determination, and how this affects the evolutionary dynamics of the mating system. Here we address 16 

these questions in the dark‑winged fungus gnat Bradysia coprophila , in which female morphs are 17 

determined by a lar ge X -linked inversion. We show that maternal reproductive morphs diverge 18 

significantly in life history traits and gene expression profile, suggesting adaptive specialisation into their 19 

reproductive roles. We further evaluate potential drivers for specialis ation and test two of them 20 

empirically, showing evidence in line with sex -specific maternal provisioning. By drawing explicit 21 

parallels with sex chromosome evolution and sexual dimorphism, our results extend core principles of 22 

reproductive specialisation t o mat -GSD systems, and underscore the potential of unusual reproductive 23 

systems for extending fundamental evolutionary theory on how selection and genomic architecture 24 

interact to shape mating system evolution. 25

# 1. Introduction 27 

Maternal genetic sex deter mination ( mat -GSD) is a unique and understudied sex -determining system, in 28 

which zygotic sex is determined by maternal genotype, rather than that of the zygote. It has independently 29 

evolved in three speciose groups of Dipteran insects: Sciaridae (dark -wing ed fungus gnats), 30 

Cecidomyiidae (gall midges), and Calliphoridae (blowflies) (Baird et al., 2023a). Outside of Diptera, 31 

examples occur in bivalves (Kenchington et al., 2002; Ghiselli et al., 2012), ants (Lagunas -Robles et al., 32 

2021), and Daphnia (Ye et al. , 2019). In some species, females produce mixed -sex broods (digenic), 33 

while in others, they are further distinguished as one of two genetically -determined reproductive morphs 34 

with specialised offspring sex ratio —gynogenic females produce exclusively female offspring, and 35 

androgenic females exclusively male offspring (Baird et al., 2023a). Such specialisation is analogous to 36 

the evolution of separate sexes, but leads to three, rather than two, stable and co -existing reproductive 37 

morphs. Yet, the foundational question of whether these morphs have distinct phenotypes remains 38 

unexplored —do the morphs differ in anatomy, physiology and behaviour beyond offspring sex 39 

determination, or are they otherwise undifferentiated? If divergence exists, is it driven by morph -specific 40 

selection and adaptive specialisation, or by constraints and fitness costs associated with the genetic 41 

architecture underlying morph identity? Untangling these alternatives is particularly important where 42 

morph identity is controlled by large non -recombining regions (supergenes), which can both facilitate the 43 

accumulation of morph -beneficial alleles and experience ineffective selection and degeneration. 44 

45 

Unlike mat -GSD, the evolution of reproductive specialisation is well -documented in canonical s exual 46 

reproduction (Bachtrog et al., 2014). Separation into male and female reproductive morphs, each 47 

producing one gamete type, is frequently accompanied by the evolution of secondary sexual traits in 48 

behaviour, physiology and life history (Glucksmann, 19 74; Andersson, 1994; Mank, 2009; Beukeboom et 49 

al., 2015). This allows the sexes to occupy distinct reproductive roles (Schärer et al., 2012; Lehtonen et 50 

al., 2016), which can contribute to the stability of the mating system (Charlesworth and Charlesworth, 51 

1978; Hadjivasiliou and Pomiankowski, 2016). At the same time, the scope for sexual dimorphism 52 

depends on the underlying sex determination mechanism (Williams and Carroll, 2009). In GSD systems, 53 

loci for secondary sexual traits can be linked to the sex det ermination (SD) locus, by recombination 54 

suppression, into a supergene encoding for dimorphic sexes. In fact, selection for sex -specific adaptations 55 

is thought to be itself a driver of recombination suppression, leading to heteromorphic sex chromosomes 56 

(Ric e, 1987; Charlesworth et al., 2005; Flintham and Mullon, 2026). In plants, theory and empirical data 57 

show that associations between sex allocation and sexually antagonistic traits can promote specialisation 58 

in the transition from hermaphroditism to dioecy (Charlesworth, 2019; Lesaffre et al., 2024). Together, 59

the degree of specialisation that can evolve —shaping the evolution and stability of mating systems. As 63

characterised; to our knowledge, no studies have yet addressed these questions under mat ‑GSD. 66

In this stu dy, we focus on the monogenic dark -winged fungus gnat Bradysia coprophila (Box 1). Across 68 

fungus gnats, monogeny appears to have repeatedly evolved from digeny (Baird, 2024), paralleling 69 

multiple independent evolutions of divergent sexes across eukaryotes. Among mat -GSD species, its 70 

genetic basis is best resolved in fungus gnats, enabling tests of whether genomic architecture promotes or 71 

hinders morph specialisation. While the offspring sex ratio in digenic species likely has a polygenic basis 72 

(Shlyakonova et al., 2026), monogeny seems to be accompanied by repeated evolution of supergenes. In 73 

B. coprophila , this region has been identified cytological and genomically as a large X -linked inversion 74 

(X’), heterozygous in gynogenic females (Metz, 1938; Carson, 19 44; Crouse, 1960; Baird et al., 2023b). 75 

The morph -limited, permanently -heterozygous, and non -recombining nature of the X’ makes it a prime 76 

spot for the accumulation of morph -beneficial alleles. Therefore, we may expect the supergene to 77 

facilitate morph -spe cific divergence. However, recombination suppression and the resulting Hill -78 

Robertson interference leads to degeneration (Hill and Robertson, 1966; Charlesworth and Charlesworth, 79 

2000; Bachtrog et al., 2011), potentially sped up by the low effective popula tion size (N e)—one -sixth that 80 

of an autosome —of the X’ inversion (Hitchcock et al., 2024). The accumulation of deleterious mutations 81 

and repeats is expected to have a negative effect on the fitness of the carrier (Nguyen and Bachtrog, 2021; 82 

Teoli et al., 2 023). Consistent with this, there is evidence of gene degeneration in the X’ of B. coprophila 83 

despite its relatively young history (<0.5 Mya) (Baird et al., 2023b), which may predict lower gynogenic 84 

female fitness. With conflicting evolutionary forces acti ng on this region, it is unclear if it facilitates 85 

morph -specific divergence or hinders it by being a region of ineffective selection, and how this dynamic 86 

contributes to the rapid turnover between monogenic and digenic systems in the family of flies. 87 

88 

Her e, using two laboratory populations of Bradysia coprophila , we quantify phenotypic divergence 89 

between female reproductive morphs, and ask whether it is driven by adaptive specialisation into distinct 90 

morphs, or rather by degradation in X’ supergene. We fin d that reproductive morphs diverge significantly 91 

in multiple measures of life history, fitness -related and gene expression traits. Contrary to predictions 92 

based on supergene degradation alone, gynogenic females were not less fit than androgenic females. 93

for reproductive specialisation and the dynamics of its genomic architecture, beyond the conventional 101

Box 1: Sex determination mechanism in fungus gnats 104 

In both monogenic and digenic species of dark -winged fungus gnats (Family: Sciaridae), sex 

determination occurs by elimination of the paternal X chromosome in early embryonic soma (Metz, 

1938). All embryos start out with a diploid number of autosomes but t hree X chromosomes —two of 

paternal origin (due to meiotic nondisjunction) and one of maternal origin. In the 7 -9th cleavage 

division of early embryogenesis, embryos destined to become female eliminate one of the paternal Xs 

(producing XX females), while th ose destined to become male eliminate both paternal Xs (producing 

XO males) (Phalle and Sullivan, 1996). Since X elimination in the embryonic soma occurs before the 

onset of zygotic gene activation, the sex of the offspring and number of X chromosomes elim inated is 

under maternal control. 

For an extensive overview of fungus gnat sex determination and other aspects of their fascinating 

biology, see (Gerbi, 1986, 2022).

# 2. Results 105 

2.1 Female reproductive morphs differ significantly at the phenotypic level 106 

We compared gynogenic and androgenic females across a number of key life history traits, in two distinct 107 

lines of Bradysia coprophila (H2 and KM). H2 (Holo2) is a long -established lab line (Metz and Smith, 108 

1931) with a wing marker that dis tinguishes the otherwise visually identical female reproductive morphs 109 

(Gerbi, 2024) (Fig. 7). However, its long laboratory history, which includes exposure to irradiation, may 110 

accelerate degeneration of the non -recombining X’ region. To avoid laboratory -line artefacts, we 111 

supplemented our study with the KM line, collected from East Lothian, Scotland in February 2023. 112 

113 

Using both lines also permits us to disentangle the effects of heterozygote advantage and sex -specific 114 

specialisation. Both lines being rel atively inbred, the permanently heterozygous, gynogenic -specific X’ 115 

(>50 Mb in a ~300 Mb genome) elevates genome -wide heterozygosity of gynogenic females and can 116 

mask recessive deleterious mutations, potentially making gynogenic females appear fitter witho ut any 117 

additional specialisation mutations. To control for this, we generated an outbred line (H2 females x KM 118 

males) to normalise genome heterozygosity between morphs. 119 

120 

Across the two inbred and one outbred line, linear/generalised linear mixed -effects m odels revealed 121 

significant divergence between the two reproductive morphs in a number of key life history traits (Fig. 1). 122 

The direction of divergence can depend on the line. For developmental rate, gynogenic females developed 123 

faster than androgenic female s in H2 ( t = -3.997, p = 7.39e -05 ) and H2xKM ( t = -2.931, p = 0.00559) lines, 124 

while the opposite was true in the KM line ( t = 7.155, p = 3.07e -12 ) (Fig. 1A). Similarly, gynogenic 125 

females were significantly more fecund than androgenic females in the H2 line (z = 3.491, p = 0.000481), 126 

while the opposite was true in the KM line ( z = -3.550, p = 0.000386) (Fig. 1E). 127 

128 

Other traits showed greater concordance across lines. In both H2 and KM lines, gynogenic females had 129 

significantly longer adult lifespans ( t = 2. 186, p = 0.02931) (Fig. 1D). For body size, while no significant 130 

difference was found between the morphs in the H2 and KM lines, gynogenic females were significantly 131 

larger than their androgenic counterparts in the H2xKM line ( t = 2.021, p = 0.0498) (Fig. 1C). Lastly, no 132 

significant differences in survival to adulthood were found in any lines (Fig. 1B). 133 

134

in gynogenic females, in which gynogenic female s are expected to be less fit. On the contrary, androgenic 136

2.2 Female reproductive morphs diverge significantly in gene expression 151 

To see if the observed phenotypic divergence is accompanied by divergence in gene expression, we 152 

compared transcriptomic data at different tissue levels —germline (ovaries), somatic reproductive 153 

(ovipositor, accessory glands, spermatheca), and somatic non -reproductive (all other tissue). Since both 154 

lines showed phenotypic divergence, only the H2 line, which had more comprehensive genetic resources, 155 

was used in this analysis. 156 

2.2.1 Significant gene expression divergence in X -linked genes 157 

Given that maternal ly acting SD loci in fungus gnats are thought to be X -linked (on the X or X’) (Gerbi, 158 

1986), some morph -specific expression was expected, especially in germline tissue where they should be 159 

acting. Supporting this, principal components analysis (PCA) showed samples first clustering by sex 160 

(male vs female), and subsequently by female morph identity (androgenic vs gynogenic). Differential 161 

gene expression (DGE) analysis of X -linked genes between gynogenic and androgenic females revealed 162 

significant differential expression in germline tissue (Fig. 2A, Fig. 2D, Table 1). Unexpectedly, we also 163 

see numerous differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in somatic reproductive (Fig. 2B, Fig. 2E, Table 1) 164 

and somatic non -reproductive tissues (Fig. 2C, Fig. 2F, Table 1). While differential expression in somatic 165 

reproductive tissue (e.g. accessory glands) may contribute to offspring sex determination, this is unlikely 166 

for somatic non -reproductive tissue. To clarify whether this dynamic reflects allele -biased expression in X 167 

and X’ gametologs, we carried out allele -specific expression analysis in gynogenic females. 168

Since maternal sex -determining genes are X -linked, our null expectation was for little to no DGE in 246 

autosomal genes; any autosomal differences would reflect trans‑regulatory effects from X/X′ and could 247

# 3. Discussion 341 

We have shown that in a monogenic species of fungus gnat, female -producing (gynogenic) females and 342 

male -producing (androgenic) females diverge phenotypically and transcriptionally in traits/tissues/gen es 343 

other than offspring sex determination. Phenotypically, gynogenic females are not less fit than androgenic 344 

females, despite being the carrier of the non -recombining X’ region. This suggests that it is unlikely for 345 

degradation of the X’ to be the only fo rce driving reproductive morph divergence. Supporting this, allele -346 

specific expression analysis between the X and X’ in gynogenic females revealed a significant number of 347 

genes with X’ -biased expression, which are also those with more conserved X’ gametolo gs relative to the 348 

X gametolog, suggesting that selection is maintaining the expression and function of a non -negligible 349 

number of genes on the inversion. The same genes are more likely to be upregulated in gynogenic 350 

females, potentially contributing to be tween -morph specialisation. While some of these genes may be 351 

involved in offspring sex determination, it is likely to be only a minority. 352 

353 

One of the more surprising findings is the significant number of autosomal DEGs between the female 354 

morphs, which are unlikely to be involved in offspring sex determination, especially in somatic non -355 

reproductive tissues, but are in line with the significant divergence in life history traits observed in the 356 

phenotypic assay. DGE in both sex chromosomes and autosomes mirro rs what is observed in species with 357 

dimorphic sexes (Ellegren and Parsch, 2007), supporting the hypothesis that the female morphs are 358 

specialising into their reproductive roles. The autosomal DEGs were not enriched for specific GO terms, 359 

suggesting a multi -trait specialisation also typical in sexual dimorphism (Parker et al., 1972; Mank, 2009; 360 

Barrett and Hough, 2013). Future work using organ -specific or cell -specific RNA -seq, as well as more 361 

lineage -specific functional annotations, could further help clari fy the traits involved in morph 362 

specialisation. This provides the first formal example of mating type specialisation at the maternal level, 363 

extending our existing understanding of reproductive specialisation. 364 

365 

However, the mechanism driving their divergenc e remains to be elucidated. We will first elaborate on the 366 

two hypotheses tested in this study —release from SAS and sex -specific maternal provisioning —before 367 

suggesting a third possible mechanism. Although theory suggests that the X’ being released from SA S368 

would result in feminisation of the gynogenic female transcriptome (Rice, 1984; Wright and Mank, 369 

2013), we did not find evidence for this. It is possible that specific genes are indeed evolving under this 370 

dynamic, but were not detectable using our method s. However, recent studies of transitions to asexual, 371 

female -only reproduction shows gene expression may change only in specific tissues, or can even be 372 

masculinised (Veltsos et al., 2017; Parker et al., 2019), suggesting that the relationship between sex -373

On the other hand, we find evidence for sex -specific maternal provisioning; instead of a handful of 381

Box 2: Hypothesis for turnover of monogeny and digeny in fungus gnats 440 

In this study, we have shown evidence for morph -specific divergence and specialisation in the 

monogenic B. coprophila . This informs a model for the extensive turnover of monogeny and digeny 

across the tree of fungus gnats. 

Step 1. We start with a digenic ancestor (yellow) that produces offspring of both sex, where the sex 

ratio is a polygenic trait. Maternal traits for producing sons or producing daughters may have divergent 

optima, causing disruptive selection for biased sex ratio in association with the beneficial maternal trait. 

This selection pressure results in female morph specialisation and split sex ratio, producing gynogenic 

females (in purple) and androgenic females (in green). Concurrently, there is also selection for linkage 

between alleles for extreme offspring sex ratios and the maternal trait beneficial for that sex. The result 

is a maternally -acting supergene (chromosomal region in purple) that determines offspring sex as well 

as a suite of other maternal traits. 

Step 2. Although recombina tion suppression allows beneficial traits to be inherited together, it also 

reduces the efficacy of selection against deleterious mutations. Additionally, the maternal supergene 

has one -sixth the N e of an autosome, driving deleterious mutation accumulation at a faster rate than the 

recombining X chromosome (red sites on the gynogenic -specific region). Although initially the 

deleterious effects may be compensated for by the beneficial impacts of specialisation, in the long term 

the mutation load borne by gyn ogenic females is likely to outweigh the benefits. Subsequently, the 

fitness of gynogenic females decreases relative to that of androgenic females. 

Step 3. Finally, as the relative fitness of these two female morphs directly correspond to population sex 

ratio, unequal fitness of the two morphs may result in local population extinction or reversion back to 

digeny. We end up at the start of the cycle.
