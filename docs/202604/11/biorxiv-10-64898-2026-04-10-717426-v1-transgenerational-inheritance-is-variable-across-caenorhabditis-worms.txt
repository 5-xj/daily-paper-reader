Abstract 22 

Transgenerational epigenetic inheritance (TEI) allows organisms to express heritable responses 23 

to environmental stresses, and can potentially contribute to adaptive evolution. The 24 

microbivorous model organism Caenorhabditis elegans is perhaps the best example, as it can 25 

learn to avoid pathogenic Pseudomonas bacteria and transmit this learned avoidance to its 26 

offspring. However, the extent to which TEI is widespread in nature remains unclear, and 27 

therefore our understanding of the generality of this response is limited. To address this, we 28 

conducted the first comparative study of TEI across five Caenorhabditis nematode worm species 29 

(C. kamaaina , C. elegans , C. tropicalis , C. remanei and C. briggsae ). These species differ in 30 

RNA interference competence and in the degree of sequence homology between Caenorhabditis 31 

worm genes and bacterial RNAs, two factors thought to influence epigenetic responses. We 32 

examined transgenerational avoidance of P. vranovensis , a pathogen that reduces fitness in all 33 

five species tested. In addition to C. elegans , we found that C. remanei also exhibited 34 

transgenerational avoidance of P. vranovensis , whereas neither learning nor inheritance was 35 

observed in the other three species. In addition, parental exposure to P. vranovensis also 36 

conferred a transgenerational survival benefit upon pathogen encounter in C. elegans , C. remanei 37 

and C. tropicalis . Our findings show that TEI of pathogen avoidance extends beyond C. elegans 38 

but is not a general response across Caenorhabditis species. This shows that TEI is a species-39 

specific response and highlights the need to understand TEI alongside other responses to 40 

environmental variability. 41 

Introduction 42 

Transgenerational epigenetic inheritance (TEI) is a form of between-generation phenotypic 43 

plasticity in which environmentally induced traits persist across multiple generations. It is 44 

mediated by epigenetic mechanisms such as small RNAs, chromatin modifications, and DNA 45 

methylation, and can be induced by a wide range of biotic and abiotic factors, often producing 46 

stressor-specific phenotypic effects (Webster and Phillips 2025; Adrian-Kalchhauser et al. 2020; 47 

Zhang and Tian 2022). Because TEI can persist across generations while arising much faster 48 

than genetic adaptation, it has the potential to influence evolutionary trajectories (Botero et al. 49 

2015; Ashe, Colot, and Oldroyd 2021; Lind and Spagopoulou 2018). An especially striking 50

example of TEI comes from Caenorhabditis elegans worms, which can learn to avoid lethal 51 

pathogenic food and transmit this learned avoidance to its naive progeny that would otherwise 52 

succumb to infection (Seto et al. 2025; Sengupta et al. 2024; Moore, Kaletsky, and Murphy 53 

2019). This example of TEI has focused on responses to Pseudomonas bacteria, which make up 54 

approximately one-third of these worms’ bacterial community (Samuel et al. 2016). While many 55 

Pseudomonas bacteria serve as nutritious food sources, P. aeruginosa (strain PA14), P. 56 

vranovensis (strain GRb0427) and P. fluorescens (strain PF15) are lethally pathogenic to C. 57 

elegans worms. Intriguingly, despite this danger, worms are inherently attracted to these 58 

pathogens, and only learn to avoid it after ingestion. More interestingly, this learned avoidance is 59 

transmitted to the offspring, who never encountered these pathogens and thus would otherwise 60 

exhibit an inherent attraction and suffer from the detrimental consequences (Seto et al. 2025; 61 

Sengupta et al. 2024; Moore, Kaletsky, and Murphy 2019). 62 

Both learning and TEI of pathogen avoidance in C. elegans is mediated by bacterial small RNAs 63 

that are processed via the worms’ RNA interference (RNAi) machinery. These bacterial small 64 

RNAs; P11 ( P. aeruginosa ), Pv1 ( P. vranovensis ), and Pfs1 ( P. fluorescens ), exhibit a 16 to 17-65 

nucleotide homology to two neuronal genes in C. elegans ; maco-1 (neuronal homolog of the 66 

human nervous-system-specific ER membrane protein Macoilin; target of P11 and Pv1) and vab-67 

1 (encodes an ephrin receptor, upstream of maco-1 ; target of Pfs1). Upon ingestion, bacterial 68 

small RNAs are processed in the intestine by the DCR-1 (Dicer) endoribonuclease and taken up 69 

via the dsRNA transporter SID-2. They are then transported to germline by the systematic RNAi 70 

channel SID-1, and subsequently from the germline to neurons via Cer1 retrotransposon-encoded 71 

virus-like particles (Moore et al. 2021). This leads to down-regulation of vab-1 and maco-1 ,72 

which in turn leads to up-regulation of daf-7 in the ASI neurons and thereby the behavioral 73 

change from attraction to avoidance (Kaletsky et al. 2020; Sengupta et al. 2024; Seto et al. 2025). 74 

However, the prevalence of TEI in nature is still elusive. Epigenetic modifications exhibit 75 

remarkable variation, and much of this variation is jointly shaped by the environment and the 76 

genome (Baduel et al. 2024). Epigenetic inheritance, therefore, is likely a unique interaction 77 

between the species and its environment. Caenorhabditis worms occupy diverse ecological 78 

niches and likely have shared and private microbial communities in nature (Berg et al. 2016; 79 

Félix and Duveau 2012). Differences in ecological context may therefore generate variation in 80

defense mechanisms across nematode species. A previous study showed that different worm 81 

species exhibit different levels of resistance against P. vranovensis strain BIGb0446 (Nicholas O. 82 

Burton et al. 2021), suggesting that while some nematode species may rely on TEI-mediated 83 

defenses to avoid pathogenic food, others may have evolved immunity against it. Nonetheless, 84 

most studies use a lab-adapted strain, C. elegans N2 (Moore, Kaletsky, and Murphy 2019; 85 

Kaletsky et al. 2020; Seto et al. 2025; but see also Moore et al. 2021; Sengupta et al. 2024), 86 

making it difficult to assess the importance of TEI in nature. 87 

The choice of pathogenic bacterial species in the assays further complicates the picture. Without 88 

using bacterial species of clear ecological relevance to nematodes, it is impossible to assess the 89 

adaptive value of the reported TEI responses. Nonetheless, while P. vranovensis GRb0427 is a 90 

natural isolate from the C. elegans microbiota (Samuel et al. 2016), other bacterial species used 91 

to study pathogen-induced TEI are not necessarily part of the natural habitat of Caenorhabditis 92 

worms. For example, while most research efforts have focused on P. aeruginosa PA14, this 93 

clinical isolate has not been detected in environmental sampling or microbiome studies despite 94 

extensive efforts, and it is unclear whether worms encounter it in nature (Sengupta et al. 2024). 95 

Other bacteria, such as Salmonella enterica Typhimurium MST1 and P. aeruginosa PAO1 96 

(Gabaldon et al. 2020; Palominos et al. 2017), also lack clear ecological relevance to worms. 97 

We set out to test the potential adaptive value of TEI. We hypothesized that TEI of pathogen 98 

avoidance should evolve as a defense mechanism in worms susceptible to P. vranovensis , as it 99 

would allow the offspring to preemptively avoid pathogenic food sources and thus confers a 100 

selective advantage. Specifically, we tested how widespread TEI is across related species, and 101 

how TEI responses affect fitness. We focus on P. vranovensis GRb0427 since it is the species 102 

which is known to be part of the ecological environment of C. elegans (Samuel et al. 2016). We 103 

investigated five Caenorhabditis species, C. kamaaina (QG122), C. elegans (N2), C. tropicalis 104 

(JU1373), C. remanei (PB4641) and C. briggsae (AF16), each representing an independent 105 

branch of the Elegans group (Fusca et al. 2025). We find TEI of pathogen avoidance in C. 106 

remanei females, in addition to the previously reported case in C. elegans (Sengupta et al. 2024). 107 

Our results therefore argue against the idea that this TEI paradigm is an irreproducible artifact. 108 

Moreover, parental exposure to P. vranovensis not only induced behavioral change in the 109 

offspring but also conferred survival benefit transgenerationally in both species. This suggests 110

Results 115 

P. vranovensis is pathogenic to Caenorhabditis worms 116 

We first tested whether P. vranovensis is pathogenic to Caenorhabditis worms. Age-117 

synchronized worms were raised to pre-adult stage (L4) and then transferred to plates seeded 118 

with either E. coli and P. vranovensis (hereafter E-trained worms and P-trained worms, 119 

respectively; Figure 1). After 24 hours of training on either bacteria, adult hermaphrodites or 120 

inseminated females were washed and transferred to 35-mm NGM plates seeded with E. coli .121 

Worms were allowed to lay eggs and were transferred to fresh plates every 24 hours. Age-122 

specific fecundity was measured as the number of larvae produced each day. For each individual, 123 

we calculated an individual-based, rate-sensitive estimation of absolute fitness (λind), which 124 

combines lifetime fertility and reproductive schedule (Lind et al. 2016). Since Caenorhabditis 125 

nematodes have a boom-and-burst population dynamics in nature (Frezal and Félix 2015), early 126 

fecundity is crucial for rapid expansion on ephemeral food sources. 127 

Exposure to P. vranovensis (strain GRb0427) reduced individual fitness (λind) in all five species 128 

tested, C. kamaaina , C. elegans , C. tropicalis , C. remanei and C. briggsae (Table 1), indicating 129 

that P. vranovensis is a general pathogen across Caenorhabditis species. P. vranovensis also 130 

significantly reduced age-specific fecundity in C. kamaaina and C. briggsae (Figure 2; Table 2). 131 

In contrast, P. vranovensis exposure did not affect age-specific fecundity in C. elegans , C. 132 

tropicalis , and C. remanei (Figure 2; Table 2). 133 

The reduction in λind in P-trained worms compared to E-trained worms, despite no significant 134 

effect on age-specific fecundity in C. elegans , C. tropicalis , and C. remanei , likely reflects the 135 

fact that λind combines overall fecundity and reproductive schedule and thus weights early 136 

reproduction more heavily. In these species, fecundity decreases with age, and P-trained worms 137 

had lower fecundity on the first day of assay (the second day of adulthood) but not on subsequent 138

TEI mechanisms varies across Caenorhabditis worms 143 

The fact that P. vranovensis is pathogenic across Caenorhabditis species suggests that worms 144 

may have evolved mechanisms to defend against it. We therefore investigated the presence of the 145 

mechanisms implicated in the TEI of pathogen avoidance, specifically Pv1-maco -1 sequence 146 

homology, which is necessary for worms to interpret exogenous RNAs. We extended this 147 

analysis across species of the Elegans group. 148 

We found that only C. elegans maco-1 exhibited a perfect 16-nucleotide homology with the 149 

bacterial small RNA Pv1. Previous study showed that a four-nucleotide mismatch is sufficient to 150 

abolish the inheritance of learned avoidance (Sengupta et al. 2024); therefore, we considered 151 

sequences with at most three mismatches. C. remanei showed a single nucleotide mismatch, 152 

whereas C. briggsae contained three mismatches. No detectable homology was observed in C. 153 

kamaaina and C. tropicalis .154 

A previous study has shown that the capacity to silence endogenous genes via ingested 155 

exogenous RNAs (RNAi competence) varies across Caenorhabditis worms (Nuez and Félix 156 

2012) Within the Elegans group, RNAi competence has been reported in C. elegans , C. wallacei 157 

and C. kamaaina ((Nuez and Félix 2012); Figure 3). 158

P. vranovensis induces TEI of learned avoidance in C. elegans and C. remanei 159 

Previous publications suggest the inclusion of sodium azide during assays measuring C. elegans ’160 

attraction or avoidance to the pathogens, because sodium azide immobolizes C. elegans and 161 

serves to preserve their first choice of food upon arrival. Our pilot results, however, suggest that 162 

worms did not readily leave their first-choice spot within the assay period (see Materials and 163 

Methods; Figure 7; Table 4). We first tested the effect of potassium azide as an alternative to 164 

sodium azide, due to local chemical safety regulations, and obtained results in C. elegans 165 

consistent with previous studies. We then show that our findings are reproducible in C. elegans 166 

both with and without the inclusion of azide during choice assays (Figure 7; Table 4). Because 167 

our aim is to examine the extent to which TEI is widespread in nature, and neither sodium azide 168 

nor potassium azide are naturally occurring chemicals, all the following assays were without 169 

potassium azide to reflect worms’ natural response. 170

We examined whether P. vranovensis exposure could induce learned avoidance in five wildtype 171 

Caenorhabditis species; C. kamaaina , C. elegans , C. tropicalis , C. remanei and C. briggsae. C. 172 

kamaaina and C. remanei are gonochoristic, we therefore analysed each sex separately. C. 173 

elegans , C. tropicalis , and C. briggsae are primarily self-fertilizing hermaphrodites (Noble et al. 174 

2021; Hodgkin, Horvitz, and Brenner 1979; LaMunyon and Ward 1997) and were not sexed. 175 

We found that, C. elegans parents showed learned avoidance of P. vranovensis after 24 hours of 176 

pathogen training (Figure 4; F(1, 46) = 22.36, p < 0.001), consistent with previous studies 177 

(Sengupta et al. 2024). Given that C. elegans populations consist predominantly of 178 

hermaphrodites, these behaviors likely reflect responses in hermaphrodites. We could not assess 179 

male responses to P. vranovensis , as individuals were not sexed; however, given the size 180 

difference between sexes, we believe all worms were hermaphrodites. Nonetheless, a previous 181 

study reported that naive C. elegans males avoid a closely related pathogen, P. aeruginosa 182 

(Moore, Kaletsky, and Murphy 2019). No significant behavioral changes were observed in the 183 

other two hermaphroditic species, C. tropicalis (F(1, 72) = 0.45, p = 0.505) and C. briggsae (F(1, 184 

71) = 2.27, p = 0.136). 185

Lastly, we asked whether parental exposure to P. vranovensis had transgenerational fitness 209 

effect. We have already shown an adaptive TEI response that helps offspring avoid the pathogen; 210 

we therefore tested whether there is any transgenerational effect when the pathogen is 211 

unavoidable. 212 

We measured survival of newly hatched F2 larvae on P. vranovensis . We reasoned that under 213 

unavoidable exposure, parental worms lay eggs directly onto the pathogen, and thus offspring 214 

survival provides a direct measure of transgenerational fitness effect independent of avoidance 215 

behavior. 216 

Indeed, when hatched on a P. vranovensis bacterial lawn (Figure 1), F2 offspring of P-trained 217 

parents showed significantly improved survival on P. vranovensis than offspring of E-trained 218 

worms in C. elegans , C. remanei , and C. tropicalis , whereas no survival differences were 219 

observed in C. kamaaina or C. briggsae (Figure 6; Table 3). 220

Discussion 221 

We showed that exposure to P. vranovensis reduced fitness in five Caenorhabditis species, C. 222 

kamaaina , C. elegans , C. tropicalis , C. remanei and C. briggsae (Table 1; Figure 2; Table 2). 223 

This suggests that these species may be under selection to evolve TEI-based defenses if they 224 

encounter it in the wild. Interestingly, susceptibility does not perfectly predict with the presence 225 

of the TEI mechanisms: most species are either RNAi incompetent (unable to silence 226 

endogenous gene expression by ingested exogenous RNAs), or lack Pv1-maco-1 sequence 227 

homology (a 16‑nt motif required for pathogen avoidance), or both (Figure 3). This implies that 228 

TEI mechanisms may be functionally redundant. 229 

We then examined learned and inherited behavioral response in the P0 and F2 generations in 230 

these species (Figure 4; Figure 5). We found that in C. elegans , P0 worms learned to avoid P. 231 

vranovensis and F2 worms exhibited inherited avoidance, both of which consistent with previous 232 

results (Sengupta et al. 2024). Interestingly, in C. remanei , the within-generation and 233 

transgenerational responses appear to be decoupled, with P0 females showing increased 234 

attraction to P. vranovensis after training (a seemingly maladaptive parental response) while F2 235 

females exhibit avoidance (Figure 4; Figure 5). This suggests that inherited responses may not 236 

simply mirror parental plastic behavior, but instead reflect a distinct adaptive process. More 237 

intriguingly, this effect appears to be female-specific, for C. remanei males showed no learned or 238 

inherited response in both P0 and F2 generations (Figure 8). No significant behavioral changes 239 

were observed in both the P0 and the F2 generations in C. kamaaina , C. tropicalis and C. 240 

briggsae (Figure 4; Figure 5; C. kamaaina males: Figure 9). 241

Finally, we investigated whether parental stress has broader transgenerational consequences. We 242 

found that parental exposure to P. vranovensis also significantly increased F2 survival on P. 243 

vranovensis in C. elegans and C. remanei (Figure 6; Table 3). Interestingly, similar survival 244 

benefit was also observed in C. tropicalis (Figure 6; Table 3), although C. tropicalis did not 245 

show learnt or inherited behavioral avoidance against P. vranovensis . Overall, our results 246 

indicate that epigenetic mechanisms likely mediate multivariate responses in a species-specific 247 

manner, whereby different species evolve distinct responses shaped by their evolutionary 248 

histories and microbial environments. 249 

TEI of pathogen avoidance may be shaped by species-specific microbiota 250 

Overall, our results showed that susceptibility to P. vranovensis did not predict the presence of 251 

TEI of pathogen avoidance. While exposure to P. vranovensis reduced fitness in all five 252 

Caenorhabditis species tested, C. kamaaina , C. tropicalis and C. briggsae failed to show learned 253 

avoidance, both within-generation and transgenerationally. 254 

We speculate that this may be due to worms’ diverse ecological niches and their shared and 255 

private microbial communities in nature. At a broad geographical scale, C. briggsae is 256 

cosmopolitan, while C. elegans and C. remanei are primarily temperate species, and C. 257 

kamaaina and C. tropicalis are mainly found in the tropics (Kiontke et al. 2011). At a local scale, 258 

C. elegans and C. remanei are often found in the same sites, albeit in different substrates 259 

(Petersen et al. 2014), while C. briggsae and C. tropicalis likely share habitats (Timothy A. 260 

Crombie et al. 2022; Tim A. Crombie et al. 2019). Moreover, in locations where C. elegans and 261 

C. briggsae co-occur, their seasonality only partially overlaps (Félix and Duveau 2012). 262 

C. elegans and C. remanei thus may encounter similar microbial communities, which may differ 263 

from those experienced by C. briggsae and C. tropicalis . The TEI of avoidance of P. vranovensis 264 

observed in C. elegans and C. remanei , but not in C. briggsae or C. tropicalis , may therefore 265 

reflect differences in ecological exposure to this pathogen. Indeed, Caenorhabditis worms 266 

inhabit ephemeral, microbe-rich substrates and encounter diverse bacterial communities in nature 267 

(Ferrari et al. 2017; Sloat et al. 2022; Frezal and Félix 2015). Although Pseudomonas species 268 

appear to be a frequent member of natural C. elegans microbiomes (Haghani et al. 2024; Samuel 269 

et al. 2016), it is possible that C. briggsae or C. tropicalis do not typically encounter P. 270

vranovensis in their natural habitats, or do so only infrequently or irregularly (Berg et al. 2016) 271 

and selection for a defense mechanism therefore is weak. 272 

If the evolution of transgenerational avoidance depends on the frequency and predictability with 273 

which particular pathogens are encountered in the natural environments of different 274 

Caenorhabditis species, one would expect a highly specific interaction between hosts and 275 

pathogens. Indeed, in C. elegans the ED3040 strain fails to learn avoidance of P. aeruginosa but 276 

can learn to avoid P. vranovensis (Sengupta et al. 2024; Moore, Kaletsky, and Murphy 2019). 277 

Moreover, worms also show distinct responses to different isolates of the same Pseudomonas 278 

species. For instance, parental exposure to P. vranovensis strains BIGb446 and BIGb468 has 279 

been reported to decrease F2 survival in C. briggsae but increase survival in C. elegans and C. 280 

kamaaina (Nicholas O. Burton et al. 2021; Nicholas O. Burton et al. 2020). In contrast, in our 281 

experiments parental exposure to P. vranovensis strain GRb0427 increased offspring survival in 282 

C. elegans but had no detectable effect in C. kamaaina or C. briggsae . Together, these findings 283 

highlight the potential for ecological context to shape transgenerational responses and suggest 284 

that future studies of natural microbiome will provide key insights into the evolution of adaptive 285 

strategies. 286 

Partial redundancy of TEI mechanisms 287 

Our results suggest that Pv1-maco -1 sequence homology between the small RNA of the 288 

pathogen (Pv 1) and the maco-1 gene in the worm is necessary for the transmission of learned 289 

avoidance, but RNAi competence (the ability to take up RNAi through feeding) may be 290 

functionally redundant. Both C. elegans and C. remanei exhibit perfect or near perfect Pv1-291 

maco -1 sequence homology but C. remanei is RNAi incompetent. On the other hand, C. 292 

kamaaina , C. briggsae and C. tropicalis either lack a Pv1-maco -1 sequence homology ( C. 293 

kamaaina and C. tropicalis ) or exhibit three mismatches in the corresponding Pv1-homologous 294 

region ( C. briggsae ; Figure 3). 295 

Alternative machineries may function alongside RNAi. dcr-1 , sid-1 , and sid-2 are key 296 

components of the canonical RNAi pathway. When exposed to intact bacterial lawns, sid-2 and 297 

dcr-1 mutants were still able to learn avoidance (Kaletsky et al. 2020), whereas exposure to 298 

purified bacterial small RNAs required dcr-1 , sid-1 , and sid-2 for learning (Kaletsky et al. 2020; 299

Sex-specific expression of an epigenetic trait 303 

Our results show that TEI of pathogen avoidance is expressed in females in the gonochoristic 304 

species C. remanei and likely reflects hermaphrodite-specific responses in the predominantly 305 

self-fertile species C. elegans .306 

Sex-specific expression may arise because males and hermaphrodites differ in how they respond 307 

to microbial challenge. In C. elegans , some immune responses are more robust in males, whereas 308 

hermaphrodites benefit more from protective microbes under pathogen challenge (Sohn et al. 309 

2025; Kloock, Peters, and Rafaluk-Mohr 2021). This suggests that hermaphrodites may rely 310 

more strongly on alternative defense strategies, like microbe-mediated protection. We 311 

hypothesize that transgenerational pathogen avoidance another strategy on which 312 

females/hermaphrodites rely to a greater extent than males. 313 

It is important to note that the transmission and expression of an epigenetic trait may represent 314 

two separate processes. While we showed that the expression of pathogen avoidance is likely 315 

female- and hermaphrodite-specific, this trait can be transmitted through both male and female 316 

germlines in C. elegans (Moore, Kaletsky, and Murphy 2019). How transmission occurs in C. 317 

remanei remains unclear; however, it is likely that gonochoristic species benefit more from 318 

transmission through both germlines. Caenorhabditis species often colonize new environments 319 

with only a few individuals (Frezal and Félix 2015). In hermaphroditic species such as C. 320 

elegans , founding individuals can transmit the epigenetic trait by self-fertilizing regardless of 321 

whether the trait is passed on via sperm or oocytes (Sabaris, Fitz-James, and Cavalli 2023). 322 

Indeed, in C. elegans , diet-induced adaptations appear to be transmitted in a sex-specific manner, 323 

with sperm primarily passing on sperm-specific adaptations while oocytes primarily passing on 324 

oocyte-specific adaptations (Pete and Hunter 2025). In gonochoristic species, however, mating 325 

with naive partners may abolish or dilute the acquired trait. These observations lead to several 326 

non-mutually exclusive predictions; for example, TEI may evolve more readily in 327 

hermaphroditic species than in gonochoristic species. 328

Situating transgenerational epigenetic inheritance within adaptive responses to 329 

environmental change 330 

The fact that transgenerational responses varied across species and showed a complex 331 

relationship with susceptibility to P. vranovensis raises the question of how TEI should be 332 

understood within the broader context of organismal responses to environmental variability. 333 

Theory suggests that two features of environmental variation are especially important here: the 334 

rate of environmental change relative to generation time, and the reliability of environmental 335 

cues as predictors of future conditions. Within this framework, TEI can be favoured when 336 

parental environments provide reliable information about conditions beyond the directly exposed 337 

generation, that is, when environmental states remain sufficiently stable or predictable across 338 

generations (Botero et al. 2015; Tufto 2015; Vinton et al. 2022; Lind and Spagopoulou 2018). 339 

Our results, showing that TEI is not a uniform or general response to pathogen exposure across 340 

Caenorhabditis , could therefore suggest either that the selective conditions favouring the 341 

evolution of TEI arose only in the species in which it was detected, or that similar conditions 342 

may have existed more broadly but TEI failed to emerge in the other species because of 343 

alternative defensive strategies, mechanistic constraints, or differences in ecological history. 344 

More broadly, our study provides an important comparative step toward understanding the 345 

evolutionary role of TEI in Caenorhabditis by linking cross-species variation in 346 

transgenerational responses to their potential fitness consequences. 347 

Future work 348 

Our findings raise questions about the evolutionary role of TEI. Future work should expand our 349 

comparative analyses across additional Caenorhabditis species (for example, species in the 350 

Japonica group) to determine how widespread pathogen-induced TEI is. Studies of natural 351 

microbial environments may then reveal whether variation in pathogen exposure predicts the 352 

presence or strength of parental responses. Finally, experimental evolution under predictable 353 

versus unpredictable pathogen environments could directly test whether and when selection 354 

favours the emergence, maintenance or decline of TEI. 355

Materials and methods 356 

Worm maintenance 357 

The following worm species were used: C. kamaaina (QG122), C. elegans (N2), C. tropicalis 358 

(JU1373), C. remanei (PB4641) and C. briggsae (AF16). For all species, stock worms were 359 

maintained on 90-mm plates containing nematode growth medium (NGM; 3 g/L NaCl, 2.5 g/L 360 

Bacto-peptone, 17 g/L Bacto-agar in distilled water, with the addition of 1 mL/L cholesterol (5 361 

mg/mL in ethanol), 1 mL/L 1M CaCl2, 1 mL/L 1M MgSO4, and 25 mL/L 1M potassium 362 

phosphate buffer (pH 6.0) after autoclaving) or high growth medium (HGM; 3 g/l NaCl, 20 g/l 363 

bacto-peptone, 30 g/l bacto-agar in distilled water, with the addition of 4 mL/L cholesterol (5 364 

mg/mL in ethanol), 1 mL/L 1 M CaCl2, 1 mL/L 1 M MgSO4 and 25 mL/L 1 M KPO4 buffer 365 

(pH 6.0) after autoclaving) seeded with 1 mL E. coli OP50 at 20°C throughout the study. 366 

Bacterial culture 367 

E. coli OP50 and Pseudomonas vranovensis GRb0427 were used in this study. To prepare liquid 368 

bacterial culture, 6 mL sterile LB broth (10 g/l tryptone + 5 g/l yeast extract + 10 g/l NaCl in 369 

distilled water) in a 15-mL Falcon tube was inoculated with a single bacterial colony picked 370 

from streak plates. Bacterial culture were then grown on a shaker at 37°C. 371 

Fecundity assay 372 

Twenty-five microliters of E. coli OP50 (OD₆₀₀ = 0.5) was seeded onto 35-mm NGM plates to 373 

form a single bacterial lawn in the center of each plate. One-day-old hermaphrodites or females 374 

with visible mating plugs were washed in M9 buffer and then individually transferred to each 375 

plate. Worms were allowed to lay eggs for 24 hours and were then transferred daily to fresh, 376 

seeded plates for three consecutive days. For each day of the assay, after the mothers were 377 

removed, plates were incubated for 48 hours to allow the offspring to hatch and develop into 378 

larvae. Plates were then heated at 85°C for 15 minutes to kill the offspring worms, and age-379 

specific fecundity was measured as the number of larvae produced each day. 380 

F2 survival assay 381 

One milliliter of an overnight culture of P. vranovensis GRb0427 was seeded onto 60-mm NGM 382 

plates with the bacterial lawn covering the entire surface. Nematode eggs were bleached onto 383

Bacterial training 386 

Bacterial training and aversive learning assay followed Moore et al., 2021 and Sengupta et al., 387 

2024 with modifications. To prepare bacterial training plates, overnight bacterial culture of E. 388 

coli and P. vranovensis were standardized in sterile LB broth to an Optical Density (OD₆₀₀) of 389 

0.5, and 1 mL of standardized bacterial culture was seeded onto a 90-mm NGM plates with the 390 

bacterial lawn covering the entire surface. Plates were dried under a sterile hood and then 391 

incubated at 25°C for 48 hours before being used. 392 

To prepare P0 worms, healthy populations (i.e., no contamination or crowding, at least one week 393 

after thawing) were bleached to derive synchronized eggs. Eggs are plated onto NGM or HGM 394 

plates seeded with E. coli OP50 and allowed to develop larval stage 4 (L4; c.a. 48-52 hours post 395 

synchronization). 396 

On the day of training, all training plates were equilibrated to room temperature (c.a. 20°C) for 397 

about 1 hour before the addition of worms. Synchronized L4 worms were washed twice and 398 

transferred to training plates. Specifically, worms were washed off plates using c.a. 5 mL M9 399 

buffer (6 g/L Na2HPO4, 3 g/L KH2PO4, 5 g/L NaCl and 1 mL/L 1M MgSO4 in distilled water) 400 

into a 15-mL Falcon tube and allowed to settle by gravity (< 5 minutes). Once worms were 401 

settled, the supernatant was discarded, and 5 mL of fresh M9 buffer was added to each tube to 402 

remove residual bacteria from their body surfaces. Total washing time was kept under 30 403 

minutes to minimize the risk of stressing or starving the worms. 404 

After removing the supernatant of the second wash, a small amount of M9 buffer (c.a. 200-500 405 

μl) was added to resuspend the worm pellet. Worms were then pipetted to the training plates. For 406 

E. coli training, a maximum of 800 worms were transferred to each training plate to avoid 407 

crowding or running out of food. For P. vranovensis training, c.a. 1600 worms were added to 408 

each training plate to account for the mortality. Worms were incubated on training plates for 24 409 

hours at 20°C. All training plates were right side up, but plates of different training treatments 410 

were stored in separate containers. 411

Aversive learning assay 412 

To prepare bacterial choice assay plates, 25 μl of standardized bacterial culture (OD₆₀₀ = 0.5) of 413 

E. coli or P. vranovensis was spotted onto opposite sides of a 60-mm NGM plate, with 414 

approximately 2 cm distance between the two spots. The left/right positioning of each bacterial 415 

species was alternated between plates. Plates were dried under a sterile hood and then incubated 416 

at 25°C for 48 hours prior to use. 417 

On the day of the assay, plates were equilibrated to room temperature for about 1 hour before the 418 

addition of worms. Worms were washed as described in Bacterial training; however, the 419 

supernatant from the first wash was retained for the collection of the next generation of 420 

offspring. 421 

For aversive learning assays testing the effect of potassium azide, 1 μl of 1 M potassium azide 422 

was pipetted onto each bacterial spot immediately before the assay. In all other assays, no 423 

potassium azide was added. Washed worms are transferred in M9 to the bottom center of the 424 

choice plate, positioned midway and equidistant between the two bacterial spots. Excessive M9 425 

was carefully removed using a Kimwipe to release the worms. Plates were then incubated 426 

undisturbed in the dark for one hour. 427 

After incubation, plates were heated at 85°C for 15 minutes to kill the worms to prevent further 428 

movement. For assays using potassium azide, we counted all worms located within 2 mm of each 429 

bacterial lawn. For assays without azide, we counted worms inside the bacterial lawn as well as 430 

those within 1 mm of the lawn edge. Choice index was calculated for each replicate as (# of 431 

worms on E. coli − # of worms on P. vranovensis )/(total # of worms). When necessary, images 432 

of bacterial spots were captured using a camera mounted on a Nikon SMZ1270 microscope with 433 

NIS-Elements software, and worms were counted from images using the open-source FIJI 434 

software. 435

Sequence analysis 442 

We aimed to identify Caenorhabditis species whose maco-1 ortholog contained the previously 443 

reported 16‑nt sequence homology between the bacterial Pv1 small RNA and the C. elegans 444 

maco‑1 gene. We focused on species within the Elegans group. We first retrieved maco-1 445

nucleotide mismatches. For each match, we documented the number of mismatches, the matched 447
