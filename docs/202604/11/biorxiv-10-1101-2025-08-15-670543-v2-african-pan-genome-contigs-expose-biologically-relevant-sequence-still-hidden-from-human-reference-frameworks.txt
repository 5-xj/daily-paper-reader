# Abstract 25 

Human reference genomes underpin biomedical discovery but remain incomplete and biased toward 26 

European populations, constraining interpretation of genetic variation in underrepresented populations. 27 

Here we characterize African Pan Genome (APG) contigs tota ling 296.5 Mb to define the sequence and 28 

functional landscape of genomic regions absent from current references. Most contigs align to the 29 

telomere -to -telomere (T2T -CHM13) genome and across 47 haplotype -resolved Human Pangenome 30 

Reference Consortium (HPRC) assemblies, with T2T -CHM13 placements enriched in centromeric and 31 

satellite repeats and overlapping 373 genes, including disease -associated loci. Mapping across HPRC 32 

assemblies revealed ancestry -associated contig enrichment, particularly in African genomes . Notably, 33 

742 contigs remained unmapped under both stringent and relaxed criteria. These sequences are largely 34 

nonrepetitive and exhibit strong functional potential, including predicted protein -coding genes, CpG 35 

islands and transcriptional activity. Toget her, these results demonstrate that functionally relevant, 36 

ancestry -enriched genomic sequences remain absent from current references, with important 37 

implications for disease variant interpretation and precision medicine. 38

# Introduction 45 

The human reference genome determines which sequences align, what variants are called and 46 

annotated, and ultima tely what biological data becomes visible to genomic research studies. Despite the 47 

availability of newer reference genome assemblies, GRCh37 and GRCh38 remain the primary references 48 

used for sequence alignment in human genomics research and clinical practi ce 1,2 . With unresolved gaps 49 

comprising approximately 7% of the GRCh38 genomes 3,4 , it reflects a limited view of human diversity, 50 

prima rily focusing on European ancestry 5. This Eurocentric reference bias affects genomic medicine by 51 

essentially rendering data that is relevant to specific ancestry groups invisible 1,2,6,7 . A clear illustration of 52 

this bias comes from genome -wide association studies (GWAS) that use platforms designed from a 53 

Eurocentric reference, have historically been conducted in primarily European po pulations, and lead to 54 

limited applicable replication across any other ancestries 8–11 . This underrepresentation can hinder the 55 

mapping of genetic risk alleles, our understanding of r egulatory gene networks that define disease biology 56 

and therefore limit the precision of genomic medicine. 57 

Advances in long -read sequencing are transformative for genome science by overcoming the limitations 58 

of existing human reference genomes, resolving previously inaccessible regions of the genome. The 59 

Telomere -to -Telomere (T2T -CHM13) assembly provides a complete, gapless linear reference genome, 60 

including centromeres and segmental duplications 3. However, both GRCh38 and T2T -CHM13 remain 61 

limited in ancestral diversity 5,12 . More recently, the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium (HPRC) 62 

has introduced a more diverse reference framework, in itially generating 47 assemblies with wider 63 

representation across genetic ancestry groups. These assemblies also capture population structural 64 

variation allowing the building of a pangenome graph that offers better sensitivity in variant calling across 65 

div erse populations 4,13 .66 

The African Pan Genome (APG) study by Sherman et al 14  provided a powerful test case for this challenge 67 

by identifyin g 296.5 million base pairs missing from GRCh38, prior to the release of the T2T -CHM13 and 68 

HPRC reference genomes. This landmark study performed whole -genome sequencing of 910 individuals 69 

of African descent, and subsequently assembled unmapped GRCh38 reads into 125,715 contigs 14 . While 70 

these contigs revealed previously uncharacterized genomic content specific to individuals of African 71 

ancestry, their biological importance and overlap with newer referenc e builds remained unclear. 72 

In this study, we investigated whether unresolved APG contig sequences, absent in GRCh38, reflected 73 

low -complexity sequence of limited consequence or biologically meaningful genomic content still 74 

excluded from modern reference f rameworks. We asked three questions: (1) how much ancestry -75

To determine how much anc estry -enriched sequence remains outside of the current reference genome 87 

frameworks, we aligned 124,240 contigs assembled by Sherman et. al 14  against T2T -CHM13 and 47 88 

HPRC linear assemblies and then la yered functional annotation, repeat analysis, and RNA -seq evidence 89 

onto mapped and unmapped subsets (see Methods, Figure 1, Figure S1) . This design allowed us to 90 

distinguish sequences recovered by modern references from sequences that remain unresolved eve n91

APG contigs map to T2T -CHM13 regions absent from GRCh38 93 

We first determined whether the 124,240 APG contig sequences missing from GRCh38 were recovered 94 

in the gapless T2T -CHM13 reference assembly. We found that 3 9.50% (49,070/124,240) of the APGs 95

alignments mapping to regions unique from GRCh38 (Figure 2A -B, Tables S1 -3) . Using the more 97 

relaxed Reasonably Good (RG) threshold increases the recovery of APG contigs mapping by 47.77% 98 

and likely reflects complexity in variation not represented as much in the assemblies available. The 99 

highest number of alignments to T2T -CHM13 were in Chromosomes 9, 21, and 14, and the least number 100 

of alignments to Chromosomes 18, 16 and Y (Figure 2C) . As anticipated, recovery was driven largely by 101 

previously unplaced contigs, where neither end of the contig sequence was previously resolved to a 102 

location on GRCh38 14  (Figure 2D) , underscoring how unmappable sequence to legacy references 103 

misses real sequence. 104

linear references, including complete genomes from African popul ations. Of the 47 HPRC assemblies, 157

respectively) (Table S11 -S13) .167

APG contig alignments to HPRC assemblies reveal population -associated enrichment 168 

Comparing NP alignment results across reference assemblies, 49,020 contigs mapped to both T2T -169 

CH M13 and the HPRC linear assemblies, whereas 53,983 contigs showed NP alignment exclusively to 170 

specific HPRC assemblies (Figure 4A) . Among these HPRC -only contigs, we observed a notable 171 

enrichment of alignments to assemblies from the AFR superpopulation (n = 7,969 ) ( p < 2.2 e -16), 172 

whereas alignments to the other reference superpopulations were less frequent (Figures 4B, Figure 173 

S5) . Odds were 1.53x higher than to American (AMR) assemblies, and substantially greater versus East 174 

Asian (EAS, OR=2.74), European ( EUR, OR=7.23), and South Asian (SAS, OR=8.55) assemblies 175 

(Figure 4B) .176 

Hierarchical clustering of the alignment patterns further revealed ancestry -associated grouping of 177 

individuals, with AFR assemblies clustering together and AMR assemblies forming a disti nct secondary 178 

cluster of contig alignments (Figure 4E) . Unsupervised clustering of alignment patterns further 179 

highlighted contigs that mapped predominantly or exclusively to the AFR and AMR populations (Figure 180 

S5A) . The largest overlap between any two supe rpopulations consisted of 8,006 contigs that aligned to 181 

both the AFR and Admixed American (AMR) populations ( Figure S5B ). Although, AFR and AMR 182 

assemblies are more heavily represented in the HPRC v1 dataset (n = 24 and n = 16, respectively), this 183 

contig al ignment overlap supports the population -associated nature of the APG contigs and suggests 184 

shared ancestry signals or structural variation between the AFR and AMR superpopulations. In contrast, 185 

shared mappings to the EAS, EUR, and SAS populations were consi derably smaller, possibly indicating 186 

a limited representation of APG contigs within these genomes (Figures 4B -C) .187

# Discussion 268 

Human reference genomes are foundational to genetic discovery, yet they are incomplete and 269 

underrepresent global genomic diversity, particularly African diversity. A central implication of this study 270 

is that completeness and representativeness are distinct . While T2T -CHM13 resolves sequence gaps 271 

and HPRC improves recovery of ancestry -enriched APG contigs, neither fully captures the functional 272 

breadth of human genomic diversity. Sequences present only in HPRC assemblies or remaining 273 

unresolved are particular ly consequential, as they are largely invisible to standard pipelines for read 274 

alignment, annotation, and variant interpretation. Recent studies now make this point harder to dismiss 275 

as a purely technical concern. Inclusive graph -based references reduce re ference bias 24 , ancestry -276 

matched references alter scRNA -seq gene assignment 25 , diverse long -read transcriptomics reveals 277 

systematic ancestry bias in gene annotation 21,26 , and pangenome -based analyses improve sensitivity for 278 

disease -relevant structural variants 27,28 . In this context , missing reference sequence represents a 279 

mechanism by which biologically relevant variation is systemically obscured, particularly in populations 280 

historically underrepresented in reference resource 29 . This distinction is critical as African genomes 281 

harbor the greatest human genetic diversity, yet remain underrepresented in the reference frameworks 282 

that shape discovery, annotation, and interpretation. 283 

By systematically characterizing African Pan -Genome contigs derived from GRCh38 unmapped reads 14 ,284 

we show that even advanced linear assemblies, including T2T -CHM13 and HPRC assemblies, 285 

incompletely capture ancestry -enriched genomic sequences. Alignme nt to T2T -CHM13 placed 286 

approximately 40% of contigs, largely within genomic regions absent from GRCh38 and enriched for 287 

centromeric satellites and repetitive elements, consistent with longstanding challenges in assembling 288

Several limitations of our study should be considered. Functional annotation of BTCs relied on 300 

computational predictions and may overestimate functional potential, particularly sequences lacking 301 

homologs in existing databases 35 . Although RNA -seq data support the expression of a subset of 302 

predicted genes, experimental validation across tissues and development contexts remains necessary. 303 

Additionally, the APG contigs were derived from an admixed African American cohort, which may limit 304 

generalizability to continental African popula tions. Uneven representation within the Phase I HPRC panel 305 

may also influence observed ancestry -associated patterns. 306 

Despite these limitations, our findings have important translational implications. Sequences absent from 307 

widely used reference genomes can contain coding and regulatory elements, indicating that clinically 308 

relevant variants within these regions are systematically overlooked 34 . Incorporating ancestry -enriched 309 

sequence into population -awar e and graph -based reference frameworks will improve variant discovery, 310 

gene annotation, and transcriptomic profiling, ultimately enhancing the accuracy and equity of precision 311 

medicine initiatives. In conclusion, characterization of APG contigs reveals tha t substantial functional, 312 

ancestry -structured genomic sequence remains beyond current reference genomes. Addressing these 313 

gaps will be critical to reducing reference bias, improving variant interpretation, and ensuring that 314 

genomic discoveries benefit all global populations. 315

African Pan Genome Contigs Data Sourcing 317 

We accessed the published African Pan Genome contigs sequences at GenBank with accession code 318

(2019) 14 . 125,715 APG contig sequences were published, however, 1475 were annotated as “dead” and 320 

were therefore excluded in all subsequent analyses ( Table S1 ). In all analyses described, we pro ceeded 321

Mapping APG contigs to GRCh38, T2T -CHM13 and HPRC linear references 323 

We sought to determine if any of the APG contig sequences are represented in the T2T -CHM13 and the 324 

Phase I HPRC linear reference assemblies (n = 47). We utilized a similar approach as outlined by 325 

Sherman et al 14 , to map and assess the representation of APG contigs among unique reference genomes 326 

representative of African, South American, European, East and South Asian ancestries (Figure S1) .327 

Specifically, we aligned the APG contigs to GRCh38.p10 as a benchmark to the Sherman et al analysis 14 ,328 

the T2T -CHM13 v2.0 reference gen ome 3, and the Phase I HPRC haploid paternal linear assemblies v2 4.329 

Alignment to the various reference genomes and assemblies was performed using bwa -mem v0.7.17 330 

with default parameters 36 . Alignments to individual locations within at least 300 bp were “chained” and 331 

analyzed as a single mapping output. 332

Gene and CpG Island Predictions and Annotation 406 

We deployed AUGUSTUS v3.5.0 44 , an ab initio gene prediction software tool trained on human gene 407 

models to annotat e potential protein -coding genes within BTCs . Contigs were individually processed, and 408 

gene structures including start and stop codons, exons, and coding sequences (CDS) were predicted. 409 

The resulting gene predictions were filtered to retain only transcript s with complete CDS features, 410 

representing putative protein -coding genes for downstream annotation ( Table S16 ). 411 

Additionally, to assess the epigenetic potential of APG contig sequences, we used CpGplot v6.6.0.0, a 412 

component of the European Molecular Biolog y Open Software Suite (EMBOSS, 413 

https://www.ebi.ac.uk/Tools/seqstats/emboss_cpgplot/ ). This tool detects CpG -rich sequences based on 414 

windowed GC content and the observed -to -expected CpG di nucleotide ratio. We used default thresholds 415 

commonly applied in genome -wide CpG prediction: a minimum island length of 200 bp, GC content ≥ 416 

50%, an observed/expected CpG ratio of ≥ 0.6, and a 100 bp sliding window. The identified CpG islands 417 

were filtered to show only contigs with islands, where we quantified the number of islands predicted. The 418 

output was parsed and converted to BED format for comparative positional analysis and overlay with 419 

predicted genes ( Table S16 ). 420 

Translated protein sequences derive d from AUGUSTUS gene models were queried against curated 421 

protein domain databases using profile hidden Markov models to identify conserved functional motifs and 422 

domain architectures, providing evidence for protein -coding plausibility beyond open reading fr ame 423 

structure alone 45 . In parallel, homology -based searches were performed against comprehensive protein 424 

sequence databases using sequence similarity alignment to detect significant matches to known pr oteins, 425 

allowing inference of evolutionary conservation and partial or divergent homology 46 .426
