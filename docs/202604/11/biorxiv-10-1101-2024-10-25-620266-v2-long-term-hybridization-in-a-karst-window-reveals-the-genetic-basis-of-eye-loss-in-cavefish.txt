Abstract :29 

Eye loss is a hallmark trait of animals inhabiting perpetual darkness, yet the 30 

precise genetic variants underlying this evolutionary change remain largely unknown .31 

The Mexican tetra ( Astyanax mexicanus ) provides a powerful model for dissecting the 32 

genetic basis of eye degeneration, as sighted surface fish and multiple independently 33 

evolved blind cave populations remain interfertile; yet despite decades of research and 34 

numerous QTL studies, the genetic basis of eye loss has remained unresolved at the 35 

level of specific variants . Here , we exploit a rare natural experiment in the Caballo Moro 36 

cave, where the collapse of a karst window created a partially illuminated pool inhabited 37 

by both fully eyed and completely eyeless cavefish of closely related genetic 38 

background. Whole -genome sequencing reveals a long -standing hybrid population 39 

between cave and surface lineages , enabling a dramatic refinement of the genetic 40 

architecture of eye degeneration to 203 candidate SNPs across 41 genes . Among 41 

these, we identified a nonsynonymous mutation in the lens gap -junction protein 42 

Connexin -50 (Cx50). CRISPR -based disruption of cx50 induces early eye loss in 43 

surface fish, and F2 laboratory crosses confirm genetic linkage between cx50 variants 44 

and eye size . Additional Cx50 mutations are present in independent cavefish 45 

populations and correlate with reduced eye size. Notably, variants in conserved regions 46 

of Cx50 also occur in other cave -dwelling fish and subterranean mammals, suggesting 47 

repeated evolutionary targeting of this gene. Introduction of the Caballo Moro mutation 48 

into mice causes cataracts and reduced eye and lens size, confirming its functional 49 

impact. Together, these findings identify the first SNP directly implicated in cavefish eye 50 

loss, demonstrate the power of natural hybrid populations to resolve the genetic basis of 51 

complex traits, and reveal Cx50 as a case of molecular convergence in vertebrate eye 52 

degeneration. 53

Cave -dwelling and subterranean organisms across diverse taxa have repeatedly 55 

evolved traits adapted to life in perpetual darkness , such as the consistent loss or 56 

reduction of eyes . Eye degeneration has been hypothesized to result from multiple , non -57 

mutually exclusive processes , including the energetic cost of maintaining visual systems ,58 

the relaxation of purifying selection on vision, and indirect selection for other traits that 59 

are genetically or developmentally coupled to eye reduction; however, the precise genetic 60 

bases of this trait remain poorly understood (1–6).61 

Despite decades of research across a variety of subterranean species, surprisingly 62 

little is known about the precise genetic basis of eye loss. M uch of the work in this field 63 

has focused on Astyanax mexicanus , a powerful model for studying cave adaptation. This 64 

species exists in two interfertile morphs : sighted surface fish and blind cavefish (7). To 65 

date, 35 cave populations of Astyanax with reduced or absent eyes have been 66 

documented (8–10 ). While these do not all represent independent origins , cave -morphs 67 

have evolved at least tw ice independently in the Sierra de Guatemala and Sierra de El 68 

Abra regions of Mexico over short evolutionary timescales (7–15 ). As such, eye loss in A. 69 

mexicanus provides a powerful natural system to investigate repeated evolution ary 70 

change .71 

Quantitative Trait Loc us (QTL) analyses in laboratory cave x surface F2 hybrids 72 

have linked eye size to at least 14 genomic regions (6, 16 –22 ). However, the resolution 73 

of these studies is limited, leaving thousands of potential causal variants within broad 74 

genomic intervals. While f ew cis -regulatory changes have been implicated in eyed 75 

degeneration , such as rx3 and cystathionine ß -synthase a (csba ) (23 –25 ), to our 76 

knowledge, no causal nucleotide variant has been identified as contributing to eye loss 77 

(4).78 

Here , we leveraged a natural experiment in the Caballo Moro cave to identify 79 

genes involved in eye degeneration. Located within the Sierra de Guatemala cave system 80 

(Fig . 1a , Fig. S1 ), Caballo Moro formed after a recent ceiling collapse that created a karst 81 

window , exposing part of an ~80 m long cave lake to sunlight (7). This results in adjacent 82 

illuminated and dark habitats without geographical barriers (Fig . 1b, Fig. S1 ). Fish 83 

inhabiting the illuminated region exhibit surface -like morphology with fully developed and 84 

functional eyes (Fig . 1c, d , Fig. S2a ), whereas blind cavefish occupy the darker regions 85 

(Fig . 1e; 25 ). As a result, eyed and eyeless morphs coexist in the same pool, providing a 86 

rare opportunity to study the genetic basis of trait divergence in the absence of geographic 87 

isolation .88 

Despite the opportunity for gene flow, fish in Caballo Moro are either fully eyed or 89 

completely eyeless, with only two individuals exhibiting intermediate eye phenotype s90 

among 75 surveyed fish across this and a previous study (26 ). The striking dichotomy in 91 

eye morphology suggests that selective pressures maintain distinct phenotypes despite 92 

the close proximity and interfertility of the morphs . Previous genetic analysis suggested 93 

a complex evolutionary history for this population, including evidence of hybridization 94 

between surface and cave lineages (26 ), provid ing an opportunity for much higher -95 

resolution genetic mapping of eye loss than is possible in traditional QTL studies. 96 

Through whole genome sequencing of eyed and eyeless Caballo Moro fish, we 97 

found that the eyed morph originated from a surface fish invasion of the cave 98 

approximately 3,300 -4,300 generations ago. Hybridization between these surface -99

Eyed Caballo Moro cavefish represent longstanding cave -surface hybrids 115 

To determine the origin of the eyed fish from the Caballo Moro ( Caballo Moro )116 

cave, we collected 17 light -dwelling eyed fish and 19 dark -dwelling eyeless fish from the 117 

cave, along with six surface fish from nearby surface environments (Fig. 1a -e). 118 

Anatomical and behavioral characterizations revealed that the eyed Caballo Moro fish, 119 

despite having functional eyes, exhibit phenotypes that are typically associated with 120 

cavefish, including altered opercular bone orientation and the presence of extra maxillary 121 

teeth ( Fig. S2 b-d; 29 ). In two visits 20 years apart, eyed fish were observed to chase 122 

eyeless fish from the illuminated side of the pool (26 ). This observation led us to 123 

investigate whether behavior contributes to the spatial segregation of the eyed and blind 124 

cavefish within the same cave environment. In a controlled resident -intruder assay 125 

conducted in the field, we exposed eyed Caballo Moro fish to both surface and eyeless 126 

intruders, separated by a glass barrier , and quantified aggressive strikes toward the 127 

intruder s (Fig. S 2e-f; 30 ). Eyed Caballo Moro fish attack ed both eyeless fish and surface 128 

fish, whereas surface fish preferentially attack ed other surface fish (p<0.0001, Kruskal -129 

Wallis test). These behavioral findings not only confirm that the eyed Caballo Moro fish 130 

have functional vision but also suggest aggression as a potential source of the 131 

geographical separation of the different morphs within the cave. 132

Connexin -50 and eye degeneration in A. mexicanus 300 

In A. mexicanus , the lens acts as a master regulator of eye regression, as 301 

reciprocal lens transplant ation experiments between cave and surface fish demonstrated 302 

that the cavefish lens is sufficient to drive eye degeneration (36 ). Both morphotypes 303 

initiate eye development similarly until approximately 24 hours post fertilization (hpf), after 304 

which the cavefish lens undergoes extensive apopto sis that ultimately leads to 305 

degeneration of the entire eye (37 , 38 ).306 

In mammals, CX50 is primarily expressed in the lens , where it forms intercellular 307 

gap junction channel s composed of 12 connexin subunits that connect adjacent cells (Fig. 308 

2c; 28 , 39 ). CX50 is the only connexin present in interior lens fibers and is essential for 309 

maintaining lens homeostasis and transparency (34 , 40 ). In humans, more than 40 310 

disease -associated mutations in CX50 disrupt gap junction function and cause congenital 311 

cataracts and microphthalmia (reduced eye size ) through impaired lens fiber 312 

differentiation and increased apoptosis 47 , 48 ). Notably , four of these pathogenic variants 313 

occur immediately adjacent to the S89K mutation identified in eyeless Caballo Moro fish 314 

(43 –49 ).315 

To evaluate the role of Cx50 in A. mexicanus eye development, we first confirmed 316 

that the gene is expressed in the developing lens . RNA -seq of lenses isolated from 317 

surface fish at 24 hours post fertilization (hpf) detected robust cx50 expression during this 318 

critical stage of eye development . The cx50 gene in surface fish lenses had a mean 319 

transcripts per million ( TPM ) value of 374 ± 75 , which is above the 98th percentile of TPM 320 

values for genes detected in the experiment. To test whether Cx50 is required for eye 321 

formation , we generated surface fish F 0 CRISPants using cx50 -targeted CRISPR guides 322 

(Fig. 2d -g, Fig. S 8b). Disruption of cx50 resulted in severe eye defects: mutants displayed 323

Independent mutations in cx50 across A. mexicanus cavefish lineages 335 

Astyanax mexicanus cavefish evolved through at least two independent 336 

colonization events from ancestral surface populations invaded caves in the Sierra de 337 

Guatemala and the Sierra de El Abra regions (11 –13 , 53 ). Given the close relationship 338 

between Caballo Moro cavefish and other Guatemala populations, we sequenced cx50 339 

in additional caves from this region , including Molino, Vásquez, Escondido, and Jineo . All 340 

populations share d the same S89K allele identified in Caballo Moro .341 

We next examined whether the same mutation occurs in cavefish from the 342 

independently derived Sierra de El Abra lineage. We used publicly available sequences 343 

of Pachón, Los Sabinos, Tinaja, Curva, and Calera populations and found that these 344 

cavefish retain the ancestral residue at position S89. However, all El Abra populations 345 

carry a different mutation in cx50 : a threonine to methionine substitution at position 39 346 

(T39M). This residue lies within the first transmembrane domain of CX50 and 347 

corresponds to a position where human mutations (e.g., T39R) are known to cause 348 

congenital cataracts (28 , 54 , 55 ). To test whether this mutation is associated with eye 349 

size variation , we generated F2 hybrids by crossing surface fish with Tinaja cavefish from 350 

the El Abra lineage. We genotyped 78 F2 hybrids for cx50 and measured eye size. We 351 

found a correlation between reduced eye size and the presence of the Cx50 -T39M variant 352 

(Fig. 2 h, p=0.0163, Kruskal -Wallis test). 353 

To evaluate the effects of this mutation under natural environmental conditions, we 354 

conducted field studies in the Calera cave, which is part of the El Abra cave lineage (9).355 

Fish from th is population exhibit a continuous spectrum of eye sizes similar to that 356 

observed in F2 hybrids (9). We scored eye sizes for 119 wild -caught Calera individuals 357 

and correlated these measurements with their cx50 genotypes. The T39M allele showed 358 

a strong association with reduced eye size in this natural population ( p<0.0001, Kruskal -359 

Wallis test; Fig. 2 i-j), demonstrating a robust genetic link between cx50 variation and eye 360 

reduction across distinct cavefish populations in the wild .361 

Together, these results indicate that mutations in cx50 contribute to eye size 362 

variation in A. mexicanus . Disruption of cx50 in surface fish produces severe eye defects, 363 

while distinct cx50 mutations are associated with reduced eye size in independently 364 

evolved cavefish lineages. Moreover, the cx50 locus lies within genomic regions showing 365 

signatures of positive selection in both the Guatemala and El Abra cave lineages. These 366 

findings suggest that selection has repeatedly targeted cx50 during the evolution of eye 367 

degeneration, even in populations experiencing ongoing gene flow with surface fish .368

Variants in Cx50 across other cavefishes and subterranean mammals 370 

Given the independent mutations in cx50 identified in two A. mexicanus cavefish 371 

lineages, we next investigated whether variation in this gene occurs in other cave -adapted 372 

fish es . We analyzed publicly available sequences from 19 cavefish species and their 373 

closest available surface relatives with a particular focus on the first 245 N -terminal amino 374 

acids of Cx50 which encompass the N-terminal helix and the first two transmembrane 375 

domains ( Figure 3a, Fig. S11, Table S5 ). These regions are highly conserved across fish, 376 

showing approximately 98% amino acid sequence homology among the species 377 

examined. Notably, eight of the 19 cavefish species carry non -synonymous substitutions 378 

in regions of cx50 known to have phenotypic effects in humans, such as the N -terminal 379 

helix (NTH) and first two transmembrane domains (TM1, TM2, Fig. S11 ). Notably, one of 380 

the other cavefish analyzed, the Chinese blind golden -line barbel, Sinocyclocheilus 381 

anophthalmus , has an S89F mutation at the same residue as the Guatemala lineage fish 382 

and a R33Q mutation at the same residue as a known human cataract phenotype ( Fig. 383 

S11 ; 62 , 63 ).384 

We extended this analysis to subterranean mammals, which have also evolved 385 

convergent eye degeneration (58 –60 ). Teleost Cx50 and mammalian CX50 share 386 

approximate ly 83% amino acid sequence similarity in the first 245 amino acids, 387 

highlighting the high conservation of this protein across vertebrates. We therefore 388 

compared Cx50 sequences from five subterranean -adapted mammal genomes and their 389 

closest available surface relatives (Fig. S12, Table S 5). Four species , the Upper Galilee 390 

Mountains blind mole -rat ( Nannospalax galili ), Damara mole -rat ( Fukomys damarensis ), 391 

Iberian mole ( Talpa occidentalis ), and star -nosed mole ( Condylura cristata ) harbor 392 

subterranean specific, nonsynonymous substitutions within the NTH or the first two 393 

transmembrane domains. 394 

To evaluate the potential functional effect s of these substitutions , we performed in 395 

silico structural analys es of Cx50/CX50 sequences from seven cavefish species, three 396 

mammals, and their closest surface relatives. We used AlphaFold2 , together with the 397 

cryo -electron microscopy -resolved CX50 structure , to model the intracellular channel 398 

conformation of the N -terminal 245 residues of Cx50 /CX50 (Fig. 3b; 45 , 67 , 68 ). Structural 399 

models were visualized in ChimeraX to examine potential effects on gap junction 400 

architecture (63 ). These static models allow for the assessment of gross changes to the 401 

pore, such as the open -state conformation, orientation of functional elements like the N -402 

terminal helix, and electrostatic potential (Fig. 3 ). Previous studies have shown that 403 

mutations within these elements of the protein, especially in the inner pore regions of the 404 

NTH, TM1, and TM2, can drastically alter the selectivity and conductance of the pore. 405 

Disruption of the CX50 pore is known to impair antioxidant and growth factor distribution, 406 

leading to cataracts, reduced cellular proliferation, and increased apoptosis (64 –66 ). We 407 

therefore examined whether the cave -associated mutations alter these structural features 408 

of the CX50 pore. 409

In contrast, the T39M mutation carried by El Abra cave fish is predicted to increase 446 

pore diameter (Fig. 3c”). In previously resolved and tested structures, T39 in TM1 on one 447 

subunit interacts with W4 in the NTH of an adjacent subunit to maintain a set distance 448

which stabilizes the open channel conformation and enhances hemichannel activity. 452 

Increased hemichannel conductance results in accumulation of calcium ions and 453 

depletion of intracellular metabolites, ultimately triggering apoptosis and cataracts (55 ).454

F. damarensis , showed the most remarkable predicted structure change ( Fig. S14d -f). 456 

The Damara mole -rat has small eyes and heavy eyelids that impair vision (68 ). In this 457 

species, CX50 carries a negatively charged glutamic acid change to a polar, uncharged 458 

glutamine in the intracellular N -terminal helix (E12Q). In several AlphaFold models, the 459 

E12Q substitution resulted in a repositioning of the NTH into the pore and a narrowing of 460 

the pore diameter relative to a mouse structural model ( Fig. S14d -e). In models where 461 

the NTH re tained the canonical down ward conformation, the mutation was still predicted 462 

to alter pore properties. Because r esidue E12 faces the interior of the pore, replacing this 463

In this study, we leveraged a unique Astyanax mexicanus cavefish population to identify 509 

the first specific genetic variant contributing to eye degeneration in this system. Previous 510 

work has identified genomic regions associated with eye size, but these regions often 511 

contain thousands to millions of candidate variants. By exploiting a naturally hybridizing 512 

cave population, we were able to narrow this space to a small set of candidate variants, 513 

including a protein -coding mutation in Connexin50 that we functionally validated in vivo .514 

Although eye loss in A. mexicanus is a complex trait shaped by multiple genetic factors, 515 

our results identify cx50 as a key locus associated with eye reduction across independent 516 

cavefish populations. Importantly, the remaining ~200 candidate variants identified here 517 

provide a tractable set of loci that may enable the genetic architecture of cavefish eye 518 

degeneration to be resolved in future work. 519 

520 

Beyond A. mexicanus , we detected additional Cx50 variants in other cave -adapted fishes 521 

and subterranean mammals that are predicted to alter gap junction function. Because 522 

Cx50 is expressed almost exclusively in the lens, mutations in this gene are less likely to 523 

affect other tissues, potentially reducing pleiotropic constraints. This restricted expression 524 

may make cx50 particularly susceptible to repeated evolutionary modification. Together, 525 

these findings suggest that locus reuse in cx50 may represent a recurrent molecular 526 

pathway underlying eye degeneration across subterranean vertebrates. 527
