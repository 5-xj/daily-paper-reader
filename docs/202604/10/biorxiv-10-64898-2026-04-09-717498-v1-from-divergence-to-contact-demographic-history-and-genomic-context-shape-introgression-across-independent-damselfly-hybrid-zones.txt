ABSTRACT 40 

Hybridisation outcomes often vary across space and time, yet the relative roles of 41 

demographic history and genomic architecture in shaping introgression remain unclear. Here, 42 

we investigate three replicated hybrid zones between the damselflies Ischnura elegans and 43 

Ischnura graellsii across Spain to test whether genomic introgression patterns are repeatable 44 

across independently formed zones. Using genome-wide data, we combined demographic 45 

modelling, genomic cline, and functional annotation of introgressed loci. Demographic 46 

inference supported three independent secondary contact events of different ages: The South-47 

east zone forming first (~207 years ago), followed by the North-west (~73.5 years) and 48 

North-central (~33 years) hybrid zones. Despite similar cline steepness across autosomes, 49 

asymmetric gene flow from I. graellsii into I. elegans was observed, with a low overlap of 50 

introgressed loci between zones. These loci were mainly associated with broad regulatory 51 

and transport-related functions in both hybrid zones, indicating repeatability at the level of 52 

gene function rather than gene identity. In contrast, the X chromosome showed steeper clines, 53 

suggesting strong intrinsic genomic constraints. Together, demographic history explains 54 

geographic heterogeneity in introgression, whereas chromosome architecture imposes 55 

consistent constraints. These findings highlight how replicated hybrid zones can disentangle 56 

contingent versus repeatable genomic responses during early stages of speciation with gene 57 

flow. 58

INTRODUCTION 61 

Hybridisation is widely recognised as a dynamic evolutionary process capable of generating 62 

diverse outcomes ranging from genetic swamping, species replacement, reinforcement, 63 

hybrid speciation, stable hybrid zones, or adaptive introgression (Abbott et al., 2013). 64 

However, these outcomes are rarely uniform, even within the same pair of species (e.g. Arce-65 

Valdés, Swaegers, et al., 2025). Growing evidence show that the consequences of 66 

hybridisation depend not only on intrinsic factors, but also on the demographic and 67 

environmental context in which secondary contact occurs (Peñalba et al., 2024). 68 

Understanding why similar species interactions lead to divergent genomic and evolutionary 69 

trajectories remains a central challenge in evolutionary biology. 70 

One key source of variation arises from differences in demographic history and the timing of 71 

secondary contact. Hybrid zones formed at different times may experience contrasting 72 

reproductive barriers, effective population sizes, genetic incompatibilities, and ecological 73 

conditions, all of which influence the strength and direction of introgression (Harrison & 74 

Larson, 2014, 2016). Distributional range shifts driven by climate change or post-glacial 75 

warming have repeatedly generated scenarios in which closely related taxa come into contact 76 

under heterogeneous demographic contexts, creating natural laboratories for studying 77 

speciation with gene flow (Taylor et al., 2015). Yet most studies focus on single hybrid zones, 78 

limiting our ability to disentangle repeatable genomic responses from historically contingent 79 

outcomes. 80 

In addition to demographic processes, genomic architecture plays an important role in 81 

shaping introgression patterns. Sex chromosomes and some genomic regions associated with 82 

reproductive isolation often show reduced permeability to gene flow (Dobzhansky, 1936; 83 

Fraïsse & Sachdeva, 2021), suggesting that intrinsic genetic barriers can impose predictable 84 

constraints across hybrid systems. At the same time, widespread variation in autosomal 85 

introgression among populations indicates that introgression patterns are not solely 86 

determined by species-specific differences but emerge from interactions between genome 87 

architecture and local demographic or ecological conditions (Abbott et al., 2016). This dual 88 

role of intrinsic genomic constraints and extrinsic historical context raises the issue of the 89 

extent to which introgression patterns are repeatable across independently formed hybrid 90 

zones within the same species pair. 91 

Comparisons among multiple hybrid zones provide a powerful framework to investigate this 92 

issue. Spatially replicated zones differing in age, demographic context, and ecological 93 

conditions allow us to evaluate whether hybridisation produces consistent genomic 94 

signatures or whether outcomes diverge depending on historical contingencies (e.g. Arce-95 

Valdés, Swaegers, et al., 2025; Harrison & Larson, 2016; Vines et al., 2003). Such systems 96 

align with the mosaic hybrid zone concept, in which geographically structured variation in 97 

selection, demography, and environmental context generates heterogeneous genomic 98 

landscapes of introgression (Rand & Harrison, 1989). Importantly, examining replicated 99

However, most previous evidence for repeatable patterns of introgression has been derived 102 

from interspecific comparisons, either across different species pairs or among independent 103 

hybrid systems (Edelman et al., 2019; Fontaine et al., 2015; Gompert et al., 2012; Martin et 104 

al., 2013; Taylor & Larson, 2019). This highlights the need for systems that allow such 105 

patterns to be evaluated within a single species pair across multiple hybrid zones. 106 

A particularly useful framework for quantifying these patterns is the genomic cline theory 107 

(Barton, 1979), which can describe how introgression rates may vary across loci and between 108 

zones. Unlike traditional clines based on geography, genomic clines use hybrid indices or 109 

admixture proportions as independent variables (Szymura & Barton, 1991). This approach is 110 

especially powerful for detecting outlier loci whose introgression patterns deviates 111 

significantly from the genome-wide neutral expectation (Bailey, 2022; Fitzpatrick, 2013). 112 

Thus, the rate and pattern of introgression at a given locus can reveal whether it is linked to 113 

reduced hybrid fitness or, conversely, to adaptive or heterotic advantages (Gompert & 114 

Buerkle, 2011). 115 

Our study system focuses on two closely related damselfly species that provide a rare 116 

opportunity to investigate recent divergence, range expansion, and hybridisation in real time. 117 

Ischnura elegans is a widespread Euro-Siberian species that has undergone rapid southward 118 

range expansion, whereas its sister species I. graellsii has a much narrower distribution, being 119 

largely restricted to the Iberian Peninsula and northern Africa (Ocharan-Larrondo, 1987). 120 

Over the last decades, and with increasing speed in recent years, I. elegans has expanded into 121 

the range of I. graellsii in Spain (Arce-Valdés, Swaegers, et al., 2025; Monetti et al., 2002; 122 

Wellenreuther et al., 2018) generating several geographically structured hybrid zones. 123 

These hybrid zones differ markedly in their ecological and geographic context, the strength 124 

and asymmetry of reproductive barriers, patterns of introgression, and evolutionary outcomes 125 

(Arce-Valdés, Swaegers, et al., 2025). The South-east (SE) hybrid zone, located along the 126 

Mediterranean coast, represents the oldest documented zone and is characterised by 127 

introgression toward I. elegans and the progressive replacement of I. graellsii by I. elegans 128 

(Sánchez-Guillén et al., 2011; Wellenreuther et al., 2018). By contrast, the North-west (NW) 129 

hybrid zone is geographically isolated and exhibits extensive bidirectional introgression, 130 

reduced interspecific genetic differentiation, and reinforcement acting primarily on 131 

mechanical barriers (Arce-Valdés, Ballén-Guapacha, et al., 2025; Ballén-Guapacha et al., 132 

2024; Sánchez-Guillén et al., 2012). Finally, the North-central (NC) hybrid zone is larger and 133 

currently expanding, showing predominantly unidirectional introgression toward I. graellsii 134 

and stronger postmating isolation involving both mechanical and fertility barriers (Arce-135 

Valdés, Swaegers, et al., 2025; Sánchez-Guillén et al., 2011). 136 

Across the three hybrid zones, genomic analyses reveal consistently reduced introgression 137 

on the X chromosome and an underrepresentation of males among admixed individuals, 138

interaction between range expansion and reinforcement, and the genomic architecture of 142 

reproductive isolation during the earliest stages of speciation. Beyond their role as a model 143 

for hybridization and reinforcement, recent studies have shown that the southward expansion 144 

of I. elegans has been accompanied by rapid ecological and physiological responses to 145 

warmer climatic conditions, including convergence in thermal tolerance and life-history traits 146 

toward the native sister species I. graellsii (Swaegers, Sánchez-Guillén, Carbonell, et al., 147 

2022). These findings highlight the colonisation of warmer environments by I. elegans is 148 

reshaping the ecological context in which secondary contact and hybridisation occur. 149 

Despite numerous studies conducted in this hybrid system, the origins of each hybrid zone 150 

remain unresolved as does the dual role that demographic conditions and genomic 151 

architecture may play in shaping the evolutionary consequences of hybridisation. For this 152 

reason, this study aims to investigate the demographic history and patterns of genomic 153 

introgression between I. elegans and I. graellsii across three hybrid zones in Spain. 154 

Specifically, we aim to (i) reconstruct the demographic history of this hybrid system by 155 

estimating the timing of the cessation of gene flow between species (origin of the allopatric 156 

distribution of the species), the formation of subsequent hybrid zones, and key demographic 157 

parameters. Given historical records of the southward expansion of I. elegans , we expect 158 

hybrid zones to have originated through independent and temporally staggered secondary 159 

contact events associated with this range expansion (Monetti et al., 2002; Wellenreuther et 160 

al., 2018; Arce-Valdés, Swaegers, et al., 2025). (ii) Model genomic clines within each hybrid 161 

zone to characterise patterns of genetic introgression. Based on previous evidence of 162 

asymmetric introgression and differences in reproductive barriers among zones, we predict 163 

that genomic clines will differ in the direction and magnitude of introgression among hybrid 164 

zones (Sánchez-Guillén et al., 2011, 2012; Swaegers, Sánchez-Guillén, Chauhan, et al., 2022; 165 

Arce-Valdés, Ballén-Guapacha, et al., 2025). (iii) Evaluate the consistency of outlier loci 166 

across hybrid zones by comparing and functionally annotating markers that exhibit excessive 167 

gene flow from one parental species into the other. If genomic architecture imposes shared 168 

constraints on introgression, we expect loci showing excess introgression to be associated 169 

with similar functional categories across hybrid zones, even if the specific loci involved differ 170 

among regions (Gompert et al., 2012; Edelman et al., 2019; Taylor & Larson, 2019). 171

populations and multiple hybrid zones differing in their evolutionary dynamics. These data 176 

comprise 26 populations sampled between 2005 and 2015, including the allopatric 177

We assembled these sequences following the Stacks v2.2 pipeline (Catchen et al., 2013; 184 

Rochette et al., 2019). First, we demultiplexed and quality-filtered raw RAD-seq reads with 185 

process_radtags , removed PCR duplicates using clone_filter with default parameters, and 186 

aligned the cleaned reads to the most recent I. elegans reference genome (Price et al., 2022) 187 

using the BWA-MEM algorithm implemented in BWA v0.7.17 (Li & Durbin, 2009). We 188 

processed the resulting BAM files with gstacks and called variants using populations with 189 

the –ordered-export flag, filtering variants to retain a minimum minor allele frequency of 190 

0.02. Using VCFtools v0.1.16 (Danecek et al., 2011), we further filtered the resulting VCF 191 

file to include only bi-allelic SNPs, recovering a total of 2,177,590 markers. 192 

Following O’Leary et al. (2018), we tested several filtering schemes to retain an optimal 193 

number of high-quality SNPs and samples (Supplementary File 1). We selected the scheme 194 

retaining genotypes with a minimum sequencing depth of 5, loci with a maximum of 10% 195 

missing data, and samples with a maximum of 15% missing data, which resulted in 10,093 196 

SNPs. We pruned markers for linkage disequilibrium using Plink v1.90 (Purcell et al., 2007) 197 

with the --indep-pairwise function, applying a sliding window of 50 SNPs and a step size of 198 

5 SNPs. Pairwise linkage disequilibrium was estimated among SNPs within each window, 199 

and one random SNP was retained when the genotypic correlation coefficient (r²) exceeded 200 

0.5. We then used allopatric samples as references for a preliminary supervised Admixture 201 

v1.3.0 analysis (Alexander & Lange, 2011) and classified individuals from sympatric 202 

populations as putative pure-species samples when their Q values were ≥ 0.999 for either 203 

parental species. We tested Hardy–Weinberg disequilibrium across the full distribution of 204 

both species using these pure-species samples in VCFtools and removed SNPs that showed 205 

significant deviation from Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium in both species simultaneously (p < 206 

0.05). Finally, for subsequent analyses, we divided the full SNP dataset into autosomal (6,922 207 

SNPs) and X-linked (236 SNPs) subsets, both comprising 237 samples. 208

of both species in Spain, we tested alternative scenarios of secondary contact and range 211 

expansion that could account for the contrasting evolutionary outcomes observed among 212 

hybrid zones. We employed the software DIYABC-RF (Collin et al., 2021) following best-213 

practice recommendations for demographic inference using genomic data (Marchi et al., 214 

2021). This framework combines Approximate Bayesian Computation with a Random Forest 215 

algorithm to compare observed genetic data against simulated datasets, enabling the 216

Using the autosomal dataset (6,922 SNPs), we compared eight alternative demographic 224 

scenarios that differed mainly in whether the hybrid zones had a single origin or originated 225 

independently or alternatively resulted from sequential colonisation events involving already 226 

established hybrid zones. These scenarios would reflect alternative hypotheses about the 227 

temporal and spatial dynamics of secondary contact following the recent southward 228 

expansion of I. elegans (details in Supplementary Figure 1). 229 

For each scenario, we performed 3,000 coalescent simulations and evaluated the fit between 230 

simulated and observed data using a principal component analysis of all summary statistics 231 

included in the software. We selected the best scenario via random forest classification using 232 

1,000 trees, which allowed for accurate discrimination among competing models despite the 233 

high dimensionality of the data. Then, we did another 40,000 coalescent simulations under 234 

the best-supported scenario to estimate key demographic parameters, including timing of the 235 

cessation of gene flow between species (assuming 0.67 generations per year, i.e., 1.5 years 236 

per generation, according to Corbet et al. 2006), effective population sizes, and admixture 237 

rates. To determine whether this number of simulations was adequate, we implemented a 238 

random forest regression approach to estimate error metrics, comparing results from the 239 

40,000 simulations with those from a randomly selected subset of 12,000 simulations. As no 240 

meaningful differences in error metrics were detected between the two simulation datasets, 241 

we considered that the results obtained with 40,000 simulations, using a random forest 242 

analysis composed of 1,000 trees and 30% of these simulations as out-of-bag testing samples, 243 

were sufficiently robust. 244 

Genomic cline analyses 245 

To describe patterns of introgression between multiple loci in each hybrid zone, we 246 

performed genomic clines analyses based on genotype frequencies along a genome-wide 247 

admixture gradient. This approach allows the identification of loci showing excessive or 248 

restricted gene flow, thereby linking zone-specific demographic histories with heterogeneity 249 

in genomic introgression (Gompert & Buerkle, 2011). Genomic cline analyses were 250 

performed for both autosomal (6,922 SNPs) and X-linked (236 SNPs) datasets. However, the 251 

SE hybrid zone was excluded from these analyses since only introgressed I. elegans 252 

individuals, as identified from SNP genomic data, were included from this region (Arce-253 

Valdés, Swaegers, et al., 2025), precluding the estimation of genomic clines based on both 254 

parental and admixed individuals. Before the analyses, we applied additional filtering criteria 255

First, we estimated hybrid indices using a single Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) run 263 

with 3,000 iterations and a burn-in of 1,000 iterations. Hybrid indices were then used to fit 264 

genomic clines through a second MCMC with 5,000 iterations and a burn-in of 2,000 265

autosomes and X-linked loci. On the other hand, statistically significant differences in cline 269 

steepness between autosomes and X-linked loci in NW and NC hybrid zones were assessed 270 

using a Wilcoxon rank-sum test (Wilcoxon, 1945). 271 

Functional annotation of introgressed loci 272 

To explore the heterogeneity of introgression across the genome and assess whether similar 273 

genomic regions are involved across hybrid zones, we classified outlier SNPs based on their 274 

cline parameters (Bailey, 2022). Specifically, SNPs with cline steepness values lower than 275 

one were classified as showing excessive gene flow, whereas SNPs with higher values were 276 

classified as showing restricted introgression. Additionally, based on cline centre estimates, 277 

SNPs with centre values greater than 0.5 were interpreted as carrying I. graellsii alleles in an 278 

I. elegans genomic background, whereas values lower than 0.5 indicated I. elegans alleles in 279 

an I. graellsii background. Loci were considered outliers only when these patterns were 280 

statistically significant (p < 0.05; Bailey, 2022). We tested for statistically significant 281 

differences in the frequency of these outlier classes using Fisher’s exact test (Fisher, 1922). 282 

Finally, we functionally annotated outlier SNPs showing excessive introgression to evaluate 283 

whether shared or zone-specific introgression patterns involved genes associated with similar 284 

biological functions. We mapped outlier SNPs to the GFF annotation file of the I. elegans 285 

reference genome (Price et al., 2022) and identified annotated coding sequence (CDS), 286 

mRNA, or exon features that directly contained the genomic position of each outlier SNP. 287 

Given the reduced representation nature of RAD-seq data, only the SNP positions themselves 288 

were considered, and surrounding genomic regions were not evaluated. For each feature, we 289 

retrieved the corresponding protein sequences from GenBank using NCBI E-utilities (Sayers, 290 

2022). When outlier SNPs were located within features associated with multiple homologs, 291 

isoforms, or protein variants, we randomly selected a single representative protein sequence 292 

per SNP. Protein sequences were then analysed using InterProScan v.5 (Jones et al., 2014) to 293 

identify conserved protein domains based on integrated protein signature databases. Gene 294

RESULTS 297 

Demographic history inference 298 

We compared eight alternative demographic scenarios describing the formation of the SE, 299 

NW, and NC hybrid zones, considering either independent admixture events or sequential 300 

formation mediated by migration among hybrid zones (Supplementary Figure 1). Using 301 

3,000 simulations per scenario, all proposed models were consistent with the observed 302 

dataset based on PCA analysis of summary statistics (Supplementary Figure 2). Among the 303 

tested models, Scenario 1, describing the hybrid zones as independently formed admixture 304 

events, with the SE hybrid zone forming first, followed by the NW, and most recently the NC 305 

hybrid zone, received the highest support according to random forest votes (517 votes, 306 

posterior probability = 0.456; Figure 2; Table 1). Under this scenario, the SE hybrid zone 307 

showed a low genetic contribution from I. graellsii (4.5%). Admixture proportions were more 308 

balanced in the NW ( I. graellsii = 54.3%, I. elegans = 45.7%), and in the NC ( I. graellsii =309 

53.5%, I. elegans = 46.5%; Figure 2). 310 

A demographic expansion preceding the cessation of gene flow was inferred around 6,306 311 

generations ago (90% CI: 9,728–2,258), corresponding to around 9,459 years ago (90% CI: 312 

14,592–3,387; assuming 0.67 generations per year, i.e., 1.5 years per generation, Corbet et 313 

al., 2006), during this period, effective population size increased from 881 at the onset of the 314 

expansion to present-day allopatric population sizes estimated for I. elegans (6,130) and I. 315 

graellsii (6,904; Figure 2; Table 2). The cessation of gene flow between I. elegans and I. 316 

graellsii subsequently estimated at approximately 3,570 generations ago (90% CI: 7,118– 317 

1,585; Figure 2; Table 2), corresponding to 5,355 years ago (90% CI: 10,677–2,378). More 318 

recently, parameter estimates indicate that the SE hybrid zone was established approximately 319 

138 generations ago (90% CI: 329–56; Figure 2; Table 2), corresponding to 207 years ago 320 

(90% CI: 494–84). The NW hybrid zone formed more recently, around 49 generations ago 321 

(90% CI: 154–13; Figure 2; Table 2), equivalent to 73.5 years ago (90% CI: 231–19.5), 322 

followed by the NC hybrid zone, which formed approximately 22 generations ago (90% CI: 323 

72–11; Figure 2; Table 2), corresponding to 33 years ago (90% CI: 108–16.5). Finally, 324 

effective population sizes estimated for the SE (Ne = 6,014) and NW (Ne = 6,592) hybrid 325 

zones were similar, whereas the NC showed a smaller effective population size (Ne = 4,048; 326 

Table 2). 327

Functional annotation of introgressed loci 373 

Among the 75 autosomal SNPs showing excess unidirectional or bidirectional introgression 374 

between parental species, we found 19 protein sequences associated with 87 Gene Ontology 375 

terms (Supplementary Table 2) representing several shared functional categories 376 

(Supplementary Figure 7). Terms associated with protein-protein interactions and catalytic 377 

activity were detected in both hybrid zones, as well as processes related to nucleotide 378 

metabolism and signalling pathways. Despite these functional similarities, some regional 379 

trends were evident. In the NW hybrid zone, annotations were more frequently associated 380 

with cellular structural organisation, including cytoskeletal and extracellular matrix-related 381 

processes, whereas in the NC hybrid zone, functions were more associated in metabolic and 382 

redox-related pathways. 383 

At the molecular function level, both hybrid zones shared protein binding, protein–protein 384 

interactions and general catalytic activities as the most common annotations. The NW hybrid 385 

zone additionally showed a higher representation of signalling-related functions, including 386 

kinase activity and receptor-mediated processes, whereas the NC hybrid zone was associated 387 

with cofactor-binding functions and enzymes involved in metabolic pathways, as those 388 

associated with aminoacid metabolism and redox processes. 389 

At the biological process level, both hybrid zones encompassed core cellular and regulatory 390 

functions. However, the NW hybrid zone was more strongly associated with processes related 391 

to cellular architecture and structural organisation, while the NC hybrid zone showed a 392 

greater contribution of metabolic processes and RNA-related regulation. 393 

Finally, the cellular component category indicated that proteins associated with these outliers 394 

SNPs are broadly distributed across intracellular compartments in both hybrid zones, 395 

primarily within the nucleus and cytoplasm. Nevertheless, NW hybrid zone included more 396 

components related to the extracellular environment and specialised cellular structures, 397 

whereas the NC hybrid zone showed a broader representation of organelle-associated 398 

components. Overall, these annotations indicate that outlier-associated loci in both hybrid 399 

zones are associated to fundamental cellular functions, with zonal differences reflecting 400 

variation in the relative contribution of structural, regulatory, and metabolic processes. 401 

DISCUSSION 402 

Why hybridisation leads to divergent outcomes remains a central question in evolutionary 403 

biology. Our results show that recent divergence, spatially replicated but temporally distinct 404 

secondary contact, and chromosome-specific permeability to gene flow jointly shape the 405 

demographic trajectories and introgression patterns observed across the three hybrid zones 406

Demographic history and genomic architecture jointly shape introgression patterns 411 

Post-glacial climatic fluctuations likely shaped the evolutionary history of I. elegans and I. 412 

graellsii . Phylogenetic estimates place the divergence between these species within the last 413 

~100,000 years (Sánchez-Guillén et al., 2020; Blow et al., 2021), with our demographic 414 

analyses indicating a more recent cessation of gene flow between 2,378 and 10,677 years 415 

ago, suggesting a prolonged history of interaction following initial divergence within the 416 

Holocene. This timing coincides with climatic warming following the Last Glacial Maximum 417 

(~21,000 years ago), which reshaped species distributions across Europe (Hewitt, 2000). 418 

Given that the Iberian Peninsula served as a significant glacial refugium (Gómez & Lunt, 419 

2007; Hewitt, 1999), the common ancestor of both species may have persisted there, with 420 

subsequent post-glacial range expansion promoting the differentiation between the 421 

Iberian/North Africa restricted I. graellsii and the widely distributed I. elegans . The strong 422 

dispersal ability and ecological flexibility of I. elegans likely facilitated rapid northward 423 

expansion and local adaptation across contrasting environments, ranging from the warm and 424 

human-modified agricultural ponds in the Iberian Peninsula (Swaegers, Sánchez-Guillén, 425 

Carbonell, et al., 2022; Wellenreuther et al., 2018) to cold freshwater systems in northern 426 

Europe (Dudaniec et al., 2018; Lancaster et al., 2015). Such post-glacial divergence coupled 427 

with rapid range expansion is increasingly recognised as a recurrent pattern in Odonata, 428 

including Ischnura , Calopteryx , and Enallagma , where high dispersal ability and ecological 429 

flexibility promote shallow genetic divergences, demographic expansions, and secondary 430 

contact among closely related lineages (Turgeon & McPeek, 2002; Wellenreuther & Sánchez-431 

Guillén, 2016). Similar post-glacial divergence and expansion scenarios have been 432 

documented across a wide range of taxa such as insects (Capblancq et al., 2015, 2019; Mas-433 

Peinado et al., 2022; Schmitt et al., 2006), fishes (Schluter & McPhail, 1992; Volckaert et al., 434 

2002), and birds (Ellegren et al., 2012; Sætre & Sæther, 2010; Warmuth et al., 2021), 435 

indicating that recent divergence followed by secondary contact is a recurrent outcome of 436 

post-glacial range dynamics. 437 

Demographic modelling indicates that secondary contact occurred independently across 438 

hybrid zones under distinct temporal and demographic contexts, providing a framework to 439 

interpret heterogeneous introgression patterns. DIYABC-RF estimates suggest the earliest 440 

contact occurred in the SE hybrid zone ~207 years ago, consistent with the first records of I. 441 

elegans in Spain (Ocharan-Larrondo, 1987) and with large effective population sizes and 442 

long-term asymmetric introgression (Sánchez-Guillén et al., 2011). In contrast, the NW (~74 443 

years ago) and NC (~33 years ago) zones represent more recent contacts with initially 444 

balanced admixture but divergent introgression trajectories between them —bidirectional in 445

NW and unidirectional in NC (Arce-Valdés, Swaegers, et al., 2025; Swaegers, Sánchez-446 

Guillén, Chauhan, et al., 2022). Together, these temporally structured histories support a 447 

mosaic hybrid zone framework (Rand & Harrison, 1989), where variation in the timing and 448 

demographic context of secondary contact shapes genomic heterogeneity, introgression 449 

patterns, and the evolution of reproductive isolation across zones (Arce-Valdés, Ballén-450 

Guapacha, et al., 2025; Ballén-Guapacha et al., 2024). 451 

At the same time, our results indicate that genomic architecture strongly influences where 452 

gene flow is constrained or facilitated. Autosomal loci show widespread excess introgression, 453 

but only a small fraction of these loci is shared among hybrid zones, suggesting that 454 

autosomal gene flow is context dependent and shaped by local demographic and ecological 455 

conditions rather than intrinsic species differences. In contrast, X-linked loci showed reduced 456 

permeability to introgression compared to autosomes and follow neutral genomic cline 457 

expectations. This neutral pattern of the X chromosome, which has been previously reported 458 

in this system (Swaegers, Sánchez-Guillén, Chauhan, et al., 2022), supports the view that sex 459 

chromosomes often act as stable barriers to gene flow (Horta et al., 2025; Presgraves, 2018), 460 

although demographic history may further modulate their resistance to introgression. 461 

Beyond these genome-wide introgression patterns, which are consistent with previous studies 462 

in this hybrid system that documented contrasting hybridisation dynamics and evolutionary 463 

outcomes between the NC and NW hybrid zones (Arce-Valdés, Ballén-Guapacha, et al., 464 

2025; Arce-Valdés, Swaegers, et al., 2025; Ballén-Guapacha et al., 2024). This aligns our 465 

results with a growing body of literature showing that genomic permeability to introgression 466 

can vary geographically within the same species pair (Harrison & Larson, 2014; Taylor et al., 467 

2015). This variation can be driven by factors such as effective population size, initial 468 

proportions of parental species, the strength of reproductive barriers at the onset of secondary 469 

contact, and the time since initial hybridisation (Barton & Hewitt, 1985; Payseur & 470 

Rieseberg, 2016). Consequently, similar barriers to gene flow can yield markedly different 471 

genomic outcomes across geographically replicated hybrid zones (Ravinet et al., 2017). From 472 

a broader perspective, these findings reinforce the view that hybrid zones are dynamic 473 

systems that change through time rather than static endpoints of species interactions. The 474 

recent divergence of these species—likely associated with post-glacial processes—and their 475 

subsequent secondary contact and rapid range expansion in the Iberian Peninsula under 476 

contemporary climate warming (Arce-Valdés, Swaegers, et al., 2025) make this system 477 

particularly informative for understanding speciation with gene flow under natural 478 

conditions. In line with patterns documented in other hybrid model systems—such as 479 

Heliconius butterflies (Edelman et al., 2019; Martin et al., 2013), Bombina toads (Fijarczyk 480 

et al., 2011), Ficedula flycatchers (Ellegren et al., 2012), and house mice (Macholán et al., 481 

2011)—the contrasting genomic patterns observed among hybrid zones of different ages in 482 

I. elegans and I. graellsii illustrate how demographic history and genomic architecture jointly 483 

interact to shape introgression dynamics and speciation trajectories in evolving landscapes. 484

Functional biases in genomic introgression 485 

Patterns of introgression observed here are consistent with a growing body of work showing 486 

that gene flow across species boundaries is not random, but instead biased toward particular 487 

functional classes of genes (Burgarella et al., 2019; Janoušek et al., 2015). Across a wide 488 

range of insect hybrid systems, loci involved in broadly acting cellular and regulatory 489 

functions —such as signalling, protein binding, transport, and general metabolic processes— 490 

are more likely to introgress than genes directly associated with reproductive isolation, which 491 

tend to remain resistant to gene flow (Horta et al., 2025), suggesting that repeatability in 492 

introgression often emerges at the level of gene function rather than gene identity. 493 

Importantly, however, such conclusions have largely been drawn from interspecific 494 

comparisons, either across different species pairs or among independent hybrid systems 495 

(Edelman et al., 2019; Fontaine et al., 2015; Gompert et al., 2012; Martin et al., 2013; Taylor 496 

& Larson, 2019). In contrast, our results show that similar patterns of functional bias in 497 

introgression can also be detected at an intraspecific scale, across multiple hybrid zones 498 

formed between the same pair of species. Despite marked differences among hybrid zones in 499 

demographic history, directionality of gene flow, and the strength of reproductive barriers, 500 

loci showing excess introgression consistently belonged to similar functions. Notably, 501 

overlap among introgressed SNPs across hybrid zones was limited, indicating that 502 

repeatability emerges at the level of gene function rather than the repeated introgression of 503 

the same loci. Although some functional categories associated with introgressed loci were 504 

specific to individual hybrid zones, these patterns may reflect local adaptation of I. elegans 505 

to warmer colonised environments. Importantly, this suggests that even within a single 506 

species pair, convergence in introgression patterns can arise from functional similarity rather 507 

than shared genetic identity. Nevertheless, these results should be interpreted cautiously, and 508 

future analyses with datasets with higher genomic representation (e.g. Whole Genome 509 

Sequencing) will be necessary to robustly evaluate these patterns through gene ontology 510 

enrichment approaches; and to confirm whether the observed functional associations truly 511 

reflect local adaptive processes. 512 

Finally, the functional categories associated with introgressed loci in our study also overlap 513 

with gene functions previously implicated in ecological and physiological responses in I. 514 

elegans . Previous work has shown that genes involved in regulation, metabolism, transport, 515 

and cellular signalling play a central role in mediating responses to thermal gradients, 516 

environmental stress, and rapid range expansion in this species (Dudaniec et al., 2018; 517 

Lancaster et al., 2015). In this context, the introgression of loci associated with broadly acting 518 

cellular functions is consistent with existing evidence that responses to new environments in 519 

I. elegans are underpinned by genes with general physiological roles, rather than narrowly 520 

specialised pathways. 521

Our results show that hybridisation between I. elegans and I. graellsii reflects a dynamic 523 

interplay between demographic history and genomic architecture, generating heterogeneous 524 

introgression landscapes across independently formed hybrid zones. Variation in the timing 525 

of secondary contact, admixture proportions, and effective population sizes was associated 526 

with strongly zone-specific introgression dynamics, whereas chromosome architecture 527 

imposed consistent constraints: autosomes remained comparatively permeable to gene flow, 528 

while the X chromosome acted as a stable barrier. Notably, autosomal loci showing excess 529 

introgression differed among hybrid zones, yet converged on similar functional categories, 530 

revealing functional repeatability despite genomic contingency. Together, these findings 531 

suggest that demographic context drives geographic variation in introgression outcomes, 532 

while intrinsic genomic architecture and functional constraints can promote partly 533 

predictable evolutionary responses during early stages of speciation with gene flow. 534
