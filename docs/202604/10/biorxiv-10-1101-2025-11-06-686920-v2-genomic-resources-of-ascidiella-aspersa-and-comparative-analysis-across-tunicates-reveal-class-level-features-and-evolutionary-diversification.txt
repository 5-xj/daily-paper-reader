Abstract (349 / 350 words) 19 

Background 20 

Ascidiella aspersa is an invasive tunicate and one of the closest relatives of vertebrates. Despite its 21 ecological nuisance, A. aspersa is anticipated to be a valuable model organism for developmental 22 studies due to its remarkably transparent embryos. However, annotated genome assemblies and 23 transcriptomic resources have not yet been fully established. Although several tunicate genomes have 24 been sequenced, most lack annotations, and a comprehensive analysis across tunicates has not yet 25 been conducted. 26 

Results 27 We performed de novo genome assembly and transcriptome analysis of A. aspersa, producing a high-28 quality 306.5 Mb genome assembly. The transcriptome was derived from nine different organs of 29 adults and embryos at six developmental stages. Ab initio and homology-based gene predictions 30 identified 24,504 genes with a BUSCO score of 92.2%. Functional annotation was added for 18,636 31 genes in the model. To understand the relative features of this species among tunicates, we conducted 32 genome-wide comparative analysis using publicly available data from 35 other tunicate genomes 33 across three classes, five orders, and 12 families, thus constructing gene models for 27 species with 34 BUSCO scores >80%. Overall, phylogenetic analysis revealed a new hypothesis regarding the 35 relationships among Phlebobranchia and Aplousobranchia families. Background 51 Tunicates belong to a sister group of vertebrates and consist of approximately 3,000 species 52 traditionally divided into three classes: Appendicularia, Thaliacea, and Ascidiacea [1]. Their unique 53 evolutionary position makes them important clades in evo-devo studies, particularly in Ascidiacea 54 species, which have sessile adults and planktonic embryos suitable for developmental experiments 55 due to their many unique merits including a short period of embryogenesis, invariant cleavage 56 patterns, and compact genomes [2]. For studying initial development, Ciona intestinalis type A 57 (Ciona robusta ) and Halocynthia roretzi are primarily used for studying initial development and have 58 led to significant discoveries in a variety of scientific fields [2, 3]. 59 Recently, Phallusia mammillata has emerged as a new model ascidian due to its remarkably 60 transparent embryos, which enable whole embryo live imaging and thereby greatly facilitate the 61 understanding of the developmental mechanisms in chordate [4]. The family Ascidiidae, which 62 includes P. mammillata , is characterized by distinctive biological traits such as transparent eggs and 63 embryos [5]. However, as P. mammillata is geographically restricted to Europe, alternative model 64 organisms with similar optical transparency are needed in other regions. 65 One promising candidate is Ascidiella aspersa, a cosmopolitan ascidian species belonging to the 66 family Ascidiidae. Although A. aspersa is an invasive species that causes biofouling in shellfish 67 aquaculture, particularly in Japan [6–8], its biological characteristics make it an exceptionally 68 valuable experimental organism. The eggs and embryos of A. aspersa are highly transparent— 69 transmitting approximately 90% of visible light—and its unfertilized eggs retain mRNA translation 70 capability [5, 9]. Moreover, a standardized developmental table and a three dimensional 71 embryological image resource (R AMNE) have already been established for this species [9]. 72 These features, together with its worldwide distribution and ease of collection, highlight A. aspersa 73 as a practical and powerful model system for developmental and molecular studies. Although low 74 fertilization efficiency has been a limitation, an improved fertilization protocol has recently been 75 developed to overcome this issue [10]. Despite these advances, a high-quality, annotated genome 76 assembly—an essential foundation for molecular and genetic analyses—has not yet been available 77 for A. aspersa .78

ANISEED currently hosts 12 tunicate genomes, and additional tunicate genomes 81 are available in NCBI; however, some lack gene annotation or contain low-quality gene annotations. 82 Furthermore, comprehensive comparative analysis across tunicate genomes has not yet been 83 conducted. 84 Here, we present a de novo genome assembly and transcriptome analysis of A. aspersa along with 85 updated gene models for 38 tunicate species. Methods 91 

Genome sequencing and assembly of A. aspersa .92 

A. aspersa specimens were collected from fixed scallop ropes at Onagawa (Onagawa Field Center, 93 Tohoku University) in October 2019. We used sperm as a haploid DNA source to simplify genome 94 assembly and obtain rich amount of DNA, according to the literature [11]. Sperm from a single adult 95 was isolated for DNA extraction. DNA was extracted using the Qiagen DNeasy Kit and quantified 96 using a spectrophotometer and bioanalyzer; subsequently, it was stored at -80°C until sequencing. 97 Hi-C was not performed. The genome sequence was obtained using Pacific Bioscience (PacBio) 98 Sequel and Illumina NovaSeq. PacBio generated over 46.7 Gb of long reads (2.9 million reads), 99 while Illumina paired-end sequencing produced 70 Gb, which was used for three rounds of error 100 correction of long-read data (Table S2). 101 For genome assembly, wtdbg2 [12], SMARTdenovo [13], Flye [14], and Canu [15] were employed 102 with three rounds of polishing. Repetitive sequences in the genome were identified and masked using 103 Repeat Modeler v2.0.4 [16] and RepeatMasker v4.1.5 [17]. Completeness of the genome assembly 104 was assessed using BUSCO v5 [18] on the gVolante platform [19], utilizing the eukaryota database. 105

Phylogenetic analysis 184 OrthoFinder v2.5.4 [54] was used to analyze tunicate amino acid sequences from multiple species’ 185 genomes. One of cephalochordate Branchiostoma floridae was selected as an outgroup and genomic 186 data were downloaded from the NCBI database via accession number GCF_000003815.2. 187 Phylogenetic analysis was performed according to Maeda et al [55]. In each orthogroup, gene trees 188 were constructed using MAFFT and FastTree [56] in OrthoFinder. A species tree with all species was 189 drawn from gene trees in orthogroups with all species present using STAG in OrthoFinder [57]. 190 Single-copy orthogroups were used in the following analysis for more precise analysis without the 191 effect of paralogues. Protein sequences were trimmed using trimAl v1.4.rev15 [58] and aligned using 192 MAFFT v7.505 [59]. Aligned sequences were analyzed using IQ-TREE2 v2.1.4-beta [60] for 193 maximum likelihood analysis. Bootstrap resampling with 1,000 pseudo-replicates was performed to 194 obtain statistical support for the nodes. The ML tree was converted to an ultrametric tree based on 195 divergence times between Cephalochordata and the clade comprising Vertebrata and Tunicata (578 196 Myr) between Stolidobranchia and the clade comprising Thaliacea, Phlebobranchia, and 197 Aplousobranchia (389 Myr); between Thaliacea and the clade comprising Phlebobranchia and 198 Aplousobranchia (296 Myr); and between Molgulidae and the clade comprising Pyulidae and 199 Styelidae (350 Myr) [1]. The conversion was conducted using r8s v1.81 [61] for subsequent analysis. 200 201 

Gene duplication analysis 202 The percentage of duplicated genes was calculated by the ratio of genes assigned to any orthogroup 203 to the total gene number. Rapidly expanded or contracted families were identified using CAFÉ v4.2.1 204 [62]. For Gene Ontology (GO) analysis, all protein sequences were used as a query to search human 205 protein sequences using BLASTP. Homologies were filtered by BLASTP e-value < 0.01. Genes with 206 the lowest e-value in each orthogroup were used as representative genes. Enrichment analysis used 207 the enrichGO function in the R package clusterProfiler v4.2.1 [63]. 208

GC content analysis of tunicate genomes 210 GC content in tunicate genomes was collected from the NCBI database or calculated using SeqKit. 211 Coding sequences (CDS) were extracted using gffread. CDS sequences, including alternative splices, 212 were converted into unique sequences for each locus, and sequences without start/stop codons were 213 omitted using R scripts. The GC content in each position of codons was calculated using R scripts. 214 Statistics were presented using the ggplot2 package in R [64]. Kernel density estimation was 215 performed using the geom_density function. Codon usage of each CDS was calculated using cusp 216 script in EMBOSS [65]. Dimension reduction of codon usage fractions used PCA utilizing the built-217 in R packages. A parameter evaluating codon usage bias was effective number of codons (ENC) 218 ranging from 20 (only one synonymous codon for encoding amino acids) to 61 (every synonymous 219 codon is used equally) calculated using CodonW v1.4.4 (http://codonw.sourceforge.net/). A smaller 220 ENC value indicates stronger codon usage bias. ENC-GC3 plots and theoretical ENC curves were 221 drawn using the formula (1) [66]. If codon usage bias is affected only by mutation pressure, then the 222 genes will be located on or near the theoretical curve, while natural selection will position them 223 below the curve [67]. 224

Comparison of gene numbers in constructed gene models 278 Species with larger genomes generally exhibited a higher number of predicted genes (Fig. S2a–b). B. 279 

schlosseri and O. longicauda exhibit an exceptionally high number of predicted genes relative to 280 their moderate genome sizes compared to other tunicates. Notably, these species show relatively low 281 BUSCO completeness scores ( ≤80%). The phylogeny of the Tunicata 514 Phylogenetic analysis in tunicates has been complicated by accelerated rates of evolution in their 515 nuclear and mitochondrial genomes, which could result in long-branch attraction (LBA) [1]. The 516 long branch observed in Oikopleura dioica reflects the high evolutionary rate of this species 517 complicating the debate regarding its basal position as the sister group of all other tunicates [76]. 518 Additionally, 18S rRNA sequences of Aplousobranchia species seem to evolve at high rates causing 519 long branches [77]. In our STAG tree, long branches were not observed in Appendicularians 520 suggesting that transcriptomic-level analysis might resolve the long branch effects in tunicates, 521 although this analysis contains the effects of paralogs (Fig. S3a). Long branches were not seen in 522 Aplousobranchia in the ML tree, and Thaliacea contained a slightly long branch (Fig. S3b). 523 Two main unresolved phylogenetic questions in tunicates are: 1) the relationships among the three 524 classes of tunicates, and 2) the relationships between Phlebobranchia and Aplousobranchia. For the 525 first question, reliable appendicularian genomes are necessary because there are still no reliable 526 phylogenetic analysis based on ML or Bayesian. Salpidae in Thaliacea were placed within 527 Ascidiacea with 100% bootstrap support in our analysis (Fig. 1b; Fig. S3b) consistent with recent 528 literature, while the monophyly of Thaliacea should be proven through phylogenomics approaches. 529 Regarding the second question, there is no consensus on the relationships among families in 530 Phlebobranchia and Aplousobranchia with completely reliable bootstrap values (Fig. S4). To address 531 this problem, comprehensive sampling of various taxa is required because there are few 532 representatives from five families in Phlebobranchia, including Agneziidae and Plurelidae—many of 533 which inhabit deep-sea environment. The problem is even more serious in Aplousobranchia, which 534 has the highest number of species and families in tunicates, making species identification relatively 535 difficult [78]. For phylogenomic approaches, several alternative strategies may be helpful: using 536 genes that are not necessarily single-copy [79] and accurately identifying orthologs [80]. 537

Biological significance of GC Content 567 The biological significance of GC variation in tunicates remains unresolved. However, our 568 comparative analyses showed that GC-related genomic features differ systematically among the 569 major tunicate classes rather than varying randomly among species (Figs. 2–5). Ascidiaceans 570 generally exhibited moderate genomic GC content, and both Ascidiacea and Appendicularia showed 571 major peaks in genome GC, CDS GC, and GC3. Appendicularia also displayed distinctive features, 572 including a bimodal GC1 distribution and a downward shift in GC2. Thaliaceans showed a different 573 overall distribution from those of Ascidiacea and Appendicularia, including low genomic GC values 574 in some species. These results suggest that GC content, GC3, and codon usage reflect class-575 associated modes of genome evolution across tunicates [81]. 576 577 One implication of this pattern is that class-level differences in nucleotide composition are 578 accompanied by differences in coding-sequence properties. Because GC3 is closely linked to 579 synonymous codon usage, the observed differences among Ascidiacea, Appendicularia, and 580 Thaliacea are likely to affect codon preference and thus influence how coding information is 581 structured in each lineage[85, 86]. In this sense, GC-related variation is not only a compositional 582 property of genomes but also a potential indicator of lineage-specific coding constraints. 583 584 Our results also suggest that the mechanisms shaping GC bias may differ among tunicate classes. The 585 class-associated patterns observed in genomic GC content, GC3, codon usage, and ENC-GC3 586 distributions are consistent with differences in the relative contributions of mutational bias, selection 587 on codon usage, and GC-biased gene conversion (gBGC) [81, 86, 87]. However, the present study 588 does not allow these mechanisms to be distinguished conclusively. Likewise, although these GC-589 related features may ultimately be linked to ecological or life-history traits, our current data support 590 this only at the level of comparative genomic tendencies, not direct functional causation. 591 592 Overall, the present analyses show that GC content, GC3, and codon usage are informative 593 comparative features for understanding genome evolution at the class level in tunicates. Conclusions 641 We constructed genomic and transcriptomic resources for A. aspersa . Other tunicate species’ 642 genomes were also annotated, and gene models were rebuilt achieving high-quality gene models 643 containing 27 species with BUSCO scores greater than 80%. Phylogenetic analysis produced a new 644 hypothesis in tunicate phylogeny, and gene duplication analysis characterized multiple taxa within 645 tunicates.
