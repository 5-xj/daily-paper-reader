Abstract 11 Conservation interventions are increasingly required for species threatened by population 12 declines and isolation due to anthropogenic pressures. Small, isolated populations are 13 particularly vulnerable to the loss of genetic diversity, increased inbreeding, and the 14 accumulation of deleterious mutations. Translocations or supplementation of allopatric 15 individuals for genetic rescue may be the only way to increase genetic diversity to increase 16 population persistence via increased adaptive potential. Here, we use an experimentally 17 admixed population of sand lizards on a small island in Sweden as a valuable model of 18 genetic rescue. This population was established approximately 20 years ago (5-6 19 generations) resulting in increased fecundity and hatchling viability. This population was 20 founded from crossings between individuals from an inbred population from the nearby 21 mainland and individuals sourced from populations in southern Sweden. Low-coverage 22 whole-genome sequencing revealed elevated genetic diversity and reduced realized genetic 23 load in this admixed population relative to the source populations. Ancestry analyses 24 indicated a greater contribution of southern Swedish genetic variation, potentially reflecting 25 contribution of beneficial adaptive variation from this region that may underlie the positive 26

1. Introduction 31 Conservation biology is increasingly confronted with the widespread erosion of genetic 32 diversity within populations and across species worldwide, predominately impacted from 33 anthropogenic activities (Shaw et al , 2025). Fragmented and declining populations suffer 34 from loss of genetic diversity when small and isolated, leading the progressive loss of 35 adaptive potential due to genetic drift and reduced fitness due to the fixation of deleterious 36 alleles in the population where purifying selection is inefficient (Robinson et al , 2023). Loss of 37 genetic diversity may seriously constrain the evolutionary potential of many populations. 38 Restricting these effects, or reversing them may rely on translocations (supplementation), 39 especially in populations where gene flow between populations cannot be established 40 through e.g. restoration of habitat connectivity and corridors. Translocation of more 41 genetically diverse individuals from other populations into one that is genetically depauperate 42 is aimed at resulting in admixture. This would increase genetic diversity and increase 43 individual fitness, leading to a recovery of population size and potentially improved 44 population persistence (Whiteley et al , 2015). Indeed, in a global meta-analysis of genetic 45 diversity across taxa, supplementation was the only conservation management action 46 associated with a statistically significant mean increase in genetic diversity over time (Shaw 47 

et al , 2025). 48 When populations have been small for a long time, they accumulate weak to moderate 49 deleterious mutations due to drift (Dehasque et al , 2024; Robinson et al , 2023). Admixture 50 into these populations can help alleviate this realised genetic load by masking recessive 51 deleterious mutations, but this will also introduce new deleterious mutations. Population size 52 recovery after translocation is a key factor in the success of such conservation measures, as 53 an increase in population size should decrease the influence of drift and allow more effective 54 selection. Together, this should prevent the fixation of weakly deleterious mutations 55 (Robinson et al , 2023) and allow purifying selection to remove previously fixed genetic load 56 (Hedrick and Garcia-Dorado, 2016). Successful genetic rescue has been exemplified in a 57 variety of species, including cases of human-assisted translocations in the Florida panther 58

2. Materials and methods 106 

2.1. Sampling 107 Sand lizards were sampled from two sites on the west coast of Sweden (Fig. 1A): 108 Asketunnan (57°22 N, 11°59´ E) and Stora Keholmen (57°29 ′ N 11°56 ′ E). Blood was 109 sampled for genetics from the vena angularis (corner of the mouth) using capillary tubes into 110 80-95% ethanol and stored at -20ºC or -80ºC for long-term storage. Sand lizards were then 111 released back to their site of capture. 112 

2.2. Laboratory procedures 113 We extracted genomic DNA from 130 individuals from Asketunnan (years 1998, 1999, 114 2011, 2012) and 112 individuals from Stora Keholmen (years 2017, 2018), selected at 115 random, using QIAGEN DNeasy blood and tissue kits following the manufacturers protocol 116 for blood with a 1-hour proteinase K digestion. We constructed sequencing libraries using an 117 in-house Tn5 protocol as per Picelli et al (2014). Individuals were uniquely indexed and 118 pooled in multiplexes of up to 65 individuals in a total of four pools. Each pool was 119 sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 S2 lane at the SNP&SEQ Technology Platform 120 (Uppsala, Sweden) and reads were separated bioinformatically based on their index. 121 Individuals were sequenced at a mean coverage of 2.16x (range: 1.3-6.2x), with largely 122 uniform coverage along the autosomal chromosomes. High coverage whole genome 123 sequencing data from four Stora Keholmen individuals was also available. 124

2.4. Genotype imputation 138 We inferred genotype probabilities with ANGSD using the GATK algorithm (-GL 2). We 139 filtered out reads with a flag above 255, multiple best hits, mapping quality < 30 and 140 improperly paired reads (-remove_bads 1 -uniqueOnly 1 -minMapQ 30 -only_proper_pairs 1) 141 and we only retained sites with a p-value < 1e-3. For these sites, we extracted the major and 142 minor allele and used them for imputation of missing genotypes with STITCH v1.6.8 (Davies 143 

et al , 2016). We imputed genotypes separately for the recently established, outbred Stora 144 Keholmen and the Asketunnan population with lower genetic variation because of their 145 markedly different population histories. For both populations, we used a generation time of 146 20 and 10 ancestral haplotypes to allow for sufficient coverage per haplotype given our 147 sample size and sequencing depth. A preliminary analysis indicated that the number of 148 inferred SNPs started to stagnate with 6 ancestral haplotypes for the Stora Keholmen 149 population. We retained SNPs with an info score > 0.4, HWE > 1e-6, and < 20% missing 150 genotypes. We re-imputed missing genotypes at these sites with Beagle v3.2.2 (Browning 151 and Yu, 2009) without a reference panel and merged the genotype probability files of the two 152 populations keeping only shared sites. We restricted all further analyses to autosomes. 153

3. Results 229 

3.1. Population structure 230 The Stora Keholmen and the Asketunnan population showed clear separation in both the 231 PCA and admixture analysis. PC1 consistently clustered Stora Keholman with other Swedish 232 populations and separated Asketunnan from all other populations (Fig. 1B). Similarly, the 233 admixture analysis split Asketunnan from all other populations at K = 2 (Fig. 1C, Supp. fig. 234 A1). The optimal K was 5 (Supp. fig. A2), which splits each population into a separate cluster 235 and suggests admixture with Asketunnan and Löderup in the Stora Keholmen population 236 (Fig. 1C, Supp. fig. A1). The historic and modern Asketunnan populations formed a single 237 cluster in both the PCA and the admixture analysis. PCadapt detected 423 genomic windows 238 that were associated with the separation between Stora Keholmen and Asketunnan (Supp. 239 table B1). These windows were spread relatively uniformly along the chromosomes with the 240 exception of chromosomes 11, 15 and 17 (NC_046322.1, NC_046326.1, and NC_046328.1, 241 respectively) that had no or very few outlier windows. 242 We identified 2512 sites that were nearly fixed for alternative alleles in the Asketunnan 243 and the Löderup populations. Inference of ancestry for these sites in the Stora Keholmen 244 population indicated that most of them were polymorphic (Fig. 2). On average, Asketunnan 245 ancestry reached about 41%. However, 408 sites had at least 75% Löderup ancestry and 246 only 36 sites had at least 75% Asketunnan ancestry. One site was fixed for Asketunnan 247 ancestry and 43 sites were fixed for Löderup ancestry (Supp. table B2). 248

4. Discussion 285 Overall, the admixed origin of the Stora Keholmen population has resulted in greater genetic 286 diversity in this population in comparison to one of its ancestral mainland populations, 287 Asketunnan, both in terms of estimated heterozygosity and nucleotide diversity. Stora 288 Keholmen was established by an introduction of relatively large number of admixed 289 hatchlings (n=454), that were the result of sperm competition/cryptic female choice 290 outbreeding experiments between individuals from Asketunnan and southern Sweden (75 291 females were copulated to 51 males in the first ovulatory cycle and 35 males in a second 292 ovulatory cycle) (Lindsay et al , 2020). These experiments were designed to analyse mating 293 system effects (polyandry) on bias in probability of paternity in clutches with mixed paternity 294 when females mated with males of varying relatedness to the female, and to their competing 295 rival (Olsson et al , 1996; Olsson et al , 1997). The studies showed that the least related male 296 in both lab- and field matings had the highest probability of paternity (Olsson et al , 1996), and 297 that the more related competing males were, the more even were their share of paternity of 298 the clutch (Olsson et al , 1997). Subsequent to hatchling release, it agrees with logic that this 299 outbreeding effect on probability of paternity is likely to have been further reinforced, but it is 300 possible that the population experienced a pronounced founder effect at this time with higher 301 survival of more outbred hatchlings. First-year survival is typically very low in sand lizards 302 (Olsson et al , 1994), with some temporal variation in survival linked to seasonal and yearly 303 fluctuations in environmental conditions (Olsson and Madsen, 2001). Despite this, substantial 304 genetic variation remains across the genome and has persisted for over 5–6 generations 305 since founding. The founder effect could have been modified by the introduction into a 306 favourable and lizard-free environment, with reduced competition and potentially lower 307 predation than on the mainland (potentially with a shift in main predators from crows ( Corvus 308 

corone ) and magpies ( Pica pica ) to kestrels ( Falco tinnunculus ) on the island; anecdotal 309 observations). The large effective population size on Stora Keholmen would facilitate more 310

The greater 320 genetic contribution of southern Swedish ancestry to Stora Keholmen likely reflects selection 321 on beneficial genetic variation from southern Sweden that entered the Stora Keholmen 322 population. Genetic diversity is generally higher and realised genetic load lower in southern 323 Swedish populations (Lillie et al , 2025), whereas diversity in Asketunnan has been shaped 324 by strong genetic drift and inbreeding, resulting in higher fixed genetic load. Increased 325 heterozygosity in Stora Keholmen could have also assisted the initial population growth 326 through increased hatchling success, as improved offspring viability has been reported both 327 in terms of increase hatchling success and reduced hatchling malformations (Lindsay et al ,328 2020). For example, the successful genetic rescue of Florida panthers was the 339 result of a translocation of just eight females from Texas into Florida, producing positive long-340 term effects on panther fitness and population size (Onorato et al , 2024). In this case, whole 341 genome sequencing has shown that admixture increased genetic diversity without swamping 342 local ancestry, but modelling suggests that future translocations would be necessary 343 (Aguilar-Gómez et al , 2025). In another case of genetic rescue in European adders, the 344 introduction of 20 novel males in 1992 resulted in a rapid increase in genetic diversity, 345 improvements in offspring viability and long-term growth of the population (Madsen et al ,346 2020; Madsen et al , 1999). The genetic rescue of this adder population may have also 347 enhanced their population resilience, as the population was able to successfully recover from 348 a later bottleneck with rapid population growth and minimal genetic effects (Madsen et al ,349 2020). This admixed population demonstrates the advantage of mixing a large gene pool for 350 genetic rescue in a conservation context (Ralls et al , 2020). The Stora Keholmen population 351 has markedly increased genetic diversity relative to natural mainland populations, alleviating 352 the low variation seen in Asketunnan. With an apparent increase in population size, these 353 circumstances may enhance Stora Keholmen’s potential for evolutionary and local 354 adaptation (Frankham, 2015; Whiteley et al , 2015). The establishment of such a population 355 will avoid issues with deleterious variation in small populations (Robinson et al , 2023), as it 356 maximises genetic variation from many founders (Ralls et al , 2020). The benefits of genetic 357 rescue may arise not only from elevated individual genetic diversity, but also from the 358 generation of new genomic variation through highly fit hybrids and subsequent population 359 growth (Fitzpatrick et al , 2020). Thus, increased genetic variation provides the substrate for 360 recombination and population size allows selection upon innovations of beneficial genetic 361 variation, enhancing population persistence. Future studies in this population to trace 362 temporal changes in allele frequencies over subsequent generations would be valuable to 363 identify putative loci under selection. Functional associations between loci and fitness traits 364 would also be valuable to uncover the ancestry or innovation of adaptive genetic variation in 365 this population. 366

16 We reflect on the feasibility of the low-coverage sequencing approach for conservation 367 practice. Low-coverage sequencing offers a cost-efficient way to survey genomic variation, 368 enabling studies of population structure, admixture, and genetic diversity even in non-model 369 species where budgets and sample availability are limited. For example, coverage as low as 370 0.5–2× can generate sufficient genetic data to infer population structure and diversity metrics 371 to inform conservation decisions (Lou et al , 2021). The analysis of our low-coverage 372 sequencing approach has relied on genotype likelihood-based analyses and imputation. 373 Computational resources to analyse genotype likelihood-based data were extremely 374 demanding in terms of RAM and CPU requirements, relying on the availability of nationally 375 centralized bioinformatics support (via the National Academic Infrastructure for 376 Super-computing in Sweden). These computational challenges, including the availability of 377 high-performance resources and bioinformatics competence may represent a major logistic 378 barrier for the application of this relatively low-cost sequencing approach by conservation 379 practitioners. Strengthening collaborations between academic researchers and conservation 380 practitioners will be essential to bridge this gap, enabling the translation of genomic research 381 into actionable conservation management (Hogg, 2024). Continued investments in 382 computational infrastructure, standardized workflows, and training initiatives can further 383 enhance the accessibility and practicality of low-coverage genomic approaches for real-world 384 conservation applications. 385 Translocations may represent the only effective conservation action option to address loss 386 of genetic diversity in threatened populations (Shaw et al , 2025). The Stora Keholmen sand 387 lizard population, thus, serves as a valuable model population of genetic rescue and how 388 large-scale admixture can be applied for strategic conservation management of fragmented, 389 small populations of threatened species. Genetic differentiation between such fragmented 390 and small populations would be primarily driven by genetic drift, loss of genetic diversity and 391 fixation of deleterious genetic variation. These factors undermine population persistence, but 392 can be alleviated via strategic translocations.
