Abstract 1

The process of speciation, through which two reproductively isolated species emerge from an 2

ancestral population, is often gradual, including a period during which gene flow still occurs, and 3

hence speciation remains reversible. However, if isolation persists, the progressive accumulation 4

of genetic incompatibilities ultimately locks the speciation process, reaching the point where hybrid 5

offspring are inviable or sterile. In plants and animals, the establishment of species barriers is quite 6

rapid: beyond a net genetic divergence of 0.02 substitutions per synonymous site, interspecific 7

introgressions become extremely rare. Here we report that in ciliates, species barriers can be 8

broken despite huge genetic divergence. We show that the genome of Paramecium sonneborni 9

contains hundreds of DNA segments that were acquired by recurrent horizontal transfers from 10 

multiple very distant lineages (synonymous divergence ~1 substitution/site). These segments 11 

approach the size of full-length chromosomes, which indicates that they were introgressed by inter-12 

specific mating. These data show that in the ancestry of P. sonneborni , hybridization events with 13 

highly divergent species occurred repeatedly and led to viable and fertile offspring. We propose 14 

that this unexpected promiscuous sexual behavior is made possible by the fact that paramecia 15 

separate somatic and germline functions in two types of nuclei: the elimination of non-self DNA 16 

fragments during the development of the somatic genome allows individuals of a species to remain 17 

phenotypically the same, despite recurrent genetic flow from distant species into their germline. 18

Significance Statement 20 

Up to what degree of genetic divergence is it possible to form viable and fertile hybrids between 21 

two distinct species? This question is key to identifying the factors contributing to speciation. In 22 

plants and animals, the exchange of genetic material between species appears to be extremely 23 

rare beyond a divergence of 0.02 substitutions per synonymous site, suggesting that genetic 24 

incompatibilities accumulate quite rapidly. By analyzing the genome of the ciliate Paramecium 25 

sonneborni , we discovered that this unicellular eukaryote recurrently acquired large DNA segments 26 

via hybridization with very distantly related species (divergence of up to 1.0 substitutions per site). 27 

We propose a mechanistic model to explain how these ciliates can escape the impact of genetic 28 

incompatibilities. 29

Introduction 33 

34 

An important issue in evolutionary biology is to understand the process of speciation, i.e. how two 35 

reproductively isolated species emerge from an ancestral population. Various factors can initiate 36 

the speciation process (e.g. isolation by geographic barriers, adaptation to distinct ecological 37 

niches, etc.), but whatever the initial factor, once two populations become reproductively isolated 38 

from each other, they start evolving independently, and hence progressively accumulate genetic 39 

incompatibilities. These so-called Bateson–Dobzhansky–Muller incompatibilities (BDMIs) 40 

decrease the fitness of hybrid individuals, and ultimately lead to their sterility or non-viability (1). 41 

The speciation process is often gradual, i.e. includes a ‘grey zone’ during which genetic 42 

introgressions are still possible between “semi-isolated” species (2). Thus, if secondary contacts 43 

occur during the early phase, then hybridizations may restore genetic homogeneity, thereby 44 

stopping the speciation process. However, as BDMIs accrue, selection is expected to favor the 45 

evolution of traits that prevent interspecific mating, thereby further reinforcing genetic isolation. A 46 

key point is therefore to unravel the factors that determine the rate at which BDMIs accumulate. 47 

The analysis of population genomics data from a wide range of taxa has shown that introgressions 48 

are restricted to species that are genetically very close: in animals, gene flow appears to be 49 

abolished as soon as the net synonymous divergence exceeds 0.02 substitutions per site, and this 50

Here we report an extreme exception to this rule, that we discovered incidentally in the course of 54 

sequencing the genomes of several paramecia species (6). We present evidence of recurrent 55 

events of introgression within the genome of Paramecium sonneborni , originating from very remote 56 

species (synonymous divergence ~1 substitution/site), and we propose a model explaining how 57 

fertile hybrids can be formed between such distant species, despite BDMIs. 58 

To facilitate the interpretation of our observations, it is useful to give some background information 59 

on the biology and genetics of Paramecium species. These are aquatic unicellular eukaryotes that 60 

are commonly found in ponds, where they feed on bacteria, yeast, or algae. They were among the 61 

first ciliates to be observed, thanks to the invention of the microscope in the late 17 th century, and 62 

since then have become a widely used model for biological studies (7). Their life cycle includes a 63 

vegetative phase (reproduction by mitotic cell divisions), followed by a sexual phase, which involves 64 

either conjugation (the pairing and reciprocal fertilization of two cells of complementary mating 65 

types) or autogamy (a self-fertilization process that evolved in an ancestor of the P. aurelia clade) 66 

(8). 67 

Like all ciliates, Paramecium cells possess two types of nuclei: a highly polyploid somatic 68 

macronucleus (MAC), responsible for gene expression, and a diploid germline micronucleus (MIC), 69 

which is not expressed, but where meiosis occurs. At each sexual generation, the parental MAC is 70 

lost, while new MIC and MAC develop from copies of the zygotic nucleus. During MAC 71 

development, many DNA segments are reproducibly eliminated, thus leading to a somatic genome 72 

that is smaller than the germline genome (9, 10). 73 

The genus Paramecium encompasses a wide diversity of species. Based on cytological 74 

observations, 19 species can be distinguished (11). However, genetic crosses have revealed a 75 

large amount of cryptic diversity within these groups. Notably, early studies have established that 76 

P. aurelia , initially considered as one ‘species’, was in fact a complex of 14 sibling species 77 

(‘syngens’) that are morphologically indistinguishable but sexually incompatible (12). More recently, 78 

phylogenetic and population genetic studies based on MAC genome sequences have confirmed 79 

that these 14 syngens correspond to bona fide species, reproductively isolated and genetically 80 

divergent (for a review, see (13)). Currently, 20 species have been ascribed to the P. aurelia 81 

species complex (14): 15 that are morphologically indistinguishable (named P. primaurelia to P. 82 

quindecaurelia ), together with P. sonneborni , P. schewiakoffi , and three sibling species of P. 83 

jenningsi (12, 15–18). This clade, whose phylogeny is now well established, encompasses a wide 84 

variety of species, genetically as distant as the most remotely related mammals (14, 19). 85 

Little is known regarding the ecological factors that may have driven the diversification of this clade. 86 

Some species are preferentially found in tropical environments (e.g. P. sexaurelia, P. octaurelia, P. 87 

tredecaurelia, P. quadecaurelia, and P. sonneborni ), or in temperate or cold climate zones (e.g. P. 88 

biaurelia and P. novaurelia ), whereas others are totally cosmopolitan (e.g. P. primaurelia or P. 89 

tetraurelia ) (20). Overall, most species have a very widespread geographical distribution and are 90 

found in sympatry on several continents (20). In the aurelia clade, the level of neutral polymorphism 91 

ranges from 0.6% in P. tetraurelia to 2.7% in P. sexaurelia and shows no clear pattern of spatial 92 

structure, no isolation by distance, indicating recent dispersal (21). One noteworthy observation is 93 

that three rounds of whole genome duplications (WGDs) occurred in the ancestry of the aurelia 94 

lineage, and the last one shortly predates the radiation of the complex (22–24). The differential loss 95 

of paralogous gene copies after a WGD is expected to be an important source of BDMIs (22, 25), 96 

and hence it has been suggested that these WGDs might have contributed to the diversification of 97 

the P. aurelia complex (22–24). 98

To investigate the organization and evolution of the germline genome, we sequenced the MIC of 7 99 

P. aurelia species (6, 19). We obtained a high-quality assembly for the P. tetraurelia MIC genome 100 

(104 Mb), which consists of ~160 tiny chromosomes (300 kb – 1.2 Mb)(6). Three genomic 101 

compartments can be distinguished, depending on their fate during the development of the new 102 

polyploid MAC: i) MIC-limited regions, that are eliminated from the MAC, ii) constitutive MAC-103 

destined regions, that are systematically retained in the MAC, and iii) MAC-variable regions, which 104 

correspond to DNA sequences that are retained in only a small fraction of MAC copies (19). These 105 

three compartments represent respectively 20%, 71% and 9% of the MIC genome. The MAC-106 

destined regions are very gene-rich (~500 genes per Mb), whereas most repeated sequences are 107 

located in the MIC-limited regions (6, 19). Two types of MIC-limited regions can be distinguished: 108 

the ‘Internal Eliminated Sequences’ (IESs, totalizing 3.9 Mb) that correspond to short sequences 109 

(typically <150bp) interspersed within MAC-destined regions and notably within genes, whereas 110 

the other eliminated sequences (16 Mb) are generally located towards the end of MIC 111 

chromosomes (6). Most other species show similar genome content, with a MIC genome ~100 Mb. 112 

The only exception is P. sonneborni , where the MIC reaches 217 Mb (6). The size of the MAC-113 

destined compartment is similar across species, ranging from 65 Mb in P. tredecaurelia to 82 Mb 114 

in P. sonneborni (19). Thus, the large size of the P. sonneborni MIC results from a huge inflation of 115 

its MIC-specific compartment. 116 

To investigate the cause of this increase, we compared P. sonneborni MIC sequences to other 117 

Paramecium genomes, and discovered that P. sonneborni contained long regions with very high 118 

sequence similarity to distantly related aurelia species. This prompted us to systematically 119 

investigate patterns of horizontal transfer across paramecia. We found evidence of recurrent events 120 

of introgression of chromosome-size segments within the MIC-specific genomic compartment of P. 121 

sonneborni , originating from very distant species. We propose that these introgressions result from 122 

allotetraploidization events, and we discuss the mechanism that could explain how these events 123 

can be tolerated despite the accumulation of BDMIs. 124

Results 126 

Evidence of massive horizontal transfer in the exceptionally large MIC genome of P. 127 

sonneborni 128 

To investigate the origin of the large MIC-limited compartment in P. sonneborni , we compared its 129 

sequences to all available genomes from 13 species of the P. aurelia complex (7 species for which 130 

both MIC and MAC genomes have been sequenced, and 6 species for which only the MAC is 131 

available). P. sonneborni is closely related to P. jenningsi (genetic distances at synonymous sites: 132 

dS=0.27), but quite distant from all the other species that we analyzed (Figure 1A), with dS values 133 

ranging from 0.8 to 1.0 substitution per site (19). This implies that outside of coding regions, 134 

sequences are generally too divergent to be aligned across these species. Yet, we noticed that 135 

several P. sonneborni MIC contigs contain long segments with very high similarity to distantly 136 

related species. For instance, we observed a P. sonneborni contig of 100kb presenting long 137 

stretches above 99% identity with a MAC scaffold from P. quadecaurelia , a level of similarity higher 138 

than with any other aurelia species, including P. jenningsi (Figure 1B). This suggests that this long 139 

genomic segment has been subjected to horizontal transfer (HT) between the P. sonneborni and 140 

P. quadecaurelia lineages. 141 

This unexpected observation led us to systematically analyze all MIC genomes (from 7 species) to 142 

search for loci presenting evidence of recent HT among the aurelia complex. For this, we divided 143 

each MIC genome (query) into non-overlapping 500 nt windows and compared (with blastn) each 144 

window with all other available Paramecium genomes (targets) (Figure 1A). We then searched for 145 

sets of neighboring windows (at least 2 kb-long in total) with conserved order and orientation 146 

between the query and the target, and showing a strong signal of HT (i.e. a level of sequence 147 

similarity significantly higher with a genome that is not the sister group of the focal species, than 148 

with any other aurelia species). 149

In most species we identified only a limited number of HT segments (1 to 22 segments, totalizing 150 

up to 144 kb; Figure 1C). Strikingly, in the P. sonneborni MIC genome, we identified 521 HT 151 

segments totaling almost 10 Mb. HT segments are on average 2 to 5 times longer in P. sonneborni 152 

(mean length = 18.4 kb) than in other species (Suppl. Fig. S1). In P. sonneborni we detected 120 153 

HT segments longer than 25 kb (totaling 5 Mb). The length of HT segments in P. sonneborni is 154 

probably underestimated because the MIC assembly is quite fragmented (N50 ~33 kb). Indeed, we 155 

found several cases where a single region in the MAC genome of a target species showed synteny 156 

with multiple P. sonneborni contigs (Figure 1B). These observations indicate that many HT 157 

segments in P. sonneborni are very long, approaching the size of an entire chromosome (>220 kb 158 

for the example shown in Figure 1B). Conversely, none of the HT segments in the other species 159 

were larger than 25 kb. 160 

Several observations allowed us to exclude the hypothesis of a DNA contamination artefact. First, 161 

contamination with DNA from the target genomes should lead to nearly identical sequences, 162 

whereas for many HT segments, the level of sequence divergence ranges between 1% and 12% 163 

(Figure 2), far too high to result from sequencing errors. Second, two of the target genomes with 164 

which we detected many HT segments ( P. quadecaurelia and P. novaurelia; Figure 1A) were 165 

sequenced in the USA (24), and were never handled in the laboratory where P. sonneborni MAC 166 

and MIC DNA were sequenced (Genoscope, France). Finally, the sequencing depth along P. 167 

sonneborni HT segments is the same as that of the rest of the genome, which would not be 168 

expected if they resulted from contaminant DNA (Suppl. Fig. S2). 169 

The HT segments detected in P. sonneborni involve four distinct lineages (represented by P. 170 

sexaurelia, P. tredecaurelia, P. quadecaurelia, and P. novaurelia ) (Figure 1A). Among HT 171 

segments detected by comparison between P. sonneborni and P. sexaurelia (the only species pair 172 

for which we had both MIC genome assemblies), we observed many conserved IESs (Suppl. Fig. 173 

S3), which demonstrates that these HT events involved the transfer of MIC DNA and not of MAC 174 

DNA (where IESs are absent). P. sexaurelia belongs to the same subclade as P. sonneborni but is 175 

nevertheless very distant (average dS=0.8; (19)). The three other lineages belong to subclade A, 176 

very divergent from P. sonneborni (dS~1; (19)). We did not detect any HT segment with the other 177 

lineages of subclade A (Figure 1A). 178 

It should be noted that MIC genome sequences are available for only 7 of the 13 analyzed species. 179 

Because of this sampling limitation, HT segments present in the MIC genome of the query were 180 

detectable only if they encompassed MAC-destined regions in the target genome (see Methods for 181 

details). Hence, the set of segments that we identified is probably an underestimate of the total 182 

amount of HT. 183 

P. sonneborni is the recipient of recurrent horizontal transfers from distantly related 184 

lineages 185 

The above results show that gene flow occurred between P. sonneborni and several other 186 

Paramecium lineages. To establish the direction of these transfers, we analyzed the distribution of 187 

HT segments in the MIC of P. sonneborni : 85% are located in the MIC-limited compartment of P. 188 

sonneborni , 14% in MAC-variable regions and only 1% in MAC-destined regions (these 189 

compartments represent respectively 55%, 7% and 38% of the MIC genome) (Figure 1A). Thus, 190 

the majority of HT segments are confined to genomic compartments that are either eliminated or 191 

variably retained during MAC development. As a result, although the HT segments correspond to 192 

MAC-destined regions in the target genomes and are therefore very gene-rich, the genes they 193 

contain are unlikely to be expressed in P. sonneborni . In total, among the 521 HT segments, we 194 

identified only 35 genes located in the MAC-destined compartment of P. sonneborni (Suppl. Table 195 

S1). Comparison with their homologs in other aurelia species showed that at least 25 of them are 196 

pseudogenes (containing nonsense or frameshift mutations), and among the ten that appear to be 197 

intact, only four show evidence of transcription (> 1RPKM) in vegetative cells. Thus, even though 198 

HT segments detected in P. sonneborni included several thousand genes, most of them have lost 199

P. sonneborni has received HT from four distinct donor lineages (Figure 1A). Several observations 209 

indicate that the detected HT segments result from numerous independent genetic transfers, and 210 

not from just four occasional events. Indeed, the level of sequence divergence observed between 211 

P. sonneborni HT segments and the target genomes varies widely among HT segments, ranging 212 

from ~0% to ~12%, even among segments coming from the same lineage (Figure 2). Two non-213 

exclusive hypotheses can be proposed to explain this large variance: i) some HT events are more 214 

ancient than others, and hence have had more time to accumulate divergence between the donor 215 

and the recipient lineages, or ii) HT segments have about the same age, but originated from multiple 216 

related lineages that vary in their level of divergence from the reference species used in our 217 

analyses. One noteworthy observation is that the length of HT segments tends to decrease as the 218 

sequence divergence increases (Figure 2): for a given donor lineage, the longest HT segments 219 

(approaching the size of full-length chromosomes) are generally less divergent from the reference 220 

target than other HT segments, whereas the most divergent HT segments are always relatively 221 

short. The most parsimonious explanation for this pattern is that after the transfer of full-length 222 

chromosomes, HT segments progressively shorten via chromosomal rearrangements (inversions, 223 

translocations, deletions) and concomitantly diverge via point substitutions. This therefore suggests 224 

that the variance in level of divergence mainly reflects the variability in the age of HT events. In any 225 

case, these observations imply that HT events from distant species occurred recurrently in the 226 

lineage of P. sonneborni. 227 

Little is known regarding the ecology of P. sonneborni . Although this species is rare, it is distributed 228 

across very wide geographical areas (the three strains reported so far are from Texas, Dominican 229 

republic and Cyprus) (15, 26, 27). The high frequency of introgression coming from P. sexaurelia, 230 

P. tredecaurelia , and P. quadecaurelia lineages might be due to the fact that like P. sonneborni ,231 

these species are preferentially found in warm climate zones (20). 232 

In principle, when two species are able to form hybrids, introgressions may occur in both directions. 233 

Unfortunately, we could not assess the presence of HT segments related to P. sonneborni in any 234 

of the four lineages from which it received DNA (represented in our data set by P. tredecaurelia, P. 235 

quadecaurelia, P. novaurelia and P. sexaurelia ): for the first three, we do not have a MIC genome 236 

assembly, and for P. sexaurelia , we do not have an appropriate sister group to detect HT from P. 237 

sonneborni . Thus, the question of whether transfers occur in both directions remains to be 238 

investigated. 239 

Discussion 240 

241 

Recurrent hybridization with genetically highly divergent species. 242 

Hybridization between closely related species is quite common in nature, but often leads to inviable 243 

or sterile offspring (5). Different types of processes cause this decreased fitness of hybrids. First, 244 

after their divergence, sister species tend to accumulate genetic incompatibilities (i.e. alleles that 245 

are neutral or beneficial in one species but that interact negatively with the alleles in the other 246 

species), so-called Bateson–Dobzhansky–Muller incompatibilities (BDMIs) (1). Notably, as each 247 

species has adapted to its specific ecological niche, the mixed set of alleles in the hybrids may turn 248

out to be unfit in both environments. Second, chromosomal rearrangements and sequence 249 

divergence impair homologous recombination, and thereby cause incorrect chromosome 250 

segregation during meiosis in hybrids (28). Hence, the probability of forming fertile hybrids (and the 251 

potential of inter-species gene flow) decreases with increasing genetic distance (5). Typically, in 252 

animals and plants, the amount of gene flow across populations/species drops drastically beyond 253 

2% of net synonymous divergence (3, 4) and in budding yeast, hybrid viability falls to almost zero 254 

above 10% of genetic divergence (28). 255 

Here we show that a large fraction of the P. sonneborni genome has been acquired via recurrent 256 

events of horizontal transfer (HT) from multiple lineages. Cases of horizontal transfers had already 257 

been reported in the P. aurelia complex (19), but they were of relatively short size (<3.5kb) and 258 

corresponded to transposable elements. Such transfers might be mediated by vectors (e.g. 259 

viruses), and therefore do not necessarily involve inter-specific mating. The size of the longest HT 260 

segments detected in P. sonneborni (> 200 kb), is in the range of germline chromosome sizes (300 261 

kb to 1 Mb) (6). This implies that they were most probably transferred as full-length chromosomes, 262 

through inter-specific conjugation. Thus, our observations reveal that the P. sonneborni lineage 263 

recurrently mated and produced fertile offspring with very distant species (synonymous divergence: 264 

dS~0.8 to 1 substitution per site). For comparison, this corresponds to the genetic distance between 265 

the most divergent mammalian taxa (e.g. monotremes vs. placental mammals (29)). 266 

Known examples of distant species able to produce viable hybrid offspring are quite rare. The three 267 

most extreme cases reported so far are two species of fern that diverged ~60 Mya (30), sturgeon 268 

and paddlefish that diverged ~184 Mya (31), and two gar fish species that diverged ~100 Mya (32). 269 

The latter case is particularly striking, because unlike the other two, the hybrids are not only viable, 270 

but also fertile (32). It has been suggested that the absence of strong BDMI in gars results from the 271 

fact that their genomes evolve extremely slowly (32). The same explanation probably holds for the 272 

two other cases: the median dS between sturgeon and paddlefish is only 0.12 (33), and in the fern, 273 

the dS between the two parental genomes is 0.1 (30). Thus, even though these species have been 274 

separated for a very long period, they are genetically quite close. Although introgressions are rare 275 

at this level of genetic divergence (3, 4), several cases have been reported. For instance, gene flow 276 

has been observed in the Drosophila yakuba clade (~3 Mya of separation), or in Ciona (sea squirts; 277 

1.5-2 Mya of separation), between species showing a dS of 0.12 to 0.14 (34, 35). But to our 278 

knowledge, P. sonneborni is the first reported case of a eukaryote showing promiscuous sexual 279 

behavior with genetically highly diverged species (dS~1). 280 

281 

Remaining the same despite hybridization with distant species. 282 

Our observations raise three main questions. First, it is unclear how such distant species could 283 

have successfully interbred. In standard lab conditions, P. sonneborni was not observed to 284 

conjugate with any other P. aurelia species, not even with the closely related P. jenningsi (15, 26). 285 

More extensive analyses in other species concluded that inter-specific conjugation may be blocked 286 

at a number of different steps: mating type recognition and agglutination, formation of conjugating 287 

pairs, cross-fertilization, and development of functional zygotic MACs. Although all P. aurelia 288 

species have two homologous mating types (O and E), inter-specific agglutination of O and E cells 289 

is only observed in some pairs of species from the same subclade, and in most cases it does not 290 

proceed to the formation of conjugating pairs (12). Inter-specific transformation experiments have 291 

shown that the E-specific transmembrane protein mtA is directly responsible for the specific 292 

recognition of O cells of the same species (36). In the lab, the need for mating type recognition can 293 

be bypassed by chemical treatments that induce the successful conjugation of a single mating type 294 

with itself (37). Chemical treatments can also induce the formation of conjugating pairs between 295 

species (e.g. between a P. aurelia species and the outgroup P. multimicronucleatum ), but in such 296 

cases genetic exchange appears to fail and surviving ex-conjugants likely result from self-297 

fertilization (37). Even in the best of cases where inter-specific conjugation can occur naturally, e.g. 298

While the origin of the P. sonneborni hybrid ancestors thus remains mysterious, a second question 301 

is how they could escape the negative consequences of BDMIs. Here the answer may lie in the 302 

peculiar nuclear dimorphism of ciliates. Indeed, the HT segments identified in the genome of P. 303 

sonneborni are essentially confined to its MIC-limited compartment and hence are not expressed, 304 

which effectively hides genetic incompatibilities. In P. aurelia species, the distinction between 305 

segments that are retained or eliminated during MAC development relies on a comparison between 306 

the maternal MIC and the maternal MAC, via small RNAs that are produced from the entire MIC 307 

genome during meiosis and are still present in the cytoplasm of the zygote after conjugation: the 308 

so-called scnRNAs mediate a genomic subtraction that targets the elimination from the new 309 

developing MAC of all maternal MIC sequences that are absent from the maternal MAC (9, 10) 310 

(Figure 3). We conjecture that in P. sonneborni , a similar mechanism also leads to the elimination 311 

of sequences that are present in the paternal MIC but absent from the maternal MAC. This would 312 

explain how, after conjugation of P. sonneborni with a distantly related species, the chromosomes 313 

from the latter may be systematically eliminated from the developing MAC in the F1 hybrid carrying 314 

the P. sonneborni cytoplasm. That the current species indeed derives from that side of the cross 315 

after each inter-specific mating is indicated by the P. sonneborni mitochondrial genome (39), whose 316 

phylogenetic position is in total agreement with the species tree inferred from MAC nuclear 317 

genomes (14) (Figure 3). In other words, the successful matings of P. sonneborni ancestors with 318 

distant relatives may have been facilitated by the capacity of P. aurelia species to exclude non-self 319 

DNA from the zygotic MAC (self DNA being defined by the maternal MAC genome). Interestingly, 320 

a similar situation has been reported in a dipteran insect, which possesses two chromosomes that 321 

have been acquired by introgression from a distant lineage, but that are restricted to the germline, 322 

and hence are not expressed in the soma (40). 323 

The third question raised is how the F1 hybrid MIC may have managed to complete meiosis to 324 

perpetuate itself. Indeed, the most likely cause of sterility in true F1 hybrids is meiotic failure, due 325 

to sequence divergence and/or karyotypic differences between the two parental genomes. We 326 

propose that in the F1 hybrid ancestors of the current P. sonneborni , fertility may have been 327 

restored by whole-genome duplications in the hybrid MIC, resulting in allotetraploid F1s in which 328 

the two sets of duplicated chromosomes can be readily distinguished by the meiotic recombination 329 

machinery - thereby allowing proper segregation during meiosis. These fertile allotetraploids would 330 

retain the original P. sonneborni phenotype essentially unchanged, however, since introgressed 331 

genes cannot be expressed. In the absence of any selective pressure to keep them functional, 332 

introgressed genes would rapidly decay into pseudogenes and, ultimately, into unrecognizable 333 

genome “dark matter”. 334 

Although our model explains how P. aurelia species might escape the deleterious consequences 335 

of inter-specific mating, it leaves open further questions. First, it does not provide any clue regarding 336 

the raison d’être of these recurrent introgressions. Because introgressed genes can hardly be 337 

expressed, these events do not appear to contribute much to the adaptive potential of P. 338 

sonneborni . Second, this model predicts that inter-specific mating can be tolerated only if it involves 339 

a species that is genetically distant enough to be recognized as non-self during MAC development 340 

and meiosis (Figure 3). Hence, selection should still favor the evolution of traits that prevent inter-341 

specific pairing. A third intriguing point is the fact that this promiscuous behavior is observed only 342 

in the P. sonneborni lineage. For P. sonneborni and P. sexaurelia , our experimental design 343 

provided the same power to detect HT events originating from subclade A. Yet, whereas we 344 

identified ~6,000 kb of HT segments coming from subclade A in P. sonneborni, we detected only 345 

76 kb in P. sexaurelia (Fig. 1A,C). Moreover, we detected very few HT segments in the five other 346 

MIC genomes analyzed, and they are all short, suggesting that they result from a different mode of 347 

horizontal transfer (e.g. via viral vectors) or that they result from much more ancient inter-specific 348

We speculate that the large size of the MIC-limited compartment in P. sonneborni (135 Mb 351 

compared to ~30 Mb in other aurelia species) might result from its propensity to hybridize with 352 

distant species. Although the HT segments that we identified in P. sonneborni cover only 7.4% of 353 

its MIC-limited compartment, they probably represent only the visible tip of the iceberg. Indeed, our 354 

study design allowed the detection of HT segments only if they encompassed MAC-destined 355 

regions from the donor genome and if they were closely related (>80% sequence identity) to the 356 

available reference genomes. Given that sequences in the MIC-limited compartment cannot be 357 

expressed, they diverge rapidly, like pseudogenes. It is therefore plausible that a large fraction of 358 

the MIC-limited compartment of P. sonneborni corresponds to ancient HT segments that are no 359 

longer recognizable. Thus, the large size of the genome of P. sonneborni might simply reflect the 360 

recurrent gains of full sets of chromosomes via allotetraploidization, followed by progressive 361 

shrinking through deletions or chromosome loss. 362 

The genome of P. sonneborni provides an interesting perspective on the definition of species. The 363 

model we propose (Figure 3) explains how it is possible for a species to persist, despite recurrent 364 

hybridization with very distant lineages. Indeed, owing to nuclear dimorphism, hybrids remain 365 

phenotypically identical to their maternal lineage, even if they have received a full set of 366 

chromosomes from a very divergent species. Moreover, they also remain interfertile with their 367 

parental lineage. Thus, as long as gene flow is maintained in the MAC-destined compartment 368 

(which represents only ~35% of the P. sonneborni genome), individuals can be considered as being 369 

part of the same species, even if their MIC-limited compartment is not shared. This tentative 370 

extension of the definition of a species echoes Sonneborn’s own hesitation in recognizing that what 371 

he initially called “varieties” of P. aurelia (8) are actually sexually incompatible species (12). 372 

Ironically, the promiscuous sibling is the one that bears the name of the father of Paramecium 373 

genetics. 374

Materials and Methods 376 

Detection of horizontal transfer across aurelia species 377 

We analyzed the MIC genome of each species (P. tetraurelia, P. octaurelia, P. biaurelia, P. 378 

pentaurelia, P. primaurelia, P. sexaurelia and P. sonneborni ) (6) to search for segments that have 379 

been subject to horizontal transfer (HT) across the aurelia complex (see Suppl. Table S2 for a full 380 

list of strains and genome assemblies used in this study). For this, the MIC genome of each focal 381 

species was cut into 500 bp-long windows, that were compared to all genomes (MAC and MIC, if 382 

available) of the 12 other aurelia species with BLASTN (version 2.5.0+; (41)) using the following 383 

parameters: 384            

> blastn -query <infile> -db <db> -out <File_Out> -task blastn -evalue 1e-6 -

385 max_target_seqs 1 386 

For each window, we retained the best blast hit on each of the target genomes. We then selected 387 

windows having their best hit with a species that is not a sister-group of the focal species (see 388 

phylogenetic tree in Figure 1A). We retained windows with a high level of sequence similarity (>80% 389 

identity), significantly higher than with any other species (> mean + 2 standard deviations). These 390 

stringent criteria were chosen to identify windows with strong evidence of recent HT. 391 

Then, starting from these ‘seed windows’, we extended the HT segment by aggregating neighbor 392 

windows (< 5kb apart) having their best hit on the same scaffold of the non-sister species. The 393 

extension was stopped as soon as a seed window matching another scaffold was reached or when 394 

the distance to the next window matching the same scaffold exceeded 5 kb. Finally, we retained 395 

HT segments that contained at least 4 seed windows, and that included at least 70% of windows 396

We first performed the HT search by comparing MIC windows of the focal species (query) against 399 

the MAC genomes of the 12 other aurelia species (targets). We then repeated the analyses using 400 

as targets the MIC instead of the MAC genomes, for the 7 species for which both the MAC and the 401 

MIC were available. In P. sonneborni , this second strategy identified 275 additional candidate HT 402 

segments, all matching with the MIC of P. sexaurelia . However, given that we only have the MAC 403 

but not the MIC genome of P. jenningsi , we could not ascertain that these segments were absent 404 

from the sister-group. In the other species, both strategies gave very similar results (with at most 8 405 

new HT segments identified). To be conservative, the analyses presented in the main text were 406 

restricted to HT segments detected with the first strategy (MAC targets). 407 

The median synonymous divergence (dS) between P. jenningsi and P. sonneborni was computed 408 

on the set of 473 one-to-one orthologs reported in a previous study (14). 409

Analysis of HT genes located in the MAC-destined compartment 411 

Based on annotations of the P. sonneborni MAC genome assembly (24), we identified 39 gene 412 

predictions located in HT segments encompassing MAC-destined regions. For each of them, we 413 

used BLASTP to identify homologs in other species of the P. aurelia complex. We computed 414 

multiple alignments to investigate the conservation of each protein family. In 36 out of 39 cases, 415 

we identified nonsense and/or frameshift mutations in the P. sonneborni predicted gene with 416 

respect to its homologs. We also analyzed the expression level of these predicted genes using 417 

RNAseq data from P. sonneborni vegetative cell cultures (14). 418

570 Figure 3. Model: how the nuclear di morphism in Paramecia might hide interspecific genetic 571 incompatibilities . Sexuality in paramecia occurs through conjugation: cells with compatible mating types pair 572 and engage meiosis. Three of the four resulting haploid nuclei are eliminated, and the rema ining one 573 undergoes a mitotic division, leading to the formation of two identical haploid gametic nuclei. The two paired 574 cells exchange one haploid nucleus (a), and then, in each cell, the haploid nuclei fuse to form the new zygotic 575 nucleus (b). After two mitotic divisions, one nucleus differentiates to form the new MAC, while the other 576 constitutes the new MIC (c) (for the sake of simplicity, only one MIC nucleus is shown in the figure; see (Zhang 577 et al., 2023) for more details), while the maternal MAC nucl eus is progressively degraded (d). During the 578 development of the new MAC, its chromosomes are amplified by endoreplication, and a fraction of the genome 579 (termed MIC -limited) is eliminated. The distinction between segments that are retained or eliminated re lies on 580 a comparison between the maternal MIC and the maternal MAC, via small RNAs produced during meiosis: 581 sequences that are present in the maternal MIC but absent from the maternal MAC are eliminated (c) (Balan 582 et al., 2024). Hence, even though the two F1 progeny have exactly the same germline genotype (f), their MAC 583 content may differ, depending on the content of their respective maternal MACs (g). The model that we 584 propose relies on the assumption that during interspecific crosses, sequences that are p resent in the paternal 585 MIC but absent from the maternal MAC are also eliminated. This would explain how crosses between distant 586 species can lead to a viable and fertile offspring. Indeed, let us consider a conjugation event between two 587 genetically divergen t species (say A and B), and let us focus on the daughter cell from the A side of the cross 588 (a). Its new MIC is allotetraploid, with the full sets of chromosomes from A and B (b). But given that B 589 sequences are divergent from the A sequences present in its maternal MAC, they will all be eliminated during 590 the development of its new MAC (c). Thus, in the side A daughter cell, the new MAC only expresses genes 591 coming from the A genome, thereby effectively hiding any BDMI with the B genome. Hence, although 592 genet ically hybrid (A+B), the side A daughter cell should be viable and retain the phenotype of its maternal 593 lineage (A). Moreover, these cells should be able to complete meiosis normally, as the divergence between 594 the two genomes limits the risk of mispairing between homeologous A/B chromosomes. In F2 individuals 595 resulting from a backcross between an AxB hybrid and an A individual, meiosis is expected to proceed 596 normally for A chromosomes, and hence, even though some of their B chromosomes mis -segregate, these 597 F2’s should lead to a viable and fertile offspring. This model can account for the introgression of DNA coming 598 from B in the MIC -limited compartment of species A. 599
