Abstract 22 

Concerns over climate change have intensified the demand for stress resistant crops like hybrid 23 

wheatgrass (HWG; Elymus hoffmannii , StStStStHH), a perennial forage species known for its 24 

exceptional salt and drought tolerance. However, hexaploidy and high heterozygosity have 25 

complicated efforts to resolve its genomic structure and evolutionary history. Here, we present 26 

high-quality, haplotype-resolved, chromosome-level genome assemblies for HWG (CDC 27 

Saltking) and its putative progenitor, bluebunch wheatgrass ( Pseudoroegneria spicata , StSt). By 28 

integrating PacBio HiFi and ultra-long Oxford Nanopore sequencing with Hi-C scaffolding, we 29 

assembled the 10.7 Gb HWG genome into 21 pseudochromosomes per haplotype. Our 30 

phylogenomic analysis redefines the origin of the H subgenome, positioning it as an intermediate 31 

between Old-World Hordeum marinum (sea barley) and Hordeum brevisubulatum . Notably, we 32 

identified significant chromosomal rearrangements, including a unique duplication on St 33 

chromosome 4. Transcriptome analysis across multiple tissues revealed a pronounced expression 34

Introduction 62 

Escalating climate change have driven an increased demand for resilient crop varieties with high 63 

adaptability to barren soils and abiotic stress conditions such as drought and salinity 1-3 . Hybrid 64 

wheatgrass ( Elymus hoffmannii , StStStStHH, HWG), is a perennial forage grass recognized for 65 

its strong tolerance to salinity and drought, offering a sustainable solution for livestock 66 

production while reducing soil erosion 4-6 . Although various breeding strategies have been 67 

proposed to improve the yield and quality of forage wheatgrasses 7,8 , achieving stable yield under 68 

highly variable, resource constrained environments remains a major challenge. In this context, 69 

generating a high-quality genome sequence of HWG and deciphering its genomic architecture 70 

and evolutionary history have become urgent priorities for making a breakthrough in genetic 71 

improvement. 72

One of the primary challenges in assembling polyploid plant genomes is their inherent genomic 74 

complexity, which largely arises from recurrent polyploidization and interspecific hybridization 75 

events 9-11 . This challenge is particularly pronounced in HWG, an outcrossing hexaploid grass 76 

that exhibits high levels of heterozygosity. To date, four cultivated varieties have been developed 77 

in this grass, including AC Saltlander 12 , NewHy 13 , RS Hybrid 14 , and CDC Saltking 5, all of which 78 

share the same StStStStHH genomic constitution 15 .79 

80 

Polyploid species are generally classified as auto- or allopolyploid based on their origin. 81 

Autopolyploids arise through genome duplication within a single diploid species, while 82 

allopolyploids result from genome doubling following the hybridization between two distinct 83 

diploid progenitors 16 . Due to the absence of high-quality genome sequences for HWG and its 84 

diploid or tetraploid relatives, the evolutionary origin of the two St subgenomes remains 85 

unresolved. In particular, it is unclear whether HWG represents an autopolyploid or an 86 

allopolyploid with respect to its St subgenome composition. Because polyploidization and 87 

hybridization are major evolutionary forces underlying the emergence of agronomically 88 

important traits and adaptation to diverse environments 17,18 , elucidating the history of these 89 

events among the three subgenomes of HWG will offer valuable insights into its genetic 90 

potential for forage crop improvement and facilitate the introgression of desirable traits into 91 

related crops such as wheat. 92 

93 

In this study, we report two high-quality, haplotype-resolved genome assemblies: one for HWG 94 

(Elymus hoffmannii , cultivar CDC Salt king) and one for its putative progenitor, 95 

Pseudoroegneria spicata (PI635993). By comparing these two newly assembled genomes with 96 

other publicly available genomic resources, we aim to: (1) resolve the evolutionary position of 97 

HWG within the Poaceae family; (2) uncover its polyploidization and hybridization history; (3) 98 

identify the closest extant relatives of its diploid progenitors; (4) characterize patterns of 99 

subgenome dominance and their genetic basis; and (5) examine population structure and 100 

introgression events within Elymus and Pseudoroegneria species. 101

genome 12 , whereas, Newhy, RS hybrid and CDC Saltking are synthetic hybrids generated 149 

through crosses involving quackgrass or AC Saltlander and P. spicata 4,5,13,14 . Despite their 150 

distinct backgrounds, all four varieties share key phenotypic improvements over their quackgrass 151 

ancestor, including enhanced tolerance to salinity and reduced rhizome 12-15 . Because the H 152 

genome is directly inherited from E. repens without additional introgression, the observed 153

Mb), specifically involving Chr1 and Chr3 of St 1 and Chr3, Chr4, Chr6 of St 2 (Figure 2b). These 185

Evolutionary Placement of HWG Subgenomes Among Cereal Crops and Its 210 

Polyploidization History 211 

Similar to the H subgenome in barley, the St subgenome in Pseudoroegneria species also 212 

proposed to be evolutionarily ancient 28 . This genomic composition makes HWG particularly 213 

informative for subgenome evolution within Triticeae. To clarify the evolutionary positions of 214 

the HWG and P. spicata subgenomes and their relationship to other sequenced Triticeae 215 

genomes, we conducted a gene family analysis across 16 subgenomes in 11 species from the 216 

Poaceae family identifying 975 single-copy orthologs. Phylogenetic relationships inferred from 217 

sequence alignments of these orthologs using a maximum likelihood method (Figure 3a) showed 218 

that the H subgenome in HWG and E. sibiricus diverged approximately 8.2 Mya and are more 219 

closely related to H. marinum than to H. vulgare within the Hordeum clade. The St subgenomes 220 

diversified around 17.6 Mya —slightly later than Hordeum (19.75 Mya) but earlier than the 221 

Secale cereale (15.18 Mya) and Thinopyrum elongatum (12.66 Mya). The divergence between 222 

St 1 and St 2 genomes was estimated at approximately 14.23 Mya, with P. spicata showing closer 223 

genetic affinity to St 2. These divergence times and genomic relationships between the H and St 224 

lineages were further supported by synonymous substitutions ( Ks) rate analyses of orthologous 225 

coding genes (Figure 3c). 226 

To further identify the donors of H and St subgenomes, we analyzed a combination of publicly 227 

available whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data and our GBS datasets, including 10 lineages 228 

that represent diverse genomic subtypes (Methods). In total, 286.45 Gb of sequencing data were 229

To further explore subgenome-specific evolutionary patterns, we constructed phylogenetic trees 234

P. spicata ). Overall, synteny was largely conserved among the St subgenomes, with only a few 240 

large-scale structural rearrangements in Chr 3, 4, and 6. In contrast, a large region of 241 

translocation between Chr4 and Chr6 was detected in the H subgenome of E. sibiricus but was 242 

absent in HWG, indicating an independent evolutionary history of HWG (Figure 3e). 243 

Given the wide distribution of tetraploid wheatgrass such as E. sibiricus (StStHH) and 244 

Pseudoroegneria species (StStStSt), an important question is whether HWG originated from an 245 

initial tetraploid of Pseudoroegneria species or from an allotetraploid StStHH ancestor. To 246 

address this aspect of HWG polyploidization history, we focus on major gene tree conflicts 247 

involving the H, St 1 and St 2 subgenomes. The predominant topology, ((St 1,St 2), H), accounted 248 

for 57.29% of gene trees, whereas the alternative topologies —((St 2, H), St 1) and ((H,St 1), St 2)—249 

were observed at much lower and nearly equal frequencies (21.72% and 20.99%, respectively 250 

Figure 3b). The prevalence of ((St 1,St 2), H) topology (>50%) supports a stepwise polyploid 251 

origin, where St 1 and St 2 initially hybridized, followed by a second hybridization with the H 252 

genome. Consistent with expectations under the incomplete lineage sorting (ILS) model, the two 253 

alternative topologies occur at similar frequencies (~21%), suggesting a short interval between 254 

divergence of the St lineages and their subsequent hybridization with the H genome. The lack of 255 

frequency bias between these minority topologies further suggests that the observed phylogenetic 256 

discordance is more likely due to the stochastic sorting of ancestral polymorphisms rather than 257 

asymmetric gene flow 29 . To independently assess the order of hybridization events, we examined 258 

LTR insertion times across subgenomes and found that LTRs in the H subgenome are 259 

consistently younger than those in the St subgenomes, providing further support to this 260 

evolutionary scenario (Supplementary Figure 12). 261 

Furthermore, the plastid phylogeny was reconstructed using whole-chloroplast genome 262 

alignment from 10 species. The results showed clear maternal inheritance of St genome during 263 

the second polyploidization event (Supplementary Figure 11). Based on these results, we propose 264 

a refined model for the polyploidization history of HWG (Figure 3d), in which two diverged St 265 

genomes first hybridized to form an allotetraploid (St 1St 1St 2St 2). This tetraploid species 266 

subsequently acted as the maternal donor in a second hybridization with H genomes, giving rise 267 

to hexaploid HWG (St 1St 1St 2St 2HH). 268

Genomic and Evolutionary Drivers of Subgenome Expression Bias 269 

To characterize the expression patterns across subgenomes, we identified 6742 (1:1:1 in 270 

H:St 1:St 2) homoeologous gene triads across the three subgenomes using synteny mapping. RNA-271 

seq analysis was performed across four tissues —flower, stem, leaf, and root —with three 272 

biological replicates per tissue. Principal component analysis (PCA) revealed clear separation of 273 

samples by both tissue type and subgenome, with PC1 (30.4%) and PC2 (29.50%) accounting for 274 

most of the variance, highlighting distinct expression profiles (Supplementary Figure 13a). 275 

Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) further corroborated this tissue-specific clustering 276 

(Supplementary Figure 13b). Based on expression patterns across four tissues, we identified 13 277 

distinct co-expression groups among 6742 homologous genes. These clusters reveal the varied 278 

expression dynamics of the HWG genome across specific tissues (Supplementary Figure 18). 279 

To analyze the relative contribution of each subgenome to the overall gene expression, we 280 

performed comparisons of pairwise gene expression fold changes among subgenomes. The 281 

results revealed that the H subgenome harbors more up-regulated genes than either the St 1 or St 2,282 

whereas St 1 and St 2 exhibited comparable numbers of up-regulated genes (Figure 4d). This 283 

pattern was observed across all four tissues examined (Supplementary Figure 17). In addition, 284 

expression divergence between homoeologs was modest, with mean log 2(H/St 1) and log 2(H/St 2)285 

ratios ranging from 0.04 to 0.08 across tissues (Figure 4e), suggesting a weak but detectable H-286 

biased expression. To further investigate homoeologous gene expression bias (HEB), we 287 

categorized homoeologous triad into balanced, dominant, and suppressed classes based on the 288 

relative expression contributions. Balanced triads predominated in all tissues, accounting from 289 

68.70% to 75.73% of total triads (Supplementary Figure 14). Interestingly, suppressed 290 

homoeologs were more frequent than dominant ones across all subgenomes (H: 7.37% vs. 291 

3.95%; St 1: 6.10% vs. 1.82%; St 2: 6.55% vs. 2.05%; Figure 4a). Notably, the H subgenome 292 

showed elevated proportions of both dominant and suppressed homoeologs relative to the St 1293 

and St 2 subgenomes. In contrast, the two St subgenomes showed more balanced expression 294 

profiles, suggesting greater expression stability between them. 295 

Next, we investigated possible factors that may contribute to the observed HEB. Long terminal 296 

repeats are known regulatory elements that can influence gene expression 30 , therefore, we 297 

examined the impact of LTR insertions on protein-coding genes across the subgenomes. 298 

Although only minor difference in overall LTR coverage were observed, the flanking regions of 299 

genes in the H subgenome showed significantly higher LTR coverage than those in St 1 and St 2300 

subgenomes (Supplementary Figure 15a). LTR density is typically negatively associated with 301 

subgenome dominance 31,32 , however, this expected relationship was not observed here, 302 

suggesting that additional mechanisms contribute to subgenome dominance in HWG. In 303 

addition, we assessed gene retention and nonsynonymous-to-synonymous substitution rate ratios 304 

(Ka /Ks ) among homoelogs across subgenomes. The H subgenome exhibited a reduced Ka /Ks 305 

ratio implying an accelerated evolutionary rate relative to the St subgenomes, likely reflecting 306

Population Structure and Asymmetric Gene Flow in the Wheatgrass Complex 313 

HWG, originally identified in Central Europe 15 , is an outcrossing polyploid species that has 314 

likely undergone extensive reticulate evolution driven by persistent gene flow. Understanding the 315 

evolutionary relationships among its close relatives—particularly within the Elymus and 316 

Pseudoroegneria genera—is therefore essential for clarifying how genetic variation is exchanged 317 

across their global distributions. However, previous studies have often struggled to clearly 318 

delineate the genetic boundaries within and between these groups 33 .319 

To characterize population structure and resolve these genetic boundaries, we performed GBS on 320 

189 globally sourced accessions, including 71 E. repens , 115 Pseudoroegneria , 1 E. dahuricus ,321 

and 2 HWG accessions (Figure 5a). By mapping GBS reads to the HWG reference genome, we 322 

identified 50,118 high-quality SNPs. Principal component analysis (Figure 5c) and phylogenetic 323 

reconstruction (Figure 5d) consistently resolved three major clades corresponding to E. repens ,324 

P. spicata , and European Pseudoroegneria species, in close agreement with their established 325 

evolutionary relationships. 326 

Admixture analysis supported K=5 as the optimal number of genetic clusters (lowest cross-327 

validation error: 0.35187). These clusters clearly distinguished P. spicata , P. geniculata , and P. 328 

tauri , while subdividing E. repens into two distinct subclusters (Figure 5b). Both HWG 329 

accessions grouped within the E. repens group. Notably, one intermediate cluster spanning P. 330 

geniculata and P. tauri consisted of multiple European Pseudoroegneria species (including P. 331 

stipifolia , P. strigosa , P. tauri , and P. cognata ) together with E. dahuricus , indicating a shared, 332 

highly admixed or hybrid genetic background among these lineages. 333 

To quantify the extent and direction of introgression underlying these patterns, we conducted an 334 

f-branch ( fb) test across the phylogeny. This analysis revealed strong evidence of gene flow, most 335 

prominently between E. repens and P. strigosa (fb = 0.464), with weaker but detectable signals 336 

between the European clade and the E. repens , HWG, and P. spicata group (G1) (Figure 5e). A 337 

striking feature of the f-branch statistic results was the asymmetric role of P. spicata . Although it 338 

showed little evidence of receiving introgression, P. spicata emerged as a major genetic donor to 339 

several lineages including HWG ( fb = 0.121), P. cognata (fb = 0.165), P. strigosa (fb = 0.036), 340 

and P. libanotica (fb = 0.096) (Figure 5e). This pronounced unidirectional gene flow suggests 341 

that P. spicata may have served as a key ancestral genetic source during the diversification of 342 

these species. Collectively, these results suggest that introgression from P. spicata has played a 343

Discussion 346 

Polyploid grass genomes are exceptionally large and complex, requiring extensive effort to 347 

resolve their genomic structure and evolutionary trajectories 34 . Although chromosome-scale 348 

assemblies have become increasingly available across the grass family, particularly for 349 

allopolyploids such as oat and wheat 10,35,36 , near-complete assembly of partially autopolyploid 350 

species remains rare due to the high sequence similarity among homologous chromosomes. In 351 

this study, we generated chromosome-level, haplotype-resolved genome assemblies for CDC 352 

Saltking and its diploid ancestors P. spicata . These assemblies provide a robust foundation for 353 

dissecting subgenome-specific architecture, interspecific variation and evolutionary history 354 

within Triticeae. 355 

For decades, the St subgenomes of hexaploid HWG were interpreted as autopolyploid 356 

derivatives based on their high sequence similarity and the assumption of genome duplication 357 

within a single lineage 15 . Our assembly challenges this simplified view and unveils a more 358 

dynamic evolutionary history. While three St 1 chromosomes are nearly indistinguishable from a 359 

canonical St genome, the remaining four chromosomes coalesce into a distinct cluster, indicating 360 

unrecognized subgenome differentiation. Most strikingly, Chr4 of the St 2 subgenome exhibits a 361 

unique duplication divergent from St 1 counterparts in both collinearity and phylogeny (Figure 362 

1d). Comparative genomic analyses demonstrate that this chromosome represents a complex 363 

rearrangement incorporating segments from both P. spicata and E. repens (Figure 1e), implying 364 

a large-scale chromosomal recombination or fusion event. Such chromosomal restructuring 365 

events are major drivers of karyotype evolution and speciation 37,38 .366 

Our phylogenomic analyses further refine the origin of the H subgenome. Rather than clustering 367 

with barley, the H subgenome of HWG is positioned phylogenetically as an intermediate 368 

between H. marinum and H. brevisubulatum , two species associated with Old-World Hordeum 369 

clades 39,40 . This finding both corroborates and extends prior observations from E. repens , where 370 

the H genome was shown to affiliate with H. brevisubulatum and H. bogdanii , highlighting 371 

repeated contributions from ecologically distinct Old-World Hordeum lineages 41,42 . Tracing the 372 

H genome’s ancestry offers a strategic blueprint for accelerating the development of salt-tolerant 373 

crops. Given the close phylogenetic relationship of H genome of HWG with H. marinum and H. 374 

brevisubulatum , the pronounced salt tolerance observed in HWG likely reflects the retention or 375 

parallel evolution of stress-adaptive traits characteristic of H. marinum 39 and H. 376 

brevisubulatum 40 , two species well known for their ability to thrive under high salinity. This 377 

evolutionary proximity strengthens the biological significance of HWG’s salt tolerance, 378 

highlighting its role of a valuable reservoir of salinity-adaptive alleles within the Triticeae, with 379 

potential relevance for improving salt tolerance in related cereal and forage crops. 380

Moreover, the functional importance of the H subgenome is further evidenced by its clear 381 

expression dominance. Interestingly, this dominance is not accompanied by reduced LTR 382 

density. Instead, this suggests that the H subgenome, as the most recent addition to the HWG 383 

complex, may be subject to strong positive selection to rapidly integrate into the host genomic 384 

background. Such selective pressure could favor the retention and upregulation of functionally 385 

important H-derived genes, enhancing overall fitness and stabilizing the hybrid genome. 386 

Interspecific introgression is a recognized driver of adaptive evolution in forage grasses, 387 

including perennial ryegrass ( Lolium perenne L. )43 , turtle grass ( Thalassia testudinum )44,45 , and 388 

turfgrass ( Poa trivialis L. )46 . Recent phylogenetic studies have proposed that P. spicata is 389 

originated from a small founder population in North America and may have evolved as a more 390 

ancient lineage rather than P. libanotica 33 . Here, our fb statistic analysis supports this 391 

evolutionary framework, identifying P. spicata as a primary genetic donor to multiple lineages, 392 

including HWG, P. cognata , P. strigosa , and P. libanotica . These findings reinforce the 393 

hypothesis that P. spicata acted as an ancestral source of gene flow that facilitated the speciation 394 

of European lineages. Furthermore, the robust signal of introgression between E. repens and P. 395 

strigosa —when compared to the gene flow between HWG and P. spicata —suggests that 396 

differential introgression events are the primary drivers of the genomic divergence observed 397 

between HWG and E. repens .398 

In conclusion, the discovery of differentiated St subgenomes shaped by chromosomal 399 

rearrangement, together with evidence for an Old-World Hordeum origin of the H subgenome, 400 

fundamentally revises prevailing models of hexaploid HWG evolution. These findings 401 

underscore the importance of high-resolution, haplotype-resolved assemblies for disentangling 402 

complex polyploid histories. Future research integrating high-resolution cytogenetics, 403 

transcriptomics, and functional genomics will be instrumental for fully elucidating the 404 

evolutionary and agronomic significance of these genomic innovations. 405
