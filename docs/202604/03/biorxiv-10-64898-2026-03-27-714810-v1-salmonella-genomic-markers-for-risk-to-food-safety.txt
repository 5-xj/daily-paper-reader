Abstract 

Foodborne non-typhoidal Salmonella remains a major public health concern, yet routine surveillance recovers large numbers of isolates from food that are not associated with human illness. Studies have shown foodborne isolates can be genetically linked to clinical cases, highlighting a critical challenge for risk assessment and outbreak prioritisation. This study aimed to determine whether genomic markers can distinguish foodborne Salmonella 

strains with an increased likelihood of causing infection. Whole-genome sequencing data from over 900 Salmonella isolates recovered from food and the environment through UK Health Security Agency surveillance were analysed using hierarchical clustering to define genetically related groups. These clusters were expanded using the global EnteroBase database to provide broader epidemiological context. Genome-wide association analyses identified genetic markers associated with clusters containing clinical isolates, including phage-associated regions. A highly conserved 7 kb marker identified in S. Agona demonstrated strong predictive performance at a global scale, with high sensitivity and specificity for infection-associated lineages and strict serovar restriction. Comparative genomic analysis revealed that all markers localised to a shared chromosomal hotspot corresponding to a prophage integration site. The 7 kb risk-associated marker formed part of a larger prophage closely related to the well-characterised S. Typhimurium Fels-2 phage, which encodes a DNA invertase linked to phase variation, a mechanism known to promote phenotypic heterogeneity and host adaptation. As these S. Agona isolates are monophasic, our findings indicate that our genome-wide association approach has

Author summary 

Foodborne Salmonella infections remain a major public health concern, but not all strains pose the same risk to human health. Here we investigated whether genetic differences could explain why some foodborne strains are more likely to cause human infection. We analysed over 900 genomes from food and environmental sources, grouping closely related strains before placing them in a global context using EnteroBase. By combining pangenome and genome-wide association analyses, we identified distinct lineages within several serovars that differed in their association with human cases. In Salmonella Agona, all clinical isolates belonged to a single lineage carrying a highly conserved 7 kb marker that was absent from low-risk strains. This marker demonstrated strong sensitivity and specificity across global datasets and was located within a prophage closely related to the well-characterised Fels-2 phage. This region encodes a DNA invertase previously linked to phase variation, a mechanism that promotes bacterial adaptability.

Introduction 

Infections caused by foodborne non-typhoidal Salmonella (NTS), which typically result in self-limiting gastroenteritis, represent a significant public health and economic burden. Although most cases resolve without treatment, complications can require antimicrobial treatment, especially in vulnerable populations [1]. Globally, NTS is estimated to cause 93.8 million cases annually [2], with over 31,000 cases occurring in the United Kingdom (UK) alone and estimated to cost the economy £200 million annually [3]. Therefore, collaborative and multi-disciplinary efforts as well as surveillance and outbreak detection are crucial for infection control. 

Effective control of Salmonella infections requires vigilant surveillance and timely detection and response to outbreaks, which for human salmonellosis surveillance in England is the responsibility of the UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA). In 2015 UKHSA implemented Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS) as a method to rapidly identify and support outbreak investigations of this notifiable pathogen [4]. Detection of an outbreak is characterised by epidemiological data in combination with Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP) analysis and sample testing to pinpoint the potential sources of infection [4]. The adoption of this approach has significantly improved result accuracy and speed of outbreak investigations [4–6]. Nevertheless, delays can still occur between the onset of illness and the recognition of an outbreak, particularly when cases are sporadic, involve heterogenous bacterial populations or when food testing data is limited. Beyond its established role in near-real time surveillance, WGS generates vast genomic datasets that are widely used for retrospective and descriptive analyses, but are less

These data offer the opportunity to better understand which serovars, or even subtypes, of Salmonella isolated from food are more likely to cause human disease, and which are not. While the presence of Salmonella in food is a public health concern, it is not the case that all foodborne Salmonella isolates carry the same risk of causing illness. The likelihood of infection depends on multiple factors, including exposure frequency, contamination levels, and the intrinsic characteristics of the strain, such as virulence potential. Nevertheless, emerging evidence suggests that only a subset of serovars are responsible for the majority clinical cases [8–10]. If higher-risk foodborne Salmonella strains 

can be reliably identified , health agencies could prioritise interventions more efficiently and target their limited resources more effectively. Our recent work explored this by examining the link between Salmonella isolates from food and those associated with human infection [7]. By applying a data-driven approach to nearly 800 Salmonella isolates recovered from food and tested by UKHSA as part of routine surveillance and official control activities over a five-year period, we demonstrated that only a small proportion (~10%) were genetically linked to clinical cases [7]. This insight highlighted the potential to stratify foodborne Salmonella by risk and provides a basis for prioritising food testing and outbreak investigations [7]. While this work demonstrated that the majority of foodborne Salmonella isolates do not contribute to human infection, it did not address whether specific genomic features could explain why certain strains pose a greater risk than others. Pangenome composition of specific serovars 

Pangenomes were individually constructed for S. Agona, S. Braenderup, and S. Infantis to explore genetic diversity and identify lineage-specific genes potentially associated with risk (Fig 2, S4-S6 Tables). Among the 258 S. Agona isolates, the pangenome consisted of 6,845 genes, with 4,027 (58.8%) core genes (present in ≥99% of isolates) and 226 (3.3%) soft-core genes (present in ≥95-99%). The remaining 2,592 (37.9%) genes formed the accessory genome (present in <95%), representing a substantial proportion of variable gene content.

Validation of candidate genomic markers in expanded datasets 

We validated both risk and low-risk candidate genomic markers in S. Agona and 

S. Braenderup by using publicly available sequence data from EnteroBase [16,19]. Marker nucleotide sequences were used to generate locus-specific allele schemes, enabling construction of marker-specific subtyping schemes (S7 Table). These subtyping schemes were first evaluated within our serovar expanded datasets, respectively comprising 258 

S. Agona and 148 S. Braenderup isolates. Per marker, allele profiles showed strong concordance with HC5-defined risk classifications, and profile complexity generally varied with region length (S8 Table). For example, the two S. Agona risk markers of 26 and 7 kb generated allele profiles containing 43 and 8 alleles, respectively. The 7 kb marker showed a single conserved allele profile which was present across all 237/239 risk-associated isolates and absent from all 19 low-risk isolates, yielding a sensitivity of 99.2% and specificity of 100% (Table 2). The 26 kb marker displayed greater allelic diversity, generating 43 alleles and 56 distinct allele profiles, with partial allele presence in some isolates (≥34 alleles, S8 Table). Despite this allelic complexity, marker presence remained confined to risk-associated HC5 clusters (S8 Table).

Selection of a candidate prophage marker for functional validation 

Following comparative genomic analysis of the marker regions, we sought to determine whether the identified prophage markers contributed directly to bacterial functions that could be attributed to clinical risk. For functional validation, we prioritised the 7 kb risk marker for targeted knockouts due to its high sensitivity and specificity across risk isolates and its complete absence from low-risk isolates, indicating a strong association with clinical risk. This marker was further supported by the availability of 14 risk isolates with high-quality hybrid assemblies, providing complete genomic context to support targeted genetic manipulation [20]. From this set, two representative isolates (112 and 126; SAL_CA4548AA_AS and SAL_CB3444AA_AS, respectively) were selected for based on their suitability for Golden Gate-assisted gene doctoring, demonstrated virulence phenotypes and prior detailed comparative genomics [20].

Discussion 

Our previous work has shown that the majority of Salmonella isolates recovered from food do not contribute to human infection, highlighting the challenge of identifying which strains pose the greatest public health risk [7]. Although more than 2,500 Salmonella serovars have been described globally [24], the food and environmental population circulating in England is far less diverse. In this study, only 87 serovars were identified among 933 isolates collected over a five-year period, with epidemiological burden further concentrated within a small subset, as 15 serovars accounted for more than 80% of all isolates. Heidelberg represented more than one-third of isolates in this study, largely reflecting targeted surveillance with UKHSA’s nationwide monitoring of Salmonella in imported food, including poultry products samples at the point of import from Brazil [25], rather than true prevalence in food. While all foodborne Salmonella isolates undergo routine WGS, many originate from reactive sampling prompted by outbreaks, complaints, or follow-up investigations after previous detection. Consequently, most food surveillance datasets inevitably contain elements of sampling bias. These discrepancies underscore the limitations of serovar-level classification for assessing public health risk and highlight the need for higher-resolution genomic approaches capable of distinguishing lineages with differing potential to cause human infection. Pangenome analysis and genome-wide association analysis 

Three Salmonella serovars - Agona, Braenderup and Infantis - exhibited distinct HC5 clusters with and without clinical isolates. Genome assemblies of all isolates within S. Agona, 

S. Braenderup, and S. Infantis were downloaded from EnteroBase. The open platform Galaxy (v23.0.6) was used to perform subsequent bioinformatic analyses. Genome assemblies were annotated using Bakta with Salmonella flagged as the genus name (v1.9.3) [40], specifying the Salmonella database and used as input to construct serovar-specific pangenomes with Roary (v.3.13.0) [41]. Roary default parameters were used including 95% for minimum blastp identity and core genes defined as presence in ≥99% of isolates. Pangenome accessory genes names and their associated annotations were manually screened using keyword-based searches to assign genes to functional categories. Prophage-associated genes were identified using words including “phage”, “capsid”, “tail”, “baseplate”, “spike”, “holin”, “portal”, “head” and “tape”. Plasmid-associated were identified using terms including “plasmid”, incompatibility group “Inc”, “type”, “conjugation”, “replication”, “efflux”, “resistance” and known antibiotic names. Mobile genetic elements were identified using keywords including “transposase”, “integrase”, “recombinase”, “insertion sequence”, “IS elements” and Salmonella Pathogenicity Island “SPI”.

Confirmation of fitness, virulence and motility of knockouts

infection model [69,70]. Larvae were chilled at 8 °C for 30 mins prior to injecting and inoculated in the second hindmost proleg with a 25 μL Hamilton syringe.
