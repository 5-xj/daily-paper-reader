Abstract 9

Cyanobacteria have garnered interest as promising biological platforms for producing renewable 10 

biofuel, chemical feedstock, and bioactive molecules. For biotechnology applications, robust well-11 

characterized genetic tools are required for genetically modifying cyanobacteria, but these tools are 12 

often developed for specific model strains. Here, we used broad host-range RSF1010-based plasmids to 13 

characterize a set of orthogonal constitutive promoters in diverse cyanobacterial strains. The promoters 14 

are random variants of the synthetic Escherichia coli PconII promoter. A library of PconII promoters 15 

driving a fluorescent reporter gene was first evaluated in Synechococcus elongatus and found to have a 16 

wide range of gene expression levels. A set of 25 promoter variants with graded strengths was selected 17 

after characterization in S. elongatus and three additional model cyanobacterial strains. To demonstrate 18 

the utility of these promoters, we isolated new genetically tractable cyanobacterial strains with high salt 19 

and alkalinity tolerance and transferred the subset of promoters into one of these newly isolated 20 

strains. Similar to the results with model strains, the subset of promoters had a wide range of expression 21 

levels in the non-model strain. These characterized promoters expand the genetic tools available for 22 

genetic engineering of model and non-model cyanobacterial strains. 23 

24 

Importance 25 

The use of cyanobacteria to produce renewable products will require engineered expression of 26 

many genes that affect cell growth, metabolism, and agronomic properties, leading to efficient 27 

production of biomass and desired products. Engineering the strength of gene transcription is an 28 

important element of overall gene expression levels. The set of constitutive promoters described here, 29 

with a wide range of expression strengths characterized in several diverse cyanobacterial strains, 30 

provides an important resource for genetic engineering required for biotechnology applications. 31

Introduction 41 

Genetic tools are fundamental to basic research and biotechnology for manipulating gene 42 

expression and metabolic pathways. Most available tools for cyanobacteria have been developed to 43 

study fundamental cell biology processes in particular species, especially in model strains that grow well 44 

in the laboratory and are amenable to genetic manipulations (1 –4). While such tools have been used 45 

and sometimes further developed for engineering cyanobacteria to produce biofuels and other 46 

biomolecules (5, 6), yields remain low, particularly, in comparison to those obtained with heterotrophs 47 

like Escherichia coli (hereafter E. coli ) (7). To advance sustainable production of biochemicals and other 48 

biotechnological applications using cyanobacteria, additional genetic tools and strains are needed .49 

Considering the diversity of the cyanobacterial phylum, some genetic tools may not be compatible 50 

across different strains, especially non-model strains (1). Types of improvements needed to optimize 51 

cyanobacterial growth and production of desired products include minimizing competing native 52 

metabolic pathways, improving metabolic flux into targeted pathways, and increasing cell tolerance to 53 

high concentrations of biochemical end products (8). The success of these modifications in increasing 54 

production of desired compounds and limiting production of harmful byproducts will require robust 55 

methods for controlling gene expression levels. 56 

Heterologous promoters, notably those from E. coli, have been adapted for use in cyanobacteria. 57 

For example, the inducible E. coli Ptrc promoter has been used in Synechocystis PCC 6803 (hereafter 58 

Synechocystis ), Synechococcus elongatus PCC 7942 (hereafter S. elongatus ), and Anabaena PCC 7120 59 

(hereafter Anabaena ) (9 –11). However, Ptrc transcriptional control is imperfect in cyanobacteria, with a60 

level of leaky basal expression occurring in the absence of inducer (12). Other E. coli promoters tested in 61 

cyanobacteria include Plac and Ptet; however, both exhibit low activity in Synechocystis (10). Other 62 

engineering strategies, such as cyanobacterial-specific promoter libraries, have been used to yield a 63 

wider range of promoter expression levels. Markley et al. mutagenized a truncated sequence of the 64 

strong cpcB promoter from Synechococcus PCC 7002, generating a set of 11 promoters spanning three 65 

orders of magnitude of YFP expression levels (13). They also tested a set of E. coli promoters from the 66 

BioBrick promoter family, which yielded an expression range of 2.5 orders of magnitude. Notably, there 67 

was only a weak correlation between E. coli and Synechococcus PCC 7002 expression levels measured by 68 

reporter fluorescence. This discrepancy could be attributed in part to differences in the promoter 69 

consensus sequences between the two organisms (13). These results illustrate that E. coli promoter 70 

libraries may not function as expected and must be empirically tested in the cyanobacterial strains in 71 

which they will be used. Furthermore, even within the cyanobacterial phylum, promoter sequences are 72

In E. coli, the sigma70 housekeeping sigma factor binds to -10 and -35 consensus regions upstream 75 

of the transcription start site to recruit RNA polymerase and initiate transcription (16). In E. coli , the -10 76 

region is conserved across sigma70 promoters, with a consensus sequence ‘TATAAT’ between 77 

positions -12 and -7 relative to the transcriptional start site (16). The positions -15 and -14 of sigma70 78 

promoters are partially conserved, comprising the extended -10 region. These conserved regions 79 

influence promoter strength, which can be modulated by altering the nucleotide bases in those regions 80 

(17). 81 

Among E. coli promoters, the synthetic conII promoter (PconII) has been used for constitutive gene 82 

expression in different cyanobacterial genera, including strains of S. elongatus , Synechocystis , and 83 

Anabaena (6, 18, 19). PconII is a synthetic unregulated core promoter combining -35 and -10 elements 84 

of the native E. coli trp and lac promoters, respectively (20). Here, we developed a set of variant 85 

promoters based on PconII that provide a wide range of expression strengths in 5 diverse cyanobacterial 86 

strains including a non-model cyanobacterium isolated from an alkaline soda lake. This set of promoters 87 

addresses a need for different levels of transcriptional gene expression for metabolic engineering and 88 

synthetic biology applications in diverse cyanobacterial strains. 89

Molecular methods 104 

Plasmid preparations were performed using the QIAprep Spin Miniprep Kit (Qiagen). PCR 105 

amplifications were carried out with Q5 High-Fidelity DNA polymerase (New England BioLabs, NEB). 106 

Restriction digests followed the supplier’s recommendations (NEB) but with longer incubation times to 107 

ensure complete digests. After PCRs or restriction enzyme digestions, DNA purification and 108 

concentration were performed using DNA Clean and Concentrator-5 (Zymo Research) and subsequent 109 

DNA concentrations were measured using a UV-Vis spectrophotometer NanoDrop 2000c 110 

(ThermoFisher). Oligonucleotide phosphorylation was performed using T4 Polynucleotide Kinase (NEB) 111 

according to the manufacturer’s instructions, with the modification that PNK reaction buffer and ATP 112 

were replaced by T4 DNA ligase reaction buffer. Complementary oligonucleotides were annealed by 113 

mixing equimolar amounts (150 to 200 nmol) of each oligonucleotide in a 50 μl reaction containing T4 114 

DNA ligase reaction buffer. The mixture was incubated at 95°C for 5 minutes, then gradually cooled 115 

down to 25°C, decreasing the temperature by 1°C every 30 seconds. Assembly of shuttle plasmids were 116 

carried out using T4 DNA ligase (NEB) or a GeneArt Seamless Cloning and Assembly Kit (Life 117 

Technologies). 118 

Construction of the PconII* promoter library in S. elongatus 119 

To produce a library of variant PconII promoters, designed as PconII*, with a broad range of 120 

promoter strengths, 2 strategies were used. Pairs of oligonucleotides (see Table S1 and Fig. 1A) 121 

corresponding to the PconII promoter sequence were synthesized with randomized bases at specific 122 

positions in the -10 and extended -10 regions. These oligonucleotides were phosphorylated and 123 

annealed to form double-stranded inserts. Two sets of oligonucleotide pairs were designed: set a for 124 

strategy 1 and set b for strategy 2. For set a (conII-n5-F_a / conII-n5-R_a, Table S2), the two nucleotides 125 

in the extended -10 region and the three most-conserved nucleotides in the -10 region of PconII were 126 

randomized. For set b (conII-n5-F_b / conII-n5-R_b, Table S2), the two nucleotides in the extended -10 127 

region and the three least-conserved nucleotides in the -10 region of PconII were randomized. In 128 

addition, a pair of oligonucleotides corresponding to the original sequence of the PconII promoter 129 

(conII-REF-F / conII-REF-R, Table S2) were used to make a positive control (pAM6017). 130 

To generate a library of PconII* promoters driving YFP in S. elongatus , we first assembled plasmid 131 

pAM5203 to integrate the PconII* library into the chromosome. Plasmid pAM5203 contains 3 devices 132 

including: 1, homology sequences for recombination into the S. elongatus chromosome at NS1 and an 133 

origin of replication for E. coli , 2, a SPT and STR resistance gene, and 3, a functional module composed of 134 

the ccdB toxic gene followed by a ribozyme insulator sequence (RiboJ), a ribosomal binding site (RBS), 135

Transformation of S. elongatus followed standard protocols (21) with slight modifications described 149 

previously (22). Isolated colonies were grown as small patches on BG-11 plates, then transferred into 2 150 

ml of liquid culture medium and grown in 96-deep-well plates. To determine YFP expression levels, the 151 

cultures were adjusted to an optical density at 750 nm (OD750) of 0.1 and YFP fluorescence was then 152 

normalized to autofluorescence from chlorophyll a (Chl a) (Fig. S1). For the PconII* strategy 1 library, we 153 

analyzed 626 S. elongatus clones after initial cloning experiments and another 311 colonies after an 154 

additional cloning experiment. We found only a small number of clones that expressed YFP at levels 155 

higher than the PconII control and a large number of clones that expressed YFP at low levels. We 156 

selected 68 clones with different expressions strengths from this strategy, but they had only a limited 157 

distribution of strong and moderate promoter strengths. These results showed that changing the more 158 

conserved nucleotides in the -10 region produced mostly weaker promoters. We therefore designed 159 

strategy 2 with the goal of obtaining more strong and medium strength promoter variants. For the 160 

PconII* strategy 2 library, 677 clones were grown and analyzed, and we obtained a more even 161 

distribution of YFP expression levels resulting in the selection of an additional 275 colonies. A total of 162 

341 colonies were selected from both library construction strategies, and these colonies had a wide 163 

distribution of YFP expression levels. 164

Construction and characterization of PconII* promoter libraries in diverse strains of cyanobacteria 165 

using a broad host range plasmid 166 

Our goal was to characterize the variant PconII* promoters in additional cyanobacterial strains to 167 

obtain a small set of promoters that produce a wide range of expression levels in diverse cyanobacteria. 168 

The final selected set of PconII* promoters and YFP coding sequences from the integrative S. elongatus 169 

neutral site 1 plasmids were cloned into a broad-host-range RSF1010-based plasmid (pAM5068), and the 170 

resulting plasmids (pAM5068-PconII*-yfp ) were transferred sequentially into several strains of 171 

cyanobacteria as described below. 172 

First, the 341 clones characterized in S. elongatus were pooled into 14 groups based on similar YFP 173 

fluorescence levels covering ± 5,000 or ± 10,000 AU (Fig. S2). DNA was extracted from each pool, and 174 

both the PconII* promoter and the YFP coding sequence were PCR-amplified with primers PLIB-175 

01F/PLIB-01R (Table S2). These PCR products were cloned into the pAM5068 backbone via seamless 176 

assembly, following removal of the toxic ccdB gene by restriction digestion with SwaI. 177 

For each of the 14 cloning reactions, over 100 E. coli colonies were pooled, resulting in 14 small 178 

libraries of E. coli carrying pAM5068-PconII*-YFP. Each of these plasmid pools were miniprepped and 179 

electroporated into an E. coli conjugal strain, AM6013. To counter-select against growth of the E. coli 180 

donor strains following conjugal transfers of the cargo plasmids into cyanobacteria, we used a 181 

diaminopimelic acid (DAP) auxotrophic strain of E. coli , AM6013, that was generated by introducing the 182 

helper plasmid pRL623 carrying MobColK, M.AvaI, M.Eco47II, and M.EcoT22I, and conferring CHL R (23) 183 

into WM3064. WM3064, originates from William Metcalf’s laboratory (University of Illinois) and derives 184 

from B2155 that carries the RP4 conjugal transfer machinery integrated into its chromosome and is a 185 

DAP auxotroph (24, 25). 186 

The pooled libraries were used to transfer the pAM5068-PconII*-YFP plasmids into Synechocystis 187 

PCC 6803 (hereafter Synechocystis ) via conjugation. Conjugation methods were performed as previously 188 

described (22, 23) with the modification that both E. coli donor cultures and conjugation plates (BG-11 189 

with 5% LB) were supplemented with 60 μg/mL DAP (~315 μM). 190 

A total of 786 Synechocystis exconjugant colonies were individually grown, adjusted to an OD 750 of 191

while also keeping representative clones from each original group in S. elongatus to achieve the goal of 194 

obtaining a final set of promoters with a wide distribution of expression strengths in diverse 195 

cyanobacterial strains. In general, we kept most clones expressing high levels of YFP, greater than PconII 196

Construction of Phylogenetic Tree 280 

The phylogenetic tree was constructed based on 16S rRNA gene sequences for identification of 281 

strain ML3B (Fig. S4). The evolutionary history was inferred by using the Maximum Likelihood method 282 

and General Time Reversible model. The tree with the highest log likelihood (-14053.35) is shown. The 283 

percentage of trees in which the associated taxa clustered together is shown next to the branches. Initial 284 

tree(s) for the heuristic search were obtained automatically by applying Neighbor-Join and BioNJ 285 

algorithms to a matrix of pairwise distances estimated using the Maximum Composite Likelihood (MCL) 286 

approach and then selecting the topology with superior log likelihood value. A discrete Gamma 287 

distribution was used to model evolutionary rate differences among sites (5 categories (+G, parameter = 288 

0.6326)). The rate variation model allowed for some sites to be evolutionarily invariable ([+I], 56.68% 289 

sites). The tree is drawn to scale, with branch lengths measured in the number of substitutions per site. 290 

The analysis involved 70 nucleotide sequences with a total of 1362 positions in the final dataset. 291

Generation of the promoter library 311 

We generated different promoter sequences by randomizing nucleotides in the -10 and extended -312 

10 regions of PconII. The variant promoters (designated PconII*) were produced using oligonucleotide 313 

pairs with randomized bases at specific positions that were annealed with each other and cloned 314 

upstream of a reporter gene. To obtain a wide range of promoter strengths, we used two strategies that 315 

targeted the 2 nucleotides in the extended -10 region and either the 3 most-conserved or the 3 least-316 

conserved bases in the -10 region of PconII (Fig. 1A). These positions are also conserved across 317 

promoters in cyanobacterial strains including Synechocystis , Anabaena , and S. elongatus (31 –33). 318 

The library of PconII* promoters was first screened in S. elongatus . We used the yfp -reporter vector 319 

pAM5203 to integrate PconII*-yfp reporter constructs into neutral site 1 in the chromosome (Fig. 1B). 320 

Chromosome neutral sites in S. elongatus allow integration of DNA sequences without affecting normal 321 

cell phenotypes (21). Individual clones were randomly selected for measurement of YFP expression, 322 

which was normalized to autofluorescence from Chl a. Modification of the most conserved nucleotides in 323 

the -10 region ( “Strategy 1 ” primers) produced promoters skewed towards high and low YFP expression 324 

with poor representation of intermediate expression levels (Fig. S1). Modification of the least conserved 325 

nucleotides in the -10 region ( “Strategy 2 ” primers) yielded a more even distribution of expression levels 326 

of the PconII* variants (Fig. S1). Clones of PconII* promoters made using both strategies were grouped 327 

together as a single library for subsequent experiments. A total of 1643 clones were initially analyzed for 328 

YFP expression in S. elongatus , and 341 PconII* promoter clones with a wide range of expression levels 329 

were selected for further characterization as described in Materials and Methods (Fig. 1D) .330

Characterization of the promoter set in a newly isolated strain 418 

To demonstrate the utility of the set of PconII* promoters, we characterized them in a newly 419 

isolated genetically tractable cyanobacterial strain. Plasmids based on the broad-host-range RSF1010 420 

plasmid can serve both as a tool for conducting genetic modifications in model cyanobacteria and as a 421 

practical method for isolating new genetically tractable strains through bioprospecting, which is the 422 

discovery of new organisms in natural habitats for biotechnological or other applications (30). In this 423 

study, we screened for environmental strains that were halotolerant and capable of growth in high 424 

levels of bicarbonate. Halotolerant strains are useful due to the appeal of using waste or brackish water 425 

for sustainable large-scale cultivation of cyanobacteria for biotechnology applications, and high-426 

bicarbonate media can increase growth rate and carbon fixation (42 –44). 427

strains with distinct 16S rRNA gene sequences (Table S3). The four exconjugant strains were grown on 434

Sequence comparison of promoter variants 487 

We examined the sequences of each of the final 25 PconII* variants in the 4 model strains as well as 488 

the newly isolated strain Cf. Nodosilinea ML3B (Fig. 5). To determine if particular nucleotide changes 489 

correspond to patterns of the higher or lower relative expression across the tested strains, we analyzed 490 

the sequences of each of the PconII* variants. PconII* promoters with a stronger expression than PconII 491 

often only had changes in the extended -10 region at positions -15 and -14. The overall strongest 492 

promoter was PconII*_se_14, which had changed only the original “TC” at positions -15 and -14 to “CT”. 493 

Changes in the least conserved bases of the -10 region, the “TAA” at positions -10 to -8, almost always 494 

yielded promoters weaker than PconII, with a few notable exceptions. For instance, the strongest 495 

promoter in S. elongatus, PconII*_se_1 has the same “CT” modification at -15 and -14 as PconII*_se_14 496 

but has a “TAA” to “TAC” change at -10 to -8. This change lowered the expression level in all other 497 

strains but increased it in S. elongatus. As noted previously, S. elongatus was somewhat of an outlier in 498 

promoter expression strengths compared to other strains, and all of the stronger promoters in S. 499 

elongatus had changes to the “TAA” at -10 to -8. Only a few promoters had changes at the most 500

Conclusions 514 

In this study, we generated a library of PconII* constitutive promoters that were initially 515 

characterized in S. elongatus and then evaluated in 3 additional strains of cyanobacteria to create a set 516 

of promoters with a wide range of expression levels. This final set of 25 variant PconII* promoters were 517 

characterized in each of the 4 diverse model strains and a cyanobacterial strain newly isolated from a 518 

saline soda lake. The set of PconII* promoters produced YFP reporter expression levels that ranged from 519 

very low, near background levels of a negative control, to levels surpassing the strong expression levels 520 

of the original PconII promoter (Fig. 2A-D and Fig. 5). Many native and heterologous promoters have 521 

been used in cyanobacteria, but they are often characterized in a single strain (1, 3, 4, 12). The 522 

promoters produced in this work were characterized in diverse cyanobacterial strains and shown to 523 

produce a broad, graded range of expression levels. Therefore, this set of promoters would be useful for 524 

cyanobacterial fundamental research, metabolic engineering, or synthetic biology applications. 525
