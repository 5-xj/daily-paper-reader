Abstract: 12 

Short tandem repeats (STRs) are among the most mutable regions of the human genome, yet 13 their somatic mosaicism remains poorly characterized due to the technical challenges of 14 distinguishing genuine mutations from high intrinsic polymorphism and sequencing noise. 15 Here, we introduce BulkMonSTR, a computational framework that combines STR-specific 16 error modelling with machine-learning classification to enable accurate detection of mosaic 17 STR mutations from bulk next-generation sequencing data. BulkMonSTR identifies 18 nucleotide-resolution mutations—including insertions, deletions, and single-nucleotide 19 variants (SNVs)—and supports both control-independent and case-control study designs. 20 Leveraging a comprehensive training dataset derived from pedigree-based validation and in 21 

silico spike-in simulations, our random forest classifier effectively discriminates true mosaic 22 events from germline variants and technical artifacts. Benchmarking on simulated and real 23 datasets demonstrates that BulkMonSTR achieves substantially improved precision and F1 24 scores across diverse coverages and variant allele frequencies. In normal samples, cancer 25 samples and controlled in silico mixing experiments, BulkMonSTR consistently outperforms 26 existing methods, capturing a broader spectrum of STR mutations—including those arising on 27 non-reference alleles—while achieving high validation rates. By enabling systematic, genome-28 wide interrogation of STR mosaicism, BulkMonSTR provides a scalable foundation for 29 investigating the contributions of somatic STR mutations to aging and disease. 30 

Introduction: 31 

STRs are repetitive elements consisting of tandem arrays of 1-6 base pair sequence motifs. 32 These regions are highly mutable, primarily due to frequent DNA polymerase slippage during 33 replication 1. Based on pedigree analysis, the de novo mutation rate of STRs is approximately 34 1.1 × 10⁻⁵ de novo mutations per locus per generation—up to ~100-1,000-fold higher than that 35 of SNVs 2. STR mutations have been implicated in gene regulation and complex traits 3–7 and 36 are associated with a range of disorders, including more than 60 neurological conditions 8 and 37 tumorigenesis 9.38

2Despite their functional significance, the landscape of mosaic STR mutations remains largely 39 unexplored. Characterizing these mutations is technically challenging. Unlike somatic 40 mutations in tumors, which often exhibit high variant allele frequencies (VAFs) due to clonal 41 expansion, non-clonal mosaic mutations typically occur at very low VAFs. Identifying low-42 frequency mosaic STR mutations is complicated by multiple sources of technical noise, 43 including PCR stutter errors 10 , sequencing errors 11 , and mapping errors. These challenges are 44 further compounded by the lack of matched normal samples, which are critical for 45 distinguishing true somatic mutations from germline variants. 46 Recent advances in small variant detection 12–15 , including image-based approaches leveraging 47 deep learning models trained on comprehensive datasets and raw sequence alignment images, 48 have achieved high sensitivity and precision in non-repetitive regions. However, these methods 49 generally show modest sensitivity in STR regions, a limitation stemming from the polymorphic 50 nature of STRs—where mutations may revert to the reference allele or shift between two non-51 reference alleles—event types that are overlooked by these methods. Moreover, these tools 52 typically exclude multi-allelic and common population variants, further disregarding the highly 53 mutable characteristics of STRs 16,17 . The recently developed prancSTR 18 accounts for STR 54 polymorphism and enables control-independent detection of mosaic STR mutations, but has 55 the following limitations: (1) it does not leverage the rich information embedded in sequencing 56 data for artifact identification; (2) it is limited to the detection of length changes. 57 Here we present BulkMonSTR, a computational framework for detecting mosaic STR 58 mutations in bulk sequencing data. BulkMonSTR identifies nucleotide-resolution mosaic STR 59 mutations, including insertions, deletions (indels), and SNVs, and is compatible with both 60 control-independent and case-control study designs. By integrating diverse features tailored to 61 STR regions, our framework effectively discriminates true mosaic mutations from germline 62 variants and recurrent artifacts. Evaluations on simulated and real datasets demonstrate that 63 BulkMonSTR achieves superior performance across diverse read coverages, variant allele 64 frequencies, and sequencing technologies, providing a robust solution for accurately detecting 65 mosaic STR mutations. BulkMonSTR is open source and available at 66 https://github.com/douymLab/BayesMonSTR/tree/main/BayesMonSTR-BulkMonSTR. 67

Overview of BulkMonSTR 69 

BulkMonSTR executes three primary steps: identification of candidate STR alleles from 70 sequencing data (Fig. 1A), likelihood-based genotyping (Fig. 1B), and machine learning (ML)-71 based mutation classification (Fig. 1C). 72 To identify STR alleles, BulkMonSTR first extracts candidate alleles by segmenting spanning 73 reads aligned to target STR from a widely used STR panel containing ~1.6 million STR loci 19 ,74 retaining the repeat sequence together with a 5-bp window of flanking sequence on each side 75 as the initial candidate allele. A major challenge in STR allele identification is the presence of 76 recurrent, non-independent technical noise. For example, mismatch errors that are prevalent in 77 STR regions often mask true mutation signals (Supplementary Fig. 1A-C). To mitigate this 78

3issue, we implemented a two-step filtering strategy consisting of read-level and allele-level 79 filtering to generate high-confidence candidate alleles. Read-level filtering removes reads that 80 fail predefined quality-control criteria, such as low mapping quality or secondary alignment 81 (Methods), thereby reducing potential alignment and sequencing artifacts. Allele-level filtering 82 further suppresses recurrent mismatch artifacts, based on empirical observations from real 83 sequencing data where such artifacts exhibit lower base quality at mismatched bases and strong 84 strand bias (Methods, Supplementary Fig. 1D-F). This strategy improved genotyping 85 sensitivity while reducing false positive rates and computational runtime in simulated datasets 86 (Supplementary Fig. 1G-I). 87 For genotype inference, BulkMonSTR applies an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, 88 making genotype inference from sequencing reads based on the high-confidence candidate 89 alleles identified in the previous step (Fig. 1B, Methods). To account for technical noise, an 90 STR-specific stutter error profile was first estimated across all analyzed samples (Methods). 91 This profile was then used to quantify locus-specific background error rates and was 92 incorporated directly into the probabilistic EM framework. 93 Finally, to further ensure high-fidelity mutation calling, BulkMonSTR employs a random 94 forest classification model, leveraging diverse read-level features to distinguish true mosaic 95 mutations from artifacts and germline variants. To this end, BulkMonSTR incorporates both 96 conventional features—such as VAF, strand bias, and mapping quality—and STR-specific 97 features, such as STR-region stutter error patterns and STR-flanking mismatch counts 98 (Supplementary Table S1). The classifier was trained on a curated dataset spanning a range of 99 sequencing depths and VAFs. It categorizes candidate mutations as mosaic mutations, artifacts, 100 or germline heterozygous variants. Furthermore, additional hard filters—such as population 101 allele frequencies from the STR reference panel 20,21 (EnsembleTR Version II, 3,550 102 individuals)—are applied to further eliminate residual germline variants (Methods). 103 104

Machine learning based mutation classification 114 

To construct a comprehensive training dataset, we employed two complementary approaches: 115 (1) pedigree-based mutation classification and (2) in-silico spike-in simulations of mosaic STR 116 mutations (Methods, Fig. 2A, Supplementary Fig. 2). For the pedigree-based approach, we 117 used an Ashkenazi Jewish trio (HG002: son, HG003: father, HG004: mother) from the Genome 118 In a Bottle (GIAB) project 22 , with both Illumina sequencing data and Element sequencing data 119 for each person (Supplementary Table S2). Candidate mutations were first identified by the 120 BulkMonSTR genotyping module and prancSTR, and then parental sequencing data were used 121 to distinguish inherited germline variants from de novo mutations. Furthermore, orthogonal 122 cross-platform sequencing data were used to identify artifacts (Methods). This strategy 123 identified 589,383 artifacts, 260,360 germline heterozygous variants, and 775 mosaic STR 124 mutations (representative examples of HG002 mosaic STR mutations are shown in 125 Supplementary Fig. 3) across different read coverages (Fig. 2A). For the spike-in approach, 126 mosaic mutations were simulated with BamSurgeon 23 (v1.4.1) across a wide range of 127 sequencing depths, VAFs, and indel lengths (Fig. 2B-C). In total, 321,781 simulated mosaic 128 STR mutations (comprising 206,896 Indels and 114,885 SNVs) were generated (Fig. 2A, 129 Methods, Supplementary Table S3). Together, these datasets formed a training set integrating 130 real and simulated mutations, comprising 1,172,299 sites in total. 131 Using this high-quality training dataset, we trained seven random forest (RF) models on data 132 from different read coverage levels (ranging from 30× to 300×), as well as a consolidated model 133 trained on pooled data from all coverages. The consolidated model achieved consistently higher 134 or comparable F1 scores on independent test sets across all coverage levels and was therefore 135 selected for all subsequent benchmarking analyses (Fig. 2D). Analysis of feature importance 136 in the selected model revealed that mosaic fraction, STR-specific genotyping likelihood, 137 flanking mismatch number, and stutter-related features were the strongest contributors to 138 classification performance (Supplementary Fig. 4). 139 To visually assess whether our simulation strategy produced realistic mosaics and to evaluate 140 the discriminative power of the read-level features, we performed a principal component 141 analysis (PCA). This analysis was performed using the same feature set as that used in our RF 142 model. In the resulting PCA space, the spike-in simulated mosaic mutations and pedigree-based 143 

de novo mosaic mutations formed a single, distinct cluster. This cluster was clearly separated 144 from both technical artifacts and germline heterozygous mutations (Fig. 2E). The tight 145 clustering of the two independent mosaic sets validates that our in silico spike-in strategy 146 effectively recapitulates genuine mosaicism, while their clean separation from other variant 147 classes visually confirms the strong discriminative power of the used read-level features. BulkMonSTR captures the full spectrum of STR mutations 302 

To evaluate BulkMonSTR's ability to comprehensively characterize STR mutations, we further 303 analyzed the mutation spectra of the GIAB reference samples (HG002, HG005, HG008) and 304 170 TCGA blood samples. Unlike conventional tools that restrict mutation detection to the 305 human reference allele 12,16 , our method models the full allele pool at each locus. This approach 306 is essential for highly polymorphic, hypermutative STRs, allowing us to capture mutations on 307 diverse allelic backgrounds. Indeed, we found that at least 15% of indel mutations and a 308 substantial fraction of mismatches within STRs occurred on non-reference alleles, reflecting 309 the true allelic diversity at these loci (Fig. 5A-C). For instance, we identified a 6-bp deletion 310 on a non-reference allele in a TCGA blood sample (Fig. 5D), as well as a complex case where 311 an A>T mismatch arose on a non-reference allele already bearing a 1-bp deletion and an SNV 312 (Fig. 5E). 313 A key advantage of BulkMonSTR is its nucleotide-resolution detection, which enables 314 simultaneous identification of both indels and mismatches—a significant improvement over 315 length-based methods. This resolution also mitigates alignment artifacts; in the complex 316 example above, conventional methods would likely misinterpret the mutation as a germline 317 variant due to misalignment of non-spanning reads. By treating each STR locus as an 318 independent haplotype unit and considering only fully spanning reads, BulkMonSTR avoids 319 such artifacts, enabling sensitive and accurate mutation detection. 320 The functional relevance of this enhanced resolution is evident in downstream analyses. While 321 length-based approaches like prancSTR miss mismatches entirely, BulkMonSTR identified 322 eight STR mutations in coding regions, six of which were mismatches. Four of these 323 mismatches were predicted to be pathogenic, underscoring the critical importance of 324 nucleotide-resolution for assessing functional impact (Fig. 5F). For noncoding STR mutations, 325 we leveraged ENCODE4 cCRE annotations and found that 11.8–19.9% of these variants 326 overlapped candidate cis-regulatory elements, pointing to their potential regulatory roles (Fig. 327 5G). 328

Discussion 350 

We have developed BulkMonSTR, a method that enables accurate, nucleotide-resolution 351 detection of STR mutations from bulk sequencing data. By addressing key limitations of 352 conventional tools—namely, their restriction to reference alleles or reliance on length-based 353 analysis—BulkMonSTR provides a comprehensive view of STR mutational diversity. 354 A key advantage of BulkMonSTR is its high accuracy, achieved through STR-specific error 355 modeling and a machine learning framework trained on a robust dataset. Across benchmarks, 356 our method achieves 55-80% precision in detecting mosaic mutations, several-fold higher than 357 existing approaches while maintaining competitive sensitivity. This accuracy enables confident 358 downstream analyses linking mosaic STR mutations to health and disease. 359 BulkMonSTR also offers nucleotide-resolution detection, a critical advance over length-based 360 methods such as prancSTR. By capturing both indels and mismatches, our method enables a 361 more complete characterization of STR mutation spectra and mechanisms, particularly for 362 mosaic mutations. Additionally, by modeling the full allele pool at each locus rather than 363 restricting to reference alleles, BulkMonSTR embraces the hypermutative, polymorphic nature 364 of STRs, allowing identification of diverse mutations on non-reference backgrounds. 365 We acknowledge current limitations. The model is trained on Illumina NGS data with a limited 366 training set; expanding to additional sample types and sequencing platforms—such as long-367 read technologies—will further improve generalizability and enable characterization of STRs 368 in regions challenging for short reads. 369 Together, these capabilities position BulkMonSTR as a powerful tool for robust and 370 comprehensive STR mutation detection in human health and disease research. 371

Methods 375 

Identification of candidate alleles 376 

The first step of BulkMonSTR involves extracting candidate STR alleles from sequencing 377 alignment files. Given the frequent mismatch errors in repeat regions, which lead to 378 inconsistent indel presentations across reads even after BWA 37 left-alignment, BulkMonSTR 379 treats each STR as a unified haplotype rather than relying on alignment-position-based calling. 380 STR alleles are extracted by trimming all spanning reads aligned to the target STR, including 381 the repeat sequences and 5 base pairs of flanking sequences. To minimize genotyping errors 382 caused by noise alleles, we employ three progressive noise reduction strategies: (1) Low-383 quality read filtering: Reads that meet any of the following criteria are filtered out: (i) 384 secondary alignments, (ii) supplementary alignments, (iii) improper paired alignments, (iv) 385 mapping quality (MapQ) lower than 20, (v) average base quality lower than 20, (vi) mismatch 386 fraction greater than 0.1, (vii) marked as duplicates, or (viii) flagged as QC-fail. (2) Single-387 read allele exclusion: To eliminate random noise, any candidate allele supported by only a 388 single read is excluded. (3) Recurrent mismatch artifact filtration: To address pervasive 389 mismatch artifacts from sequencing (e.g., phasing noise 11 ), we designed a dedicated algorithm. 390 This algorithm assumes that a less-supported allele is likely an artifact derived from a more-391 supported allele of the same length with the lowest edit distance (defined as the number of 392 mismatches between the two alleles). The algorithm includes two main components: (i) Artifact 393 derivation identification: For each candidate allele, we identify its most probable artifact source 394 allele based on the criteria mentioned above. (ii) Artifact confirmation criteria: An allele is 395 classified as an artifact if it meets both of the following conditions: (a) The average base quality 396 of its mismatch bases is less than 20, or the proportion of mismatch bases with quality less than 397 20 exceeds 50%; (b) The allele is supported exclusively by reads from a single strand. Alleles 398 confirmed as artifacts based on these criteria are subsequently filtered out. 399 

Estimation of the stutter error model 400 

A core principle of the BulkMonSTR algorithm is to determine whether the observed fraction 401 of a mosaic allele is significantly higher than the stutter error rate. Accurate estimation of the 402 stutter error model for each STR locus is therefore essential. We assume that the simultaneous 403 occurrence of mosaic mutations at the same STR locus across multiple individuals is 404 exceedingly rare. With a sufficient number of samples, this enables precise estimation of the 405 per-locus stutter error model, minimizing stutter error over-estimation and preserving 406 sensitivity to true mosaic events. Similar to the approach in HipSTR 19 , we employ a generalized 407 stepwise error model to characterize per-locus stutter errors. As the sample genotypes are 408 initially unknown, they are treated as hidden variables. An EM algorithm is then used to 409 iteratively estimate the stutter model parameters. The EM algorithm continues until the 410 maximum number of iterations (t = 500) is reached, or the parameter change falls below 0.0001, 411 yielding a stutter error model for each STR locus. Pedigree-based mutation classification 512 

We labeled mosaic, artifact, and germline heterozygous in HG002 using trio sequencing data 513 (Supplementary Fig. 2A). First, the HG002 300× Illumina BAM file and 250× Element BAM 514 file were processed using prancSTR and BulkMonSTR's mosaic genotyping. Candidate loci 515 were initially selected based on the presence of at least three reads supporting the mutant allele 516 in the orthogonal sequencing data; loci that did not meet this requirement were labeled as 517 artifacts. Next, we required that the VAF of the mutant allele in the parental sequencing data 518 be below 0.2. Loci with a VAF exceeding this threshold were classified as germline 519 heterozygous. Furthermore, considering the prevalence of recurrent noise in repeat regions, we 520 hypothesized that if the VAF of the mutant allele in the called samples was not significantly 521 higher than the VAF observed in both parents' sequencing data (binomial test with P > 0.05), 522 the locus should be flagged as an artifact. Loci passing all these steps were designated as 523 candidate mosaic STR mutations. To construct a high-confidence ground truth for HG002 524 mosaic STR mutations, all candidate loci were manually reviewed using IGV (v2.11.9). 525 Ultimately, we labeled 129 indel mosaic mutations, 24 mismatch mosaic mutations, 51,539 526 indel germline heterozygous, 5,205 mismatch germline heterozygous, 107,960 indel artifacts, 527 and 47,036 mismatch artifacts. These loci were then downsampled to various depths (30×, 50×, 528 100×, 150×, 200×, 250×, and 300×) to facilitate model training across different sequencing 529 depths. 530

BulkMonSTR case-control mode 593 

BulkMonSTR was adapted for somatic STR mutation calling in a paired case-control design. 594 Case and matched control samples were processed jointly, with mosaic and germline genotypes 595 estimated for each sample. A somatic STR mutation is identified when an allele is present in 596 the case sample but absent from the control sample's germline genotype. Loci classified as 597 artifacts by the BulkMonSTR RF model were excluded. To exclude residual germline variants 598 and recurrent technical noise, the following filters were applied: (1) Mutations with VAF ≥ 20% 599 in the matched control sample were removed. (2) Mutations for which mutant allele read counts 600 in the case sample were not significantly higher than those in the control sample were removed 601 using an exact binomial test with P > 0.05. (3) Mutations for which mutant allele read counts 602 in the case sample were not significantly higher than unassigned read counts were removed 603 using an exact binomial test with P > 0.05. 604

While it is restricted to 672 phasable loci (specifically those with a germline heterozygous SNP near the STR loci), we 673 considered it an unbiased approach for comparing the performance of different tools. Given 674 the assumption that mosaic mutations are rare, it is highly unlikely that they occur 675 independently at the same genomic position on both the maternal and paternal copies of the 676 genome. Therefore, each phasable candidate mosaic mutation was classified as follows: (i) 677 Loci that resulted in three haplotypes were classified as mosaics (hap=3). (ii) Loci that resulted 678 in two haplotypes were classified as germline heterozygous variants (hap=2). (iii) Loci that 679 resulted in more than three haplotypes were classified as artifacts (hap>4). To perform read-680 based phasing, we obtain germline heterozygous SNPs of TCGA blood samples. The BAM 681 files were processed using the GATK (v4.2.4.1) 48 best practice workflow. Strict filtering 682 criteria were applied to ensure high-quality germline SNPs, including: (i) excluding VQSR 683 FAIL variants, (ii) using a two-tailed binomial test to remove variants deviating from a VAF 684 of 0.5, (iii) filtering out variants with genotype quality ≤20, and (iv) excluding variants with a 685 depth <20. To reduce the impact of random events on the accuracy of haplotype counting, only 686 high-quality phasable loci were used, which met the following criteria: (i) at least 10 phasable 687 reads, (ii) at least two reads supporting each phased haplotype. After validation using these 688 methods, all confirmed mutations from different tools were compiled into a sample-specific 689 ground truth set, which was subsequently used to assess the performance of each tool. 690 

Profile recurrent errors within STR regions 691 To characterize the recurrent error profile of STR regions, we used HG002, a widely adopted 692 benchmark sample with a highly accurate genome assembly 49 . By leveraging this well-693 characterized sample, we could precisely quantify and characterize the types of noise that 694 commonly occur in STR regions. Given the high noise levels in STR regions and the rarity of 695 mosaic mutations, we hypothesized that the most recurrent non-germline alleles, supported by 696 at least two reads, are likely artifacts that could significantly affect mosaic mutation calling. 697 To capture these recurrent artifacts, we focused on loci with unambiguous germline alleles, 698 applying the following stringent criteria: (1) homozygous sites, to minimize the impact of 699 confounding unknown noise sources; (2) loci located within well-assembled genomic regions, 700 where STR allele sequences are confidently assembled; and (3) exclusion of loci within 701 segmental duplication regions to avoid mapping errors due to the limitations of short-read 702 sequencing. Estimation of the stutter model from different sequencing platforms in HG002 708 

To estimate the stutter error of the GIAB HG002 sample, we first assumed that loci with the 709 same motif length and similar total length exhibit similar stutter error rates, and that mutations 710 are rare across these loci. Next, homozygous STR loci with a determined sequence in the 711 assembled regions were selected. Sequencing reads with an allele length different from the 712 assembled sequence were considered stutter errors. Based on this, a generalized stepwise error 713 model (Equation 1) was used to construct a stutter model for different motif lengths within 714 specific STR total length ranges. The insertion rate corresponds to the proportion of all stutter 715 insertion reads, the deletion rate represents the proportion of all stutter deletion reads, and the 716 geometric step-size distribution parameter reflects the proportion of stutter reads relative to the 717 total indel length change. Stutter errors that are integer multiples of the motif length are defined 718 as in-frame stutter errors, while non-integer multiples are defined as out-of-frame stutter errors. 719 Using this approach, in-frame and out-of-frame stutter error models were constructed for 720 different sequencing technologies. A simplified, motif-length-based stutter error model was 721 then used to visualize the stutter errors across different sequencing technologies 722 (Supplementary Fig. 16, Supplementary Table S11). 723
