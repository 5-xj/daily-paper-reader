Abstract 9

10 

The Capripoxviruses (CaPV) comprise three species: goatpox virus (GTPV), sheeppox virus 11 

(SPPV) and lumpy skin disease virus (LSDV). They are large double-stranded DNA viruses 12 

with highly conserved core genomes and variable terminal regions. Previous studies have 13 

described variation in CaPV gene content, their broader population structure and the 14 

contribution of non-coding and structural variation remains opaque. This study investigated 15 

the genomic diversity and evolutionary history of GTPV and SPPV using an integrative 16 

framework combining phylogenetics, pangenome variation graphs (PVGs), and gene-specific 17 

analyses. We found marked differences in population structure between the two viruses. 18 

GTPV comprised three deeply divergent and genetically stable lineages with limited evidence 19 

of recent gene flow, whereas SPPV had weaker clade separation consistent with an 20 

ancestral bottleneck followed by recent population expansion. PVG-based analyses indicated 21 

that GTPV has a comparatively closed pangenome, while SPPV remains open, particularly at 22 

the genome termini. Structural and haplotypic variation was concentrated at the inverted 23 

terminal repeats (ITRs), which moderate host immunity and specificity. In several lineages, 24 

extended putative ORFs spanning adjacent terminal genes were observed, indicating 25 

recurrent structural plasticity at the genome ends. Patterns of gene-specific conservation and 26 

divergence highlighted loci under strong constraint and lineage-specific structural changes 27 

that may contribute to host specificity. Together, these results demonstrate how graph-based 28 

genome models complement gene-based analyses in resolving poxvirus genome evolution 29 

and provide a resource for improved comparative and population genomic studies of large 30 

DNA viruses. 31

Keywords 33 

Virus, pangenome, pangenome variation graph, goatpox, sheeppox, poxvirus 34 

35 

Significance 36 

37 

Capripoxviruses are economically important livestock pathogens, yet the genomic 38 

mechanisms underlying their diversification and host specificity remain poorly resolved. By 39 

applying pangenome variation graphs alongside phylogenetic and gene-level analyses, this 40 

study reveals fundamental differences in how goatpox and sheeppox viruses have evolved. 41 

Goatpox virus had a deeper, more stable lineage structure, whereas sheeppox virus was 42 

more recent and diverse. Importantly, structural variation at the inverted terminal repeats 43 

emerged as a major driver of genomic diversity, including lineage-specific haplotypes and 44 

variable gene structures. These findings demonstrated the value of graph-based genome 45 

representations for resolving complex variation in large DNA viruses and provides a 46 

framework for improving genomic surveillance, comparative analyses, and future 47 

investigations into host range, virulence and tropism. 48

Introduction 50 

51 

Goatpox virus (GTPV) and sheeppox virus (SPPV) belong to the Capripoxvirus (CaPV) 52 

genus in the family Poxviridae and primarily infect goats and sheep as hosts, respectively. 53 

The other species in the CaPV genus is the lumpy skin disease virus (LSDV) whose 54 

preferential host is cattle. Collectively, the CaPV are re-emerging transboundary dsDNA 55 

viruses that impair animal health, and consequently cause significant economic harm (Casal 56 

et al 2018, Vinitchaikul et al 2023). They are all notifiable diseases according to the World 57 

Organization for Animal Health (WOAH) (WOAH 2010) because of their wide geographic 58 

spread and their economic importance. GTPV and SPPV are endemic across parts of Africa, 59 

the Middle East, and Asia, and phylogenetic analyses identify geographically associated sub-60 

clades in these regions. Additionally, SPPV has deep ancestry within Europe dating back 61 

several centuries (Binois-Roman et al 2025), with possible replacement by Clade 3.3 (also 62 

called clade A1), which has spread into southern Europe in recent years (§an et al 2024). 63 

Consequently, the potential for GTPV and SPPV to cause disease outbreaks is a global 64 

challenge in relation to animal welfare, economic stability and transboundary spread. 65 

66 

GTPV and SPPV can survive in the environment for long periods of time, and can transmit 67 

via contact, unlike LSDV that typically needs an insect vector (Sprygin et al 2025). GTPV can 68 

infect cattle and sheep as well, though less effectively (Bhanuprakash et al 2010, 69 

Ramakrishnan et al 2017). In the same way, SPPV can experimentally infect goats and 70 

cattle, and LSDV goats and sheep. There is vaccine cross-protection between GTPV and 71 

SPPV contrasting with the partial protective effect of LSDV vaccination against SPPV, and 72 

likewise for SPPV vaccination against LSDV (Hamdi et al 2010). This has resulted in the 73 

wider use of GTPV as a CaPV vaccine component thanks to its broader cross-protective 74 

profile, such as reducing the risk of LSDV infection (Haegeman et al 2025). 75 

76 

CaPV possess a linear genome with lengths of about 150 Kb, are AT-rich (75%), and 77 

typically have ~147-156 orthologous genes (Tulman et al 2001). The estimated interspecies 78 

genome-wide similarity is about 96%, stemming from the ancient diversification of LSDV, 79 

GTPV and SPPV (Tulman et al 2002). All share diverse inverted terminal repeats (ITRs) at 80 

both genome termini whose genes moderate host range, interactions and virulence (Kara et 81 

al 2003, Zhang et al 2025). These ITRs form complementary hairpin structures at both ends, 82 

which are essential for concatemer resolution and the formation of mature genomes during 83 

replication (Shenouda et al 2022). These ITRs are also structurally dynamic, frequently 84 

exhibiting deletions, duplications, and rearrangements that are associated with attenuation, 85 

host adaptation, and vaccine derivation. The 5’ (LSDV ORFs 1-23) and 3’ (LSDV ORFs 124-86 

156) are more variable and typically support functions linked to immunomodulation and 87 

virulence. The central genomic region (LSDV ORFs 24-123) is highly conserved and encodes 88 

core replicative and structural machinery (Tulman et al 2001, Tulman 2002, Haga et al 2024). 89 

In addition, there are nine genes in LSDV that are pseudogenised or truncated in GTPV and 90 

SPPV that moderate virulence and host range (Tulman et al 2002). 91 

92 

The genome evolution of CaPV remains understudied, particularly for GTPV and SPPV. To 93 

address this, this study uses pangenome variation graphs (PVGs) (also known as 94 

pangenome graphs or sequence graphs) to illuminate genomic diversity and the evolution of 95 

GTPV and SPPV in all available high-quality genome assemblies (Downing 2025). By 96 

representing multiple genomes as paths through a shared graph structure, PVGs enable the 97 

joint analysis of SNP, indel and structural variation (Eizenga et al 2020, Garrison & Guarrcino 98 

2023). Here, a gene-level analysis of genome-wide diversity in both species was created to 99 

probe their population structure and genetic variability, including the ITRs. Additionally, this 100 

study linked GTPV and SPPV evolution to that of LSDV using a consistent nomenclature 101 

throughout based on the Oman LSDV reference genome (Wright et al 2026) to provide a 102 

consistent framework for genes that are intact in LSDV but altered in GTPV and SPPV. 103

Methods 110 

111 

Genome assembly collection, alignment and phylogenetic analysis 112 

113 

All available complete and valid GTPV (n=14) and SPPV genomes (n=30) were downloaded 114 

from the NCBI nucleotide database (Sayers et al 2024) (5 th August 2025) (Table S1). 115 

KT438551.1 and KT438550.1 were also two listed SPPV complete genomes, but were 116 

excluded because their genome assembly quality was low. 117 

118 

This initial dataset was aligned with Mafft v7.453 (Katoh & Standley 2013) using automatic 119 

optimisation and default parameters. Phylogenetic reconstruction of the samples’ 120 

evolutionary relationships was conducted using RAxML-NG (Randomised Axelerated 121 

Maximum Likelihood) v1.2.0 (Kozlov et al 2019) with a GTR (general time reversible) model 122 

and gamma substitution rate heterogeneity selected as the best-fit model by modeltest-ng 123 

(Darriba et al 2020). Bootstrap support was calculated using both Felsenstein Bootstrap 124 

Proportion (FBP) and Transfer Bootstrap Expectation (TBE) methods with 1000 replicates 125 

where support values ≥90% were displayed. These phylogenies were mid-pointed rooted and 126 

visualised using R v4.3.2 (R Core Team 2024) packages ape v5.8-1 (Paradis & Schliep 127 

2019), phangorn v2.12.1 (Schliep 2011), treeio v1.28.0 (Wang et al 2020), ggtree v3.12.0 (Yu 128 

et al 2017) and ggplot2 v2_3.5.2 (Wickham 2016). Principal components analysis (PCA) was 129 

implemented using R based on the genome-wide SNP data. As above, the corresponding 130 

sequences for each gene were extracted, aligned with mafft, their phylogenies were 131 

reconstructed with RAxML. PCA was implemented to detect population structure patterns not 132 

apparent in the phylogenies. 133 

134 

Pangenome variation graph creation, analysis and annotation 135 

136 

Panalyze v1.0 (Tennakoon et al 2025) implemented PVG construction, analysis and 137 

annotation of these GTPV (n=14) and SPPV (n=30) genomes. Panalyze created PVGs in 138 

Graphical Fragment Assembly (GFA) format using pangenome graph builder (PGGB) v0.4.0 139 

(Garrison et al 2024) and wfmash v0.7.0 (Guarracino et al 2021), with Multiqc v1.14 (Ewels et 140 

al 2016) to collate and assess PVG metrics. Panalyze analysed these PVGs using ODGI 141 

(optimized dynamic genome/graph implementation) v0.8.3 (Guarracino et al 2022) to extract 142 

SNPs, indels and compound mutations using VG from the PVG paths (Garrison et al 2018). 143 

Subgraphs were selected using gfatools v0.4 (Li 2023), evaluated with gfastats v1.3.6 144 

(Formenti 2023), analysed with gfastats v0.1 (Formenti 2023), and visualised with VG 145 

(Garrison et al 2018). Panalyze visualised the PVGs with ODGI (Guarracino et al 2022) and 146 

ggplot2 v2_3.4.4 (Wickham 2016). Panalyze used gfautil v0.3.2 (Kubica 2023) to get 147 

mutation rates, coordinates for samples at each path and site, and VCF files. Mutation 148 

density and population genetic statistics were computed for the 5’ end (100-13,850 bp), core 149 

(13,851-106,910 bp) and 3’ end (106,910 to 150,000 bp) as per previous work (Haga et al 150 

2024). 151 

152 

The PVGs were annotated using Prokka (Seeman 2014) and indexed with SAMtools v1.19 153 

(Danecek et al 2021). Using the reference genome annotation for NC_004002 and 154 

NC_004003, BUSCO v5.8.0 (Seppey et al 2019) showed all GTPV and SPPV had all 18 155 

expected BUSCO poxvirus genes. The annotated GTPV, SPPV and LSDV genomes were 156 

aligned and compared using NC_004003, NC_004002 and PV877838 (Wright et al 2026) to 157 

extract gene boundaries for CDSs present in LSDV that were absent in GTPV and SPPV 158 

(Table S2). The Prokka GFFs were converted to gene transfer format (GTF) with gffread 159 

(Pertea & Pertea 2020) and parsed. The annotation position information was merged with the 160 

ODGI (Guarracino et al 2022) rendering of the PVGs, creating one CSV annotation file per 161 

PVG, which was visualised using Bandage v0.8.1 (Wick et al 2015). 162

165 

Panalyze inferred PVG growth using a sequence-based approach with Panacus v0.2.3 using 166 

default coverage thresholds that included all segments (Parmigiani et al 2024a), and a k-mer-167 

based method in pangrowth (Parmigiani et al 2024b). Both estimated the total number of 168 

mutations ( n) in a sample collection ( N) for an estimated parameter gamma as: 169 

n = ĸ Ngamma , where gamma reflected a closed PVG if negative and an open PVG if positive 170 

based on Heap’s Law, and ĸ was a fitting parameter. They also estimated the first derivative 171 

of this, the rate of new mutations per sample ( Δn)as: Δn =  kN -alpha , which is based on 172 

alpha<1 denoting an open PVG and alpha>1 a closed one, and k was a fitting parameter 173 

(Downing & Decano 2019). This modelled the rate of addition of new nodes or k-mers as 174 

each sample was added to the PVGs. Here, a new nodes or new k-mer indicated a new 175 

mutations Pangrowth also estimated the core PVG size based on k-mer differences. 176 

Panalyze also used ODGI heaps (Guarracino et al 2022) to infer PVG growth using 1000 177 

simulations. 178 

179 

Community detection and visualisation 180 

181 

Community detection was performed on PVGs, where these communities reflect the relative 182 

similarity of samples based on PVG data. Here, these were identified with Panalyze using 183 

wfmash (Guarracino et al 2021) and PGGB (Garrison et al 2023) with a k-mer of 19 bp, a 184 

window size of 67 bp, a threshold of 80% sequence mash-level identity, and ten mappings 185 

per node. Panalyze evaluated these communities with PGGB scripts (Garrison et al 2023) 186 

that built a network with the contigs as nodes and mappings as weighted edges, where the 187 

latter were proportional to the mapping’s similarity and length (Guarracino et al 2023). 188 

Panalyze visualised these networks with igraph tools (Csardi & Nepusz 2006). The mappings 189 

threshold was the number of detected haplotypes minus one for the given region (Guarracino 190 

et al 2023). 191 

192 

Tests for selective processes 193 

194 

We tested for selection using the direction of selective (DoS) metric expressed as: DoS = 195 

DN /(D N +D S ) - P N /(P N +P S) (Stoletzki & Eyre-Walker 2011) where D N and D N stand for the 196 

number of ancestral nonsynonymous and synonymous substitutions (respectively) between 197 

the GTPV and SPPV species, and P N and P S stand for the number of current population-level 198 

(within-species) nonsynonymous and synonymous mutations (respectively). A DoS value of 199 

about 0 indicated neutral effects 0, purifying selection if DoS < 0 and potentially adaptive 200 

evolution if DoS. This analysis included an existing dataset of 121 LSDV genomes for 201 

comparison (Wright et al 2025). 202

Contrasting levels of population structure within goatpox virus (GTPV) and sheeppox 206 

virus (SPPV) 207 

208 

Pangenome variation graph (PVGs) were created for available GTPV (n=14) and SPPV 209 

(n=30) genomes to explore their genomic diversity (Table S1). GTPV and SPPV had marked 210 

differences in their population structures and evolutionary histories, consistent with previous 211 

work (L’Hote et al 2026). GTPV had three separate clades named 2.1 (n=6 samples), 2.2 212 

(n=5) and 2.3 (n=3) based on phylogenetic, PCA, network-based interpretation of genome-213 

wide SNPs (Figure 1A). Here, an existing clade naming system was used (Biswas et al 214 

2019). In GTPV, the first PC (56% of variation) split clades 2.1 and 2.2 from 2.3, and the PC2 215 

(30%) separated all three clades (Figure 1B). This suggested long-term genetic differentiation 216 

of these GTPV clades, with no evidence of recent admixture in this dataset (Figure 1C). 217 

Clade 2.3 was the only clade without vaccine-related specimens and contained the sole 218 

African sample (MN072624), together with two Middle Eastern isolates. As shown below 219 

using LSDV-rooted phylogenies, this clade also had the deepest evolutionary root. 220

The rooting of these GTPV and SPPV genomes was explored using LSDV sample 256

GTPV and SPPV pangenome variation graph structure, openness and diversity 265 

266 

Although there were fewer GTPV samples, its complete PVG length was almost as long, 267 

spanning 160,732 bp compared to SPPV’s 161,135 bp. The GTPV PVG had more nodes 268 

than the SPPV PVG (7,801 vs 3,044) and more edges (10,644 vs 4,208). The median PVG 269 

length was 147,684 bp for GTPV and 148,183 bp for SPPV, reflecting the median genome 270 

size (Figure S4) that was marginally smaller than LSDV. The shared PVG for GTPV was 271 

smaller than for SPPV (142,411 bp vs 144,593 bp) (Figure S4), implying that if more GTPV 272 

genomes were acquired, its shared PVG would shrink, whereas for SPPV it would not. 273 

274 

The distinct GTPV population structure meant it had a closed PVG (alpha=1.76 from 275 

Pangrowth), indicating that few new mutations would be found if more GTPV genomes were 276 

sampled (Figure S5). These estimates were confirmed with Panacus for GTPV (alpha=1.63) 277 

(Figure S6). In contrast, the SPPV PVG was open (alpha=0.69) (Figure S5), which suggested 278 

that new mutations would be found if more SPPV genomes were examined. The Panacus 279 

SPPV value was more marginal (alpha=1.01) (Figure S6), suggesting that moderate SPPV 280 

sampling may have been achieved. Nonetheless, the lack of samples from African may be 281 

limiting for both species. 282

To explore genetic variation across GTPV and SPPV, diversity at each gene was examined 418 

using the LSDV gene nomenclature for consistency, including regions encoding genes in 419 

LSDV that are not CDSs in GTPV or SPPV (Figure S9). GTPV genes had more SNPs/Kb 420

A genome-wide scan for selective processes identified additional patterns, including a 433 

comparatively low correlation between the GTPV and SPPV DoS metrics (rho=0.23, 434 

p=0.0087) and evidence of long-term conservation across genes (median D N /D S =0.63). 435 

Although the median DoS values were similar (GTPV 0.031 vs SPPV -0.038), the low 436 

correlation was driven by a smaller standard deviation (SD) in the DoS values for GTPV 437 

compared to SPPV (0.26 vs 0.39). In addition, there was a higher P N/P S ratio within SPPV 438 

compared to GTPV (0.98 vs 0.56), which could be associated with the relatively more ancient 439 

origin of GTPV allowing more time for selection to remove deleterious nonsynonymous 440 

alleles. Four genes had DoS > 0.291 in GTPV and DoS > 0.36 in SPPV (corresponding to the 441 

respective median+SD values): LSDV004, LSDV023, LSDV068 and LSDV129. In addition to 442 

these, five GTPV and 12 SPPV genes met the species DoS criteria (Supplementary Text). 443 

Lastly, the total number of derived nonsynonymous changes was higher for SPPV than 444 

GTPV (1,127 vs 892): both were higher compared to an existing set of 121 LSDV genomes 445 

(542) (Wright et al 2025). 446

Discussion 481 

482 

This study explored the evolution and genomic diversity of GTPV and SPPV using 483 

phylogenetics, PVG-based and gene-specific approaches. GTPV and SPPV cause extensive 484 

disease outbreaks, along with LSDV (WOAH 2010). Understanding better the genetic basis 485 

for these differences is pivotal. This study used an integrative strategy to reveal clear 486 

contrasts in population structure, evolutionary history, and genome evolution between GTPV 487 

and SPPV. A central finding was that GTPV had a deeper and more stable population 488 

structure, comprising three genetically distinct clades with little evidence of recent admixture, 489 

whereas SPPV was a mosaic of more recent variation with weaker clade separation. For both 490 

viruses, genome-wide diversity patterns were consistent with an ancestral population 491 

contraction followed by expansion. 492 

493 

This work builds on earlier gene-centric CaPV pangenome analyses (Xie et al 2024) to use 494 

using PVGs that do not rely on gene annotation, capture non-coding and structural variation, 495 

and quantify PVG openness using multiple complementary approaches. Importantly, 496 

pangenome openness here reflected both biological diversity and uneven sampling, 497 

particularly the limited representation of samples from Africa, which remains a shared 498 

constraint for both species. Across all genomes, the shared number of genomic bases was 499 

smaller for GTPV than for SPPV (142.4 vs 144.6 kb), reflecting GTPV’s deeper evolutionary 500 

structure. However, diversity at the 5 ′ and 3 ′ genome ends was higher relative to the core 501 

genome in SPPV than GTPV, consistent with the 5’ and 3’ end genes’ roles in encoding non-502 

essential virulence and host immunomodulatory factors. The open PVG inferred for SPPV 503 

indicated that broader geographic and temporal sampling will continue to reveal novel 504 

variation, whereas the closed GTPV PVG suggests limited returns from additional sampling 505 

in Eurasia. Lastly, the representative PVGs constructed here provide a practical resource for 506 

improved short-read mapping and variant detection in future CaPV work (Wright et al 2025). 507 

508 

PVG-based analyses were particularly informative at the ITRs, revealing substantial 509 

structural and haplotypic variation centred on regions homologous to LSDV003 and 510 

LSDV154. These genes are annotated as encode ER-localised apoptosis regulators that 511 

interfere with host mitochondrial function and apoptosis (Tulman et al 2001). LSDV003 is 512 

predicted to have a signal peptide, a transmembrane domain, and insert into the endoplasmic 513 

reticulum (Liaci & Förster 2021). The protein it encodes is similar to MYXV M004, an 514 

essential virulence factor (Enow 2025). The LSDV003 and LSDV154 truncations in vaccine-515 

related SPPV Clade 3.1 were consistent with attenuated virulence following serial passage in 516 

non-natural hosts. The longer putative LSDV003 ORFs in six SPPV samples across Clades 517 

3.2 and 3.3 may be associated with expression regulation of this protein via protease 518 

cleavage. This potential structural ITR plasticity could mean that these extended ORFs 519 

encode mRNAs with different expression levels or post-translational functions. In addition, 520 

the retention of the non-CDS regions suggests a continued functional relevance, perhaps to 521 

facilitate recombination for those at the ITRs. 522 

523 

Beyond the ITRs, patterns of gene-specific diversity highlighted candidate loci associated 524 

with host specificity. 18 SPPV genes had either zero or one SNP, implying strong functional 525 

constraint and potential roles in species-specific host interactions in SPPV. In contrast, GTPV 526 

only had one gene with high conservation, suggesting selective processes maybe operating 527 

on each clade differently, perhaps linked to their higher geographic population structure. 528 

There were signals consistent with adaptive evolution at three genes, including LSDV068 529 

encoding a polyA polymerase small subunit. The second was: LSDV023, which had 530 

sequence-based and structural homology on viro3D (Litvin et al 2025) with MYXV m018L 531 

gene that encodes non-essential cytoplasmic protein (Higley & Way 1997). The third was 532 

LSDV129, which had similarity to MYXV M130R, whose product has substantive 533 

immunomodulation and virulence effects in rabbit hosts (Barrett et al 2009). In addition, the 534

three major GTPV lineages were consistently differentiated by divergent haplotypes at 535 

regions corresponding to LSDV002, LSDV009, LSDV013, LSDV026 and LSDV153. 536 

537 

In summary, GTPV evolution is structured by deep, clade-specific divergence, whereas 538 

SPPV has a more diffuse, ongoing diversification, with ITR-associated haplotypes and 539 

immune-related genes driving much of the observed variation. Several broader questions 540 

emerge from this study. First, the retention of long non-coding regions at loci that are genes 541 

in LSDV only (LSDV002, LSDV004, LSDV009, LSDV013, LSDV026, LSDV132, LSDV153, 542 

LSDV155) suggested that these regions may be subject to functional or structural constraints 543 

beyond protein coding. ITRs are constrained by replication mechanisms, whereas the 544 

preservation of internal non-CDS loci across divergent lineages argues for targeted functional 545 

testing. Second, many of the complex rearrangements described here, especially at the ITRs, 546 

are likely under-resolved in short-read assemblies. Long-read sequencing and haplotype-547 

resolved assemblies will be essential for improving CaPV genome annotation, resolving ORF 548 

structure, and linking genomic variation to viral phenotype. Third, there were vaccine-linked 549 

specimens with unique ITR mutations: these recombination-prone regions may be as 550 

hotspots for new adaptive variation, underscoring the need for genomic surveillance of 551 

vaccine-linked strains using PVGs (Guarracino et al 2023). Fourth, GTPV isolates infect both 552 

sheep and goats (Biswas et al 2019) and yet SPPV may be the most basal among the 553 

capripoxviruses (L’Hote et al 2026), highlighting continued evolutionary questions that may 554 

impact perspectives on host tropism. 555
