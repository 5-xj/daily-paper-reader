20 Abstract 

21 Astyanax Baird & Girard, 1854 is a widely distributed and species-rich genus of 

22 Acestrorhamphidae, whose abundant populations in Neotropical basins play a crucial 

23 ecological role at the trophic level. Taxonomic uncertainties persist within the genus, as seen 

24 in Astyanax sp. (formerly designated as A. fasciatus ) from the Magdalena basin in Colombia. 

25 Concerns about its genetic status are heightened due to ecological threats posed by 

26 hydroelectric dams, from habitat loss to river connectivity. We isolated and characterized 17 

27 microsatellite loci to assess the population genetics of this species in a broad sample from 

28 the middle and lower sections of the Cauca River, now interrupted by the Ituango dam. 

29 Furthermore, a multidisciplinary approach integrating phylogenetic analyses of 

30 mitochondrial ( COI ) and nuclear ( rag2 ) markers with geometric morphometric analyses was 

31 employed to evaluate potential cryptic diversity within Astyanax sp . Microsatellites revealed 

32 two genetic groups in the studied area, strongly supported as distinct lineages by phylogenetic 

33 analyses. Unexpectedly, one of these lineages of Astyanax sp. was recovered in an unresolved 

34 clade with samples of A. microlepis and allopatric samples of A. viejita from the Maracaibo 

35 Lake basin. Each genetic group showed high genetic diversity, but also evidence of recent 

36 bottleneck events and significant-high values of inbreeding. Morphometric analyses provided 

37 evidence of significant phenotypic differentiation among A. microlepis , Astyanax sp. 1 

38 (Asp1), and Astyanax sp . 2 (Asp2). Morphological patterns ranged from the robust profile of 

39 A. microlepis to the streamlined shape of Astyanax sp . 2 (Asp2), with Astyanax sp . 1 (Asp1) 

40 displaying intermediate traits and localized differences in head length and fin placement. 

41 Statistical support from permutation tests and a high overall classification accuracy (95.65%) 

42 underscore the existence of distinct morphospecies, suggesting that phenotypic 

43 differentiation is well-established, despite the complex evolutionary history of the group. 

44 This study suggests the presence of cryptic diversity within Astyanax sp. and provides 

45 valuable genetic information for the conservation and management of their populations in 

46 the Magdalena basin.

48 Introduction 

49 Astyanax Baird & Girard, 1854 is a genus of tetra fishes with broad distribution across the 

50 American continent, extending from Texas to Patagonia, and is especially abundant in the 

51 Amazon and Orinoco basins. The genus currently includes 122 valid species, comprising 

52 approximately 2.8% of the taxonomic diversity of the entire order Characiformes [1], and 

53 recently was reallocated into the family Acestrorhamphidae from its traditional placement in 

54 Characidae [2]. Due to non-exclusive diagnostic characters, phenotypic plasticity, and 

55 limited resolution by COI -based molecular analyses, Astyanax remains a non-monophyletic 

56 group [2-5], complicating species-level taxonomic identification within the genus. 

57 In Colombia, 22 Astyanax species are reported, nine of them in the Magdalena basin [6], 

58 including Astyanax sp., formerly designated as Astyanax fasciatus (Cuvier, 1819) [7]. 

59 Astyanax fasciatus was reallocated in Psalidodon Eigenmann, 1911 [5] and its geographic 

60 distribution restricted to the São Francisco basin in southeastern Brazil [5], leaving at least 

61 five allopatric species from remote cis-Andean or even trans-Andean basins that are still 

62 listed as synonyms of P. fasciatus [8], in a taxonomic limbo. 

63 Species of Astyanax from the Magdalena basin have been reported as omnivorous, with 

64 seasonal life strategies, early maturation, body size ranging from 60 to 183 mm of standard 

65 length (SL), and short-range migrations [9]. They are also considered the most abundant 

66 species both in numerical abundance and biomass in the Magdalena basin, even in reservoir 

67 systems, and play a fundamental role in the food chain [9]. Local fishermen recognize that 

68 Astyanax species are among the first populations to leave floodplains and signal upstream 

69 migrations for other species during periods of water descent [9,10]. Some of these species, 

70 such as A. magdalenae Eigenmann & Henn, 1916 and Astyanax sp., have also been reported 

71 for ornamental and/or consumption purposes in the last two decades [9]. 

72 Despite Astyanax’s high species diversity, taxonomic uncertainty, and multiple threats, few 

73 studies have examined its microevolutionary genetics scale. These studies have focused 

74 mainly on populations from Mexico and Brazil [11-14]). In Colombia, only one study has 

75 assessed population genetics of A. caucanus (Steindachner, 1879), using species-specific 

76 microsatellite loci [15]. That study reported high gene flow along a 340 km stretch of the 

77 Cauca River, even among sectors recently isolated by the Ituango dam, as well as high genetic

78 diversity (as in other congeners) and large effective population sizes [15]. However, it also 

79 found evidence of recent bottleneck events and significant inbreeding [15]. 

80 Astyanax is characterized by high phenotypic plasticity and a remarkable capacity to adapt 

81 to diverse habitats [16,17]. This plasticity has motivated numerous studies using geometric 

82 morphometrics, a tool for quantifying body shape variation among genera, species, 

83 populations, and sexes [18]. Such data are crucial for refining species boundaries and 

84 assessing the influence of both genetic and environmental factors [19-23], especially in 

85 groups like Characidae, where morphological conservatism often masks underlying genetic 

86 differences. 

87 Several studies have combined genetics and morphometrics to explore their interplay in 

88 Astyanax and related genera [23-27]. For instance, ecologically distinct morphs of Astyanax 

89 have been identified in the San Juan River basin (Central America), with marked 

90 morphological differentiation (one morph with elongated body and predatory features and 

91 the other morph with deep body and generalist traits), despite limited genetic divergence 

92 between them [22]. Morphological distinction has also been corroborated by genetic 

93 evidence in the Psalidodon scabripinnis (Jenyns, 1842) complex, where P. paranae 

94 (Eigenmann, 1914) exhibits a more fusiform body than P. rivularis (Lütken, 1875) [26]. 

95 However, a clear correspondence between morphological and genetic patterns is often absent. 

96 Studies on sympatric populations of A. caballeroi (Contreras-Balderas & Rivera-Teillery, 

97 1985) and A. aeneus (Günther, 1860) in Mexico have revealed significant differences in body 

98 depth, head profile, and fin morphology that are not reflected by the molecular data [19]. 

99 Geometric morphometrics also enables analyses of environmental effects on body shape, 

100 such as habitat type and water flow [28-31]. Ecomorphological divergence is well-

101 documented in Astyanax and related genera. A common pattern is the correlation between 

102 body shape and water velocity, species inhabiting lotic environments, such as Psalidodon 

103 rivularis and P. paranae , typically have fusiform bodies adapted to swimming against 

104 currents, whereas species from lentic habitats, like A. abramis (Jenyns, 1842), A. lacustris 

105 (Lütken, 1875), and A. asuncionensis Géry, 1972, exhibit deeper, laterally compressed 

106 bodies, suited for maneuverability [32]. This pattern also occurs within single species. In 

107 river populations, A. lacustris have a fusiform shape with a deep caudal peduncle, while their

108 lagoon-dwelling counterparts are deeper-bodied with a shallower caudal peduncle [33]. 

109 Likewise, A. bimaculatus (Linnaeus, 1758) from reservoirs have deeper bodies and longer 

110 fin bases than those from rivers [32]. Some species that occupy both environments, such as 

111 P. fasciatus , display an intermediate body shape, reflecting their ecological flexibility 

112 [23,32]. 

113 In the present work, we designed and evaluated a set of microsatellite loci to assess genetic 

114 diversity and population structure of Astyanax sp. in the middle and lower sections of the 

115 Cauca River, spanning sectors upstream and downstream of the Ituango dam. Additionally, 

116 COI and rag2 sequences were compared within a broad phylogenetic framework (with 

117 emphasis on trans-Andean representatives of Astyanax and species of the resurrected 

118 Psalidodon [5], including the true nominal P. fasciatus ), to determine whether groups 

119 potentially identified through microsatellites correspond to divergent lineages within the 

120 genus. Finally, geometric morphometric analyses were conducted to investigate potential 

121 drivers of diversification in Astyanax .

122 123 Materials and methods 

124 Sampling 

125 The Cauca River, stretching 1,350 km from Laguna del Buey in the south to its confluence 

126 with the Magdalena River, flows along the inter-Andean valley and canyon delimited by the 

127 Western and Central Cordilleras of the northern Andes mountains, with its upper section at 

128 around 900 m asl, the final 500 km features a steep, narrow canyon, dominated by rapids and 

129 waterfalls, which posed challenges for migrating fishes, even before the Ituango dam 

130 construction. This dam is the largest in Colombia, standing at 225 m high, and it is expected 

131 to contribute approximately 2,400 MW of power annually [34,35]. 

132 Between 2019 and 2021, a total of 324 muscle and fin tissue samples of Astyanax sp. were 

133 collected from eight sections in the middle and lower parts of the Cauca River ( Fig 1 ). High-

134 resolution photographs were captured for each specimen and standardized for landmark 

135 digitization, providing the framework for subsequent geometric morphometric analyses. 

136 These sections were chosen due to their contrasting hydrological and geographical

151 152 Isolation and characterization of microsatellite loci 

153 We designed and assessed 42 specific primer pairs (Afa01-Afa42; S1 Table) to amplify 

154 microsatellite loci containing tri-, tetra-, and pentanucleotide motifs to assess population 

155 genetics in samples of Astyanax sp. We applied Illumina's MiSeq v2 technology and followed 

156 a previously established methodology [36] to identify and isolate microsatellite loci, using a 

157 sample (P730) of Astyanax sp. collected in the Cauca River. Prinseqlite v0.20.4 was used to 

158 refine the 250-base paired-end reads generated in the sequencing, followed by Abyss v1.3.5 

159 assembly and PAL_FINDER v.0.02.03 analysis to locate microsatellite loci with perfect 

160 repeats [37]. Primer pairs for amplification were designed via Primer3 v.2.0, and electronic 

161 PCR validation ensured accurate primer alignment for the amplifiable loci. 

162 PCR amplifications were conducted in a final reaction volume of 10 μl, following a three-

163 primer methodology [38,39]. The reaction mixture contained 1X Platinum™ Multiplex PCR 

164 Master Mix, 0.25% Enhancer/GC, 0.20 pmol/μl of forward primer with a universal tail, 0.40 

165 pmol/μl of reverse primer, and 0.20 pmol/μl of fluorescently labeled tail. The thermal profile 

166 involved an initial step at 95°C for 3 minutes, followed by 35 cycles of 90°C for 30 seconds 

167 and 56°C for 35 seconds, without final extension. Fragment separation was carried out on a 

168 SeqStudio Genetic Analyzer, using LIZ600 as an internal molecular marker. Allelic

169 assignment was performed using GeneMarker v3.0.0, while Micro-Checker v.2.2.3 [40] was 

170 employed to identify potential genotyping errors in the data matrix. 

171 We employed 36 random samples representing all sections of the studied river to characterize 

172 each of the amplified loci. These samples were used to calculate various genetic diversity 

173 metrics (detailed below), including polymorphic information content (PIC), using CERVUS 

174 v3.0.7 [41]. 

175 176 Population genetics analyses 

177 Genetic differentiation and structure among sections were assessed using three 

178 complementary approaches: (i) Bayesian analysis of co-ancestry performed in Structure 

179 v2.3.4 [42], with optimal number of populations determined via estimators in Structure 

180 Selector [43]; (ii) analysis of molecular variance (AMOVA) and the calculation of pairwise 

181 F´ ST and Jost's D EST indices using GenAlEx v6.502 [44]; and (iii) discriminant analysis of 

182 principal components (DAPC) implemented with the Adegenet package [45] in R v3.4.0. 

183 To assess genetic diversity and demographic events, we calculated genetic diversity metrics 

184 (Na: average number of alleles per locus, H O, H E: observed and expected heterozygosity, 

185 respectively) and the inbreeding coefficient (F IS ) for each section and genetic group, using 

186 Arlequin v3.5.2.2 [46]. Additionally, allelic richness (Ar) was determined using FSTAT v.2.9 

187 [47]. Deviations from Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE) were analyzed using the web 

188 version of GENEPOP v4.7.5 [48]. The effective population size (N e) was estimated through 

189 NeEstimator v2.1 [49]. Furthermore, identification of genetic evidence for recent bottlenecks 

190 was assessed using the excess heterozygosity method in BOTTLENECK v1.2.02 [50] and 

191 the calculation of the M index [51]. Statistical significance in multiple comparisons was 

192 evaluated after applying the Bonferroni sequential correction.

204 Phylogenetic reconstruction was conducted using the 'A la Carte' workflow integrated in 

205 NGPhylogeny.fr [52]. Sequences were aligned with MAFFT [53] and the resulting alignment 

206 was optimized using TrimAl [54] to remove poorly aligned regions and gaps. The best-fit 

207 evolutionary model was determined using IQ-TREE [55], which identified TIM2e+I+R2 as 

208 the optimal model for the dataset based on the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). 

209 Bayesian Inference was performed in MrBayes v3.2 [56] implementing as selected 

210 evolutionary model, a General Time Reversible (GTR) model with a proportion of invariant 

211 sites and Gamma-distributed rate variation (+I+G; nst=6, rates=invgamma). The Metropolis-

212 coupled Markov Chain Monte Carlo analysis consisted of two independent runs (nrun=2), 

213 each utilizing four chains (one cold and three heated), for 2,000,000 generations. To ensure 

214 scientific reproducibility, the analysis was executed with fixed starting seeds (Seed=5, 

215 Swapseed=5). Parameters and trees were sampled every 500 generations, and a 25% burn-in 

216 fraction was applied, discarding the initial 500,000 generations. Convergence and stationary 

217 distributions were assessed using Tracer v1.7.2 [57], ensuring that all parameters reached an 

218 Effective Sample Size (ESS) greater than 200. The consensus tree was rooted using Jupiaba 

219 polylepis as the outgroup and visualized using Newick Display [58] and iTOL [59].

226 227 228 Geometric morphometrics 

229 A total of 138 specimens were collected during the dry season. Each specimen was 

230 photographed in left lateral view (Fig 2), inside a diffuse light box, using a 12 MP Samsung 

231 digital camera, with focal length of 47 cm. Landmark digitization was conducted using the 

232 XYOM v. 3.0 application [60] (https://xyom.io/), marking on each image, eight type I 

233 landmarks (juxtaposition of tissues) and one type II landmark (tip of snout; [61]). The 

234 digitization process was repeated twice to ensure precision, and a Generalized Procrustes 

235 Analysis [62] was applied to align landmark coordinates (S2 Table), removing variation 

236 due to size, rotation, and position [63]. Body shape variables (partial warps and uniform 

237 components) were analyzed using linear discriminant analysis, implemented with the R 

238 packages MASS [64] for analysis, and ggplot2 [65] for visualization. Additionally, a 

239 classification approach based on cross-validation was applied using the R package caret 

240 [66] to evaluate model performance. The statistical significance of Euclidean distances to 

241 explore differences among genetic groups was assessed through 1,000 permutations using 

242 CLIC software [60]. Additionally, a multivariate regression was used to assess the effect of 

243 size on shape (allometry). When significant allometry was detected, size correction of 

244 shape was performed if the allometric slopes fitted a common model, which was evaluated 

245 using multivariate analysis of covariance (MANCOVA). A thin-plate spline graphical 

246 analysis was conducted using tpsRegr v.1.45 [67] and mean objects (residual coordinates 

247 obtained after Procrustes superimposition), generated with CLIC, to illustrate body shape 

248 deformations.

254 Results 

255 STR development and polymorphism 

256 Among the initially 42 evaluated loci, 17 were polymorphic, consistently amplified, and 

257 exhibited PIC values above 0.500 (Table 3). The number of alleles per locus ranged from 4 

258 to 31, with an allelic range (Ra) of 105 to 381, and expected heterozygosity values between 

259 0.656 and 0.965. Some of these loci exhibited a high fixation index, and 11 showed 

260 significant deviation from Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium. Due to limited sample quantity, loci 

261 Afa18, Afa36, and Afa38 were not amplified in the broader sample set. Consequently, a final 

262 suite of 14 loci was selected for subsequent population genetic analyses.

Discussion 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted March 31, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.28.714954 doi: bioRxiv preprint

Neotropical freshwater fish evolution reflects a complex interplay between contemporary demographic processes and deep-time cladogenesis. In this study, we integrated newly developed microsatellite loci to disentangle these scales in Astyanax sp. from the Cauca River. Although microsatellites are established tools for testing microevolutionary hypotheses [37; 68, 69], this work represents only the second instance in Colombia where species-specific markers have been successfully developed for this group, following the previous effort on Ctenobrycon caucanus (formerly Astyanax caucanus ) [15]. The high-resolution of these new loci enabled the detection of fine-scale genetic discontinuities in the middle and lower sections of the basin, and provided highly informative baselines for monitoring the genetic health of Astyanax populations. This is particularly relevant in sectors impacted by the Ituango dam, where microevolutionary forces are actively shaping the genomic landscape. Genetic diversity in terms of alleles and expected heterozygosity was similar and high between both genetic stocks, compared to other Neotropical characiforms (Na: 10.1, H E:0.675;[70]) and cis-Andean congeners (e.g. Astyanax scabripinnis ) [12]. Nonetheless, both genetic stocks showed high inbreeding levels, particularly higher in Asp2 from the lower section of the river. These levels of genetic diversity and inbreeding were comparable to those reported for Ctenobrycon caucanus (H E: 0.832-0.840; F IS : 0.307-0.351; [15]), another sympatric species in the Cauca River, and to Central American congeners such as A. aeneus 

and A. caballeroi [14] .

Although much is still unknown about the life cycle of Acestrorhamphidae species from the Magdalena basin, similar genetic features among coinhabiting species, like Astyanax sp. and 

C. caucanus , such as inbreeding, genetic diversity, and bottleneck events (see [71]), may reflect a shared biogeographic history, coupled with as-yet-unknown intrinsic biological factors of their life cycle. Despite the observed genetic evidence indicating bottleneck events in the samples analyzed, the estimated effective population size for Asp1 was high, consistent with reports for other species of the Magdalena basin [71]. This is also congruent with abundant populations [72-74], as observed in Astyanax sp. and A. microlepis , reported to be the most common species, in terms of abundance and biomass in the basin, even in dammed environments [9]. Such

demographic robustness suggests that while these populations experience historical or contemporary fluctuations, they maintain large reproductive pools capable of preserving high levels of genetic variation. The implications of these population-level findings extend beyond contemporary demographics. To determine whether the Asp1 and Asp2 genetic stocks represent incipient speciation or established evolutionary lineages, we bridged the gap between micro- and macroevolutionary scales by integrating a dual-gene phylogenetic approach ( COI and rag2 )with geometric morphometrics. This framework allowed us to assess the taxonomic congruence of the clusters detected by microsatellites in a broader evolutionary context, providing a robust characterization of the diversity within trans-Andean Astyanax .Collectively, these independent lines of evidence are essential for a more comprehensive understanding of the complex biodiversity of trans-Andean ichthyofauna. Such integration is critical as taxonomic uncertainties at the species level, together with the non-monophyletic condition defining the sensu lato concept of Astyanax (still in use) , have led to the conspicuous proliferation of samples identified only to the genus level in ichthyological collections [4,5]. This has also been the case for Astyanax sp. from the Magdalena basin in Colombia. This species had been traditionally identified as Astyanax fasciatus [7], until this nominal species was reallocated in the recently resurrected 

Psalidodon , along with evidence indicating that known species of this genus are geographically restricted to Atlantic basins of Brazil, as the Rio São Francisco (type locality of P. fasciatus ) and to the Paraná-Paraguay basin [2,5]. This taxonomic uncertainty of the Magdalena species raises concerns for its populations from the Cauca River, given their trophic importance into the local fish assemblages, reports of increasing human consumption, and environmental threats in the basin, by pollution of aquatic habitats and by habitat fragmentation due to the Ituango dam [9,35,75]. Phylogenetic analyses showed that the two genetic stocks identified by microsatellites also fall into two distinct lineages strongly supported using both mitochondrial and nuclear genes. Lineage 1 consisted of samples from Asp1, but surprisingly, this lineage also encompasses samples of A. microlepis (from its type locality, in the upper basin of the Cauca River), and 

A. viejita (from the Maracaibo Lake basin).

Astyanax microlepis has been reported throughout the Cauca River [10,76] and was recovered as sister to the clades A. bimaculatus and A. argentatus [5]. Conversely, sequences of Astyanax viejita are exclusive from the Maracaibo Lake basin in Venezuela [3], with no previous reports of its presence in Colombia [6]. Lineage 2 comprised samples from Asp2, including sequences of Astyanax sp. collected in the main channel of the middle section of the Magdalena River. Despite genetic similarity between Astyanax microlepis and Asp1 (based at least on partial 

cox1 sequences), results of the linear discriminant analysis revealed clear morphological differentiation between both species. Sympatric divergence in Astyanax has been documented in the Nicaraguan lakes Managua [comprising the deep-bodied A. nasutus Meek, 1907 and A. nicaraguensis Eigenmann & Ogle, 1907, and the elongate A. bransfordii (Gill, 1877)] and Nicaragua (where A. nicaraguensis and A. bransfordii also co-occur). In both lakes, these species exhibit differences in premaxilla shape, in addition to differences in body configuration. Such variations correlate strongly with trophic ecology, suggesting adaptive divergence [22,73]. Similar findings have been reported for A. aeneus and A. caballeroi in Lake Catemaco, Mexico [14,19]. This discordance between phenotypic and genotypic variation can be explained by some non-mutually exclusive hypotheses. One possible explanation is that morphotypes are undergoing incipient speciation. In this scenario, the lack of widespread genetic differentiation is an expected consequence of recent divergence, which leads to incomplete sorting of ancestral lineages [77-79]. Furthermore, a divergence-with-gene-flow model may explain how strong selection on a few "genomic islands" can still create distinct phenotypes under these conditions [79,80]. Our results are concordant with this model, which is supported by examples in related species like A. aeneus and A. caballeroi that maintain distinctiveness amid significant admixture [14]. The discordance observed between phenotypic and mitochondrial data for A. microlepis and Asp1 can be explained by a scenario of allopatric divergence followed by secondary contact. This evolutionary model has been previously invoked to explain the lack of correspondence between pronounced morphological divergence and genetic structure in sympatric Astyanax 

morphotypes [19]. In such cases, the maintenance of distinct phenotypes despite gene flow

depends on the strength of natural and sexual selection, as well as the robustness of reproductive isolating barriers [19, 81]. Under this scenario, our findings suggest a process of unidirectional introgression, where recent hybridization has eroded the molecular signals of previous isolation at the organellar level. Similar patterns of secondary contact and subsequent genetic exchange have been documented in other Neotropical contexts; for instance, the complex hydrological history of the San Juan River basin seems to reflect secondary contact between deep- and elongated-bodied Astyanax morphs [22], while significant introgression has been reported in Herichthys , following recent contact between divergent lineages [82]. Taken together, these precedents suggest that the phenotypic integrity of A. microlepis and Asp1 is likely maintained by strong divergent selection, even if their mitochondrial lineages have been homogenized. However, as mitochondrial DNA represents only a single maternal locus, further research using high-resolution genomic tools is required to confirm the extent, directionality, and nuclear impact of this gene flow. A third possibility is that these morphotypes represent a case of stable polymorphism within a single species. Under this model, divergent morphs develop from a single gene pool, where the developmental trajectory is maintained by balancing selection or frequency-dependent mechanisms rather than a transitional phase towards speciation [82-85]. This scenario has been strongly suggested for other Astyanax populations, where marked morphological divergence occurs in the absence of genetic structure, such as in Lake Catemaco [14] and the San Juan River basin [22]. Similar examples of stable trophic polymorphism include the cichlid Herichthys minckleyi [82] and the mouth asymmetry in Perissodus microlepis [86]. Closely related to this is the hypothesis of phenotypic plasticity, where a single genotype produces alternative phenotypes in response to environmental cues. In Neotropical fishes, plasticity is frequently invoked to explain ecomorphological divergence in the absence of genetic differentiation. For instance, some authors suggested that the extreme trophic morphotypes in Mesoamerican Astyanax (formerly Bramocharax ) are plastic expressions within a single lineage [17]. Similarly, the body shape in Astyanax and Psalidodon has been shown to be directly influenced by lentic-lotic gradients of their habitats [23]. However, both hypotheses (stable polymorphism and phenotypic plasticity) are difficult to reconcile with the geographical and ecological patterns observed in the species of our study.

First, geographic distributions are discordant: while Asp1 is widespread throughout the Magdalena and Cauca river drainages, A. microlepis is notably absent in the Magdalena River [6,76]. If these forms were merely plastic responses or a localized polymorphism, similar phenotypic ranges would be expected in the whole Magdalena basin. Second, and most importantly, both phenotypes occur syntopically across all sampled habitats in the Cauca River (including lotic, lentic, and artificial reservoir environments; [76]; this study). The fact that A. microlepis and Asp1 maintain their distinct morphologies, despite sharing the same range of habitats suggests a significant heritable component, rather than purely environmentally induced morphs. In summary, while the mitochondrial genome shows lack of differentiation (potentially due to recent introgression or a selective sweep), the persistence of these phenotypes in syntopy points toward a stable evolutionary divergence. Therefore, A. microlepis and Asp1 likely represent separate evolutionary lineages with divergence occurring at the nuclear level, a pattern that requires confirmation through high-resolution genomic tools to fully unravel the history of this complex group. Conversely, the more genetically divergent pair of Asp1 and Asp2 displayed greater overall morphological similarity. Despite their general resemblance, our analysis detected fine-scale yet significant differences, particularly in head length, position of pectoral and adipose fins, and displacement of the origin of the dorsal and anal-fins. This phenomenon, where lineages diverge genetically while their morphology remains constrained, points toward non-mutually exclusive evolutionary mechanisms. An explanation for the conserved morphology in the face of genetic drift is the action of strong stabilizing selection, coupled with niche conservatism [87]. If both lineages inhabit similar ecological contexts, as their sympatric distribution in the Cauca River basin suggests, any significant deviation from an optimal, time-tested body plan would likely be disadvantageous and selected against. Consequently, while neutral genetic markers accumulate differences over time, functional morphology of organisms remains evolutionarily conserved by selection. This process results in the formation of cryptic species, which are genetically distinct but physically almost identical, a pattern increasingly

uncovered in diverse taxonomic groups, including Neotropical freshwater fishes (e.g. [88-90]). Alternatively, the subtle yet significant differences observed in head profile and relative position of fin insertions between Asp1 and Asp2, may reflect divergent evolutionary trajectories resulting from their independent histories, rather than specific adaptations to micro-niches within their shared habitat. Common inherited genetic architecture may impose developmental limitations to respond to selective pressures similarly [91]. Therefore, the morphological similarity between these stocks suggests a conserved body plan, potentially maintained by stabilizing selection or representing a symplesiomorphic condition within the clade. Distinguishing between these scenarios (the retention of ancestral traits [92] versus parallel evolution toward a common optimum [93]) will require broader morphometric analyses. Integrating additional species from the same clade into a phylomorphospace framework would clarify whether this shared morphology is a derived convergence or reflects the ancestral pattern of the group. In conclusion, integrated molecular and morphometric approaches provide compelling evidence for the existence of two distinct evolutionary lineages within Astyanax sp. in the Cauca River. Additionally, the grouping of A. microlepis , A. viejita , and Asp1 into a single mitochondrial lineage, despite their differing geographic distribution and morphological distinctness, introduces a novel hypothesis regarding the taxonomic status and evolutionary history of these species. This study not only challenges traditional taxonomic boundaries but also provides a crucial molecular foundation for future systematic revisions. Ultimately, recognizing these lineages is essential for the design of effective management and conservation strategies, aimed at preserving the ichthyofaunal genetic diversity in the Magdalena and Maracaibo basins.
