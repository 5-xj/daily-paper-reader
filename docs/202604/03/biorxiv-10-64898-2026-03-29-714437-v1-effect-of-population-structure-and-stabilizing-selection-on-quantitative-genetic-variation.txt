Abstract 8

We study one of the simplest scenarios of polygenic selection that can be imagined: 9

a subdivided population of diploid individuals expressing an additive trait under spatially 10 

homogeneous stabilizing selection. We are interested in the amounts of variation that can be 11 

maintained at mutation-selection-migration-drift equilibrium, at individual loci and at the 12 

level of the trait, within and among subpopulations. We derive analytical approximations for 13 

variance components and summary statistics such as FST and QST under the assumptions 14 

of the infinite-island model and compare these with individual-based simulations. We find 15 

that: (i) There is a critical migration threshold (which depends on effect sizes of trait 16 

loci) below which population structure strongly inflates genic variance in the subdivided 17 

population to levels well above those in a panmictic population. Variation within each 18 

subpopulation is maximized close to the critical migration rate. (ii) The genetic basis of 19 

trait variation across subpopulations is most similar close to this migration threshold and 20 

(counter-intuitively) decreases for higher migration rates. This has consequences for the 21 

ÔÇİportabilityÔÇÖ of Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) between subpopulations, i.e, 22 

the extent to which loci with large contributions to variance in one subpopulation explain 23 

variance in other subpopulations. (iii) An analytical mean-field approach based on the single-24 

locus diffusion approximation, together with effective migration and selection parameters (to 25 

account for associations between loci), very accurately predicts various quantities. 26 

# Introduction 27 

Large, outbreeding populations typically harbour high levels of molecular and phenotypic vari-28 

ation. However, we still do not fully understand how such variation emerges from the interplay 29 

between different evolutionary processes (Ch. 28, Walsh and Lynch (2018)). Early work focused 30 

on whether high trait variation might reflect a balance between mutation and stabilizing selec-31 

tion towards an intermediate trait optimum (Lande, 1975; Turelli, 1984; B┬¿ urger et al., 1989). 32 

More recent work suggests that a model of stabilizing selection acting on multiple traits is 33 

broadly consistent with the findings of Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) for a suite 34 

of quantitative traits in humans (Simons et al., 2018, 2025). 35 

This still leaves questions as to how much other factors, such as balancing or fluctuating 36 

selection and spatial population structure, contribute to the maintenance of variation, and 37 

whether observed patterns of variation might be consistent with several alternative evolutionary 38 

histories and scenarios (Barton and Keightley, 2002; Sella and Barton, 2019). Theoretical 39 

models show that selection that varies across time (B┬¿ urger and Gimelfarb, 2002; Bertram and 40

One general mechanism by which spatial population structure can increase genetic variabil-46 

ity is if different subpopulations occupy different ÔÇİadaptive peaksÔÇÖ, i.e., harbour distinct genetic 47 

combinations that are approximately equally fit (Wright, 1935). Alternative fitness peaks might 48 

arise due to spatially heterogeneous selection, e.g., if different trait values are favoured across 49 

different geographic regions or ecological niches. However, they can also arise under spatially 50 

uniform selection, provided that the genetic architecture of selected traits is sufficiently ÔÇİre-51 

dundantÔÇÖ, i.e., if many genetic combinations correspond to approximately the same phenotype 52 

(Goldstein and Holsinger, 1992; Phillips, 1996; Lythgoe, 1997; Barghi et al., 2020). 53 

There has been much interest recently in how such genetic redundancy in adaptive traits 54 

could lead to (dis-)similar genomic signatures of adaptation in natural or experimental repli-55 

cates (Yeaman et al., 2018; Barghi et al., 2019; H┬¿ ollinger et al., 2023). However, all of this 56 

work assumes that replicate populations evolve independently. By contrast, most natural pop-57 

ulations consist of sub-populations connected by historical or ongoing gene flow, which may 58 

cause the genetic basis of trait variation to become more similar across subpopulations, despite 59 

redundancy. This leads us to ask: do the same or different loci contribute to variation across 60 

different subpopulations? And how does this depend on selection and the genetic architecture 61 

of traits on the one hand, and dispersal and demography on the other? Put another way, to 62 

what extent does selection on polygenic traits perturb patterns of allele sharing at trait loci in 63 

a structured population, causing these to stand out from the neutral genomic background? 64 

These questions are relevant to various efforts to predict phenotypes and make inferences 65 

about selection from genomic data while accounting for population structure. For instance, how 66 

well genomic prediction based on GWAS generalises across populations depends crucially on the 67 

extent to which the same genetic variants underlie phenotypic variation across populations with 68 

different ancestry (Duncan et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Yair and Coop, 2022). Similarly, our 69 

ability to distinguish signatures of local vs. global adaptation in genomic data also depends 70 

on whether the same or different alleles underlie (either kind of) adaptation in different sub-71 

populations (Ralph and Coop, 2015; Booker et al., 2021). However, even low levels of gene flow 72 

between populations can qualitatively alter theoretical expectations, which makes it crucial to 73 

model the combined effects of selection and population structure on trait variation. 74 

As a step in this direction, we analyse the simplest possible scenario of a polygenic trait 75 

subject to spatially uniform stabilizing selection across a large number of ÔÇİislandsÔÇÖ or sub-76 

populations connected via migration. We explore the effects of selection and structure on 77 

diversity at individual trait loci as well on the total trait variance, which also depends on 78 

statistical associations (or linkage disequilibria; LD) between trait loci. The goal is to describe 79 

and predict patterns of variation at different ÔÇİscalesÔÇÖ: globally (across the full population) vs. 80 

locally (within sub-populations), and at the locus vs. the trait level. 81 

Previous work on the effect of (uniform) selection and spatial structure on quantitative ge-82 

netic variation is largely simulation-based (Goldstein and Holsinger, 1992; Latta, 1998; Le Corre 83 

and Kremer, 2003, 2012; McDonald and Yeaman, 2018) or, otherwise, derives analytical results 84 

by assuming strong selection and deterministic evolution (Phillips, 1996; Lythgoe, 1997). This 85 

makes it difficult to link theory to patterns in genomic variation, which are significantly affected 86 

by genetic drift. Here, we address this gap by developing detailed analytical theory that ac-87 

counts for the combined effects of mutation, stabilizing selection, drift and population structure 88 

on trait variants, thus allowing for a more comprehensive understanding. Our analysis relies 89

Accounting for LD using effective parameters. 240 

So far, we have neglected the effects of LD between trait loci within demes. However, both 241 

stabilizing selection and migration build up LD, leading to non-independent evolution of trait 242 

loci. In this section, we will discuss each of these two sources of LD, and describe how the 243 

effects of LD on a given locus can be encapsulated via a suitably defined effective selection 244 

coefficient and effective migration rate. These effective rates depend on allele frequencies at all 245 

other trait loci, allowing us to obtain a mean field (i.e., self-consistent) solution for all the allele 246 

frequencies jointly. This approach of representing the effects of LD using effective parameters 247 

is very similar in spirit to the Quasi Linkage Equilibrium (QLE) approximation and applies as 248 

long as the dynamics of LD are much faster than the dynamics of allele frequencies (Kimura, 249 

1965). This, in turn, requires recombination to be much faster than all other processes that 250 

influence single-locus dynamics, which is the case in a model with unlinked loci. 251

increasing (or, equivalently, trait-decreasing) alleles within a deme. This causes the genetic 253 

variance (i.e., the variance of trait values) to be less than the genic variance (Bulmer, 1971). 254

Simulations 362 

We test analytical predictions by comparing against discrete-generation, individual-based sim-363 

ulations which track multi-locus genotypes of all diploid individuals across all demes. In each 364 

generation, the life cycle consists of mutation, followed by selection and mating within demes, 365 

which is then followed by migration. We perform two kinds of simulations: the first with un-366 

derdominant selection on a single locus (Fig. 1), and the second with stabilizing selection on 367 

an additive trait influenced by L loci, where effect sizes at trait loci are either all equal (as in 368 

Fig. 2) or drawn from a distribution (Fig. 3). See also the Model description above. 369 

Simulations are performed with two different kinds of initial conditions ÔÇô we either draw allele 370 

frequencies at each locus in each deme independently from a beta distribution Beta [4 N ╬╝, 4N ╬╝ ], 371 

or first draw the mean allele frequencies {p1, p 2, . . . , p L} at each locus from the beta distribution 372 

and then set the allele frequencies in individual demes to be equal to the respective pi. As 373 

shown in Fig. S5, SI, the time taken for the population to reach equilibrium can be quite 374 

different, depending on the initial conditions (especially at low migration rates), even though 375 

the equilibrium state itself is independent of initial conditions. Simulations are run for D = 100 376 

demes and N = 200 individuals per deme, and a maximum of 10 6 generations, which is found 377 

to be sufficient for equilibration in all cases (including very weak migration), with equilibration 378

# Results 382 

As discussed above, stabilizing selection on a polygenic trait essentially manifests as under-383 

dominant selection on individual trait loci when the trait mean is at the trait optimum. Thus, 384 

as a precursor to analysing the polygenic model, we first consider the behaviour of a single 385 

locus under underdominant selection in the infinite-island setting. We then build upon this 386 

to investigate how variation in the polygenic trait ÔÇô at individual loci (genic variance) and in 387 

trait values (genetic variance) ÔÇô is partitioned within and among demes. This requires us to go 388 

beyond single-locus theory and also account for LD between trait loci, which we do via effective 389 

selection and migration parameters (see Methods). Finally, we investigate questions of direct 390 

empirical relevance: how many and what kind of loci make atypically large contributions to trait 391 

variance, and to what extent such outliers are shared across demes. Throughout, we compare 392 

the results of individual-based simulations with LE and LD predictions, thus clarifying to what 393 

extent LD influences patterns of variation and whether we can describe its effects succinctly in 394 

terms of a handful of effective parameters. 395

# Discussion 736 

This paper investigates how genetic variation is maintained in a polygenic trait under stabilizing 737 

selection in a subdivided population. We show that the diffusion approximation for allele 738 

frequencies, coupled with basic quantitative genetic theory, can accurately predict the combined 739 

effects of selection and structure on quantitative genetic variation, at least under a simple 740 

island model of population structure. Moreover, the effects of LD can be absorbed by effective 741 

migration and selection coefficients, allowing for a succinct description. 742 

How much (more) variation in structured populations? We find that strong population 743 

structure can significantly increase diversity at trait loci across the population as a whole by 744 

allowing different alleles to persist at high frequency in different demes (fig. 2a). However, since 745 

alleles only occur in specific multi-locus combinations (with trait values close to the optimum), 746 

trait variance remains quite constrained and is only modestly increased at intermediate levels 747 

of migration (fig. S6).Thus, under spatially uniform stabilizing selection, (strong) population 748 

structure is mainly a source of cryptic variation that may be ÔÇİunlockedÔÇÖ, e.g., via experimental 749 

crosses between individuals from different subpopulations. 750 

These conclusions are in line with those of other models with two demes (Phillips, 1996; 751 

Lythgoe, 1997; McDonald and Yeaman, 2018) or of continuous space (Goldstein and Holsinger, 752 

1992; Lande, 1991). In addition, our analysis furnishes an analytical prediction for the critical 753 

migration threshold, N m crit , below which trait loci exhibit bistability and high levels of poly-754 

morphism. Under the island model, we find: N m crit ÔëêÔêÜ(╬╝/s )2+2( ╬╝/s )+3( ╬╝/s )2(1 ÔêÆ4( ╬╝/s )) for N s Ôë▓ 1 and 755 

2( ╬╝/s ) < 1/2, where 2( ╬╝/s ) is also the expected heterozygosity at a trait locus in a panmictic 756 

population. Thus, in a scenario where typical heterozygosity per trait locus is (say) Ôë▓ 0.1 in a 757 

panmictic population, N m between demes must be at most Ôê╝ 0.3 for population structure to 758 

significantly increase trait variation. Thus, broadly speaking, the rather weak levels of popula-759 

tion structure found in most natural populations (see, e.g., Morjan and Rieseberg, 2004) cannot 760 

significantly inflate quantitative genetic variation, at least under spatially uniform stabilizing 761 

selection and with island-model migration (i.e., no isolation by distance). 762 

There are two caveats to this. First, we focus on populations at mutation-selection-migration-763 

drift equilibrium , which may not be attainable over biologically relevant timescales. For instance, 764 

imagine a scenario where a set of long-isolated populations (each with its own set of trait alleles) 765 

come into contact. While equilibrium predictions suggest that the same allele should eventually 766 

spread across all subpopulations at any locus of sufficiently large effect, this requires popula-767 

tions to cross a ÔÇİfitness valleyÔÇÖ (arising from selection against rare alleles at any locus), which 768 

can take very long under weak migration (see also Fig. S2). Thus, structured populations may 769 

maintain much more variation than predicted by equilibrium theory. 770 

Second, even though the expected variance contributed by (most) trait loci hardly changes 771 

beyond N m Ôê╝ 2 (see, e.g., Figs. 2b and 3d), the distribution of variance contributions is 772 

sensitive to much higher levels of migration (fig. 4c,d). For instance, in fig. 4d, we see that 773 

the proportion of V (gen ) 

> W

explained by loci with atypically large contributions is very different 774 

for N m = 2 vs. N m = 10. This further supports the idea that spatially restricted sampling of 775 

individuals (e.g., from a local subpopulation that is embedded in a larger population) may give 776 

a rather different picture of trait architecture compared to more widespread sampling (see also 777 

Battey et al., 2020; Steiner et al., 2025), even without local adaptation and under rather weak 778 

population structure. 779

In general, we expect different sets of alleles to be nearly fixed in populations with a long his-785

is actually accelerated by stabilizing selection, which increases the rate at which minor alleles 787 

are lost (Yair and Coop, 2022). As discussed above, migration between populations alters this 788 

expectation, causing the same allele to reach high frequency across all demes, with the critical 789 

migration threshold, N m crit , at which this occurs being lower for loci of larger effect. A second 790 

observation is that while FST at trait loci is always higher than the neutral expectation under 791 

weak migration, this pattern is actually reversed at higher levels of migration (Fig. 3e). 792 

These findings bear upon recent studies that aim to distinguish between alternative modes 793 

of selection (purifying vs. stabilizing vs. none) based on expected allele sharing at putative 794 

QTL between humans of different ancestries (Patel et al., 2024). These studies neglect gene 795 

flow between ancestries, which is known to have occurred (see, for instance Jouganous et al., 796 

2017). This may bias conclusions, since, as we show here, even moderate levels of gene flow can 797 

affect allele sharing (e.g., as measured by FST ) at selected loci (see, e.g., fig. 3e). Moreover, 798 

since sites assayed in GWAS are often not causal themselves (but only in imperfect LD with 799 

the causal locus), gene flow may also affect the strength of LD between the assayed site and the 800 

causal locus it tags, thus further complicating interpretation. 801 

Our results are also relevant to recent work exploring whether introgression between popula-802 

tions adapted to the same trait optimum may be impeded by stabilizing selection in the recipient 803 

population (Veller and Simons, 2024; Ragsdale, 2025). Such selection disfavours descendants 804 

of migrant individuals, that are more phenotypically variable than residents, provided V (gen )

history of nearly complete isolation. 809 

Do the same or different alleles underlie trait variation in different subpopulations? 810 

We find that an allele is more likely to be significantly associated with trait variation across 811 

different subpopulations when migration between populations is moderate (Figs. 4e, 4f). This 812 

is because very low or very high migration between demes drives alleles towards local or global 813 

fixation respectively. This shifts the distribution of per-locus variance contributions to lower 814 

values, making it less likely that a locus will contribute significantly to multiple demes. This 815 

contradicts the general expectation that higher migration between populations should neces-816 

sarily lead to more shared standing variation, thereby increasing parallel adaptation, e.g., in 817 

response to changing environments. Here, we demonstrate that while high migration necessar-818 

ily increases allele sharing (by causing the same alleles to nearly fix) across sub-populations, it 819 

actually decreases shared polymorphism and standing variation (4f). 820 

What does this mean for mapping the genetic basis of variation in extended populations? 821 

Our results suggest that spatially limited sampling of individuals may actually be more useful 822 

(than broad sampling) for identifying trait loci. This is because the typical variance contribu-823 

tions of trait loci tend to be higher within individual subpopulations than across the population 824 

as a whole, especially for loci of large effect (contrast, e.g., Figs. 4a,b with fig. S3). This 825 

phenomenon is well known in the context of GWAS, where alleles significantly associated with 826 

trait variation in one population are usually not globally significant. This limits the ÔÇİportabil-827 

ityÔÇÖ of so-called polygenic scores (and other methods of genomic prediction) across populations 828 

(Duncan et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020). As found by Yair and Coop, 2022, stabilizing selection 829

Extensions and open questions. We consider a simple model of selection in which fitness 860 

depends on a single polygenic trait under spatially uniform stabilizing selection. However, in 861 

reality, genetic variants may affect multiple traits, some of which may be subject to spatially 862 

uniform and others to divergent selection. Such a scenario, while highly relevant to evolutionary 863 

inference, remains under-explored in theoretical work (though see Guillaume and Whitlock 864 

(2007); MacPherson et al. (2015)). In particular, we lack analytical predictions for how selection 865 

on multiple correlated traits and gene flow between populations together shape genetic variances 866 

and covariances across traits and loci. The diffusion approximation can be readily extended to 867 

allow for a more general relationship between the selection coefficient at a given locus and its 868 

effect on a focal trait (see, e.g., Simons et al. (2018)). Thus, the theory developed here provides 869 

a natural starting point for investigating migration-selection equilibrium across many traits. 870 

We also make various simplifying assumptions about trait architecture, namely that trait loci 871 

are unlinked, have additive effects (no phenotypic dominance) and that there is no mutation bias 872 

(so that expected trait means coincide with the trait optimum). The last assumption is crucial, 873 

since biased mutation (say, towards trait-increasing alleles) would cause the trait mean to deviate 874 

from the trait optimum, thus generating directional selection (in addition to underdominance) 875

Finally, we consider a very simple island model of population structure, which has the 881 

advantage of being analytically tractable, thus allowing for detailed prediction. Such many-882 

deme models represent at least as (un-)realistic a reference point for natural populations as 883 

two-deme models, which underlie the bulk of theory on selection in structured populations. 884 

However, both many and two-deme models fail to capture the complexity of selection across 885 

continuous space, wherein allele frequencies change over a characteristic spatial scale determined 886 

by the balance between dispersal and selection per locus (Barton, 1999). For weakly selected 887 

loci, this spatial scale may be comparable to that associated with neutral isolation-by-distance 888

SI.b Effective migration rate at a trait locus. 979 

In this section, we derive an expression for the effective migration rate meff at a site that is 980 

unlinked to any trait locus. This derivation follows the basic argument in Surendranadh and 981 

Sachdeva (2025), which we recapitulate here for completeness. 982 

In general, meff < m if immigrants and/or their descendants have lower fitness, on average, 983 

than the residents in any given deme (where ÔÇİresidentsÔÇÖ will be defined more precisely below). In 984 

this case, immigrant alleles are selected against, even if they are neutral, due to their association 985 

with less fit genetic backgrounds. However, as long as the focal allele is unlinked to any selected 986 

locus, such associations break down over Ôë▓ 10 generations ÔÇô much faster than the timescales 987 

that govern single-locus dynamics (which depend on 1 /m , 1 /s , N , etc.). Thus, we can describe 988 

the effect of indirect selection arising from these transient associations simply by substituting 989 

an effective migration rate (instead of the raw migration rate) into the single-locus equations 990 

for allele frequency dynamics (Sachdeva, 2022). 991 

In the limit m ÔåÆ 0, the ratio meff /m is simply equal to the average reproductive value of 992 

migrants (Kobayashi et al., 2008), which is their long-term contribution to the gene pool of the 993 

recipient population, relative to that of residents. Alternatively, meff /m can be thought of as 994 

the factor by which the fixation probability of a neutral migrant allele is reduced relative to 995 

that of a neutral resident allele. Further progress can be made by noting that under random 996 

mating, the probability of mating between individuals with recent migrant ancestry is O(m2), 997 

and thus, can be neglected for m Ôë¬ 1. Thus, in this limit, migrant individuals mate primarily 998 

with residents to produce F 1 hybrids, which mate primarily with residents to produce first-999 

generation backcrosses and so on. 1000
