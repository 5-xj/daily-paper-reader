# Abstract 17 DNA is the largest biopolymer in nature, and chromatin contact maps are widely interpreted 18 as quantitative readouts of its three-dimensional organization. However, the validity of such 19 interpretations critically depends on how these maps are processed. 20 Here, we identify a previously overlooked but fundamental source of bias in chromatin 21 contact data analysis. We demonstrate that a widely adopted preprocessing convention, 22 namely whole-matrix percentile clipping, systematically distorts sparse contact maps by 23 collapsing their dynamic range. This effect is strongest in near-diagonal interactions, 24 precisely the regime encoding chromatin domains and looping structures, thereby 25 compromising quantitative interpretation while preserving superficial structural features. 26 We show that this distortion represents a sparsity-dependent failure mode of current 27 preprocessing standards and affects the comparability of datasets and computational 28 methods across technologies and sequencing depths. To address this, we introduce a 29 statistically consistent preprocessing framework based on nonzero-percentile clipping and 30 log-space normalization, which preserves the intrinsic dynamic range of observed contacts. 31 Building on this foundation, we present CCUT, a modular deep learning framework for 32 chromatin contact map reconstruction. Under corrected preprocessing, reconstructed maps 33 recover domain organization, contact decay, and scaling behavior consistent with polymer 34 physics. Importantly, we demonstrate quantitative agreement between reconstructed maps 35 and simulated contact patterns derived from a kinetic Monte Carlo loop extrusion model, 36 enabling direct comparison between experimental data and physical models. 37 Together, our results establish preprocessing as a decisive determinant of the physical 38 interpretability of chromatin contact maps and provide a principled framework for robust and 39 comparable analysis across chromatin conformation capture technologies. 40 41 Keywords: Chromatin Architecture; polymer organization, Deep Learning; Pore-C; Super-42 resolution; Genome Organization, loop extrusion, preprocessing bias 43

# Introduction 44 Chromatin contact maps are widely interpreted as quantitative readouts of genome 45 organization, yet their physical interpretability critically depends on statistical preprocessing 46 choices that are rarely examined. Despite their interpretation as physical measurements of 47 an underlying polymer system, the statistical processing of these data has not been 48 systematically aligned with this interpretation. 49 Conformation Capture techniques (3C) such as Hi-C have become fundamental tools for 50 investigating higher-order genome organization, enabling the mapping of structures that 51 influence gene expression through their three-dimensional configuration in the nucleus 52 (Lieberman-Aiden et al., 2009; Rao et al., 2014; Rao et al., 2017; Zhong et al., 2023). Such 53 methods measure the interaction frequencies between different loci in the genome and can 54 be used to locate specific structures such as A/B compartments, chromatin loops, or 55 topologically associated domains (TADs) in the genome. These secondary DNA structure 56 features can influence gene expression via their spatial configuration within the nucleus 57 (Zhong et al., 2023). More recent methods such as Micro-C (Krietenstein et al., 2020) allow 58 for a more fine-grained picture of the spatial organization of chromatin in the nucleus. The 59 reconstruction of the three-dimensional arrangement of these components is essential for 60 deciphering the relationships between chromatin organization and genetic function 61 (Krietenstein et al., 2020; Lieberman-Aiden et al., 2009; Rao et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2022; 62 Zhong et al., 2023). Despite the advances achieved using these approaches, 3C-based 63 methods still face obstacles in the characterization of higher-order chromatin structures. 64 These limitations largely derive from canonical short-read sequencing biases: nonuniform 65 coverage across GC-rich or GC-poor loci, diminished detection of large structural 66 rearrangements, and ambiguous read alignment within repetitive DNA (Browne et al., 2020; 67 Roberts et al., 2021; Weissbach et al., 2021). These challenges are discussed in detail in 68 excellent reviews by (Li & Durbin, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Pore-C aims to close this gap 69 and has been used extensively to generate new insights by leveraging Oxford Nanopore 70

Its major drawback 80 however is the cost of nanopore sequencing associated with the need for deep coverage in 81 3C based experiments. 82 Current computational enhancement methods implicitly inherit preprocessing conventions 83 that were originally established for dense Hi-C datasets, without re-evaluation for sparse 84 data regimes, namely a preprocessing pipeline originating in DeepHiC (Hong et al., 2020): 85 Knight-Ruiz-balancing followed by clipping at a fixed threshold of 255, a value corresponding 86 to the approximate 99.9th percentile of KR-balanced 10 kb contact counts that is specific to 87 this specific dataset generated in the GM12878 cell line (Rao et al., 2014). This threshold 88 was adopted by HiCARN (Hicks & Oluwadare, 2022), HiCSR (Dimmick et al., 2020), and 89 ScHiCAtt (Menon et al., 2025). HiCDiff (Wang & Cheng, 2024) and SHICEDO (Huang et al., 90 2025) differ in their assumptions: HiCDiff uses percentile clipping on ICE balanced matrices 91 and SHICEDO adopts the distance stratified BandNorm, that is specific to single cell 3C 92 experiments. While this convention might be reasonable for Hi-C, where count distributions 93 are relatively stable across experiments, it is not statistically appropriate as a general 94 strategy. As we demonstrate below, 3C technologies differ dramatically in their count 95 distributions: Pore-C contact matrices are substantially sparser in comparison to Hi-C or 96 Micro-C contact matrices, with a higher proportion of zero entries and their non-zero count 97 distributions are highly sensitive to sequencing depth. 98

5Applying whole-matrix percentile clipping under these conditions creates a shifted 99 distribution with high zero counts and thus shifts the clipping thresholds to a relatively small 100 value. This represents a sparsity-dependent failure mode, in which the dominance of zero 101 entries shifts percentile thresholds into the bulk of the observed signal. The clipping value 102 scales with the sparsity of the matrix, which in turn depends on the sequencing technology 103 and depth. This means that a preprocessing protocol optimized for one experiment type will 104 systematically distort data from another, and that performance comparisons between 105 methods are confounded whenever preprocessing choices differ. 106 Compressing contact counts into a narrow range is a practical requirement for min-max 107 scaling to [0, 1], which empirically stabilizes neural network training. The clipping can 108 therefore serve as a workaround for high dynamic ranges and heavy-tailed distributions of 109 raw contact data. Nonetheless, this workaround comes at the cost of discarding a big 110 proportion of the signal structure and it makes a lossless or near-lossless reversal to original 111 count space essentially impossible. We resolve this by introducing a log-space normalization 112 pipeline that uses nonzero-percentile clipping at p99.95, followed by a log1p transform, after 113 which per-chromosome min-max scaling to [0, 1] is applied using per-chromosome 114 percentiles and maxima. The resulting predictions are thus directly interpretable in terms of 115 the original contact counts. 116 Here, we demonstrate that preprocessing is a decisive determinant of chromatin contact 117 map fidelity and introduce a framework that restores their physical interpretability. First, we 118 characterize how whole-matrix clipping severely degrades pixelwise similarity metrics in 119 matched comparisons and is specifically harmful to the near-diagonal signal by flattening the 120 near diagonal values, representing close range interactions. Nonzero-percentile clipping, 121 which restricts the percentile calculation to observed contacts and thereby preserves the 122 natural dynamic range of the data, eliminates this distortion and yields results consistent with 123 raw unclipped matrices. Second, we introduce the Chromatin Capture Upsampling Toolbox 124 (CCUT), a modular deep learning framework centered on HiCNet, a hierarchical network 125 architecture adapted for chromatin contact matrix enhancement. We also note an 209 apparent anomaly in the SSIM results: the cross-preprocessing comparison of whole-clipped 210 downsampled versus raw high-resolution matrices yields SSIM of 0.993, higher than the 211 matched whole-clipped comparison (0.918). This is a known artefact of SSIM's sensitivity to 212 dynamic range (Dosselmann & Yang, 2011). The stability constants in the SSIM formula 213 scale with the observed data range L; whole-matrix clipping compresses L severely, making 214 these constants small and magnifying the relative contribution of any residual difference 215 between matrices. This behavior illustrates why cross-preprocessing SSIM comparisons are 216 unreliable and further complementary metrics should be considered. 217

# Discussion 416 Our results demonstrate that preprocessing is not a technical detail, but a determinant of 417 whether chromatin contact data can be interpreted as physically meaningful measurements. 418 Computational enhancement methods offer a practical route to reducing these costs, but 419 their development has been shaped by preprocessing conventions that were established for 420 specific experimental contexts. To our knowledge, these methods have not been 421 systematically re-evaluated as the field has expanded to sparser data types. Here, we 422 address this gap by systematically evaluating how preprocessing choices affect contact 423 matrix fidelity and enhancement benchmarking, and by introducing CCUT, whose predictions 424 are consistent with polymer models of chromatin organization. 425 

Preprocessing conventions are technology-specific 426 The preprocessing pipeline that has dominated the field since its introduction normalizes 427 contact matrices by clipping at a fixed percentile or hardcoded maximum, followed by min-428 max scaling to the unit interval. This convention was developed and validated on dense Hi-C 429 datasets where the fraction of zero entries is moderate and the whole-matrix count 430 distribution is reasonably well-behaved compared to Pore-C. Under these conditions, whole-431 matrix percentile clipping produces a threshold that falls sensibly within the tail of the 432 nonzero count distribution, and the resulting distortion of the dynamic range is limited. The 433 strong performance reported by existing methods on these datasets reflects both genuine 434 model quality and the fact that the preprocessing artefacts we characterize here are 435 relatively mild when sparsity is low. 436 We observe that with increasing matrix sparsity zero entries start to dominate the count 437 distribution, as they do in Pore-C data where up to 94.6% of bins are unobserved at typical 438 sequencing depths. The whole-matrix percentile thresholds impact is highly influenced by 439

As we 440 demonstrate, this produces clipping thresholds that fall orders of magnitude below those 441 obtained by restricting the percentile calculation to observed contacts for all three 442 sequencing paradigms, especially for Pore-C. The consequence is not simply a cosmetic 443 change to the matrix appearance but that the near-diagonal high-count entries that encode 444 TAD structure and chromatin loops are truncated to near-zero. A model trained to map a 445 clipped-to-noise low resolution matrices onto equally clipped high-resolution targets is bound 446 to learn a noisy chromatin structure representation. 447 This sparsity-dependence explains why the problem went unnoticed in the Hi-C 448 enhancement literature. On the datasets where these methods were developed and 449 benchmarked, the distortion of metrics like SSIM and insulation scores is small enough that 450 models still learn useful mappings and metrics still reflect genuine improvement. Importantly, 451 this effect is not specific to Pore-C, but reflects a general sparsity-dependent limitation of 452 percentile-based normalization strategies. It is only when the same conventions are applied 453 to substantially sparser data as sparse, deeply downsampled matrices or technologies like 454 Pore-C, that the failure becomes severe. Nonzero-percentile clipping resolves this by 455 anchoring the threshold to the observed contact distribution regardless of sparsity, making 456 the preprocessing robust across technologies and sequencing depths. 457 

Whole-matrix clipping affects pixelwise but not structural metrics 458 Whole-matrix clipping selectively degrades pixelwise similarity metrics while leaving higher-459 order structural features intact. Pixelwise metrics as PCC and SSIM are severely degraded 460 in matched comparisons, while higher-order structural metrics including insulation score 461 correlation, TAD boundary F1, and contact decay correlation remain essentially unchanged 462 across preprocessing conditions. This dissociation has implications in two directions. On the 463 one hand, it suggests that biological conclusions drawn from studies using whole-matrix 464 clipping are likely robust, since the spatial organization of the genome as reflected in domain 465 boundaries and compartment structure is preserved for experiments like Hi-C even when the 466 absolute contact values are heavily distorted. If instead a fixed constant is used, the model implicitly learns a 501 scaling relationship between LR and HR rather than a true structural reconstruction, since 502 the LR input is not compressed into the same dynamic range as the HR target. In 508 CCUT, we normalize each matrix using per-chromosome statistics derived from that matrix 509 alone, applied consistently during both training and inference. We believe that this is one 510 possible approach, but we note that a rigorous empirical evaluation of the downstream 511 effects of different inference-time normalization strategies and their interaction with model 512 generalization across sequencing depths remains an open problem that the field would 513 benefit from addressing systematically. 514

Normalization Pipeline and Invertibility 605 Raw contact count matrices exhibit heavy-tailed distributions driven by a small number of 606 very high-frequency near-diagonal entries. Applying a log transform directly to unclipped 607 counts does not resolve this: extreme values are compressed but not removed, and minor 608 differences in log space correspond to large differences in count space upon inversion, 609 which destabilizes reconstruction. A clipping step prior to the log transform is therefore 610 necessary. 611 For each chromosome, we compute the 99.95th percentile of nonzero entries only, treating 612 zero-valued bins as structural absences rather than observations. The full matrix is then 613 clipped at this threshold. This nonzero-percentile strategy ensures that the clip threshold 614 reflects the actual observed contact distribution rather than the zero mass, which would 615 otherwise dominate the percentile calculation and collapse the threshold into the bulk of the 616 nonzero signal. By construction, clipping at the 99.95th nonzero percentile retains 99.95% of 617 observed contact information exactly, with only the top 0.05% of nonzero entries truncated to 618 the threshold value; we therefore characterize this step as near-lossless. 619 Following clipping, we apply a log1p transform, to the clipped matrix. This compresses the 620 dynamic range of the nonzero signal into a well-conditioned range suitable for neural 621 network training while mapping zero entries exactly to zero. Each chromosome is then 622 scaled by its per-chromosome maximum of the log1p-transformed matrix, producing values 623 in [0,1]. Because the minimum of the log1p-transformed clipped matrix is always zero, this 624 reduces to a division by the per-chromosome maximum; no minimum subtraction is required. 625 This pipeline admits a well-defined inverse. Given a model prediction in [0,1], the inverse 626 transform proceeds as: multiply by the stored per-chromosome maximum, apply expm1, i.e. 627 exp(x)−1, and clip any negative values to zero to handle floating-point deviations. The result 628 is a reconstructed matrix in the original count space, accurate up to the 99.95th nonzero 629

Hyperparameter Optimisation 688 Hyperparameters for HiCNet were optimized using Optuna (Akiba et al., 2019) with 30 trials. 689 The search space covered architecture parameters (feature width and network depth), 690 generator and discriminator learning rates, loss component weights for pixel loss, structure 691 consistency loss, insulation loss, and distance decay loss, loss function type (L1, MSE, or 692 Huber), GAN configuration (enabled or disabled, gradient penalty weight, label smoothing 693 coefficient), batch size, and early stopping patience. Within each trial, training was 694 terminated by early stopping monitored on validation SSIM. The trial achieving the highest 695 validation SSIM at the point of early stopping was selected as the final model configuration. 696

Pore-C 747 Pore-C library preparation was performed following the restriction enzyme Pore-C protocol 748 from Oxford Nanopore Technologies with minor modifications 749 (https://nanoporetech.com/document/extraction-method/restriction-pore-c). Briefly, 750 approximately 5 × 10  cells were crosslinked with 1% formaldehyde and the reaction was 751 quenched with glycine. Cells were permeabilized and chromatin was denatured prior to in 752 situ restriction digestion using NlaIII. Digestion was carried out at 37 °C for 18 h to fragment 753 chromatin while preserving spatial proximity of interacting loci. Following digestion, 754 restriction enzymes were inactivated and proximity ligation was performed using T4 DNA 755 ligase at 16 °C to generate concatemers representing higher-order chromatin interactions. 756 Proteins were degraded by Proteinase K treatment and crosslinks were reversed. DNA was 757 subsequently purified by phenol–chloroform extraction followed by ethanol precipitation, 758 resuspended in TE buffer, and quantified using the Qubit dsDNA HS assay.
