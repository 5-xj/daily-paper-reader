Abstract 24 

Palytoxin, first isolated from Palythoa toxica , is among the most potent marine toxins known. 25 

Despite decades of biochemical investigation, genetic bases underlying its potential biosynthesis 26 

in Palythoa remain unresolved. Here we present four high-quality genome assemblies of 27 

Palythoa species, including Palythoa cf. toxica , and integrate these with a chromosome-scale 28 

genome assembly of P. caribaeorum . Performing comparative genomic analyses, we screened 29 

for candidate genes potentially involved in palytoxin biosynthesis and examined patterns of 30 

genome evolution. Unexpectedly, we identified only two classes of ketosynthase (KS) 31 

domain-containing genes in Palythoa : fatty acid synthases (FAS) and bacterial-like polyketide 32 

synthases (PKSs). Contrasting other anthozoans, animal FAS-like PKS (AFPK) genes common 33 

to all Palythoa species were not detected. We found no evidence for lineage-specific expansion 34 

of PKS genes unique to Palythoa , suggesting that if palytoxin/palytoxin-like molecule 35 

biosynthesis is host-encoded, it may involve functional modification or co-opting pre-existing 36 

FAS and/or bacterial-like PKS pathways. Comparative analyses revealed expansions of gene 37 

families associated with transport and binding functions in Palythoa , potentially reflecting 38 

molecular adaptations linked to their sand-incorporating body structure. We identified TPT1 and 39 

CLEC4A as rapidly evolving genes in multiple Palythoa species, consistent with possible roles 40 

in growth regulation and host-microbe interactions. Additionally, comparison between 41 

azooxanthellate and zooxanthellate species revealed mutations within conserved protein 42 

domains of LePin, which has been implicated in cnidarian endosymbiosis, suggesting 43 

lineage-specific modifications associated with symbiotic state. This study establishes a44 

foundation for zoantharian genomic research, provides insights into lineage-specific genomic 45 

signatures, and advances molecular and evolutionary biological knowledge of this ecologically 46 

important group. 47

Introduction 48 

Palytoxin is one of the most potent sea toxins known and was first isolated from Palythoa toxica 49 

(Cnidaria: Anthozoa) in 1971 (Moore and Scheuer 1971). With a reported median lethal dose 50 

(LD 50 ) of 25–450 ng/kg in several animal models (Wiles et al. 1974) and an exceptionally large 51 

and complex molecular structure (Moore and Bartolini 1981, Uemura et al. 1981), palytoxin 52 

represents an extreme example of secondary metabolite biosynthesis in marine organisms. Such 53 

structurally complex compounds are typically synthesized by polyketide synthases (PKSs), 54 

multifunctional enzymes that assemble carbon chains through iterative condensation reactions, 55 

with β-ketoacyl synthase (KS) domains serving as their core catalytic components (Cane et al. 56 

1998, Rein and Borrone 1999). Although PKS genes and animal fatty acid synthase-like PKS 57 

(AFPK) genes have been widely identified in marine metazoans (Torres et al. 2020, Li et al. 58 

2023, Lin et al. 2024) and in toxin-producing marine microorganisms (Verma et al. 2019, Van 59 

Dolah et al. 2020, Fallon et al. 2024), systematic investigation of PKS repertoires in Palythoa 60 

remains limited. 61 

Zoantharians are an order belonging to Hexacorallia, an ancient and ecologically 62 

important cnidarian lineage that also includes sea anemones (Actiniaria) and reef-building 63 

corals (Scleractinia). Molecular studies have suggested that hexacorals originated in the 64 

Cryogenian (~711 Ma), considerably earlier than previously estimated (McFadden et al. 2021). 65 

Although genome assemblies are now available for multiple actiniarian and scleractinian 66 

species (Shinzato and Yoshioka 2024), greatly advancing our understanding of anthozoan 67 

genome evolution (Shinzato et al. 2021, Salazar et al. 2022, Zhou et al. 2023, Zimmermann et al. 68 

2023), high-quality genomic resources and genomic studies for zoantharians remain 69 

comparatively sparse (Fourreau et al. 2023, Santos et al. 2023, Yoshioka et al. 2024), limiting 70 

broader evolutionary inferences across Hexacorallia. 71 

Zoantharians are globally distributed and occupy diverse habitats ranging from 72 

shallow coastal reefs to the deep sea (Reimer et al. 2014, Santos et al. 2026). Most zoantharian 73 

species incorporate sand grains and detrital particles into their tissues, a distinctive trait thought 74 

to reinforce their body structure (Haywick and Mueller 1997, Reimer et al. 2010). Ecologically, 75 

zoantharians are often divided into two suborders. Macrocnemina species span a broad depth 76 

range and frequently engage in symbioses with a variety of marine organisms (Kise et al. 2023), 77 

whereas Brachycnemina are predominantly shallow water taxa, most of which harbor 78 

photosynthetic dinoflagellates of the family Symbiodiniaceae (Reimer et al. 2011). Within 79 

Brachycnemina, Palythoa and Zoanthus are among the most abundant and conspicuous genera 80 

on coral reefs, where Palythoa species can exert strong ecological influences (Irei et al. 2015, 81 

Reimer et al. 2023) and, in some cases, produce palytoxin and/or palytoxin-like molecules 82 

(42-hydroxy-palytoxin) (Deeds et al. 2011, Ciminiello et al. 2014, Aratake et al. 2016, Fraga et 83

Recently, genome sequences of two closely related, azooxanthellate Palythoa species 85 

(P. mizigama and P. umbrosa ; Irei et al. 2015) revealed unique genomic features associated 86 

with life in low-light reef caves and the loss of algal symbiosis (Yoshioka et al. 2024). However, 87 

it remains unclear which genomic characteristics are broadly shared across Palythoa spp. and 88 

which are specific to azooxanthellate lineages. Although we previously identified genes 89 

containing KS domains in azooxanthellate Palythoa genomes, detailed analyses were not 90 

performed. 91 

Here we present high-quality whole-genome assemblies and gene models for two 92 

zooxanthellate Palythoa species, Palythoa cf. toxica (Ptox) and Palythoa sp. sakurajimensis 93 

(Psak), together with improved genomes of the azooxanthellate species, P. mizigama (Pmiz) and 94 

P. umbrosa (Pumb) (Fig. 1). Combined with a chromosome-scale genome assembly of Palythoa 95 

caribaeorum (Pcar) from the Wellcome Sanger Institute (2025), we perform comparative 96 

genomic analyses to identify genomic signatures of Zoantharia, an early branching lineage 97 

within Anthozoa. By examining gene family expansions, putative gene losses, and candidate 98 

biosynthetic genes, this study provides new insights into zoantharian genome evolution and 99 

establish a genomic framework for understanding symbioses, ecological adaptation, and the 100 

genetic basis of palytoxin-associated traits. 101

Materials and methods 103 

Sample preparation, DNA extraction, and long read sequencing 104 

Ptox and Psak were collected between September 16-17, 2023 from reefs around Iriomote 105 

Island, Okinawa, Japan, at depths of ~20 m and 5 m, respectively, and maintained for 106 

approximately twenty months in aquaria at the Yaeyama Station, Fisheries Technology Institute 107 

(Okinawa, Japan). For DNA extraction, a single polyp was collected from each colony, cut into 108 

small pieces using a razor blade, and briefly dissociated with Trypsin/EDTA (T4049, 109 

Sigma-Aldrich) for 5 min at room temperature. After digestion, the suspension was centrifuged 110 

at 500 × g for 5 min, and the resulting pellet was washed with artificial seawater filtered through 111 

0.22 μm membranes (FSW). High molecular weight DNA was extracted from the pellet using 112 

the MagAttract HMW DNA kit (Qiagen) following the manufacturer’s protocol. Sequencing 113 

libraries were prepared using the Pacific Bioscience HiFi DNA protocol for Psak and the HiFi 114 

ultra-low input DNA protocol for Ptox. Libraries were sequenced on the PacBio Revio systems 115 

(Pacific Bioscience). 116 

In addition, to improve previously reported gene models of Pumb, polyps of the 117 

species were also collected and processed in the same manner as the other two species from a 118 

specimen collected on September 16, 2023 from Nakano Beach, Iriomote Island at 5 m depth. 119

De novo genome assembly 129 

HiFi reads potentially originating from non-cnidarian organisms were identified with FCS-GX 130 

v0.5.4 (Astashyn et al. 2024) and were removed from further steps. Mitochondrial genomes 131 

were first assembled from the filtered HiFi reads using the MitoHiFi pipeline v3.2.1 132 

(Uliano-Silva et al. 2023). Reads mapping to mitochondrial genomes were subsequently 133 

removed prior to nuclear genome assembly. 134 

Contaminants- and mitochondria-free HiFi reads were assembled using Hifiasm 135 

v0.23.0-r691 (Cheng et al. 2021). Primary contigs generated by Hifiasm were used for 136 

downstream analyses. Haplotigs were removed using a combination of the kmerDedup pipeline 137 

(https://github.com/xiekunwhy/kmerDedup) and purge_dups v1.2.5 (Guan et al. 2020). 138 

Assembly polishing was performed using Inspector v1.3 (Chen et al. 2021) with filtered HiFi 139 

reads. 140 

Assemblies were evaluated using BlobTools v1.1.1 (Laetsch and Blaxter 2017). Contigs 141 

classified as taxa other than “Cnidaria” or “no-hit” were considered as non-Palythoa sequences 142 

and excluded from further analyses. Genome completeness was assessed using BUSCO v6.0.0 143 

(Tegenfeldt et al. 2025) with the “metazoa_odb12” dataset ( n = 672). Genome size was 144 

estimated using the backmap.pl v0.6 pipeline (Pfenninger et al. 2022), in which filtered HiFi 145 

reads were mapped back to the draft assembly using minimap2 v2.26-r1175 (Li 2018) with the 146 

option (-ax map-hifi). 147

Protein-coding gene annotation 167 

RNA-seq reads were quality trimmed using FASTP v1.0.1 (Chen et al. 2018) with a minimum 168 

read length of 50 bp. Filtered reads were assembled de novo using TRINITY v2.15.1 (Grabherr 169 

et al. 2011) with default parameters. To remove low-abundance transcript, reads were mapped 170 

back to the transcriptome assembly, and only concordantly mapped reads were retained as in 171 

(Seiko et al. 2025). These reads were aligned to the genome assembly using HiSAT v2.2.1 (Kim 172 

et al. 2015). 173 

Gene prediction was performed using BRAKER pipeline v3.0.8 (Hoff et al. 2019, Gabriel 174 

et al. 2021, Bruna et al. 2024), with soft-masked genomes, RNA-seq alignments, and Metazoa 175 

OrthoDB v12 as input. Gene models were further refined using PASA v2.5.2 (Haas et al. 2003) 176 

with genome-guided transcriptome assemblies generated by TRINITY. Genes with CDS length 177 

greater than 100 bp were retained. Gene identifies were assigned sequentially based on contig 178 

position (e.g., c0001.g001 is the first gene of contig c0001). Gene model completeness was 179 

assessed using BUSCO v6.0.0 with the “metazoa_odb12” dataset. RNA-seq data used for gene 180 

prediction are listed in Table S2. 181

Orthogroup classification and gene duplication analysis 183 

For each species, the longest CDS per gene was selected and translated into protein sequences 184

(2022). Orthogroups (OGs) were inferred using OrthoFinder v2.5.5 (Emms and Kelly 2019). 186 

Duplicated genes were classified as dispersed, proximal singleton, tandem, or whole genome 187 

duplication (WGD)/segmental using MCScanX v1.0.0 (Wang et al. 2012). 188

Results and Discussion 248 

High quality genome assemblies and gene models of Palythoa species 249 

We generated 50 Gb and 66 Gb of PacBio HiFi reads for Psak and Ptox, with mean read lengths 250 

of 5,433 bp and 5,599 bp, respectively (Table S1). The resulting draft genome assemblies 251 

spanned 881 Mb for Psak and 649 Mb for Ptox, with N50 values of 12.2 Mbp and 0.82 Mbp, 252 

respectively (Table 1; Fig. S1). Genome size estimates based on read depth were 819.3 Mb for 253 

Psak and 624.8 Mb for Ptox, indicating that the assemblies closely approximated the expected 254 

genome sizes (Fourreau et al. 2023). In addition to these newly assembled genomes, we curated 255 

previously published genome assemblies of the azooxanthellate Palythoa species Pmiz and 256 

Pumb by removing contigs likely derived from contaminants (Fig. S2). BUSCO completeness 257

for Pumb were also improved by incorporating transcriptomic data, resulting in 22,498 261 

protein-coding genes. BUSCO completeness of the gene models was 86.2% (1.6% duplicates) 262 

for Psak, 75.0% (2.2% duplicates) for Ptox , 86.2% (1.8% duplicates) for Pcar, and 77.8% (1.2% 263

Molecular phylogeny and genome-wide comparison among anthozoans 271 

Using 1,887 single-copy orthologs, we reconstructed a maximum likelihood phylogeny of 272 

anthozoans (Fig. 1d). Consistent with previous analyses based on ultra-conserved elements 273 

(McFadden et al. 2021), zoantharians were recovered as the sister group to a clade comprising 274 

Actiniaria, Antipatharia, Corallimorpharia, and Scleractinia. Within Palythoa , phylogenetic 275 

relationships inferred from nuclear single copy orthologs were identical to those obtained using 276 

the mitochondrial rrnL marker, resolving three distinct clades: clade I (Ptox), clade II 277 

(azooxanthellate Pmiz and Pumb), and clade III (Psak and Pcar) (Fig. S4). This topology is 278 

congruent with previous phylogenetic reconstructions of zoantharians (e.g. Swain 2018). 279 

Genome size varied substantially among anthozoans, including within Palythoa (Table 1; 280 

Table S2). Although more than 100 anthozoan genome assemblies are now publicly available 281 

(Shinzato and Yoshioka 2024), comparative analyses of genome-wide statistics across 282 

anthozoans remain limited. Previous work has suggested a correlation between assembly size 283 

and transposable element (TE) content in zoantharians (Fourreau et al. 2023). Extending this 284 

analysis across anthozoans, we found that genome size was strongly correlated with total TE 285 

content ( R = 1, p < 0.01) and moderately correlated with average gene length ( R = 0.7, p < 0.01) 286 

(Fig. 2). In contrast, neither protein-coding gene number nor average CDS length showed a 287 

significant correlation with genome size. These results indicate that expansion of non-coding 288 

regions, including intron and intergenic sequences, is a major contributor to genome size 289 

variation and genomic diversity in anthozoans. This evolutionary trend is often observed in 290 

animals (Suetsugu et al. 2013, Glick et al. 2024), suggesting that anthozoan genomes also 291 

behave in the same manner. 292

Genomic expansion events in Palythoa 294 

To infer the transposition history of TEs, Kimura substitution levels were calculated for all TE 295 

copies in each genome (Fig. S5). Lower Kimura distances indicate more recent TE activity, 296 

whereas higher values reflect ancient transposition events. Clade I species (Ptox) displayed 297 

three pronounced peaks at Kimura distances of substitution levels, clade II species (Pmiz and 298 

Pumb) showed a diffuse pattern without clear peaks, and clade III (Psak and Pcar ) exhibited a 299

gradual decline with a peak at low divergence levels. Relatively high numbers of recent TE 300 

copies were commonly detected in all Palythoa clades, suggesting that genome size increases 301 

with TE amplification occurred relatively recently. In Hydra , studies of transposon-driven 302 

genome expansions are well studied, and expansion of TE elements produces genomic diversity 303 

(Kon-Nanjo et al. 2025), suggesting that expansion of TE elements also contributes to the 304

Gene duplication also contributed to genome expansion, although to a lesser extent than 306

among anthozoans (Table S3). Psak, Pumb, Pmiz, and Pcar harbored 22,448 genes, whereas 308 

Ptox encoded 44,006 genes—nearly, twice the average (Table 1). Furthermore, the proportion of 309 

intron-containing genes was markedly lower in Ptox (53.2%) than in other Palythoa species 310 

69.8–78.9% (Table 1), suggesting extensive duplication of intron-less genes. Analysis of 311 

duplication mode revealed that dispersed duplications predominated (48–59%) in all Palythoa 312 

genomes, with Ptox exhibiting approximately twice as many dispersed duplications as other 313 

species (Fig. 3a). The wide spread of dispersed duplicates has been considered to be due to 314 

mechanisms mediated by transposons (Wang et al. 2012). Most duplicated gene pairs showed 315 

Ks values below the median Ks of single copy orthologs among hexacorals (Fig. 3b), indicating 316 

that the majority of duplication events occurred after Palythoa diversification. 317

Gene family evolution specific to Palythoa 319 

To investigate the molecular basis of zoantharian evolution, we examined gene family evolution 320 

across anthozoans. From 366,162 protein-coding genes, 93.6% were assigned to 26,532 321 

orthogroups (OGs) (Table S4). Among these, 5,473 OGs were conserved across hexacorals, 342 322 

OGs were specific to zoantharians, and 16 OGs were absent from zoantharians (Table S5). 323 

Comparison of copy numbers for the 5,473 conserved OGs revealed that 49 OGs were 324 

significantly reduced and 76 OGs were significantly expanded in zoantharians relative to other 325 

hexacorals ( p < 0.05; Figure 4a; Table S6). Given that scleractinian corals exhibit elevated gene 326 

numbers due to lineage-specific tandem duplications (Noel et al. 2023), reduced OG sizes in 327 

zoantharians do not necessarily reflect gene loss. In contrast, expanded OGs in zoantharians 328 

likely represent lineage-specific evolution. Functional enrichment analysis showed that 329 

expanded gene families were enriched for transport-related functions (e.g., “monoatomic anion 330 

transmembrane transport”, “cargo receptor activity”, and “water channel activity”) and 331

Among the 342 zoantharian-restricted OGs, 289 (85%) were present as single copies or 341

were retained, indicating that Palythoa species likely maintain the capacity to synthesize all 352 

essential amino acids. Compared with our previous study based on fragmented genomes and 353 

uncurated transcriptomes (Yoshioka et al., 2024), the present results differ in some details. 354 

Nevertheless, the loss of a gene homologous to spermatogenesis-associated protein 7 ( SPATA7 ), 355 

involved in photoreceptor ciliary function in mammals (Dharmat et al. 2018), was consistently 356 

detected, supporting its genuine absence in zoantharians. In addition, the visual pigment-like 357 

receptor peropsin (RRH ) was also inferred to be lost, suggesting partial loss of 358 

photoreceptor-related functions across Palythoa , irrespective of symbiotic status. 359

are responsible for synthesizing saturated fatty acids from acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA, were 365 

identified in all examined hexacoral species but were not detected in the sampled Palythoa . A 366 

single AFPK gene was identified in Pmiz, whereas no AFPK genes were detected in the other 367 

Palythoa species. Most cnidarian FAS and AFPK proteins contained canonical domain 368

Clade-specific evolutionary changes within Palythoa 381 

We identified 890, 107, and 484 and OGs exclusive to clade I (Ptox), clade II (Pmiz and Pumb), 382 

and clade III (Pcar and Psak), respectively (Table S9). These OGs contained 143, 586, and 3,006 383 

genes, respectively. The majority of these genes lacked functional annotation: 83.9% (120 384 

genes) in clade I, 75.1% (440 genes) in clade II, and 84.1% (2,529 genes) in clade III. Notably, 385 

9.3% of clade III-specific genes encoded proteins with reverse transcriptase (RVT) domain, 386 

including transcriptase and nuclease activities (Fig. S9; Table S9), suggesting lineage-specific 387 

expansion, particularly in Ptox. 388 

Although OGs exclusive to each clade were inferred to have arisen independently, 389 

homologous Pfam domains were often shared across clades, indicating convergent evolution. 390 

For example, genes containing RVT_1 (Pfam entry: PF00078) and rve (Pfam entry: PF00665) 391 

domains were abundant in both clades II and III (Fig. S9; Table S9). Expansions of 392 

transcriptase- and nuclease-related genes may have contributed to increased gene numbers 393 

through RNA-mediated duplication mechanisms (Kaessmann et al. 2009). Interestingly, 394 

extraction of HMW DNA or high-quality RNA from Palythoa species is challenging, and 395 

despite using standard protocols, mean HiFi read lengths were relatively short in this study (see 396 

Table S1). The presence of multiple nuclease genes may contribute to rapid nucleic acid 397 

degradation, as reported in planarians (Li et al. 2025), highlighting a potential target for 398 

improving extraction methods in zoantharians. 399

Conclusions 452 

We present high-quality genome assemblies and gene models for four Palythoa species, 453 

providing a comprehensive comparative genomic framework for zoantharians. Our analyses 454 

show that genome size variation among anthozoans is driven primarily by the expansion of 455 

intergenic regions and introns. Zoantharians exhibit lineage-specific expansions in transport-456 

and cytoskeleton-associated gene families, as well as many genes of unknown function. 457 

PKS-related genes are present but do not show Palythoa -specific expansion, suggesting that if 458 

palytoxin/ palytoxin-like biosynthesis is host-derived, it likely involves modification of 459 

pre-existing enzymatic pathways. Clade-specific gene duplication, gene loss, and rapid 460 

sequence evolution point to distinct evolutionary trajectories associated with symbiotic state and 461 

habitat specialization. Together, these genomic resources provide a foundation for future 462 

functional, ecological, and biochemical studies aimed at understanding secondary metabolite 463 

production, symbiosis evolution, and adaptive diversification in cnidarians. 464
