Abstract 26 

Microsatellites are abundant genomic elements that contribute to genetic diversity and disease-27 

associated regulatory variation. Although long-read sequencing enables accurate resolution of repetitive 28 

regions, computational methods for fully resolved microsatellite genotyping remain limited. Here, we 29 

introduce variant motif where (vmwhere), a computational framework for identifying, genotyping, 30 

decomposing, and visualizing complex tetrameric microsatellites from long-read sequencing data. Using 31 

simulated error-free reads, vmwhere accurately measures several genotyping metrics, including allele 32 

length, repeat length, maximum consecutive repeat length, and motif density. Applied to long-read 33 

whole-genome sequencing data, vmwhere identified sequence interruptions, motif-specific differences in 34 

repeat architecture, and ancestry-associated allele variation, including long repeat alleles that exceed 35 

short-read sequencing limitations. We applied vmwhere to GGAA microsatellites in Ewing sarcoma, an 36 

aggressive pediatric cancer driven by EWS-FLI1 fusion oncoprotein, which binds to microsatellites and 37 

remodels chromatin. Genome-wide integration of long-read-defined microsatellite architecture with 38 

chromatin accessibility and EWS-FLI1 binding revealed that GGAA repeat structure was associated 39 

with chromatin state, with longer consecutive repeat microsatellites exhibiting increased EWS-FLI1 40 

binding and chromatin accessibility. Cell line–specific expansions and contractions of GGAA 41 

microsatellite repeat length were associated with gains and losses of chromatin accessibility. Further, we 42 

identified haplotype-specific chromatin states, with preferential binding and accessibility at longer 43 

alleles. Together, these results establish vmwhere as a scalable framework for resolving population-level 44 

microsatellite variation and linking repeat architecture to chromatin state. Repeat structure and length 45 

characteristics provides insights into genotype–function relationships at microsatellite repeats in cancer. 46

Introduction 48 

Microsatellites are tandemly repeated DNA motifs 1–6 bp in length that are abundant and widely 49 

dispersed throughout the human genome. Variation in microsatellite length is a major source of genetic 50 

diversity and structural variation, and expansions at specific loci have been implicated in numerous 51 

human diseases (Gymrek et al. 2016; Mirkin 2007). Microsatellites are highly polymorphic among 52 

human populations, with mutation rates exceeding those of standard nucleotide substitutions (Ellegren 53 

2000; Willems et al. 2016) and common allele frequency differences across ancestries. To date, 54 

population-scale genetic studies have largely relied on short-read sequencing, which limits analysis to 55 

shorter (< 80 bp) and uninterrupted microsatellite repeats (Ichikawa et al. 2023; Ziaei Jam et al. 2023). 56 

As a result, complex repeat structures, including motif interruptions, sequence variants, and longer 57 

repeat alleles, remain under characterized, constraining our understanding of how microsatellite 58 

variation contributes to population diversity, disease susceptibility, and chromatin states 59 

The release of the complete Telomere-to-Telomere (T2T) reference genome has markedly improved the 60 

annotation of repetitive regions, enabling more accurate mapping of expected repeat coordinates and 61 

generalized internal structures (Aganezov et al. 2022; Nurk et al. 2022). Although many existing 62 

microsatellite genotyping algorithms were originally developed for short-read sequencing (Chiu et al. 63 

2021; Dashnow et al. 2018, 2022; Dolzhenko et al. 2019, 2020; Gymrek et al. 2012; Mousavi et al. 64 

2019; Willems et al. 2017), the emergence of long-read sequencing technologies has opened new 65 

opportunities for repeat analysis. Long-read sequencing overcomes key limitations of short-read 66 

approaches, including read-length constraints and mapping ambiguity, and is PCR-free, reducing 67 

technical artifacts such as stutter (Gymrek 2016). Continued improvements in base-calling accuracy 68 

offer increasingly reliable sequence-resolved characterization of complex repeats using long-read data 69 

(Mahmoud, Agustinho, and Sedlazeck 2025). These advances have created a need for specialized 70 

computational frameworks capable of handling long, interrupted, and highly polymorphic microsatellite 71 

alleles. Recent tools such as TRGT, LongTR, ATaRVa, and Straglr (Chiu et al. 2021; Dolzhenko et al. 72 

2024; Sivakumar et al. 2025; Ziaei Jam et al. 2024) have begun to address some of these algorithmic 73 

gaps, yet opportunity remains for improved resolution of repeat structure and allelic diversity. 74 

Ewing sarcoma (EwS) offers a compelling biological context in which a specific microsatellite has been 75 

linked to chromatin state and gene regulation. EwS is a pediatric bone and soft tissue malignancy driven 76 

by the EWS-FLI1 fusion oncoprotein (Delattre et al. 1992). Through the FLI1-derived DNA binding 77 

domain, the oncoprotein is directed to canonical ETS DNA binding sites containing a central GGAA 78 

sequence. However, the oncoprotein gains neomorphic activity and is also directed to GGAA containing 79 

microsatellites, where it remodels chromatin and creates de novo regulatory elements  (Gangwal et al. 80 

2008; M. Patel et al. 2012; Riggi et al. 2014).  Limited studies at loci near EwS-associated genes have 81 

shown that sequence interruptions and repeat architecture, rather than repeat count alone, can alter EWS-82 

FLI1 binding and transcriptional activity. At the 6p25 susceptibility locus, targeted long-read sequencing 83 

revealed extensive polymorphism in GGAA repeat length across populations, with both repeat length of 84 

both the primary and interrupting sequence correlating with EwS risk (O. W. Lee et al. 2023). Similarly, 85 

a microsatellite near EGR2 showed that single-nucleotide substitutions can alter repeat architecture, 86

However, they have yet to explore whether such relationships extend genome-wide, nor how allele-91

limitation of prior genome-scale analyses is their reliance on reference-derived repeat length 93

Consequently, systematic genome-wide analyses integrating read-defined microsatellite architecture 95

To explore these relationships, we developed variant motif where (vmwhere), a computational 97 

framework for identification, sequence-resolved genotyping, and decomposition of complex tetrameric 98 

microsatellites from long-read sequencing data. We benchmark vmwhere against existing tools using 99 

simulated long-read data and apply it to the top 10 most frequent tetrameric repeat motifs, spanning 100 

176,778 loci in 100 genetically diverse individuals from the 1000 Genomes Project (Gustafson et al. 101 

2024), to characterize population-level variation in repeat length and structure . We then focus 102 

specifically on GGAA microsatellites in EwS cell lines, integrating repeat length and sequence 103 

architecture with chromatin accessibility and EWS-FLI1 binding. Together, this work establishes a 104 

scalable framework for resolving population-scale microsatellite variation and demonstrates how 105 

sequence-resolved repeat architecture at GGAA microsatellites influences chromatin state in EwS. 106 

Results 107 

Computational pipeline for tetrameric microsatellite identification and genotyping from long-read 108 

sequencing 109 

We developed variant motif where (vmwhere) as a modular pipeline to identify, genotype, and visualize 110 

tetrameric microsatellites from long-read sequencing data (Figure 1). vmwhere pipeline (Figure 1A) 111 

comprises three core modules, find, genotype, and visualize, that together enable sequence-resolved 112 

characterization of microsatellite alleles at genome scale. 113

The find module defines candidate tetrameric microsatellite loci coordinates from a reference genome 124 

sequence for a user-specified 4-mer motif (Figure 1B). Tandem repeats exceeding a minimum repeat 125 

threshold are detected, nearby occurrences are merged into a single locus based on a user-defined 126 

distance, and extended genomic coordinates (tandem repeat start/stop coordinates +/- additional 127 

buffer/flanking sequence before and after repeat) are output for downstream genotyping and integration 128 

with functional genomics data. Importantly, the reference is used to define the candidate coordinates for 129 

genotyping analysis, not for repeat length annotations. 130

each user provided locus. Briefly, this module performs several key steps (Figure 1B). Reads are filtered 132

Benchmarking vmwhere against existing long-read genotyping tools on simulated data 142 

To assess genotyping accuracy, we benchmarked vmwhere against four open-source long-read repeat 143 

genotyping tools, Straglr (Chiu et al. 2021), LongTR  (Ziaei Jam et al. 2024), ATaRVa (Sivakumar et al. 144 

2025), and TRGT  (Dolzhenko et al. 2024), using simulated, error-free long-read data generated from 30 145 

tetrameric microsatellite loci present in the reference genome (Figure 2). Benchmark inclusion criteria 146 

were tools that are actively maintained, infer repeat length directly from base called read sequences, 147 

operate on reference-defined genomic coordinates, and do not require haplotype-resolved or phased 148 

BAM inputs. Accuracy was evaluated against simulated truth for multiple genotyping metrics, including 149 

allele length, repeat length, maximum consecutive repeat length, and motif density (Figure 2A). 150

Genotyping sensitivity was 100% for vmwhere, TRGT, and ATaRVa, and 97% for LongTR and Straglr. 160 

vmwhere achieved the highest accuracy for exact allele length (100%), outperforming TRGT (95%), 161 

ATaRVa (90%), LongTR (81,7%), and Straglr (65%) (Figure 2B). With a ±1 bp tolerance, TRGT 162 

reached 96.6% accuracy, and ATaRVa reached 93.3%. Notably, vmwhere, ATaRVa, and LongTR 163 

correctly resolved allele-specific repeat deletions in non-reference alleles, whereas TRGT and Straglr 164 

defaulted to homozygous reference calls, and may therefore underestimate deletions rates at 165 

microsatellites. 166 

For repeat length estimation, TRGT showed the highest exact match accuracy (96.7%), followed by 167 

vmwhere (95%) and Straglr (21.7%) (Figure 2C). Though TRGT and vmwhere offer similar accuracy 168 

for repeat length estimation, only vmwhere detects deletions of repeat sequences. Straglr’s reduced 169 

performance could be explained by its reliance on Tandem Repeats Finder, which is optimized for 170 

uninterrupted repeats and exhibits lower performance on alleles containing frequent sequence 171 

interruptions. TRGT was developed for PacBio HiFi data and embeds error-aware models optimized for 172 

HiFi read characteristics. As a result, performance in this benchmark may differ from that reported in the 173 

original study, which evaluated TRGT on empirical PacBio datasets rather than idealized, error-free 174 

simulations. 175 

vmwhere uniquely reports maximum consecutive repeat length and motif density , metrics derived 176 

directly from sequence decomposition. Compared to simulation truth, vmwhere identified maximum 177 

consecutive repeat number with 96.7% accuracy (Figure 2D), and motif density showed near-perfect 178 

concordance with ground truth (R² = 0.993) (Figure 2E). These features enable discrimination between 179 

pure and interrupted alleles and are not accessible through existing genotyping tools. 180 

Genome-wide identification of tetrameric microsatellites in the T2T human reference 181 

Using vmwhere find, we systematically catalogued tetrameric microsatellite loci in the T2T-CHM13v2 182 

human reference genome. We first defined a set of 42 unique tetrameric microsatellite sequences 183 

according to four criteria: (a) non-homopolymeric (e.g., AAAA excluded); (b) combining of reverse 184 

complements (e.g., GGAA and TTCC considered equivalent); (c) exclusion of dinucleotide repeats (e.g., 185 

ATAT); and (d) exclusion of phased permutations of the same sequence (e.g., GGAA distinct from 186 

GAAG, AGGA, and AAGG). vmwhere find applied to the T2T reference genome identified 176,778 187 

tetrameric microsatellite loci with at least four perfect repeats, with repeats within 100 bp merged into a 188

To benchmark sensitivity, we compared vmwhere identified repeat microsatellite loci to those defined by 191

and RM may not detect shorter tetrameric microsatellite repeat loci. 196 

Consistent with prior analysis of other human reference genomes (Subramanian, Mishra, and Singh 197

intragenic regions, subsets showed bias (e.g., ATCG and ACGT confined to intragenic regions; ACAG, 200 

AGCC, CTGG, and AAACT enriched in intergenic regions). 201 

For downstream population-scale analyses, we restricted subsequent analyses to the 10 most frequent 202 

tetrameric repeats, which occurred at 154,468 loci, accounting for 87.4% of all tetrameric microsatellite 203 

loci in the T2T genome. 204 

Tetrameric microsatellites exhibit widespread sequence interruptions and variable motif density 205 

First, we applied vmwhere at the previously annotated EwS-associated, highly polymorphic, 6p25 206 

GGAA microsatellite loci (Machiela et al. 2018). vmwhere identified 18 distinct alleles across the 207 

cohort, all of which were previously observed (O. W. Lee et al. 2023) and resolved internal sequence 208 

interruptions within longer GGAA repeat segments (Figure 3A). The three most common alleles 209 

matched prior reports, validating both repeat length estimates and internal sequence decomposition 210 

genotyping capabilities of vmwhere on real world long-read data. 211

We next applied vmwhere to whole-genome Oxford Nanopore Technology (ONT) sequencing data from 226 

100 individuals from the 1000 Genomes Project, spanning five global superpopulations (AFR, AMR, 227 

EAS, EUR, SAS) (Gustafson et al. 2024). All population-scale analyses were restricted to loci 228 

corresponding to the top 10 most frequent tetrameric motifs identified in the reference as noted 229 

previously. 230 

Genome-wide decomposition of alleles revealed that many tetrameric microsatellites exhibited 231 

discontinuous architectures (e.g., 4AGAT–2GGAT–4AGAT; three segments) rather than contiguous 232 

tandem repeats (e.g., 8GGAA; one segment). Segment counts varied by canonical sequence: AAAC 233 

repeats were frequently uninterrupted, whereas ATGG, GGAA, and AGAT loci were commonly 234 

interrupted, often comprising two to three distinct segments (Figure 3B). An example of a complex 235 

microsatellite dominated by imperfect repeat alleles illustrated the structural complexity that sequence-236 

interrupted microsatellites can display within a population (Supplementary Figure 2A). Consistent with 237 

this pattern, longer alleles more frequently exhibited discontinuous architectures, reflecting increased 238 

sequence interruption with repeat length. 239

We next examined how motif density changed as a function of total repeat length across canonical repeat 260 

sequences. AAAG motifs maintained the highest motif density with increasing repeat length, whereas 261 

AATC and AAAC repeats showed a sharp decline in density as repeat length increased (Figure 3C). 262 

These patterns indicated that core sequence continuity was dependent on both repeat motif and allele 263

Overall, these results indicated that interruptions to the core sequence were common across tetrameric 265 

microsatellite motifs and frequently affected a substantial proportion of alleles, highlighting interruption 266 

structure as a major contributor to allelic variation in microsatellite architecture. 267

Discussion 458 

In this study, we present vmwhere, a comprehensive pipeline to identify, genotype, decompose, and 459 

visualize tetrameric microsatellites from long-read sequencing data. Using simulated long-read data, we 460 

benchmarked vmwhere against four publicly available genotyping tools and demonstrated high accuracy 461

across multiple metrics, including allele length, repeat length, maximum consecutive repeat length, and 462 

motif density. Application of vmwhere to population-scale long-read data further revealed substantial 463 

sequence heterozygosity and ancestry-associated allelic variation at tetrameric microsatellite loci, 464 

extending prior observations limited by short-read sequencing limitations. 465 

Across the genome, GGAA microsatellites emerged as a distinct class of tetrameric repeats, 466 

characterized by bimodal length distributions and enrichment of long, consecutive repeat lengths. 467 

Leveraging this sequence-resolved framework in EwS, we identified a genome-wide association 468 

between maximum consecutive GGAA repeat length and chromatin state, specifically chromatin 469 

accessibility and EWS-FLI1 binding. Accessibility and binding increased above a threshold of 470 

approximately 11 consecutive repeats, and allele-specific analyses demonstrated preferential EWS-FLI1 471 

occupancy and chromatin accessibility on the longer allele at heterozygous loci. Finally, we observed a 472 

small number of GGAA microsatellites undergoing sample-specific expansions or contractions. These 473 

loci were associated with corresponding gains or losses in chromatin accessibility, linking consecutive 474 

repeat length variation to changes in chromatin state in EwS. 475 

Long-read sequencing provides an opportunity to study microsatellite variation in ways that were 476 

previously inaccessible. Beyond enabling improved genotyping through PCR-free library preparation 477 

and the ability to span entire repeat loci within single reads, long-read data support targeted, locus-478 

focused interrogation of microsatellites. Our population-scale analyses indicate that longer and 479 

interrupted tetrameric alleles often occur at low frequencies and are therefore likely underrepresented in 480 

standard whole-genome datasets. Deep, targeted interrogation of microsatellite loci may be required to 481 

capture this variation robustly. Long-read sequencing strategies that support targeted enrichment, such as 482 

adaptive sampling on Oxford Nanopore platforms, offer a practical means to achieve the depth necessary 483 

to resolve rare alleles and multiallelic loci. Such approaches have the potential to uncover previously 484 

overlooked microsatellite variation with functional relevance, including variation that may contribute to 485 

disease susceptibility (Miller et al. 2021). Applied at scale across diverse populations, these strategies 486 

could enable systematic investigation of how microsatellite architecture shapes genetic diversity. 487 

Notably, one limitation of our population-scale variation study is the reliance on the T2T-CHM13v2 488 

reference genome, which represents a single individual. Future incorporation of pangenome reference 489 

may better capture ancestry-specific microsatellite diversity. 490 

Our results support the current conceptual shift in the broader tandem repeat field. Rather than 491 

representing microsatellites by a single repeat length metric, features such as maximum consecutive 492 

repeat length and internal sequence interruptions emerge as distinct and potentially biologically 493 

informative properties. Decomposition of read-defined microsatellite alleles into contiguous sequence 494 

segments reveals architectural and allele specific variation that is not captured by reference-derived 495 

length annotations. This perspective has important implications for genome-wide association studies, in 496 

which disease-associated single-nucleotide variants frequently overlap complex and discontinuous 497 

repeat loci and may alter repeat architecture rather than acting independently (Grünewald et al. 2015; 498 

Postel-Vinay et al. 2012). More broadly, studies of population-scale tandem repeat variation have 499 

demonstrated that uninterrupted repeat length of the primary sequence and length of sequence 500

Methods 526 

vmwhere algorithm and implementation 527 

vmwhere is freely available as a Python package via PyPl or at GitHub (https://github.com/pirl-528 

unc/vmwhere). The software is implemented in Python and R and provides a command-line interface 529 

with three subcommands: find, genotype, and visualize. Installation instructions and usage guidelines 530 

are available in the GitHub repository documentation. 531 

find module 532 

The vmwhere find module identifies microsatellite loci by performing an exact string-based search for a 533 

user-specified motif within a reference FASTA sequence. For each contig, the algorithm scans the 534 

reference sequence for exact matches to the motif on both the forward strand and its reverse 535 

complement. Each detected motif match serves as a seed position from which the algorithm extends 536 

sequentially in motif-length increments to identify consecutive, perfectly matched repeat units. 537 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted April 10, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.08.717017 doi: bioRxiv preprint

Extension terminates when the next motif-length window no longer matches the motif sequence. The 538 

start and end coordinates of the maximal contiguous repeat block are recorded, along with the total 539 

number of repeat units detected. Only exact (perfect) motif matches are counted on this first pass of the 540 

algorithm. 541 

After scanning the full reference, repeat loci containing fewer than a user-defined minimum number of 542 

perfect repeats are dropped. Remaining repeat loci are consolidated through a merging process that 543 

combines loci whose coordinates fall within a specific maximum gap distance, producing a single 544 

consolidated microsatellite region. Following merging, a user-defined buffer is added upstream and 545 

downstream of each merged locus to capture local flanking sequence context. Final merged regions are 546 

assigned unique identifiers and reported as BED-formatted coordinates representing buffered 547 

microsatellite loci. 548 

genotype module 549 

The vmwhere genotype module performs microsatellite genotyping by analyzing sequencing reads 550 

aligned to user-specified loci and characterizing the structural composition of each allele. User input 551 

consists of BED-formatted coordinates (typically output from the find module), an aligned BAM file, 552 

and the reference FASTA sequence. 553 

For each microsatellite locus, the algorithm retrieves all reads overlapping the target region plus user-554 

defined flanking buffer sequence. Reads are filtered to retain only primary alignments, excluding 555 

secondary and unmapped reads. The algorithm extracts the aligned portion of each read spanning the 556 

locus and determines the microsatellite boundaries within that sequence by identifying regions flanked 557 

by at least two consecutive perfect motif repeats at both ends. 558 

Within the identified microsatellite-containing region, the algorithm decomposes the sequence into a 559 

structural representation by distinguishing between perfect motif matches and intervening non-motif 560 

sequences. For each motif-length window, the algorithm checks for exact matches to the motif or its 561 

reverse complement. Consecutive perfect motifs are collapsed into a single block annotated by repeat 562 

count and motif sequence (e.g., "5GGAA"). Non-motif intervening sequences are further classified: 563 

sequences ≤4 bp or consisting of a single nucleotide (homopolymers) are annotated as -is, single 564 

nucleotide variants of the motif are annotated with the variant sequence, and longer complex sequences 565 

are recursively decomposed by minimizing edit distance to candidate repeat units or retained as unique 566 

sequences if no clear pattern is identified. The resulting structural representation is a concatenated string 567 

describing the full allele composition (e.g., "3GGAA_1GGAT_2GGAA"). 568 

For each locus, reads with identical structural representations are grouped together, and the read support 569 

for each unique allele structure is quantified. The algorithm computes allele frequency as the proportion 570 

of total read support at that locus. Alleles are called using a two-tier frequency threshold approach: if 571 

any allele exceeds a user-defined homozygous threshold (default 0.8), only alleles above this threshold 572 

are retained; otherwise, all alleles meeting a lower heterozygous threshold (default 0.20) are retained. 573

The genotype module employs parallel processing to analyze chromosomes concurrently. Final 576 

genotyping results are output in a tab-separated format where each row represents a single microsatellite 577 

locus and includes genomic coordinates, reference allele structure, alternate allele structures, genotype 578 

call, allele lengths, total repeat length, maximum consecutive repeat length, and read support metrics for 579 

each called allele. 580 

visualize module 581 

The vmwhere visualize module generates graphical representations of allele frequency and structure at 582 

individual microsatellite loci. Input consists of the tab-separated output from vmwhere genotype 583 

containing all samples of interest, a unique region identifier, and a minimum allele frequency for 584 

filtering. 585 

Allele structure is displayed as a horizontal stacked bar plot in which each bar represents a unique allele 586 

and colored blocks indicate sequence composition. Allele frequency at the locus is represented by gray 587 

bars positioned to the left of each allele structure. This visualization enables rapid assessment of allelic 588 

diversity, structural variation, and relative abundance of microsatellite variants within a cohort. 589 

Simulated benchmark 590 

Simulated loci and read generation 591 

Error-free long-reads were simulated at 30 tetrameric microsatellite loci identified using the vmwhere 592 

find module applied to the T2T-CHM13v2 reference genome. For each locus, two distinct alleles were 593 

simulated to capture both repeat length variation and sequence-level interruptions. Alleles differed in 594 

motif repeat number as well as in the presence and pattern of interrupting sequence motifs within the 595 

microsatellite sequence. All microsatellites contained a minimum of one four consecutive repeat 596 

segments per the reference defined sequence. Microsatellite bounds were defined as the first and last 597 

occurrence of the motif repeat where 2 or more repeats occur within 20 base pairs of a core defined 598 

microsatellite. 599 

Simulated reads were generated to a total depth of 30× per locus, with 15× coverage per allele. Each 600 

read spanned the full microsatellite allele sequence and included 200 bp of flanking sequence on each 601 

side of the repeat region. Reads were written to FASTQ format with uniformly high base quality scores 602 

to represent error-free long-read data. 603

Inferred alleles were matched to the two simulated alleles using a minimum absolute difference 626

The vmwhere find module was applied to the T2T-CHM13v2 reference genome to identify tetrameric 653 

microsatellite loci. Loci containing a minimum of four consecutive repeats were retained and repeat 654 

occurrences within 100 bp were merged into a single microsatellite locus. For population-scale analyses, 655 

the top ten most frequent tetrameric repeat motifs were selected: AAAC, ATTC, AATG, AAAT, ACAT, 656 

AGGG, AAAG, GGAA, AGAT, and ATGG. 657 

Microsatellite genotyping across all samples was performed using the vmwhere genotype module with 658 

default algorithm parameters. By default, allele sequences were clustered based on sequence similarity, 659 

with alleles within an edit distance of four grouped together. Within each cluster, the allele supported by 660 

the highest number of reads was retained as the representative allele. Alleles were required to meet a 661 

minimum allele frequency threshold of 0.2, and loci with ≥80% support for a single allele were 662 

classified as homozygous. No assumptions of diploidy were imposed. These default settings were 663 

applied uniformly across all loci and samples. 664 

As part of genotyping, vmwhere performed sequence decomposition of each allele into contiguous 665 

segments based on changes in sequence composition across the repeat sequence. Segments were defined 666 

such that any change in repeat sequence, regardless of whether the sequence was canonical or non-667 

canonical, initiated a new segment. As a result, alleles could contain multiple segments alternating 668 

between canonical and non-canonical repeat motifs. The number of segments per allele was defined as 669 

the total count of these distinct sequence blocks across the full allele sequence. 670 

Genotyping results from all samples and all loci corresponding to the top ten motifs were concatenated 671 

for downstream population-scale analyses. For each microsatellite locus, allele counts and frequencies 672 

were calculated across the cohort. Singleton alleles—defined as alleles supported by a single read 673 

structure at a given locus—were excluded from population-level analyses to reduce spurious allelic 674 

calls. 675

Haplotype resolved alignment and read processing 750 

A haplotype-resolved de novo assembly was generated from long-read whole-genome Oxford Nanopore 751 

sequencing data of Ewing cell line A673 using hifiasm (v0.25.0) (Cheng et al. 2021, 2022, 2024). Contig 752 

names were verified to be unique, and the two haplotypes were concatenated into a single FASTA file to 753 

serve as a combined haplotype reference. 754 

A673 ATAC-seq and EWS-FLI1 ChIP-seq FASTQ reads were aligned to the combined haplotype 755 

reference using bwa-mem2 (Vasimuddin et al. 2019). Resulting alignments were sorted by read name 756 

using samtools, and samtools fixmate was applied to ensure proper pairing of paired-end ATAC-seq 757 

reads (ChIP seq reads are single end, so this step is skipped). PCR duplicates were marked and removed 758 

using samtools markdup, and BAM files were indexed with samtools index. 759 

Reads were filtered to retain only primary, high-confidence alignments by excluding secondary, 760 

supplementary, unmapped, and quality-control–failed reads, and by requiring a minimum mapping 761 

quality of 20. To ensure allele-specific assignment, multi-mapped reads were removed by retaining only 762 

reads with an NH tag equal to 1. For ATAC-seq data, filtered BAM files from biological replicates were 763 

merged, sorted, and indexed prior to downstream analyses. ChIP seq for EWS-FLI1 and IP did not 764 

contain replicates. 765

To quantify allele-specific chromatin accessibility and EWS-FLI1 binding at GGAA microsatellites, 767 

microsatellite loci were first identified directly in the haplotype-resolved de novo assembly. The 768 

vmwhere find module was applied to the combined haplotype FASTA generated by hifiasm using 769 

default parameters (minimum of four consecutive GGAA repeats, with repeat occurrences within 100 bp 770 

merged into a single microsatellite). For each GGAA microsatellite, the center of the repeat array was 771 

determined, and a fixed window extending ±250 bp from the center was defined for read quantification. 772 

Allele-specific read counts were obtained from the merged replicate, combined haplotype BAM files 773 

using featureCounts (Liao, Smyth, and Shi 2014). Because both the microsatellite coordinates and the 774 

aligned reads were represented in haplotype-specific contig space, read counts could be assigned 775 

independently to each haplotype. For ChIP-seq data, EWS-FLI1 binding signal was normalized using 776 

the corresponding input (IP) control reads. 777
