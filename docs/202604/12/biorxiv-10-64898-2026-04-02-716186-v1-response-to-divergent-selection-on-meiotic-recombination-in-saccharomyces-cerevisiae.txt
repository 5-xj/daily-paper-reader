# Abstract 23 Meiotic recombination is a key driver of evolution in sexually reproducing organisms, reshaping 24 genetic diversity by generating novel allelic combinations. The rate of recombination varies sub-25 stantially across living organisms depending on cis - or trans -acting genetic elements, as seen in 26 many species, including the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae . Here, we report on an experimental 27 evolution-based study to better understand the factors shaping this natural variation. Starting with a 28 genetically diverse population of S. cerevisiae , we have carried out recurrent divergent selection on 29 recombination rate using a fluorescence-based sorting approach in four independent lineages. After 30 ten generations, we observed an average response of recombination rate of +28% after positive se-31 lection and -24% after negative selection, within the interval used for selection. # Introduction 43 Meiosis is considered one of the major evolutionary innovations of eukaryotes. The associated ge-44 netic recombination is at the heart of many evolutionary processes, both under natural selection 45 (Burt and Bell 1987) and in the genetic improvement of domesticated species (Hill and Robertson 46 1966; Tourrette et al. 2019). Crossovers (COs) occurring during prophase of the first meiotic divi-47 sion have two major consequences: they generate a physical link between homologous chromo-48 somes, which allows a balanced segregation of homologs at anaphase I, required for the viability of 49 the gametes, and they shuffle the parental haplotypes to produce novel allelic combinations in the 50 recombinant gametes. During meiotic prophase I, a large number of programmed DNA double-51 strand breaks are produced on the chromatids. They are then repaired by several possible pathways 52 which use the homolog as a template — leading to the formation of a minority of COs, which are 53 reciprocal exchanges of genetic material between homologs, and a majority of non-crossovers, 54 which are non-reciprocal short tract exchanges producing gene conversions (GCs), or which use the 55 sister chromatid as a template with no genetic consequence. Two distinct pathways have been 56 demonstrated to exist in many organisms for CO formation (Higgins et al. 2004; Housworth and 57 Stahl 2003; Falque et al. 2009), one of them being sensitive to the phenomenon of interference that 58 reduces the probability of two COs occurring close to each other (Sturtevant 1915), and the other 59 one being insensitive to interference. 60 CO number and distribution along chromosomes are strongly regulated (Pan et al. 2011). The 61 number of COs per chromosome usually ranges between one and three, and is similar across spe-62 cies, in spite of very large differences in physical chromosome sizes (Fernandes et al. 2017). distribution of COs along chromosomes, often referred to as the CO landscape, is usually uneven, 64 e.g. in yeast (Mancera et al. 2008), which is due to a combination of different factors, including in-65 terference (Morgan et al. 2024). Large pericentromeric regions are almost void of COs in many or-66 ganisms (Haenel et al. 2018), and some very small regions can have high densities of COs 67 (hotspots). CO regulation shows several similar features in various species, see review in 68 (Dluzewska et al. 2018). Among them, (1) CO assurance (Jones 1984) ensures that at least one CO 69 occurs per bivalent during meiosis, allowing faithful chromosome segregation, (2) CO homeostasis 70 (Martini et al. 2006) keeps the rate of COs almost constant even if the number of double-strand 71 breaks is reduced, (3) CO interference (Sturtevant 1915; Morgan et al. 2024) leads to COs being 72 more regularly spaced than if they were placed at random, (4) heterochiasmy (Sardell and Kirkpat-73 rick 2020) leads to different levels and landscapes of recombination in male vs female meiosis, and 74 (5) natural genetic variation of CO rate distribution is observed within many species (Kong et al. 75 2010; Bauer et al. 2013; Brekke et al. 2023; Johnston 2024). Finally, the recombination rate (hereaf-76 ter noted RR) also shows some phenotypic plasticity, for instance as a response to variation in tem-77 perature (Lloyd et al. 2018) or nutritional state (Abdullah and Borts 2001; Novak et al. 2026). 78 There are two types of genetic determinants of CO formation: cis DNA sequences that modulate 79 RR locally in their vicinity and thus change the recombination landscape, and some trans factors 80 that affect RR genome-wide. Both contribute to the natural variability of RR, and we estimated their 81 respective contributions to 38% and 17% of the variance of RR in S. cerevisiae (Raffoux et al. 82 2018a). Cis effects may involve local heterozygosity (Dluzewska et al. 2018). For significant se-83 quence divergence, recombination decreases as heterozygosity increases (Raffoux et al. 2018a), as 84 expected if strong divergence suppresses COs through the mismatch-repair system (Hunter et al. 85 1996). Recombination QTLs acting in trans have also been detected in several species (Pan et al. 86 2017; Petit et al. 2017; Ziolkowski et al. 2017). Moreover, RR could be increased in Arabidopsis 87 and in a few crop species by knocking out anti-CO genes (e.g. FANCM, FIGL1 or RECQ4), over-88 expressing pro-CO genes (e.g. HEI10), or changing the ploidy context in Brassicaceae, see review 89 in Blary and Jenczewski (2019). 90 Although CO formation is highly regulated, the evolutionary pressures behind such regulations 91 remain unclear. There must be at least one CO per bivalent for gametes to be viable, and there 92 seems also to be a soft upper limit to CO number in most species studied (Fernandes et al. 2017), 93 but the underlying selective pressures remain hypothetical. Recombination is strongly associated 94 with the occurrence of sex, so the fitness cost of recombination itself (crossovers) may not always 95

Sex involves spending both time and energy, and has a ‘two-97 fold cost’ because the females transmit their genes at only half the rate of asexual equivalents (Bell 98 1982). Recombination may also increase or decrease fitness (Charlesworth and Barton 1996): it can 99 break beneficial allelic combinations built up by selection, producing the ‘recombination load’ 100 (Otto and Lenormand 2002), e.g. in D. melanogaster where decreased recombination led to higher 101 female fecundity (Barton 2010). Too many COs may also be mutagenic (Liu et al. 2017) or could 102 be mechanically deleterious for meiosis, although evidence for this is still lacking. Yet despite these 103 costs, sex and recombination are maintained in the great majority of species, which is referred to as 104 the ‘paradox of sex’ (Otto and Lenormand 2002). On the other hand, shuffling alleles through re-105 combination can be beneficial for adaptation by increasing the genetic variance (Barton 2010). Ex-106 perimental evolution of yeast populations revealed that sexual reproduction favored adaptation to a 107 new harsh environment, whereas no effect was observed in a new benign environment (Goddard et 108 al. 2005), and that sex both speeds up adaptation and allows natural selection to more efficiently 109 sort beneficial from deleterious mutations (McDonald et al. 2016). Altogether, the relationship be-110 tween recombination and fitness is difficult to study because it is mostly indirect: COs do not di-111 rectly change fitness (except a small number of COs required to keep euploidy through meiosis), 112 but they modify the potential to exploit standing genetic variation to produce new haplotypic com-113 binations, which may be beneficial (or deleterious) for fitness. Finally, CO interference can also be 114 an important player for the adaptive consequences of recombination since it lowers the probability 115 that two close COs bring together three favorable alleles linked in repulsion phase (Otto and Pay-116 seur 2019). 117 RR shows intraspecific diversity, and many adaptive processes have been found to be associated 118 with the evolution of recombination modifier genes in theoretical (Gandon and Otto 2007) as well 119 as empirical (Korol and Iliadi 1994; Aggarwal et al. 2015) studies. Thus, recombination seems to be 120 able to respond to indirect selection, and it should also be possible to change RR by applying direct 121 recurrent artificial selection. However, selecting on RR is challenging because of the difficulty to 122 measure recombination phenotypes. COs may be detected by studying recombination between ge-123 netic markers in linkage mapping experiments (Bauer et al. 2013) or by direct cytological observa-124 tions. In all cases, these methods are destructive, and they are difficult to bring to 128 a high throughput as required for experimental evolution. 129 Nevertheless, direct selection experiments on RR have already been reported a long time ago in 130 Drosophila, based on the segregation of morphological genetic markers. After six generations of 131 positive or negative selection on CO rate in the interval between loci 'dichaète' and 'sepia', no re-132 sponse to selection was first observed (Gowen 1919), but later on, during 30 generations of selec-133 tion for low CO rate in the interval between 'white eyes' and 'miniature wings' loci, a continuous re-134 duction of RR was observed (Detlefsen and Roberts 1921). In the same paper, eight generations of 135 selection for high RR in the same interval also produced a reduction of recombination, which was 136 interpreted by the authors as a positive response to selection producing many double COs. Several 137 similar experiments have been performed in other genomic regions of Drosophila (Parsons 1958; 138 Chinnici 1971a; Kidwell 1972; Abdullah and Charlesworth 1974; Charlesworth and Charlesworth 139 1985a), which confirm that recurrent artificial selection for high or low recombination rate most of-140 ten results in a genetic response. Further studies on the genetic architecture of the response showed 141 that it is polygenic (Chinnici 1971b; Charlesworth and Charlesworth 1985b) and not due to chromo-142 somal rearrangements. In the insect Tribolium castaneum , significant response to selection for high 143 RR has also been reported during 10 generations, after which a plateau was reached, and RR was 144 stable for five more generations (Dewees 1975). In the same paper, when selecting for low RR, 145 there was no significant decrease of RR along generations, but the average RR over the 15 genera-146 tions was significantly lower than the initial value, suggesting a response limited to the first genera-147 tion. In Schistocerca gregaria , the response to selection was significant for low RR but not for high 148 RR, although there was a trend suggesting a weak response in the second case (Shaw 1972). In silk-149 worm, response to selection was obtained for both low and high RR, as cited by Ebinuma and Yo-150 shitake (1981). Apart from insects, a similar experiment has been achieved in Lima bean (Allard 151 1963), resulting in significant responses to selection for high RR but no response to selection for 152 low RR. In animals and fungi, we did not find any published results on long-term experimental evo-153 lution carried out with artificial selection on RR. 154 Such studies on the evolvability of CO number can provide useful insights on the evolutionary dy-155 namics of recombination modifier genes that can play a crucial role in adaptation from standing var-156 iation, particularly in changing environments (Charlesworth 1993). # Discussion 764 The present divergent selection experiment showed a response of local recombination rate after 765 ten generations of direct selection of recombinant or non-recombinant spores in a region of chromo-766 some VI. The experiment was designed to investigate evolutionary mechanisms during a limited 767 number of generations. The response to selection is thus most likely due to selection and recombi-768 nation among allelic variants present in the starting population, rather than accumulated novel muta-769 tions. To further limit the occurrence of mutations, we kept the number of mitotic cell divisions low 770 (approximately 10 mitotic cycles per sexual generation). On the other hand, to maximize the stand-771 ing genetic variation, four of the five founder parents used to build the initial SGRP-4X population 772 were chosen to capture as much as possible of the genetic diversity of the S. cerevisiae species (Cu-773 billos et al. 2013). Moreover, the genomic interval chosen for selecting recombinant spores was pre-774 viously found to show a high level of variation in recombination rate across these five founders 775 (Raffoux et al. 2018a). Indeed, we observed a plateau in the response to selection after about eight 776 generations ( Figure 2A ), suggesting that we've reached the limits allowed by the initial genetic var-777 iation and thus that the impact of novel mutations on that response was probably limited. 778 The experimental protocol used in this work was designed to sort 300,000 cells at each generation. 779 The main bottleneck in our experiments is due to FACS, so population size should never be less 780 than 300,000 throughout all steps of all sexual generations. Genetic differentiation between G0 and 781 Sel= populations is thus expected to be mainly due to unwanted selection (e.g. on growth, sporula-782 tion, viability) rather than genetic drift. Therefore, using Sel= populations as a control should allow 783 us to investigate the effect of selection on recombination without confounding effects of drift or un-784 wanted selection on other traits. 785 We do not know of any similar experiment performed previously in S. cerevisiae . In S. pombe , an 786 experiment of evolution under selection for recombinant or non-recombinant spores was reported in 787 a recent PhD dissertation (Berenguer Millanes 2025).
