ABSTRACT 16 

Demographic history can shape the genetic load of populations by influencing the efficacy of selection, levels 17 

of heterozygosity, and  the  incorporation of new variants via gene flow.  Understanding these dynamics is 18 

crucial for identifying threats to population viability and predicting evolutionary trajectories of invasive 19 

populations translocated by humans into non -native environments.  We investigate these processes in 20 

Trinidadian guppies ( Poecilia reticulata ) by estimating genetic load s across  multiple populations, with a 21 

particular focus on a single expanding population  in which  translocated individuals  have  rapidly spread and 22 

replaced native  individuals . Overall,  we observe  the expected  negative relationship between neutral genetic 23 

diversity and relative genetic load. In the translocated population, patterns differ between strongly and weakly 24 

deleterious mutations . Strongly deleterious alleles  are  purged at the isolated translocation site but tend to 25 

accumulate along the expansion front. In contrast,  the  genetic load  estimated  based on weakly deleterious 26 

variants declin es along the expansion gradient. These  differing  patterns  can be explained by  admixture with 27 

native populations  (which carried fewer weakly deleterious mutations ) reduc ing  the overall genetic load  of  the 28 

population  at the expansion front . However, admixture  has  also increased genetic diversity and introduced new 29 

strongly deleterious alleles,  thereby  revers ing  the  purging  effect . Together, our findings  illustrate  the complex 30 

interactions  determining  genetic load in  subdivided  populations, offering important insights into the 31 

evolutionary  aspects  of biological invasions. 32

INTRODUCTION 34 

Genetic load refers to  the  decrease  in fitness  caused by  deleterious variants in a genome  (Crow 1989; Bertorelle 35 

et al. 2022) . Most of the deleterious mutations  have slight  effects in heterozygous carriers  and remain  rare 36 

in  large panmictic populations  (Eyre -Walker and Keightley 2007) . Such deleterious  variants  present in 37 

heterozygous genotypes  are  often referred to as  “heterozygous  genetic  load ” (Bertorelle et al. 2022) . Under 38 

some demographic scenarios, deleterious variants can  increase in frequency  and  become  exposed in 39 

homozygotes as a  “homozygous  genetic  load ”, which may  threat en  population  survival  (Mathur and DeWoody 40 

2021) . In such  a situation , deleterious alleles  may  either  become common, or eve n fix ed , in a population 41 

decreasing its  mean  fitness,  or  they may  be  eliminated from  the  population  by  “purging ” (Dussex et al. 2023; 42 

Robinson et al. 2023) .43

Demographic changes, such as a  population reduction , are expected to  affect genetic variation and  the  genetic 44 

load of a population through  increased  genetic drift  (Wright 1932)  and  inbreeding  (Wright 1922) . Genetic  drift 45 

increases  the  variance around  the  mean frequency of  deleterious mutations,  allow ing  some deleterious alleles 46 

to  drift to high  frequenc ies  (de Pedro et al. 2021; Zeitler et al. 2023; Stewart et al. 2017) . Thus, in  small 47 

populations  in which  selection fails to remove deleterious mutations of weak effects , these variants accumulate 48 

over time  (Kimura and Ohta 1969)  and may  even tually  fix  (Kimura et al. 1963) . This  accumulation reduces 49 

fitness , which may further reduc e population size,  potentially  result ing  in a vicious circle of  “mutational 50 

meltdown ” (Lynch et al. 1995) . Under this scenario , such  “genetic erosion ” in small, isolated populations of 51 

endangered species  should relate to their estimated neutral genetic  variation, reflecting “effective population 52 

size”. In other words, genome -wide  genetic variation  underlies  fitness and thus provide  crucial information for 53 

conservation  (Kardos et al. 2021; DeWoody et al. 2021; Willi et al. 2022) . Consistent with these theoretical 54 

predictions, a negative  relationship between neutral nucleotide diversity and  homozygous  load  was  indeed 55 

found , for example  in an  endangered rattlesnake  (Mathur et al. 2023a)  or Montezuma quail  (Mathur et al. 56 

2023b) .57 

However,  the relationship  between genetic load and neutral diversity  depend s on  the  timescale of evolutionary 58 

processes that generate these correlations  (Mathur et al. 2023a, 2023b)  and m ay  be  changed  by recent 59 

demographic events.  For  instance , a recent  transient population size reduction  might  increase a population’s 60 

mean  fitness  through several processes . First, alleles at low frequencies, such as strongly deleterious mutations, 61 

are likely to be lost by drift. Second,  reduced  population size  increases  the  probability that alleles carried by 62 

mating partners are identical by descent, leading to increase d homozygosity in a population  (Wright 1922)  thus 63 

changing masked load into realized load . Highly deleterious variants at low frequencies  in  the  pre -bottleneck 64 

population  thus  become exposed to natural selection  (Charlesworth and Willis 2009) . If selection can overcome 65 

genetic drift, it  may  purge large effect deleterious variants, ultimately  decreasing  both  heterozygous  and total 66 

genetic load  (Glémin 2003; Crnokrak and Barrett 2002; Keller and Waller 2002; Barrett and Charlesworth 67 

1991) . New genomic data  suggest that purging occurs  more often tha n previously thought  (Dussex et al. 2023) ,68 

however,  its  effectiveness differ s between species  (Lan et al. 2025)  and  its  consequences for  population 69 

viability and extinction risk  remain  unclear  (Kardos et al. 2025) . Consequently,  Teixeira and Huber  (2021) 70 

argued against the importance of neutral genetic diversity for the conservation of wild populations , and  instead 71

Such investigations might also  be  important  in the context of  biological invasions  (Estoup et al. 2016) . Even 77 

though invasi ons  and endangered species reflect opposite ends of a  spectrum of ecological success, they 78 

experience similar challenges  (Colautti et al. 2017) . Both natural and artificial  translocations  that give  rise to 79 

invasions  usually introduce  small numbers  of  often related  individuals,  and  thus  the  initial stages of invasion 80 

involve  small effective population size s. Maintaining invasive potential despite expected loss of genetic 81 

diversity and increased genetic load was  termed  the  “genetic paradox” of invasions  (Estoup et al. 2016; 82 

Kołodziejczyk et al. 2025) . It has been suggested that purging  may  explain this paradox and  contribute to  the 83 

invasive potential of  translocated  population s (Estoup et al. 2016) . Purging in  invasive species  has, however, 84 

been documented  in  only  a few cases  (Facon et al. 2011; Lombaert et al. 2025; Zayed et al. 2007) , making 85 

generaliz ation difficult . Following  an  initial bottleneck, invasive species  often  experience rapid range 86 

expansion. Such expansions can  involve  multiple subsequent bottlenecks, increasing  the  effects of  purging and 87 

genetic drift especially at the expansion  front  (Peischl et al. 2015) . This process  might  then  lead to  further 88 

accumulation of weakly deleterious variants  and purging of  strongly deleterious ones  (Rougemont et al. 2023) .89 

However , following  the  initial bottleneck,  successful invasions  are  often  associated with  admixture  between 90 

diverge nt  genotypes  (when  translocations  occur  multiple times ) or  with  hybridization between translocated 91 

and  native  populations  (Barker et al. 2019; Kołodziejczyk et al. 2025; Colautti et al. 2017) . Potential  benefit s92 

of such  admixture  include  increased genetic variation and  incorporation of alleles associated with local 93 

adaption into  the  genetic pool of invasive species , enabling adaptation to novel habitats  (Wagner et al. 2017; 94 

Kołodziejczyk et al. 2025) . In terestingly , genome -wide  genetic diversity was not  significantly  reduced in m any 95 

invasive populations , likely due to  recent  admixture or hybridization  (Kołodziejczyk et al. 2025) . However, 96 

little  is known  about  the effect of  these  admixture  events  on the dynamic s of  genetic load.  On one hand, 97 

admixture  might be expected to  mask  the  homozygous  genetic load of  an  expanding population , not only  for 98

Here, we  examine  the fate of genetic load during  the  invasion of Trinidadian guppies  (Poecilia reticulata ) into 106 

a diverged, allopatric population, during which  translocated individuals rapidly spread and replaced na ti ve 107 

individuals . Guppies  occur  naturally  in  the  fresh waters of  Trinidad, Tobago  and South America , but  have been 108 

translocated  and  successfully  invaded  multiple  sites  across the globe  (Deacon et al. 2011) . Natural  Trinidadian 109 

populations have been intensively studied by generations of ecologists and evolutionary biologists  (Magurran 110 

2005)  and  some of these studies included translocation experiments  (Fitzpatrick et al. 2016; Fraser et al. 2015) .111 

One such  trans location occurred in the  Turure  River  in 1957  (Endler 1980; Reznick and Bryga 1987; Shaw et 112 

al. 1992) . Fish from  the  lower Guanapo  River (Caroni drainage) were moved to  a site in the  upper  Turure  River 113 

(Oropouche drainage)  which previously lacked guppies  (Magurran 2005) . 200 guppies, approximately half 114 

females, were introduced  (Shaw et al. 1992; Sievers et al. 2012) . Importantly, the  source  and recipient 115 

drainages are  inhabited by  differentiated guppy  populations  (Willing et al. 2010) , with long separate histories 116 

(estimated to have split  0.18 -1.2 mya , based on net divergence  (Fajen and Breden 1992; Whiting et al. 2021) ),117 

and evidence of partial reproductive isolation  (Russell and Magurran 2006) . The Or opouche fish  are  more 118 

diverged  from Caroni  than from other  fish  on other islands  (e.g. Tobago) , suggesting that they  may  represent 119 

dis tinct species  (Schories et al. 2009) , though  evidence for b arriers to gene flow  is  weak  (Devigili et al. 2018; 120 

Magurran 2005) . The differentiation documented by these studies  reflects  both isolation and  adaptation to 121 

different local environments  (Reznick et al. 1990; Bassar et al. 2010; Kemp et al. 2018) . However, after 122 

translocation , genetically  foreign individuals spread rapidly, and largely replaced native  individuals  in the 123 

lower parts of the Turure  River. Furthermore,  several sources of evidence demonstrated  ongoing  admixture 124 

from resident populations  (Becher and Magurran 2000; Willing et al. 2010; Sievers et al. 2012) .125

Genetic load  and its  correlation  with  neutral diversity 187 

We calculated three measures  of  genetic load  for  each re -sequenced individual : Relative  Loss -of -Function L oad 188 

(RLL) , Relative Genetic Load  (RGL)  and  Relative Missense Load  (RML) . The three measures are expected to 189 

reflect the effects of different classes of deleterious mutations.  As  loss -of -function (LoF) mutations are 190 

expected to be  the most severe , with large expected effects on  individual fitness  on average , thus  we  used  RLL 191 

to reflect the effects of the  most deleterious  class of  mutations.  The  RGL  estimate was  based on conservation 192 

scores (CS) calculated  from  the observed  number of substitutions  in  a multi -species  alignment,  relative to the 193 

number of  expected substitutions  under neutrality  (Davydov et al. 2010) . This  provides  information 194 

on  evolutionary constrain ts. Mutations with high CS scores, have been  used  to estimate genetic load in  many 195 

studies and are already  a well -established approach  (Bertorelle et al. 2022)  that  likely captures  a wider range 196 

of  selection coefficients  than  LoF  mutations . Finally, many missense mutations are weakly deleterious , thus 197 

the  genetic load estimated  from  these variants ( RML ) should reflect  the least deleterious  classes of  mutations. 198 

To  estimate genetic load , the  guppy  reference genome  was  first  polarized  using  Illumina data from two 199 

outgroup species,  Xiphophorus  maculatus  and  Micro poecilia  picta  (14x and 23x  coverage,  respectively ). We 200 

inferred ancestral  states for the sites  used  in  CS  analyses ; 42,860,381 sites (5. 9% of the genome)  yielded 201 

information about both ancestral allele and  CS . We called genotypes  for these sites  separately  within each 202 

guppy population  using stringent filtering criteria  (see Supplemental Tab. S2 for effects of filtering on  the 203 

number of  called genotypes ). RGL  value  was  then  calculated  per individual,  as  the  sum  of  presumably 204 

deleterious mutations  (CS>2)  weighted by the CS value,  relati ve  to  the value for  all derived variants . RLL and 205 

RML were  respectively  calculate d as the ratio of loss -of -function (LoF) and missense  derived  variant number s206

DISCUSSION 244 

The accelerating pace of global change, driven by climate shifts and habitat loss, has led to widespread species 245 

migration, range fragmentation, and population isolation  (Johnson et al. 2017) . These rapid demographic shifts 246 

can profoundly influence the dynamics of genetic load,  posing  challenges for conservation biology. To date, 247 

most research in this area has centered on endangered, highly vulnerable, or even extinct taxa  (Kuang et al. 248 

2020; Von Seth et al. 2021; Kleinman -Ruiz et al. 2022; Ochoa et al. 2022; Xie et al. 2022) . The role of genetic 249 

load in biological invasions has received less attention  (de Pedro et al. 2021; Rougemont et al. 2020; Tayeh et 250 

al. 2013) . Our study addresses this gap by investigating how genetic load accumulates in invasive, non -251 

threatened species  (Deacon et al. 2011; Lindholm et al. 2005; Rosenthal et al. 2021) , thereby offering insights 252 

into the mechanisms of invasion —particularly when accompanied by admixture with native populations.  Our 253 

large population -genomic dataset of hundreds of guppy genomes from multiple wild populations  show s that 254 

genetic loads are strongly affected by  historical effective population size (inferred from neutral diversity).  We255 

also  found  that translocated populations  appear to  have purged strongly deleterious mutations,  and that 256 

subsequent  admixture along the expansion gradient  restored this load .257

Admixture reshapes  the  landscape of genetic load in  an  expanding population 350 

Our investigation of the Turure expansion gradient revealed that total RGL and RML decline further 351 

downstream (Fig.  5),  which  could, in principle, reflect repeated purging events following successive 352 

bottlenecks.  However,  this  would  cause a gradual loss of nucleotide diversity and a depletion of rare variants, 353 

while purging should primarily reduce  heterozygous  load by targeting the most deleterious mutations . In 354 

contrast,  we  found  increased nucleotide diversity and lower Tajima’s D values in the middle and lower Turure 355 

populations  compared with  the up -river  population. Moreover, the decline in genetic load is driven mainly by 356 

reductions in  homozygous  load, whereas  heterozygous  load remains largely unchanged or only moderately 357 

affected. Strikingly, for the most deleterious loss -of -function alleles, genetic load actually increases rather than 358 

decreases. 359 

We therefore  suggest  that changes in genetic load  along  the Turure  River  primarily  reflect  admixture occurring 360 

below the waterfall barrier in the middle and lower reaches of the river.  The  upper and lower populations 361 

indeed  differ substantially in their genetic composition, with the lower populations showing pronounced 362 

admixture with other ancestries (Fig.  1A ). Depending on the genetic makeup of the admixed sources and the 363 

degree of divergence between them, such admixture could have profoundly reshaped the landscape of genetic 364 

load by increasing  the  heterozygo te frequencies  and introducing new haplotypes into the population. 365

inferred from neutral nucleotide diversity (Fig.  3). Rivers in the Oropouche drainage generally exhibit higher 367 

nucleotide diversity than those in the Caroni drainage (Fig.  2), suggesting  that  a lower total RGL  should be 368 

expected  in Oropouche populations.  This pattern is  also  supported  in  our comparisons of total RGL between 369 

the two drainages ( Supplementa l Table  S5 ). If the native Turure ancestry from the Oropouche drainage 370 

followed this trend, it would have carried fewer moderately deleterious mutations than the Caroni guppies 371

patterns.  These find ings are relevant to  understanding  genetic processes in  invasive species , which in a globally 388

Our results highlight that while d eleterious alleles accumulate  over thousands of generations  (Wootton et al. 392

when populations mix . Yet, it should be stressed that  our analyses did not take into account several processes 394 

which may affect our interpretation,  such as  epistatic interactions, and local adaptation  resulting from different 395

correlational, and future studies should attempt  to investigate  the  fitness consequences of the inferred genetic 397 

load.  Furthermore, the temporal scope of our study reflects a relatively recent population expansion, and the 398 

long -term consequences of such demographic processes may differ over extended evolutionary timescales. 399
