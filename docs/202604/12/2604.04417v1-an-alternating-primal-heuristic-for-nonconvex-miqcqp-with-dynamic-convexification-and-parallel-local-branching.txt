# An Alternating Primal Heuristic for Nonconvex MIQCQP with Dynamic Convexification and Parallel Local Branching 

## Yongzheng Dai 1 and Chen Chen 21ISyE, Georgia Institute of Technology, Atlanta, GA, USA 

2ISE, The Ohio State University, Columbus, OH, USA 

Abstract 

We develop a novel primal heuristic for nonconvex Mixed-Integer Quadrat-ically Constrained Quadratic Programs (MIQCQPs). The method is built around a convex approximation that is dynamically adjusted within a feasibility-pump-style alternating heuristic. Approximations are adjusted based on the structure of the MIQCQP instance. Additionally, paral-lelized local branching is incorporated to further refine detected solutions. This paper builds upon the second-place finalist submission in the 2025 Land-Doig MIP Computational Competition. Our results are validated with computational experiments on instances from QPLIB, finding feasi-ble solutions for three previously unsolved cases and improving the best-known solutions for fifteen instances within five minutes of runtime.

We categorize MIQCQPs into three classes: 1. MIBQP : mixed-integer box-constrained quadratic programs. MIQCQP with a quadratic objective function (1a), box constraints (1d), and integer constraints (1e), and without any other linear or quadratic constraints. 2. MIQP : mixed-integer quadratic programming. MIQCQP with quadratic objective function (1a) and at least one general linear constraint (1c), and without quadratic constraints. 3. MIQCP : mixed-integer quadratically constrained programming. MIQCQP with at least one quadratic constraint ( m1 вүҘ 1). These classes are substantially different from the perspective of a feasibil-ity heuristic. Generating feasible solutions for MIBQPs is trivial (though NP-hard in general to approach optimality), as any point satisfying the variable bounds and integrality conditions is feasible. For MIQPs, a feasible solution can be detected using existing primal heuristics developed for Mixed-Integer Linear Programs (MILPs), such as those in [29, 30, 54]. However, for MIQCPs, the presence of nonconvex quadratic constraints makes feasible solution detec-tion significantly more challenging and thus computationally expensive (see, e.g. [62]). In this work, we develop a primal heuristic to find feasible solutions for nonconvex MIQCQPs, which consists of two stages: (i) detecting a feasible so-lution with an alternating method and (ii) refining the feasible solution with local branching. The alternating method involves a dynamic, eigenvalue-based convex approximation scheme that iteratively refines approximations of non-convex quadratic terms. This scheme is integrated with integer rounding in a feasibility-pump-like alternating heuristic approach; subsequently, the feasible solution is refined with parallel local branching. 

## 1.1 Literature Review 

MIQCQP generalizes both Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) and (continuous) Quadratically Constrained Quadratic Programming (QCQP), en-abling the representation of problems that combine discrete structures and non-linear relationships. Such problems arise in a wide range of real-world applica-tions, including power systems [3, 43], water systems [28], pooling and mixing processes [4, 20], signal processing [51], finance [23], and machine learning [10]. There is a wide range of solution techniques for MIQCQPs, but this paper focuses primarily on primal heuristics (see [8] for a recent survey), i.e. algo-rithms that aim (but are not guaranteed) to find high-quality feasible solutions 2quickly, e.g. [21, 22, 44]. The work in this paper was initiated by Yongzheng Dai as a submission for the Land-Doig MIP Computational Competition 2025 1

on primal heuristics for MIQCQP. This competition included submissions such as a Frank-Wolfe-based primal heuristic [47] and a classification-and-relaxation method. This paper was inspired especially by primal heuristics that have been successfully incorporated into MILP solvers: the feasibility pump [29], local branching [30, 49], large neighborhood search [54], and rounding heuristics [48]. Many attempts have been made to extend these techniques nonlinear settings, and this paper is an additional contribution to this literature. Specifically, our approach involves a unique combination of dynamic convex approximation (e.g. [5, 13, 19, 35, 62]) , alternating heuristic (e.g. [6, 26]), and local branching (e.g. [41]) for nonconvex MIQCQP. We also leverage parallelization in local branching, and so contribute to the growing literature on incorporating parallelization in Mixed-Integer Program-ming (MIP) solvers (see, e.g., [1, 9, 16, 38]), and parallel primal MIP heuristics in particular [42, 47, 55, 56]. We note also substantial efforts in massively par-allel computing for MIP, e.g. [27, 39, 52, 53, 58, 59], as well as parallel presolv-ing [24, 25, 34]. 

## 1.2 Organization 

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 discusses existing convex reformulations or relaxations to nonconvex MIQCQPs and our proposed convex approximation. Section 3 presents the novel primal heuristics based on the proposed convex approximation, and Section 4 develops the parallel local branching. Finally, numerical experiments is shown in Section 5.

We construct Algorithm 4 based on two kinds of projections: the projection onto the feasible region of Approx(ЛҶ u), F(Approx(ЛҶ u)), which solves a convex MIQCP and corresponds to the вҖңprojection stepвҖқ in [18]; and the projection onto the feasible region of the original MIQCQP, F(Q M IQCP ), which solves a (nonconvex) MIQCP and corresponds to the вҖңrounding stepвҖқ in [17]. Though both projections are NP-hard, we only aim to get a feasible solution within the time limit (10 seconds) instead of an optimal solution. Line 1 conducts a local NLP solver for the continuous relaxation of Q M IQCP .Line 2 initializes ЛҶ u. Line 4 solves a convex MIQCP with a time limit. If line 4 does not find any feasible solution, we increase ЛҶ u in line 6 to enlarge the feasible region of Approx(ЛҶ u). If we get x(1) , line 8 projects x(1) onto F(Q M IQCP ), which solves a nonconvex MIQCP with a time limit. If line 8 does not find any feasible solution, we tune ЛҶ u based on x(1) and the fixed-point relation. ## 3.4 Comparison with Feasibility Pump Literature 

In this subsection, we compare our proposed methods with similar primal heuris-tics based on the feasibility pump. Algorithm 1 starts from a solution of the continuous relaxation of Approx( u), as with typical feasibility pumps [29, 31]; likewise with flipping of binary variables. Algorithm 2 is an extension to Algorithm 1, in which we want the detection solution close to the start point, which potentially has a better objective value, as well as is integer-feasible. Therefore, when flipping binaries, we conduct a domain propagation to check the feasibility of different assignments of binaries, in the spirit of feasibility pump 2.0 [31]. Algorithm 3 extends Algorithm 2 for nonconvex MIQCQP with LcМё = вҲ…, in which Approx( u) is not an exact convex reformulation. As discussed in Sec-tions 2.2 and 2.3, we develop a dynamic convex approximation Approx(ЛҶ u) to refine convex models iteratively, which may result in a better feasible solution with Algorithm 2. To our knowledge, such an approximation scheme for pro-jection is unique in the literature. Algorithm 4 and Algorithm 5 stray further from the pumping idea and could be viewed as part of a more general family of alternating heuristics. Algorithm 4 may be described as a modified feasibility-pump-style two-projection method, whereas Algorithm 5 can be considered a pump-inspired alternating feasibility-repair method. Moreover, both involve our convex approximation updates. In contrast, the MINLP literature on feasibility pumps follow more closely the orig-inal MIP template, alternating between continuous relaxation (NLP) feasibility and integrality feasibility via projection, rounding, or mixed-integer distance minimization [6, 26].
