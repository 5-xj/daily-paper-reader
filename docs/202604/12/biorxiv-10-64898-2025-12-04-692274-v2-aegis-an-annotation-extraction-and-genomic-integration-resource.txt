Abstract 11 The GTF/GFF3 formats are the standard for storing and exchanging genome 12 annotations. However, their flexibility often results in inconsistent and poorly 13 formatted files across different sources, creating a major bottleneck for downstream 14 bioinformatics analyses. Here, we present Annotation Extraction and Genomic 15 Integration Suite (AEGIS), a comprehensive and user-friendly command-line toolkit 16 designed to parse, validate and standardise genome annotation files. AEGIS 17 robustly corrects common structural and formatting errors, ensuring interoperability 18 with downstream tools. Beyond standardisation, the suite provides advanced 19 modules for analysis, such as flexible sequence extraction (e.g. genes, CDS, 20 proteins) with isoform handling, customisable promoter region definitions and 21 targeted DNA motif searches. A key feature of AEGIS is its integrated workflow for 22 comparative genomics, which combines multiple lines of evidence (i.e., sequence 23 homology, synteny and coordinate-based lift-overs) to enable a robust gene ID 24 correspondence and orthology assessment. We demonstrate the utility of AEGIS by 25 comparing two major Arabidopsis thaliana annotations (TAIR10 vs. Araport11), 26 successfully identifying and quantifying complex structural changes such as gene 27 splits and fusions. AEGIS provides a unified solution for annotation quality control, 28 feature extraction and comparative genomic analysis, simplifying complex workflows 29 and enhancing reliability in bioinformatic research. Introduction 33 The efficient representation and description of genomic feature annotations are 34 fundamental requirements in modern bioinformatics. Standardised and reliable 35 genome annotations are essential to accurately interpret gene evolution, expression, 36 and regulation across species. While several related specifications exist, such as the 37 more restrictive Gene Transfer Format (GTF), the General Feature Format version 3 38 (GFF3) has become the standard for genome annotation exchange since its release 39 in 2004, underpinning widely used annotation pipelines, such as MAKER (Cantarel 40 et al., 2008) and BREAKER (Gabriel et al., 2024), as well as genome browsers such 41 as IGV (Robinson et al., 2011) and JBrowse (Buels et al., 2016). This format has 42 been integrated into most tools requiring genome annotation data as input. It 43 consists of nine tab-delimited columns, with the ninth column, 'attributes', containing 44 a semicolon-separated list of tag-value pairs. These define the relationships between 45 features, for example, by linking exons to a parent transcript using ‘ID’ and ‘Parent’ 46 tags in a hierarchical structure representing the complex organisation of genes and 47 their products. 48 Despite the existence of a formal specification, the flexibility of the GFF3 format has 49 led to a lack of strict adherence, which has become a significant obstacle for 50 standardised downstream processing. Annotation files vary across organisms, 51 frequently exhibiting inconsistencies due to the use of different source software 52 packages or notation criteria. These discrepancies can range from simple formatting 53 errors to more complex structural problems, hindering fundamental tasks like 54 extracting coding sequences (CDS) for translation, calculating basic annotation 55 statistics, or isolating promoter regions for the inspection of regulatory elements. 56 Common issues include missing features, incorrect or inconsistent feature 57 coordinates, improper strand orientation and incorrectly calculated phase information 58 (in the case of coding sequences). While certain tags like 'ID' and 'Parent' have 59 predefined meanings, different annotation providers often use non-standard tags or 60 model the same data with different notation styles. For instance, the hierarchical 61 relationship between an exon and its parent gene can be represented differently 62

This variability often results in 64 files where child features reference parent IDs that do not exist, leading to parsing 65 errors in downstream applications. Many analysis tools rely on a consistent GFF 66 structure as input, and inconsistent or poorly formatted files can lead to a variety of 67 errors, from outright tool failure to more subtle and potentially misleading 68 misinterpretations of the data. Such inconsistencies force users to develop ad hoc ,69 and often brittle, scripts tailored to specific file sources. 70 Here, we introduce AEGIS (Annotation Extraction and Genomic Integration Suite), a 71 tool capable of parsing and standardising GFF files from various sources to ensure 72 their interoperability and the reliability of subsequent bioinformatic analyses. AEGIS's 73 dual-layer design provides a powerful command-line interface (CLI) for end-users, 74 while also allowing the use of its functionalities as a versatile Python library, making 75 it also a foundational component for developers building custom analysis pipelines or 76 other bioinformatics software that requires reliable handling of genomic annotations. 77 To showcase AEGIS's capabilities, we have developed a range of different CLI 78 modules for downstream analyses, including simple tasks such as calculating 79 annotation statistics and extracting feature sequences, comparable to functionalities 80 found in AGAT (Dainat, 2022), gffread (Pertea & Pertea, 2020), or gffutils, but also 81 more complex comparative genomics studies. The toolkit integrates workflows for 82 analysing gene correspondence between annotation versions in the same assembly, 83 and for assessing orthology by combining gene model overlaps via tools like Liftoff 84 and LiftOn with additional approaches such as reciprocal BLAST searches, 85 OrthoFinder clustering and synteny-based analyses with MCscan, thus creating a 86 unified solution for annotation quality control and biological inquiry. 87

Implementation 88 The AEGIS toolkit comprises a suite of modular CLI tools designed to address 89 distinct tasks in genome annotation processing and analysis. Its broad operating 90 system (OS) compatibility is due to its full Python implementation. Each tool 91 command follows the aegis {tool} format and can be called from a terminal as long 92 as installation requirements are satisfied. All of the CLI tools make use of a set of 93 purpose-built Python classes (Supplementary Figure S1) providing a solid framework 94 for custom-made genome annotation functionalities as well as facilitating future 95 AEGIS updates. To ensure the long-term reliability of these functionalities, an 96 extensive suite of unit tests have been implemented covering core parser functions 97 and CLI operations. This framework is integrated into a continuous integration 98 pipeline via GitHub Actions, which validates every commit to the repository and is 99 transparently documented in the package's GitHub history. 100 While the majority of AEGIS modules such as those for file standardization (tidy, 101 reformat), feature extraction (extract), and coordinate-based overlap analysis 102 (overlap), are implemented as native Python functions, the orthology command 103 requires the local installation of several external bioinformatic tools: DIAMOND for 104 sequence homology, MCscan (from the JCVI toolkit) for synteny analysis, 105 OrthoFinder for orthogroup inference, and Liftoff/LiftOn for annotation transfer. Due 106 to the complex installation of these dependencies and the need for a specific fork of 107 LiftOn to ensure full compatibility with the AEGIS workflow, we provide a pre-108 configured Docker image that encapsulates the entire environment, ensuring 109 reproducibility without the need for manual dependency management. Furthermore, 110 we have established an automated release workflow using GitHub Actions. Upon 111 every new release, the system automatically publishes the aegis-bio package to the 112 Python Package Index (PyPI). Simultaneously, a new Docker image is built from the 113 repository’s Dockerfile and uploaded to both Docker Hub and the GitHub Container 114 Registry (GHCR), ensuring that users always have access to synchronised versions 115 across all platforms. 116 To systematically present the breadth of the current capabilities (Figure 1), we first 117 describe the core utilities for foundational file manipulation, standardisation and data 118 integration. Additionally, the tool includes flexible filtering parameters to selectively retain or 173 exclude specific feature classes, such as coding or non-coding elements, 174 pseudogenes, and transposable elements. 175 The extract command can output multiple types of genomic features, including 176 whole genes, CDSs, their translated protein sequences and putative promoter 177 regions. The handling of RNA features takes a more sophisticated approach allowing 178 precise export of all annotated transcripts or filtering for specific biotypes. Detecting and quantifying feature overlaps 229 To identify spatial overlaps between features, the overlap command includes a 230 method where the algorithm operates in two primary modes: (1) detecting overlaps 231 between features within a single genome annotation file (intra-annotation), and (2) 232 comparing features between two different annotation files (inter-annotation). A 233 fundamental prerequisite for inter-annotation comparison is that both annotation files 234 must share the same genome assembly, ensuring a common coordinate system. 235 This is feasible when the annotations are different annotation versions of the same 236 genome assembly, but for other cases, the use of external tools such as Liftoff 237 (Shumate & Salzberg, 2021) or LiftOn (Chao et al., 2024) in advance is 238 recommended. Alternatively, the orthology CLI described below may be more 239 appropriate. 240

Orthology 284 Whilst overlap focuses on comparing annotation files associated with the same 285 genome, orthology is designed to extensively compare annotations associated with 286 different genomes. The outputs of multiple tools (internal and external) are integrated 287 into an automated pipeline designed to generate a set of orthologues sorted into 288 several confidence tiers. 289 The approach combines four complementary lines of evidence: 290 1. Sequence homology: the fast protein aligner DIAMOND performs reciprocal 291 sequence searches (Buchfink et al., 2015), processing the results to identify 292 BLAST results above a certain coverage ( ≥30% by default) and an e-value 293 (≤1x10 -5 by default) threshold. These hits are then used to determine 294 reciprocal best hits (RBHs), reciprocal best BLAST hits (RBBHs) and the 295 remaining results which will be only forward (fw) or reverse (rv) results 296 depending on which annotation is considered the ‘query’. An additional 297

Synteny and collinearity: to incorporate genomic context, AEGIS uses the 300 JCVI toolkit's MCscan modules (Tang et al., 2024). By analysing the relative 301 order of genes between two genomes, it identifies conserved syntenic blocks. 302 Orthologue pairs found within these blocks receive additional support, as their 303 relationship is preserved by both sequence similarity and genomic location. 304 3. Annotation lift-over and overlap analysis: AEGIS integrates annotation 305 transfer tools like Liftoff (Shumate & Salzberg, 2021) and LiftOn (Chao et al., 306 2024) to project gene models from a source genome to a target genome. 307 Uniquely, AEGIS then applies its native spatial overlap algorithm to the 308 transferred annotations and filters overlap results using an overlap score 309 threshold of ≥6. 310 4. Multi-species orthogroup inference: To incorporate phylogenetic and 311 evolutionary information, AEGIS performs a full OrthoFinder analysis (Emms 312 & Kelly, 2019). It extracts the primary proteome for each input species 313 annotation, runs the OrthoFinder pipeline to cluster genes into orthogroups, 314 and integrates the resulting pairwise orthologues from the OrthoFinder 315 analysis into its final summary. 316 As output, AEGIS consolidates all sources of evidence into a single and easy-to-317 parse summary table with a confidence score for each putative orthologue pair. The 318 scoring is hierarchical, where pairs classified as high confidence are primarily 319 defined by strong evidence from lift-over based mapping, requiring a positional 320 overlap score of ≥ 7 from either Liftoff or LiftOn (Supplementary Table S2). Pairs 321 lacking this positional evidence are designated as medium confidence, provided they 322 are supported by the combination of being an RBH and part of a syntenic block 323 identified by MCscan, as well as pairs identified as RBBH alone. All other pairs 324 identified through less stringent criteria are categorised as lower confidence, 325 involving relationships supported by only one of the primary methods (e.g. an RBH 326 without syntenic confirmation). 327

Comparative analysis of consistency and divergence in Arabidopsis genome 329 

annotations 330 To showcase AEGIS's capabilities in comparative genomics, firstly for evaluating the 331 consistency and evolution of gene models across annotation versions within the 332 same genome, we performed an intra-species overlap analysis between TAIR10 and 333 Araport11 (Cheng et al., 2017; Lamesch et al., 2012). Both annotation sets are built 334 on the latest Arabidopsis thaliana TAIR10 genome assembly, allowing direct 335 comparison of feature coordinates. Using the overlap tool, we quantified the 336 similarity between gene models in these two major A. thaliana annotations 337 (Supplementary Table S3). This analysis revealed genes with conserved structural 338 definitions across versions, as well as those showing substantial changes in their 339 models, including structural modifications and, in some cases, changes in gene 340 identifiers. 341 The initial gene-level overlap analysis revealed a high degree of concordance for the 342 majority of Araport11 genes (31,733 with a ≥10 overlap score). This is expected for 343 two annotations of the same genome, where genes retain their original identifier in 344 both TAIR10 and Araport11 but where some of them may show differences in their 345 genomic span or exonic structure. Conversely, the overlap analysis also captured 346 cases where a single gene from the older TAIR10 annotation splits into multiple gene 347 models in Araport11. This fragmentation often indicates a re-evaluation of gene 348 boundaries based on improved prediction algorithms or the incorporation of 349 additional transcriptomic evidence, where a previously annotated single locus is now 350 recognised as two or more distinct genes. For instance, the gene ID AT2G52240 in 351 TAIR10 was found to have a partial overlap with AT2G52240 and AT2G52245 in 352 Araport11, with the latter being a small transcript variant coming from the previous 353 model (Figure 4A). The opposite scenario (i.e., gene merge cases), was also 354 observed, where two or more distinct TAIR10 genes were reported to overlap 355 significantly with a single, larger Araport11 gene (e.g. AT1G27595 ) (Figure 4B). This 356 analysis allows users to track structural gene model changes over annotation 357 updates, which are not always provided in such a level of detail. 358

The same tool can be used with a different 398 parameter to produce a lighter output (Supplementary Table S4). In this case, 399 ~125,000 orthologue gene-pairs were identified, which still manages to find 400 orthologue matches for a sizeable portion of annotated genes (e.g. more than 50% 401 for both Arabidopsis and tomato). This drop mainly affects the lower-confidence 402 orthologues (Supplementary Figure S2A) and there is still a benefit in combining the 403 different results of each tool (Supplementary Figure S2B). 404

Computational performance and benchmarking 425 To evaluate the computational efficiency of AEGIS, we benchmarked its core utilities 426 against comparable functionalities in an existing state-of-the-art toolkit, specifically 427 AGAT. Benchmarks were executed on a workstation equipped with an Intel Core i9-428 10900 and 32 GB, recording both total execution time and memory usage (RAM). 429 To ensure reproducibility and consistent execution environments, all evaluated tools 430 were containerised and executed using Docker. Upon the initialization of a tool's 431 container, the monitoring script continuously polled the Docker Engine API via the 432 native docker stats command to capture real-time memory usage. Data points were 433 recorded at a sampling interval of 0.1 seconds throughout the entire lifecycle of the 434 containerised process. The resulting telemetry data was logged as a time-series 435 dataset. 436 The comparative analysis was performed using small ( Chlamydomonas reinhardtii ), 437 medium ( Arabidopsis thaliana ), and large genomes ( Homo sapiens ). We evaluated 438 performance across three standard bioinformatic tasks: annotation parsing and 439 standardisation, conversion to GTF, and protein extraction. 440

Across all tested benchmarks and genome sizes, AEGIS demonstrated a 441 significantly higher computational efficiency compared to AGAT (Supplementary 442 Figure S3). In terms of execution time, AEGIS completed the tasks of GFF tidying, 443 GTF conversion, and protein extraction around 3-6 times faster than the 444 corresponding AGAT scripts. This performance gap was particularly evident in the 445 

Homo sapiens dataset, where for instance, in GTF conversion, AEGIS finished in 446 194 seconds compared to AGAT’s 1,101 seconds. 447 Regarding memory management, although AEGIS reaches a peak memory usage 448 more rapidly than AGAT, it maintains this footprint for a much shorter duration, 449 drastically reducing the total computational cost. While AGAT showed a steady and 450 prolonged memory plateau, AEGIS optimised resource release immediately upon 451 task completion. These results indicate that AEGIS is highly scalable and particularly 452 well-suited for large genomic annotations. 453 

Discussion 454 Implemented in Python, AEGIS provides both a powerful command-line interface for 455 pipeline integration and an object-oriented library for custom script development, 456 offering a flexibility absent in tools designed for one or a few tasks. It incorporates 457 functionalities found across several tools, including robust file parsing, format 458 conversion, filtering, and statistical reporting, matching the feature sets of mature 459 tools like AGAT. The analysis of genome annotation files is supported by a diverse 460 software range, which can be broadly categorised into comprehensive command-line 461 toolkits (e.g. AGAT, GenomeTools), specialised utilities (e.g. GffCompare, 462 TransDecoder), and programmatic libraries (e.g. gffutils). While established toolkits 463 provide several functionalities for genome annotation handling, this new package 464 offers an advantage in terms of parsing capabilities, being able to process complex 465 annotation files that cause other tools to fail. For instance, AGAT successfully 466 resolves overlapping exons within individual transcripts, but it fails to consolidate 467 identical exons shared across multiple transcripts. Instead, AGAT redundantly 468 duplicates these shared features, assigning each iteration a unique ID and restricting 469 them to a single parent. In contrast, AEGIS can optionally produce a much more 470 compact GFF3 format by making use of the multi-parent feature of the official GFF3 471 specification, thereby reducing unnecessary data redundancy. Moreover, some 472

known errors in AGAT, such as the “Bio::Root::Exception” caused by long lines in 473 FASTA files when extracting feature sequences, have been avoided when designing 474 AEGIS. AEGIS is specifically engineered to handle such edge cases, with a 475 codebase designed for robustness and modular reuse. Python was selected as the 476 implementation language to maximise these attributes, favouring maintainability and 477 flexible data handling over the performance optimisations typical of lower-level 478 languages. 479 In terms of performance limitations, the AEGIS package has been benchmarked 480 favourably against AGAT for the functionalities that were compared. Nevertheless, 481 the performance speed of AEGIS or AGAT compared to other C/C ++ tools such as 482 GFF Utilities and GenomeTools is still limited based on the chosen languages 483 themselves, Python and Perl, respectively. For basic standard operations GFF 484 Utilities and GenomeTools may still be the option of choice, however, these offer 485 less flexibility and robustness with respect to edge cases. Moreover, whilst AEGIS is 486 able to deliver GFF3 recommendations more effectively in the outputs, AGAT offers 487 greater flexibility when dealing with particular GTF format flavours. 488 AEGIS includes several high-level analytical capabilities not found in other examined 489 software (Supplementary Table S5). It provides modules for identifying a gene’s 490 main isoform, extracting a range of sequences, manipulating FASTA files together 491 with their corresponding annotation files, detecting overlaps between features, and 492 comprehensive orthology analysis. We showed that we were able to quantify and 493 categorise intra-species changes between annotation versions using the overlap 494 command. It generated a detailed and up-to-date table of gene model 495 correspondences, extending beyond a static list of obsolete identifiers (such as the 496 ones provided in Araport11; (Cheng et al., 2017), where users can inspect and trace 497 gene model differences between annotation releases. 498 Moreover, AEGIS can be used to compare annotation files from different genome 499 assemblies, addressing a complex comparative orthology analysis that lies beyond 500 the scope of existing tools like GffCompare (Pertea & Pertea, 2020). While 501 GffCompare focuses on RNA processing, comparing coordinates primarily at the 502 intron and exon levels to match intron chains, AEGIS overlap is more focused on 503

It 504 calculates mutual overlap percentages at the gene, exon, and CDS levels, 505 specifically validating that CDS segments maintain the same reading frame and 506 retrieving exact metrics for each pairwise correspondence. The orthology tool 507 integrates the overlaps and the output of several other tools and can be used to 508 study genome evolution and even assist in the identification of core and dispensable 509 genes when dealing with pangenomes. We found grapevine orthologues for 24,350 510 tomato genes and tomato orthologues for 24,099 grapevine genes, a substantial 511 increase over previous studies, which reported 16,454 and 15,631 orthology 512 relationships in tomato and grapevine, respectively (Ambrosino et al., 2018). By also 513 providing medium and lower confidence orthologues, we highlight the ability to 514 extract more comprehensive evolutionary signals from genome annotations, 515 providing a stronger basis for comparative genomics and functional inference. 516 While the orthology example use case focuses on plant genomes, we expect that 517 this tool and the rest of the AEGIS suite will be broadly applicable to the analysis of 518 animal and other eukaryotic genomes as well. We anticipate that AEGIS will be a 519 valuable framework as it unifies essential manipulation tasks in genome annotation 520 around a flexible object-oriented core from which further functionalities can be 521 reliably developed. 522
