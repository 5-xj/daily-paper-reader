Abstract 7

The information encoded in DNA sequences can be rigorously quantified using Shannon en-8

tropy and related measures. When placed in an evolutionary context, this quantification offers 9

a principled yet underexplored route to constructing gene regulatory networks (GRNs) directly 10 

from sequence data. While most GRN inference methods rely exclusively on gene expression 11 

profiles, the regulatory code is ultimately written in the DNA sequence itself. Here we review 12 

the mathematical foundations of information theory as applied to gene sequences, survey ex-13 

isting computational methods for GRN inference—with emphasis on information-theoretic and 14 

sequence-based approaches—and examine how evolutionary conservation constrains sequence 15 

entropy to preserve biological function. We then propose a four-layer integrative framework that 16 

combines per-position Shannon entropy profiles, evolutionary conservation scoring via Jensen– 17 

Shannon divergence, expression-based mutual information and transfer entropy, and DNA foun-18 

dation model embeddings to construct GRNs from sequence. Through worked examples on the 19 

Escherichia coli SOS regulatory sub-network, we demonstrate how conservation-weighted mutual 20 

information improves edge discrimination and how transfer entropy resolves regulatory direc-21 

tionality. The framework generates testable predictions: edges supported by low-entropy regu-22 

latory regions should show higher experimental validation rates, and cross-species entropy pro-23 

file conservation should predict GRN topology conservation. This work bridges three scales of 24 

biological information—nucleotide-level entropy, evolutionary constraint patterns, and network-25 

level regulatory logic—establishing information entropy as the natural mathematical language for 26 

sequence-to-network regulatory inference. 27

vation, transfer entropy, DNA foundation model, sequence information content 29

Information theory, founded by Shannon [Shannon, 1948], provides a natural mathematical frame-43 

work for quantifying the functional information encoded in DNA sequences. Shannon entropy 44 

H(X) = − ∑ p(x) log 2 p(x) measures uncertainty in a random variable; applied to a column in a mul-45 

tiple sequence alignment, it quantifies how conserved—and therefore how functionally important— 46 

each nucleotide position is. The complement of entropy defines information content (IC), which Schnei-47 

der and colleagues [Schneider et al., 1986] demonstrated equals the information needed to locate 48 

functional sites in the genome, establishing a deep connection between sequence-level bits and bio-49 

logical function. 50 

Mutual information (MI), conditional MI (CMI), and transfer entropy (TE) extend this framework 51 

to capture pairwise dependencies, direct interactions, and directional information flow, respectively. 52 

These quantities have been applied extensively to expression data for GRN inference [Zhang et al., 53 

2012, 2015, Kim et al., 2021], but their application to sequence-level features for network construction 54 

remains fragmented. Meanwhile, evolutionary biology provides a critical missing link: natural se-55 

lection constrains the entropy of functionally important sequences across phylogenetic time [Koonin, 56 

2016], and an estimated 5.5% of the human genome is under selective constraint [Siepel et al., 2005]. 57 

This evolutionary signal, quantifiable through information-theoretic measures, encodes which se-58 

quences carry regulatory information and how tightly constrained that information is. 59 

In this article, we review the mathematical foundations of information entropy applied to gene 60 

sequences (Section 2), survey information-theoretic methods for GRN inference (Section 4), examine 61 

how evolution shapes sequence entropy and regulatory network architecture (Section 5), and pro-62 

pose a novel four-layer integrative framework (Section 6) that uses sequence information entropy in 63 

evolutionary context to construct GRNs. We illustrate the framework with a worked example on the 64 

E. coli SOS regulatory sub-network (Section 7). 65

2.4 Compression-Based Complexity Measures 104 

Kolmogorov complexity K(s)—the length of the shortest program computing string s—provides the 105 

theoretical gold standard for sequence complexity but is not computable. Practical approximations 106 

use the Lempel–Ziv (LZ) algorithm, whose normalized complexity CLZ = c(n) · log α(n)/n converges 107 

to the entropy rate. Applied to genomes, LZ complexity reveals that coding regions are nearly in-108 

compressible while introns and repetitive regions show variable compressibility [Orlov and Potapov, 109 

2004]. LZ-based distance measures enable alignment-free phylogenetic reconstruction, recapitulating 110 

known evolutionary relationships from mitochondrial genomes. 111 

3 Information Content of Regulatory Elements 112 

3.1 Position Weight Matrices and Molecular Information Theory 113 

Schneider’s molecular information theory [Schneider et al., 1986] established that the total informa-114 

tion in a set of binding sites approximates the information needed to locate those sites in the genome: 115 

Rsequence ≈ Rfrequency = log 2(G/γ), where G is genome size and γ is the number of sites. For E. coli 116 

ribosome binding sites, this predicts ∼10 bits—matching observation. Sequence logos [Schneider 117 

and Stephens, 1990] visualize these quantities: stack height equals IC, and individual letter heights 118 

equal pb × IC (Figure 1C). 119 

3.2 k-mer Approaches and Deep Learning 120 

The gapped k-mer SVM [Ghandi et al., 2014] uses combinations of short gapped k-mers for enhancer 121 

prediction, achieving 2 × improvement in precision over PWM-based methods. The “bag-of-k-mers” 122 

approach [Mejia-Guerra and Buckler, 2019] uses Shannon entropy to filter low-complexity k-mers, 123 

demonstrating that regulatory k-mers occupy a characteristic entropy range. 124 

Deep learning models have transformed regulatory element prediction. DeepSEA [Zhou and 125 

Troyanskaya, 2015] predicts 919 chromatin features from 1 kb sequences. Enformer [Avsec et al., 126 

2021], a transformer-CNN hybrid processing 100+ kb context windows, models enhancer–promoter 127 

interactions through self-attention—functionally analogous to MI between sequence positions. DNA 128 

foundation models including DNABERT-2 [Zhou et al., 2023] and Evo 2 learn implicit entropy land-129 

scapes through cross-entropy loss minimization. Critically, Gummadi and Yella [Gummadi and 130 

Yella, 2025] demonstrated that DNA sequence perplexity —exponentiated cross-entropy from lan-131 

guage models—reveals evolutionarily conserved patterns in cis-regulatory regions across diverse 132 

species: regulatory regions consistently exhibit lower perplexity than flanking regions, establishing 133 

foundation-model perplexity as an evolutionarily conserved information-theoretic signature of reg-134 

ulatory function. 135 

4 GRN Inference: A Survey of Information-Theoretic Methods 136 

4.1 Classical Expression-Based Methods 137 

ARACNE [Margolin et al., 2006] computes pairwise MI and applies the Data Processing Inequality 138 

(DPI) to prune indirect interactions: for any Markov chain X → Z → Y, I(X; Y) ≤ min {I(X; Z), I(Z; Y)},139 

so the weakest edge is removed (Figure 3C). CLR [Faith et al., 2007] normalizes MI against adaptive 140 

backgrounds using z-scores. GENIE3 [Huynh-Thu et al., 2010], using Random Forest regression, won 141 

the DREAM4/5 challenges and remains a GRN inference cornerstone. 142

4.3 Transfer Entropy for Directionality 150 

TENET [Kim et al., 2021] applies TE to pseudo-time-ordered single-cell data, discovering regulatory 151

SCENIC+ [González-Blas et al., 2023] constructs enhancer-driven regulons (eRegulons) by integrat-157

uses lifelong neural networks combining single-cell multiome data with atlas-scale external data for 159 

GRN inference, achieving substantial accuracy improvements over existing methods. These methods 160 

demonstrate that incorporating sequence features substantially improves GRN inference, yet none 161 

systematically uses information entropy of sequence as a core inference feature. Table 1 summarizes 162 

these approaches. 163 

5 Evolution Shapes Sequence Entropy 164 

5.1 Conservation, Entropy, and Functional Constraint 165 

The fundamental principle linking information theory to evolution is that natural selection reduces 166 

entropy at functionally important positions [Koonin, 2016]. Sites with low entropy have low dN /dS 167 

ratios, confirming that information content correlates with functional constraint (Figure 1D). Phast-168 

Cons [Siepel et al., 2005] and phyloP [Pollard et al., 2010] formalize this through phylogenetic hidden 169 

Markov models. An estimated 5.5% of the human genome is under selective constraint, of which 170 

∼1.5% encodes proteins and ∼3.5% has regulatory functions. 171

5.2 Differential Entropy Across Genomic Compartments 172 

Information entropy systematically varies across genomic compartments. Promoters show the lowest 173 

entropy (highest conservation) reflecting fixed TF binding motifs; exons show intermediate entropy 174 

constrained by coding function; introns approach maximum entropy (Figure 1A). Per-triplet mea-175 

surements confirm this hierarchy: coding regions average ∼5.6 bits versus ∼5.82 bits for non-coding 176 

regions out of a maximum of 6 bits. This gradient has a direct interpretation: regions with low en-177 

tropy encode more biological information and are under stronger selective constraint. 178 

Ultra-conserved elements (UCEs)—481 segments >200 bp with 100% identity between human, 179 

rat, and mouse—represent the extreme of zero positional entropy and maximum information content. 180 

The probability of UCEs arising under neutrality is < 10 −22 .181 

5.3 Coevolution and Phylogenetic Correction 182 

Detecting coevolution through MI requires correction for phylogenetic signal. The APC [Dunn et al., 183 

2008] subtracts expected background MI. Colavin et al. [Colavin et al., 2022] demonstrated that ex-184 

tracting phylogenetic dimensions of coevolution reveals hidden functional signals, using phyloge-185 

netic distance as an analytical dimension to identify functional sectors that coevolve on different 186 

timescales. A minimum of ∼400 sequences at <62% identity is needed for meaningful MI-based 187 

coevolution predictions [Marino Buslje et al., 2009]. 188 

5.4 Evolution of Regulatory Network Architecture 189 

Regulatory networks evolve primarily through binding site turnover. Davidson and Erwin demon-190 

strated hierarchical conservation: core specification subcircuits (“kernels”) are extremely ancient, 191 

while peripheral connections evolve rapidly. A 2025 Nature Genetics study showed that regulatory 192 

element position is conserved between mouse and chicken embryonic hearts despite sequence diver-193 

gence, with 5 × more orthologous regulatory elements found by position-based than alignment-based 194 

methods. This suggests that entropy-based analysis of regulatory properties may capture conserva-195 

tion invisible to sequence alignment. 196 

6 A Four-Layer Integrative Framework 197 

6.1 The Integration Gap 198 

Current GRN inference operates in separate paradigms. Expression-based methods capture statisti-199 

cal relationships but ignore the sequence basis of regulation. Sequence-based methods (motif scan-200 

ning, PWMs) identify potential binding sites but cannot determine functional activity. Evolutionary 201 

methods (phylogenetic footprinting) identify constrained elements but do not construct networks. 202 

No framework systematically connects sequence-level information content through evolutionary 203 

conservation to network-level regulatory inference. 204 

6.2 Framework Architecture 205 

We propose a four-layer framework (Figure 2B): 206 

Layer 1: Sequence Information Landscape. For each gene’s regulatory region (promoter, prox-207 

imal enhancers, UTRs), compute: (i) per-position Shannon entropy from multi-species alignments 208 

(Eq. 1); (ii) foundation model perplexity from DNA language model predictions; (iii) Lempel–Ziv 209 

complexity profiles. These capture complementary aspects: positional conservation, deviation from 210 

learned genomic grammar, and higher-order sequential patterns. 211

7 Worked Example: E. coli SOS Sub-network 232 

To illustrate the framework, we apply it to the well-characterized SOS DNA damage response in 233 

E. coli , regulated by the LexA repressor and RecA activator (Figure 3). 234 

7.1 Step 1: Expression Data and MI Computation 235 

Consider six SOS genes: lexA , recA , uvrA , uvrB , sulA , and umuD . From a simulated expression matrix 236 

across 12 conditions (Figure 3A), we compute the pairwise MI matrix (Figure 3B). The highest MI 237 

values are: I(lexA ; recA ) = 0.72, I(uvrA ; uvrB ) = 0.80, I(recA ; uvrA ) = 0.65. 238 

7.2 Step 2: DPI Pruning 239 

Applying the DPI to the triplet ( lexA , recA , uvrA ): since I(lexA ; uvrA ) = 0.55 < min (0.72, 0.65 ), the 240 

lexA –uvrA edge is pruned as potentially indirect (mediated through recA ; Figure 3C). However, this 241 

is biologically incorrect—LexA directly represses uvrA . This illustrates a known DPI limitation: it can 242 

remove true direct interactions. 243

8 Predicted Performance and Testable Hypotheses 258 

Based on published benchmark results and the theoretical advantages of incorporating sequence-259 

level information, we project that the proposed framework should outperform expression-only meth-260 

ods (Figure 4A). The entropy–conservation landscape (Figure 4B) reveals clear separation between 261 

genomic compartments, providing natural thresholds for edge confidence. 262 

The framework generates three testable predictions: (1) edges mapped to low-entropy regula-263 

tory regions will show higher experimental validation rates in ChIP-seq and perturbation datasets; 264 

(2) cross-species conservation of regulatory entropy profiles will predict GRN topology conserva-265 

tion, enabling regulatory knowledge transfer between organisms; (3) foundation model perplexity 266 

will outperform alignment-based conservation for predicting active regulatory elements, because it 267 

captures non-linear patterns that linear alignment misses. 268 

9 Discussion 269 

We have presented an integrative framework that uses information entropy of gene sequences in 270 

evolutionary context to construct gene regulatory networks. The central insight is that information 271 

entropy provides the natural mathematical language connecting three scales of gene regulation: the 272 

nucleotide sequence where regulatory information is encoded, the evolutionary process that shapes 273 

and constrains this information, and the network that executes it. 274 

Several methodological considerations merit discussion. First, the entropy measures are sensitive 275 

to alignment quality and depth—a minimum of ∼400 sequences at <62% identity is recommended 276 

for meaningful MI-based coevolution [Marino Buslje et al., 2009]. Second, the conservation weight 277 

wcons assumes that high conservation implies functional importance, which generally holds but may 278 

miss recently evolved regulatory innovations. The framework addresses this partially through the 279 

foundation model perplexity component, which captures functional signatures independent of con-280 

servation. 281 

The relationship to existing hybrid methods deserves clarification. SCENIC+ and LINGER in-282 

corporate sequence features through motif scanning but do not explicitly use information-theoretic 283

10 Conclusion 298 

Information entropy, from the 2 bits per nucleotide position to the directed information flow across 299 

thousands of genes, provides a unified mathematical language for gene regulatory network construc-300 

tion. The convergence of foundation models trained on thousands of species, single-cell multi-omics 301 

data, and validated GRN benchmarks creates an unprecedented opportunity. The framework pro-302 

posed here provides the principled mathematical scaffolding to integrate these data types, bridging 303 

the persistent gap between sequence-level information content and network-level regulatory logic. 304 

We anticipate that evolutionary information-theoretic approaches will become an essential comple-305 

ment to expression-based methods in the next generation of GRN inference tools. 306
