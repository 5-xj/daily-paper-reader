Abstract 18 

Fusarium graminearum is a fungal pathogen that causes scab or head blight in small grain 19 

cereals and threatens global cereal production. Disease progression varies widely among 20 

individual plants of the same cultivar, reflecting both genetic and environmental heterogeneity. 21 

This complicates the identification of early host responses, because each individual plant in a 22 

population is at a slightly different phase of disease progression. Here we apply single plant -23 

transcriptomics to a population of 121 malt -barley exposed to F. graminearum , enabling us to 24 

reconstruct a temporal sequence of regulatory processes during early infection . We identif ied 25 

several disease -resistance associated genetic variants that are already endemic in this high -26 

yielding cultivar, suggesting potential as breeding targets. These variants were within proteins 27 

involved in ROS -burst production, a lectin -kinase PRR, and enzymes with DON -detoxification 28 

activity. Single plant -transcriptomics offers a novel strategy for characterising early plant -29 

pathogen interactions, turning intra -population heterogeneity from an experimental barrier into 30 

an asset. 31

Fusarium head blight (FHB) or scab, caused by the fungal pathogen Fusarium graminearum 35 

Schwabe , is a seed and residue -borne disease of cereal crops such as wheat, barley, oat, and 36 

rye. Currently the disease is widespread in North America, and around the Mediterranean sea, 37 

but it is thought to have some presence in all wheat growing countries in the world 1,2,3 .38 

Fusarium graminearum infection causes direct yield losses through infecting plant vascular 39 

tissue (wilt), and at anthesis through penetration of the developing seed and subsequent 40 

necrotrophic growth (head blight) which can cause total loss of yield in favourable conditions. 41 

A serious problem associated with FHB infection is the production of mycotoxins in the grain 42 

leading to grade loss. Of the various different mycotoxins associated with FHB in barley ,43 

deoxynivalenol (DON) produced by F. graminearum is of a primary concern as it affects malting 44 

quality and can cause vomiting or intestinal distress when ingested by humans or used as 45 

animal feed 4,5 . In some cases a fungal infection will produce enough DON to render the seed 46 

unusable, even though the yield appears high 6. The Canadian Grain Commission's survey 47 

estimated economic losses due to FHB predicted to be 1 billion CAD in 2016. 48 

A challenge in understanding how plants respond to F. graminearum infection is the 49 

heterogeneity of the plant’s response, even within a single cultivar 7,8 . This contrasts with many 50 

other cereal diseases such as rusts or powdery mildews where resistant crops tend to be very 51 

consistent in their phenotypes 9. In barley, heterogeneity in response to F. graminearum 52 

infection is derive d from both intrinsic and extrinsic sources. Intrinsic heterogeneity stem s53 

from genetic factors governing the response to infection; for instance, genetic factors 54 

associated with awn physiology often impact Fusarium resistance in barley 10 . Extrinsic factors 55 

include the density and viability of the initial spore inoculum, as well as microenvironmental 56 

conditions influencing spore germination and heterogeneous infection rates . Furthermore , F. 57 

graminearum strains vary in DON isoform production and secretion rates, that further 58 

contribute to variable disease symptoms 11,12 . Consequently , at any given time post infection ,59 

individual plant s will be at different phase s of disease progression and/or immune response. 60 

Single plant -omics provides a framework to resolve these asynchronous biological processes .61 

In single cell RNA -seq analysis, individual cells are ordered by their transcriptional trajectories 62 

to produce a pseudo -time series, often revealing developmental or temporal patterns in 63 

expression 13 . Similarly, performing transcriptomics on a large population of individual plants 64 

permits post -hoc ordering by transcriptional trajectories , in our case order ing the barley plants 65 

based on transcriptional signatures of infection progression. We can then map how 66 

physiological factors and gene expression values change over the pseudo -time course , to 67 

determine the order of transcriptional events that occur during the infection process and 68 

identify specific alleles or gene expression variation patterns that may contribute to infection 69 

progression. Single plant -omics has previously been used to dissect transcriptomic variation 70 

associated with yield heterogeneity in field trials 14 , describe gene expression noise over a 71 

diurnal cycle 15 , and unravel the transcriptional dynamics during developmental phase 72 

transitions 13, 16 . However, this technique has not previously been deployed to investigate 73 

immune responses in any plant species. 74 

Additionally, we identified genetic variants contributing to the heterogeneity in F. graminearum 75 

responses in the cultivar, as no breeding process completely eliminates SNPs from the seed 76 

pool. Traditionally, variants associated with disease response are identified through a genome -77 

wide association study (GWAS) across genetically diverse line s17 . Here, we show that it is 78

Differentially expressed genes in F. graminearum -treated barley are associated 90

A single plant -transcript omics experiment was performed on cv. AAC Synergy barley plants 92 

(Fig . 1), derived from three self -pollinated plants from the breeder seed source . AAC Synergy is 93 

an elite , popular Canadian malt barley cultivar , sharing alleles with many other popular field 94 

cultivars 18 . It has intermediate FHB resistance and heterogeneous responses between 95 

individuals , making it well suited to capture a broa d range of disease responses 18 .96 

A total of 121 plants were spray inoculated with F. graminearum spores ( Fg -treated ) and 41 97 

were mock -treated . For each sample, we measured ph enot ypic factors including plant height, 98 

Fusarium Damaged Kernels (FDK) and total kernels at three timepoints post -inoculation, final 99 

head weight, and DON concentration in head tissue (Table S1 ). RNA -seq was performed using 100 

the topmost two spikelets harvested five days after inoculation, to measure gene expression 101 

and identify genetic variants in the coding sequences (Table S2 ). As breeder seed source was 102 

used , the line was extremely pure . RNA -seq identified a total of 3 910 SNPs in exons across the 103 

population relative to the expected reference , although it is important to note that variant 104 

calling quality is correlated to expression levels and so there are likely many undiscovered 105 

SNPs in non -coding or poorly expressed genes. 106

scaffolds and those that were differentially expressed between the two sub -populations , so the 111 

impact of the sub -population structure does not dominate our results (Fig . S1 C). The treatment 112 

and control plants were indistinguishable in the PCA of genetic variants, confirming that the 113 

genotypic patterns in the data were not primarily driven by infection -associated expression 114

A transcriptional trajectory reveals defence processes associated with fungal 140 

transcript abundance 141 

Transcriptional profiles of F. graminearum -infected barley revealed substantial expression 142 

heterogeneity between plants (Fig. S3 ); notably, these gene expression levels correlate d143 

significantly with disease -associated traits ( Fig. 2 C). Th ese data indicate an underlying 144 

transcriptional trajectory (pseudotime) among infected barley plants , reflecting the 145 

asynchronous progression of the disease . Peudotime was defined as an ordering of individual 146 

plants in which genes in a specified set change their expression monotonically, as in Redmond 147 

et al 13 . Two separate pseudotime rankings were constructed on a training set of F. 148 

graminearum infected plants (n=93) , using the sets of genes that showed either higher or lower 149 

expression in Fg -treated plants compared with controls. 150

The sequence of gene expression events in response to F. graminearum challenge is intuitive .168 

Early stage genes ( calcium ion signalling, general -purpose chitinase, and UDP -169

Each plant would be expected to be at different phases of disease progression due to different 175 

fungal loadings. We hypothesized that the proposed plant orderings we developed 176 

(pseudotimes ) represent the progression of response to F. graminearum infection . Supporting 177 

this hypothesis, we found that both pseudotimes (developed using either up - or down -178 

regulated genes) were more strongly correlated with disease symptom -related traits than dry 179 

weight ( Fig . 3D ). The pseudotime associated with up -regulated genes was more strongly 180 

associated with percent fungal RNA than the one made using down -regulated genes, but the 181 

inverse was true of AUDPC and DON , consistent with our earlier findings .182 

Genetic variants in an elite barley cultivar are associated with disease progression 183 

Despite being a single cultivar, AAC Synergy harbours some genetic heterogeneity. We 184 

hypothesized that some of these variants may be associated with disease symptom severity, 185 

which could serve as potential markers for breeding for Fusarium resistance. Moreover, as 186 

there are sparse numbers of gene variants within a single cultivar (with all variants shown in Fig .187 

S5), we hypothesized that GWAS conducted on this dataset may be enriched for causal variants 188 

compared to a traditional GWAS , where the causal variants are often hidden within large 189 

linkage disequilibrium blocks. 190 

Supporting this hypothesis, we performed a GWAS and found that nearly all the variants 191 

associated with traits of interest lie within genes whose functions have previously been linked 192 

with the plant immune regulatory network ( Fig . 4, Table S6 ). Variants associated with 193 

pseudotime were found to be associated with ROS production 28 , gylycosyltransferase activity 25 ,194 

receptor kinase activity and downstream CDPK activity 29 , and transcriptional reprogramming 195 

(Fig . S6 ). Over 60% of variants that were found to be associated with pseudotimes and disease 196 

phenotypes were within gene bodies of known plant immunity genes. These results suggest 197 

that performing GWAS within a single near isogenic population may be an effective strategy for 198 

identifying intra -population variants associated with traits of interest that may be causal. 199

One challenge is that each barley spike experiences a different fungal load due to the spray -204 

based inoculation strategy, which is further exacerbated by temporal variation in F. 205 

graminearum spore germination and infection events . These factors increase heterogeneity in 206 

disease symptoms due to factors that are independent of the plant’s genetic background. To 207 

reduce this environment -driven variability, we include two technical replicates, i.e., samples 208 

taken from two heads of the same barley plant. These two heads share the same genetic 209 

background but are exposed to distinct fungal loads. Disease -related traits were positively 210 

associated with each other across technical replicates ( Fig . 5A ). To reduce the heterogeneity in 211 

these traits caused by environmental factors, we took an average across both replicates in 212 

subsequent analysis. 17 genetic variants that were found in at least one GWAS were present in 213 

our validation set (Fig. S 7). 214 

We confirmed that these variants were associated with disease symptoms. PC1 was more 215 

highly correlated with AUDPC, especially how large the lesion was in the earliest time point ,216 

Disease Progression Index (DPI) day 7, suggesting that PC1 captures how quickly lesions form 217 

(Fig . 5B ). On the other hand, PC2 is more correlated with % fungal load and DON 218 

concentration, indicating that this PC is associated with severity of infection ( Fig . 5B ). Genes 219 

that contributed to PC1 and PC2 are highlighted in Fig . 5C . A more careful examination of the 220 

PCA plots reveals that plants that have high values of PC1 and PC2 tend to have high AUDPC 221 

(Fig . 5D ) and DON ( Fig . 5E ). On the other hand, plants that had low PC1 and PC2 tended to 222 

have high AUDPC and percent fungal RNA ( Fig . 5F ). This reveals that plants containing genetic 223 

variants that have low PC1 and PC2 would have an optimal combination of traits. The genetic 224 

variants found in the GWAS analysis were associated with fusarium symptoms in an 225 

independent set of plants, demonstrating the potential of our approach for suggesting targets 226 

for marker -assisted selection of Fusarium resistance. 227 

# Discussion 228 

We set out to characterise symptom heterogeneity within a single barley cultivar, AAC Synergy, 229 

with the aims of (i) associating early gene expression with later disease -related traits (ii) 230 

identifying variants that are already present within this elite cultivar that could be used in 231 

marker -assisted selection of more Fusarium resistant barley plants. Our work provided 232 

fundamental insights into early response to F. graminearum infection in barley. In doing so, we 233 

demonstrate that single plant -omics is a powerful new tool for investigating plant -pathogen 234 

interactions. 235 

Our single plant transcriptomics approach yielded critical insights into the relationship 236 

between fungal load, visible symptoms and DON mycotoxins within the barley population. 237 

Early fungal load was not associated with later symptoms. Gene expression and % fungal RNA 238 

measurements are derived from the top two spikelets, while AUDPC and DON information is 239 

derived from the remainder of the head. Therefore, the weak association between percent 240 

fungal RNA and later symptoms may be a result of spatial heterogeneity in fungal load. An 241 

alternative explanation is that heterogeneity in immune responses within the population 242 

weakens the association between initial fungal load and later symptoms. 243 

Genes that were differentially expressed early in F. graminearum infection were associated with 244 

the current fungal load and with later visible symptoms (AUDPC) and DON mycotoxin 245 

concentrations. Genes that were up -regulated during infection were more closely associated 246

indicator that the plant’s immune response was not effectively coping with the infection. 250 

Overall, our approach was able to uncover most major known pathways involved in response to 251 

F. graminearum challenge (Fig . 4), highlighting several key players associated with visible 252 

symptoms and DON mycotoxin concentrations. 253 

The gene families associated with lowered AUDPC included those predicted to encode protein 254 

kinases ( CDPKs or CPKs )29,30 , plant lectins (ricin B -like lectin) 31 , actin cytoskeleton 32,33 , reactive 255 

oxygen species (ROS) activity 28 , E3 ubiquitin ligases 34 and zinc finger transcription factors 35 ,3 6.256 

These families have a well -studied role in defence response, playing an integral role in 257 

extracellular recognition of pathogens and producing an immune response (often collectively 258 

referred to as PTI). It is hard to assess whether upregulation of receptor -kinases and similar 259 

plant immunity proteins is a response to pathogen infection or causes a reduction in 260 

symptoms, or if successful infection reduces the expression of critical R -genes. However, 261 

conventional wisdom regarding plant immunity indicates that the latter is more likely: in general 262 

R-genes are found to be highly expressed in steady -state growth conditions (eg in sufficient 263 

quantity that their stimulation produces an immediate and effective reaction) . It is unclear how 264 

a recognition event producing increased receptor transcription and translation would provide a 265 

short -term benefit to the plant . Instead , the recognition event would be expected to have a 266 

regulatory effect on the downstream mechanisms of defence such as via zinc -finger 267 

transcription factors and subsequent synthesis of antimicrobials, cell wall strengthening, 268 

withdrawal of resources from the infected area, etc. Furthermore, it is well established that 269 

successful biotrophic or hemi -biotrophic infection suppresses the plant immune response 270 

against all pathogens, not just the infecting species, as secondary infection by an avirulent 271 

pathovar is possible once the immune system has been suppressed by the virulent pathovar 37 .272 

The genes associated with DON mycotoxin levels were primarily glycosyltransferases /beta -273 

glucosidases. This category of protein has been previously identified as having a role in 274 

detoxification of DON via glycos ylation and preventing its effects 25 ,38. Additional genes 275 

associated with low DON levels and not with low infection in general were those associated 276 

with lipid and polysaccharide metabolism, and cell cycle /homeostasis (including ATP 277 

synthase) 39,40 . In this case, the role of these genes is unclear: it may be that high levels of DON 278 

disrupts these pathways to the pathogen’s benefit rather than that their expression produces 279 

low DON levels. 280 

A central goal in studying plant -pathogen interactions is to understand the sequence of 281 

regulatory processes that occur during the onset of infection. The most common experimental 282 

approach for investigating these dynamics is a time -series design, in which samples are 283 

collected at regular intervals following infection. However, individual plants often progress 284 

through infection at different rates due to asynchronous responses, making fine -scale mapping 285 

of regulatory processes difficult. An alternative strategy is to leverage this asynchrony. By 286 

sampling individual plants that were infected simultaneously but are at different stages of 287 

infection, we can order them according to their degree of disease progression (a pseudotime). 288 

This allows us to observe how gene expression patterns change along the pseudotime axis, 289 

providing a clearer picture of the transcriptional sequence underlying early infection. One 290

limitation of this approach is that, while it can reveal overarching trajectories of transcriptional 291 

states across plants, it does not identify the source of heterogeneity. For example, differences 292 

among trajectories could arise from variation in the initial fungal load, differences in the rate of 293 

immune response, or a combination of both factors. 294 

Among genes that had higher expression under Fg -treated barley than controls, we found one 295 

cluster that was expressed early in the pseudotime that included chitinase activity and calcium 296 

binding. These processes are both important in early infection, where calcium ion fluxes act as 297 

early intracellular signals in response to fungal elicitors like chitin oligomers triggering 298 

downstream immune response and chitinases physically break chitins in fungal cell walls or 299 

cause cell wall remode lling during infection 41,42. Glutothionine -related genes are expressed 300 

next in our pseudotime. Glutothionine is known to modulate hormone and redox responses to 301 

infection, as well as trigger leaf -to -leaf calcium signalling 43. This phase also coincides with the 302 

activation of glycosylotransferase genes, which play a pivotal role in DON detoxification , as 303 

previously highlighted 25 . Finally, late in the pseudotime genes are expressed that are 304 

associated with the extracellular region and strictosidine synthase, which catalyses the first 305 

step of monoterpene alkaloid synthesis, a key aspect of a plants’ chemical defense. 306 

This pseudotime was strongly associated with percent fungal RNA and, to a lesser extent, later 307 

symptoms. Therefore, the inherent transcriptional trajectory within the population is 308 

biologically meaningful, although we cannot determine whether initial fungal load or 309 

heterogeneous responses to infection led to these trajectories. 310 

A key outcome of the analysis is a collection of genetic variants that are associated with 311 

transcriptional trajectories (pseudotimes), percent fungal RNA, visible symptoms and DON 312 

concentrations. Almost every one of these variants are within known defense -related genes, 313 

suggesting that these variants are enriched for causal variants. This is in contrast with 314 

traditional GWAS outcomes, where the causal variant is often hidden within a large linkage 315 

disequilibrium block 17 . The variants include those within a respiratory burst oxidase B -like, 316 

which generates oxidative bursts early in the fungal defense response 45 and COP9 signalosome, 317 

a key ubiquitination factor that orchestrates ROS and hormone signalling in response to 318 

pathogens 46.319 

The collection of variants that were identified are significantly associated with disease 320 

symptom severity in an independent set of plants, suggesting that these variants could be used 321 

in marker -assisted selection to develop sub -populations of AAC Synergy plants with reduced 322 

Fusarium susceptibility. Since the se genetic variants all exist endemically within the AAC 323 

Synergy population, a high -yielding variety, and are only weakly associated with traits like dry 324 

mass, they are less likely to have a pronounced negative impact on yield -related traits than a 325 

Fusarium -resistance variant that is identified from a non -elite variety. 326 

Single plant -omics has been used extensively to investigate plant developmental transitions, 327 

but here we show that it has much broader applications in investigating other traits with 328 

asynchronous timing, such as defense. 329 

The approach taken here recapitulates a good deal of the past 20 years of molecular research 330 

into plant immune systems (Fig . 4). Without deliberately setting out to identify known 331 

components of the conventional plant defense response pathways, our single plant omics 332

# Online Methods 480 

Plant material and growth conditions 481 

As well as being the most popular malting barley cultivar in western Canada, AAC Synergy 482 

(pedigree: TR02267/Newdale; TR02267 = T253/AC Metcalfe) is derived from elite Canadian 483 

barley breeding germplasm, and shares alleles with many other lines grown in the field. AAC 484 

Synergy is a Canadian 2 -row malting spring barley cultivar developed in 2012 by Agriculture and 485 

Agrifood Canada 18 . Seeds (Breeder seed source) of barley cultivar AAC Synergy were obtained 486 

from Agriculture and Agri -Food Canada. Three plants were grown to maturity under 487 

experimental conditions and self -pollinated to generate the seed stock used in this study. 488 

Seeds were sown individually into 1 L vertical tube pots (30 cm depth) filled with Sunshine Mix 489 

#1 (Sun Gro, Canada) supplemented with Miracle -Gro slow -release fertilizer. Plants were 490 

cultivated in a Conviron PGR15 reach -in growth chamber (Conviron, Winnipeg, Canada) under a 491 

24/19°C day/night cycle, with a 16 h photoperiod, 800 lumens LED light intensity, and 70% 492 

relative humidity. Post -inoculation humidity occasionally rose to 80 –90% but returned to 493 

baseline within one hour. Plants were watered every other day at the base to avoid wetting the 494 

heads and upper leaves. Weekly monitoring included measurements of height and 495 

developmental stage, and plants were rotated within the chamber to ensure uniform exposure 496 

to light and humidity. Inoculation with Fusarium graminearum spore suspension or distilled 497 

water procedural control was performed at anthesis, determined by manual inspection. 498 

Treatment assignment was randomized using the Excel RAND() function. Physiological 499 

measurements are detailed in Supplementary Data Set 1. 500

The F. graminearum strain SK -17 -97 , a 3ADON chemotype isolated from wheat in 502 

Saskatchewan, was used for inoculation. A five -day -old colony grown on 2% PDA at 24°C in 503

Disease inoculation and phenotyping 509 

During half -anthesis (Zadoks stage 65), heads were spray -inoculated with 1 mL of conidial 510 

suspension or ddH₂O control from a distance of 10 cm, following removal of the flag leaf. 511 

Inoculated heads were enclosed in pre -wetted plastic bags for 48 hours to facilitate infection. 512 

Each plant received one inoculation, typically on the primary head, with additional heads 513 

treated when development permitted. Disease severity was assessed at 7-, 11 -, 15 -, and 20 -514 

days post -inoculation (dpi) by calculating the proportion of spikelets exhibiting white mycelium. 515 

The area under the disease progress curve (AUDPC) was computed using the formula: 516 

proportion × number of days 47 . Secondary infections and mechanical damage were recorded. 517 

Mature heads were retained for DON analysis. Inoculation and infection tracking details are 518 

provided in Supplementary Data Set 1. 519 

DON quantification and chemotype profiling 520 

Mature infected and control heads were dried with silica gel for three days, weighed, and 521 

ground into fine powder using a Qiagen Tissuelyser II (Qiagen, USA). Samples were aliquoted 522 

into tubes containing <= 100 mg of tissue. Each aliquot was extracted with 1 mL of LC -MS grade 523 

acetonitrile containing 0.1% formic acid, nutated for 1 hour at room temperature, and 524 

centrifuged at 10,000 × g for 2 minutes. Extracts were analyzed using an LTQ Orbitrap XL Hybrid 525 

Ion Trap mass spectrometer (Thermo Scientific) coupled to a Dionex Ultimate 3000 UHPLC 526 

system. Separation was performed on a Kinetex C18 column (50 mm × 2.1 mm, 1.7 μm; 527 

Phenomenex) at 30°C with a flow rate of 0.35 mL/min. Mobile phases were water (A) and 528 

acetonitrile (B), both with 0.1% formic acid. The gradient included: 95% A for 0.5 min, linear 529 

ramp to 95% B over 4 min, hold at 95% B for 3.5 min, and re -equilibration at 95% A for 7 min. 530 

HRMS was conducted in positive electrospray ionization mode (ESI ⁺) with the following 531 

settings: m/z 100 –2000, resolution 30,000; sheath gas 40; auxiliary gas 5; sweep gas 2; source 532 

voltage 4.0 kV; capillary temperature 320°C; capillary voltage 35 V; tube lens voltage 100 V. 533 

Samples were injected in randomized order. DON and 3/15ADON were quantified using in -534 

house standards and Xcalibur 4.1 Quan Browser (Thermo Scientific), with result units 535 

expressed as μg per plant or μg per mg of tissue. Supplementary Data Set 1 includes tissue 536 

weights and DON concentrations. 537

Data filtering steps 560 

Only genes whose sum of their CPM across all samples was greater than one were used in our 561 

analysis, with the threshold determined by visual inspection of an elbow plot (Fig S1A). A 562 

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was performed (prcomp, stats package version 4.3.2 ) on 563 

the variants (0 is homozygous matching reference genome, 1 heterozygous, 2 is homozygous 564 

different from reference). Two distinct clusters were identified (Fig S1B) across the first 565 

principal component (PC), and all variants with an absolute value of the loadings greater than 566 

0.02 along this first PC were removed from the subsequent analysis. Furthermore, genes that 567 

were differentially expressed between these two clusters were identified by a non -parametric 568 

Mann -Whitney U test 54 . All genes with a p -value less than 0.05 and whose absolute value of the 569 

log2 fold -change in expression between the two groups was greater than 1 were also filtered out 570 

from future transcriptomic analysis. 571 

Differential expression analysis 572 

Three different differentially expression analyses were performed: (i) Fusarium treatment 573 

(n=121) versus control group (n=41) (ii) top 25% versus bottom 25% AUDPC (within Fusarium 574 

treatment group) (iii) top 25% versus bottom 25% total DON μg/mg (within Fusarium treatment 575 

group). Comparison (i) was performed using a nonparametric Mann -Whitney U -test 54 , due to 576 

the large number of replicates, while (ii) and (iii) were performed using DESeq2 577 

(version1.48.2) 55 , using the raw read counts. In all cases, the p -value threshold was set at 0.05 578 

and the log2 -fold -change threshold was set at 1. For each gene that was identified as being 579 

differentially expressed, a Spearman’s correlation was calculated between its expression levels 580 

and the measured traits in each barley plant. 581

Pseudotime analysis 589 

The pseudotime analysis was only performed on plants that were treated with Fusarium and 590 

where only a single head was sampled. Two different gene sets were used to generate 591 

pseudotimes: genes with higher expression under treatment (up set) and genes with lower 592 

expression under treatment (down set). In both cases, we partitioned these gene lists into 300 593 

equal sets of genes. For each partition, we used the pseudotime inference method from 594 

Redmond et al. (2024) 13 . First, we scaled the gene expression values of each to be between 0 595 

(minimum expression) and 1 (maximum expression), then took a sum of those genes’ 596 

expression for each plant. Next, we ranked the plants from lowest to highest expression. We 597 

observed that the pseudotimes were consistent across the partitions so we determined the 598 

final pseudotime by ordering the plants by their average rank. The outcome of this method is 599 

that – overall – genes within this set will increase in their expression over the pseudotime. Gene 600 

expression over pseudotime was visualised using the pheatmap package version 1.0.13, with 601 

clusters determined by complete linkage hierarchical clustering 58 . GO enrichment for each 602 

cluster was determined, as previously described. For each pseudotime (generated from the up 603 

and down gene sets), Spearman’s correlation was calculated between the pseudotime rank 604 

and the traits measured. 605 

Genome -wide association study 606 

There are not many genetic variants within gene bodies within a single cultivar of barley, so we 607 

hypothesised that some of these are likely causally linked to phenotypes. For this reason, we 608 

only performed a very limited filtering of the variant list to construct a genetic map for GWAS. 609 

First, variants were filtered to only include ones where the major genotype was found in less 610 

than 90% of the samples, leaving 3198 variants in the genetic map. The statgenGWAS package 611 

was used to perform a GWAS analysis 59 , with kinship calculated using the Identical by State 612 

method and significance calculated by an False Discovery Rate metric (p -value threshold of 613 

0.05) as proposed by Brzyski D. 60 which identifies phenotype -aware representatives from each 614 

cluster of co -localised markers – a strategy that seemed most aligned with our decision to only 615 

do light filtering of the genetic map. Only plants that were treated with fusarium and where only 616 

one sample was sequenced by RNA -seq were included in this analysis. Manhattan plots were 617 

generated. To further investigate the significant variants, the 400bp region around the variants 618 

were extracted from the genomic DNA and then searched in NCBI BLASTx version 2.17.0 619 

(clustered nr database: https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Blast.cgi ) to identify the closest 620 

orthologs 61.621 

Validation of markers 622 

Our validation set consisted of plants that were treated with fusarium and had two heads 623 

separately sampled. All genetic variants that were associated with pseudotime up, pseudotime 624 

down, AUDPC, Total DON ug per mg, or percent fungal RNA were compiled. These variants 625 

were used to perform a PCA, with scaling prior to eigenvector decomposition, using the prcomp 626 

package 62. Spearman correlation was calculated between the two first principal components 627 

and the average value of the traits between the two technical replicates. 628
