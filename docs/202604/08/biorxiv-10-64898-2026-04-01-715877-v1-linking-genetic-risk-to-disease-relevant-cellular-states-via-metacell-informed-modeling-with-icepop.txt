# Abstract 10 

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have implicated thousands of loci in complex diseases, but trans-11 

lating these population-level signals into specific cellular contexts remains a central challenge. Integrating 12 

GWAS with single-cell transcriptomics data has enabled systematic identification of disease-relevant cell 13 

types, yet existing methods face a fundamental tradeoff: approaches like seismic that optimized for statistical 14 

power operate at the annotated cell-type level and miss heterogeneous disease signals concentrated in spe-15 

cific cellular states, while single-cell-resolution approaches like scDRS that capture such heterogeneity often 16 

lack sufficient power to detect subtle associations. Here we present ICePop ( Informative Ce ll Pop ulations), 17 

a framework that resolves this tradeoff by performing disease-cell type association at metacell resolution, 18 

thus achieving statistical power comparable to cell-type-level methods while detecting heterogeneous disease 19 

signals within cell types. In simulations against seismic and scDRS, ICePop maintains appropriate false 20 

positive rates and demonstrates superior power when disease effects are concentrated in cellular subpopu-21 

lations. Applied to Tabula Muris across 81 traits and 120 cell types, ICePop identifies 2,178 disease-cell 22 

type associations, including the preferential vulnerability of differentiated gut epithelial cells in ulcerative 23 

colitis and loss of cell identity in immune-stressed lung capillary endothelial cells underlying their association 24 

with lung function. Clustering diseases by metacell association profiles reveals groupings that diverge from 25 

genetic risk-based clustering, including separation of blood cell count traits from immune diseases despite 26 

shared genetic architecture, reflecting differences in cellular rather than genetic etiology. In autism spec-27 

trum disorder, ICePop identifies preferential enrichment of genetic risk in specific enteric neuron subtypes, 28 

implicating dysfunction of the enteric nervous system in gastrointestinal comorbidities. ICePop’s resolution 29 

of disease-relevant cell states within annotated cell types enables generation of testable, cell-state-specific 30 

hypotheses about disease mechanisms and therapeutic targets. 31 

Keywords: Single-cell RNA-seq, Metacell analysis, Disease-cell type association, GWAS integration 32 

# Introduction 33 

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified thousands of disease-associated loci, yet trans-34 

lating these population-level signals into specific cellular contexts remains a major challenge. A central 35 

question is which cell types and cellular states mediate genetic risk [1]. Resolving this question is critical for 36 

understanding disease mechanisms and prioritizing therapeutic targets. 37

To address this problem, many computational frameworks integrate GWAS signals with single-cell gene 38 

expression data to infer disease-associated cell types. A dominant strategy aggregates SNP-level associations 39 

into gene-level disease scores, typically using MAGMA [2], and then tests whether genes that are highly 40 

associated with disease are enriched among cell-type-specific expression programs. A representative method 41 

in this category is scDRS [3] defining putative disease-associated genes by selecting top-ranked genes based 42 

on MAGMA z-scores. For each individual cell, it samples control genes matching expression distribution of 43 

disease genes and gene set size, then tests whether the putative disease genes have higher expression than 44 

the matched controls. Statistical testing is performed at the single-cell level and subsequently aggregated 45 

to the cell-type level. This single-cell resolution enables scDRS to capture within-cell-type heterogeneity. 46 

scDRS implicitly assumes that disease-associated genes should exhibit higher overall expression than control 47 

genes, which can be overly restrictive. In practice, many disease-associated genes are not highly expressed 48 

but instead have high specificity for particular cellular contexts. By emphasizing absolute expression rather 49 

than specificity, such approaches may miss subtle, context-dependent signals, especially for complex traits 50 

where genetic effects are modest and distributed [4]. 51 

A more recent representative method is seismic [5], which improves statistical power by quantifying disease-52 

cell type associations through linear regression between gene-level disease score (MAGMA z-scores) and 53 

cell-type expression specificity. Because a positive association reflects concordance between genetic risk and 54 

expression specificity–rather than requiring uniformly high expression of top-ranked disease genes–seismic is 55 

more sensitive to specificity-driven disease signals. Accordingly, compared to scDRS, seismic demonstrated 56 

increased sensitivity in causal simulations while maintaining good calibration in null settings [5]. However, 57 

this gain in power comes with an important limitation: seismic performs association testing only at the 58 

cell-type level and does not provide a mechanism to resolve how disease risk varies across heterogeneous 59 

cellular subpopulations within a cell type. 60 

Cellular heterogeneity in disease association is pervasive and represents a critical component of disease 61 

mechanisms and therapeutic target prioritization [6, 7]. A canonical example is the association between 62 

microglia and Alzheimer’s disease. Only a specific subpopulation - disease-associated microglia - is enriched 63 

around amyloid plaques, a hallmark of Alzheimer’s pathology, where they contribute to synaptic disruption 64 

and neurodegeneration. In contrast, the majority of homeostatic microglia do not contribute directly to 65 

disease progression [8, 9, 10]. This example illustrates that disease risk is often concentrated in specific 66 

cellular states rather than uniformly distributed across an entire cell type, underscoring the need for methods 67 

that can resolve disease-relevant heterogeneity without sacrificing statistical power. 68 

A key challenge in resolving disease-cell type associations while accounting for cellular heterogeneity is 69 

the tradeoff between resolution and statistical stability. Analyses at the level of individual cells offer fine-70 

grained resolution but are severely affected by technical noise and dropout, whereas analyses at the level of 71 

annotated cell types improve robustness at the cost of obscuring heterogeneous disease signals within a cell 72 

type. Metacells provide an intermediate representation by aggregating transcriptionally similar cells into 73 

homogeneous groups, reducing technical noise while preserving biologically meaningful heterogeneity [11]. 74 

This representation offers a natural unit for modeling disease associations that vary across cellular states 75 

within a cell type. 76 

Building on this idea, we developed Informed Ce ll Pop ulation (ICePop), a framework that models disease-77 

cell associations by leveraging metacells as the fundamental unit to capture cellular heterogeneity. ICePop 78 

extends specificity score-based regression to the metacell level, enabling association testing at an interme-79 

diate resolution that balances noise reduction with preservation of biological variation. To address depen-80 

dencies arising from similar expression profiles across metacells–which can otherwise inflate cell type-level 81 

associations–we introduce a covariance-aware aggregation scheme for deriving cell type-level signals. ICe-82 

Pop further applies a weighted FDR framework to prioritize strongly associated metacells within each cell 83 

type, facilitating detection of heterogeneous disease signals. In addition, it incorporates a DFBETAS-based 84 

approach to identify influential genes by quantifying metacell-level contributions and emphasizing those 85 

from associated metacells. To enhance interpretability, ICePop generates an interactive report for exploring 86

# Results 95 

Overview of ICePop for disease-cell type association and heterogeneity 96 

As shown in Fig. 1A , ICePop began by aggregating single cells into metacells, which represent transcription-97 

ally homogeneous cellular states. We generated metacells using MetaQ [13], a scalable framework designed 98 

for metacell generation in large single-cell datasets. For each gene and each metacell, we computed an ex-99 

pression specificity score that quantifies how selectively a gene is expressed in a metacell relative to other 100 

metacells. In parallel, gene-level disease association statistics were derived from GWAS summary statis-101 

tics using MAGMA [2]. These gene-level association scores were then regressed against metacell-specific 102 

expression specificity scores to obtain metacell-level disease association coefficients. 103 

To infer cell-type-level disease associations, coefficients of all metacells within each cell type were aggregated 104 

using a weighted averaging scheme that emphasizes metacells with high cell-type purity (the proportion of 105 

cells within a metacell belonging to a given cell type) and strong disease association. Because transcription-106 

ally adjacent metacells tend to exhibit correlated expression profiles and regression coefficients, we explicitly 107 

accounted for the covariance structure among metacells when aggregating associations to the cell-type level. 108 

As the weighting procedure may distort association scores from a standard Gaussian distribution, we esti-109 

mated statistical significance using a permutation-derived null distribution of disease-cell type associations 110 

(see Methods). 111 

We implemented influence diagnostics based on DFBETAS [14] to prioritize genes that disproportionately 112 

contribute to disease-cell type associations. These diagnostics explicitly incorporated the same weighting 113 

scheme used in the metacell-to-cell type aggregation step, thereby emphasizing gene contributions arising 114 

from metacells that are strongly associated with the disease and represent relevant cellular subpopulations 115 

within each cell type. In addition, ICePop supports the computation of disease associations for user-defined 116 

cell groups, enabling flexible exploration beyond predefined cell-type annotations. 117 

To assess cellular heterogeneity within cell types, we applied weighted Benjamini-Hochberg procedures [15] 118 

to identify significantly associated metacells while accounting for contribution of each metacell to its corre-119 

sponding cell type. Significance assignments at the metacell level were then propagated to individual cells, 120 

allowing us to quantify the proportion of significantly associated cells within each cell type and thereby 121 

characterize within-cell-type heterogeneity. 122 

For each disease, ICePop generates an interactive report that allows users to explore associated cell popula-123 

tions, contributing genes, and enriched biological processes. Together, these features provide a comprehensive 124 

and interpretable view of disease-cell type relationships, capturing within-cell-type heterogeneity and offering 125 

insight into the contextual roles of genes in specific disease-cell type combinations. 126 

ICePop is well calibrated and powerful under heterogeneous disease-cell type 127 

associations. 128

We next evaluated statistical power under scenarios in which true disease-cell type associations were present. 144 

Our simulation framework assumes that a cell type is disease-associated when disease-risk genes overlap with 145 

genes specifically expressed in that cell type. Based on this principle, we generated association signals using 146 

cell-type-specific expression patterns and designated a subset of specifically expressed genes as causal, with 147 

non-causal genes modeled as noise. We varied key parameters, including the fraction of causal genes, noise 148 

level, and signal scaling factor, to represent realistic disease signals. To model heterogeneous associations 149 

within a cell type, we further restricted signal generation to a subset of cells in a cell type. To preserve 150 

the empirical distribution of GWAS statistics, simulated gene effects were mapped onto gene-disease scores 151 

within randomly selected traits. For each parameter setting, we performed 100 simulations and defined 152 

power as the proportion of runs in which the target cell type reached FDR 0.1. The entire procedure was 153 

repeated 10 times per setting to assess variability (see Methods). 154 

First, across simulations varying key parameters, metacell size had minimal impact on overall power ( Sup-155 

plementary Fig. 5a-c ). Under low sampling rates, we observed a slight decrease in power for larger 156 

metacells, but performance remained broadly comparable across sizes ( Supplementary Fig. 5d ). Strati-157 

fying results by cell-type size yielded a similar pattern: metacell sizes of 30 and 50 showed higher power at 158 

lower sampling rates ( Supplementary Fig. 6d ), whereas no meaningful differences were observed under 159 

other parameter settings ( Supplementary Fig. 6a-c ). Although smaller metacells showed slight advan-160 

tages in low-sampling scenarios, larger metacells are expected to provide more robust expression specificity 161 

estimates due to increased signal aggregation. Given its comparable performance across simulation settings, 162 

we selected a metacell size of 75 for subsequent analyses, beginning with the comparison of ICePop to seismic 163 

and scDRS. 164 

Across simulations varying the fraction of causal genes, noise, and signal strength, ICePop and seismic showed 165 

similar performance. scDRS performed well when signals were strong, but its power dropped sharply as signal 166 

strength decreased (e.g., fewer fraction of causal genes, higher noise, or lower signal scaling factors). When 167 

we restricted disease signals to a subset of cells to model within-cell-type heterogeneity, ICePop consistently 168 

outperformed both seismic and scDRS. The gap between ICePop and seismic increased as the sampling rate 169 

decreased. Although scDRS is designed to detect heterogeneity at single-cell resolution, it showed a marked 170 

loss of power under lower sampling rates ( Fig. 2B ). Stratifying by cell-type size showed similar trends. 171 

ICePop and seismic had comparable performance across most size bins and generally outperformed scDRS. 172 

In larger cell types ( > 140 cells), ICePop showed slightly higher power than seismic, especially under weaker 173 

signals. Seismic performed slightly better in very small cell types ( < 40 cells), likely because both true signal 174 

and noise, such as technical noise from limited cell numbers, were directly incorporated into the simulated 175 

signal ( Supplementary Fig. 7a-c ). As seismic operates on cell-type-level, this may give it an artificial 176 

advantage in small cell types where noise can dominate. Across all size bins, reducing the sampling rate 177 

substantially decreased power for scDRS, while ICePop maintained comparable or better performance than 178 

seismic, particularly for larger cell types ( > 140 cells) ( Supplementary Fig. 7d )179

ICePop detects meaningful disease-cell type association from real data, particu-180 

larly with cell-type heterogeneity 181 

To evaluate the performance of ICePop in identifying disease-cell type associations across a broad range of 182 

cell types and diseases in real data, we applied the method to the TM FACS dataset . We curated traits 183 

spanning seven major disease categories and included 120 cell types in the analysis, together encompassing 21 184 

tissues and 81 traits ( Supplementary Data 1 ). For ICePop, we used a metacell size of 75 cells to construct 185 

disease associations, as justified in Supplementary Note 3 . ICePop identified 2,178 significant disease-cell 186 

type associations in total, a number higher yet similar in magnitude to seismic (n=1,786), whereas scDRS 187 

detected substantially fewer (n=903). Associations identified by ICePop shared the most with those detected 188 

by seismic (n=849) ( Fig. 3B ). 189 

Associations identified by all three methods (ICePop, seismic, and scDRS; n=746) recapitulated well-190 

established disease-cell type relationships. These included blood cells with hematological traits, immune 191 

diseases with immune cell types, neurological disorders with neuronal cells, metabolic traits with hepato-192 

cytes, and height with chondrocytes ( Fig. 3A, Supplementary Fig. 11 and 12 ). The concordance across 193 

methods indicates strong and robust disease-cell type signals that are consistently detectable regardless of an-194 

alytical framework. Associations identified by both ICePop and seismic (n=849) included many non-obvious 195 

links that were less frequently detected by scDRS ( Fig. 3A, Supplementary Fig. 11 ). These include links 196 

between pancreatic cells and neurological traits, supported by the fact that insulin-secreting pancreatic cells 197 

relay systemic metabolic information (e.g., glucose levels) to the brain through sensory neurons, supporting 198 

biologically meaningful cross-organ associations [18]. ICePop and seismic consistently co-identified that hair 199 

pigmentation is associated with keratinocytes ( Fig. 3A, Supplementary Fig. 11 ). Keratinocytes, as 200 

the primary structural cells of the hair shaft, receive and retain melanin produced by melanocytes, thereby 201 

involving determination of pigmentation [19]. 202 

A smaller subset of associations was uniquely shared between ICePop and scDRS (n=84) and tended to reflect 203 

heterogeneous associations. For instance, both ICePop and scDRS found that coronary atherosclerosis was 204 

heterogeneously associated with endothelial cell of the coronary artery (ICePop: 72.68% significant cells, 205 

scDRS: heterogeneity level FDR=0.060) ( Fig. 3A, Supplementary Fig. 12 ). Not all endothelial cells are 206 

equally susceptible to plaque formation. Atherosclerotic lesions preferentially develop in regions of disturbed 207 

flow, such as vascular branch points, rather than in streamlined segments [20]. Similarly, ICePop and scDRS 208 

found that the FEV1/FVC ratio (the ratio of forced expiratory volume in one second to forced vital capacity, 209 

a measure of lung function) was associated with lung capillary cells (ICePop: 56.8% significant cells, scDRS: 210 

heterogeneity level FDR=0.014) ( Fig. 3A, Supplementary Fig. 12 ). The heterogeneity underlying this 211 

association is discussed in greater detail below. 212 

We further characterized ICePop-specific associations (n=499), typically corresponding to cases where just 213 

a fraction of cells within a cell type are associated with a disease, many of which reflecting biologically 214 

meaningful heterogeneity. For example, ICePop identified a heterogeneous association between atrial fib-215 

rillation and fibroblasts (52.4% significant cells), consistent with evidence that only activated fibroblasts 216 

differentiate into myofibroblasts involved in fibrillation pathogenesis [21] ( Fig. 3A ). ICePop also identified 217 

an association between ulcerative colitis and large-intestinal enterocytes (34.3% significant cells), consistent 218 

with heterogeneous enterocyte responses during intestinal inflammation ( Fig. 3A ) (discussed below). 219 

Associations shared between ICePop and Seismic usually occurred in cell types where most cells were asso-220 

ciated with the trait. In contrast, ICePop-specific associations had a much lower fraction of associated cells, 221 

indicating that ICePop can detect heterogeneity within cell types ( Fig. 3C ). Associations shared between 222 

ICePop and scDRS also showed low fractions of associated cells, suggesting that both methods can detect 223 

heterogeneous signals. However, ICePop-specific associations generally showed lower overall heterogeneity 224 

compared to scDRS ( Fig. 3C ). When assessing shared associations stratified by tissue, we observed a similar 225 

overall trend: seismic showed greater overlap with ICePop than scDRS did. Tissues such as brain, immune, 226 

blood, and liver exhibited a high proportion of shared disease-cell type associations across methods, indicat-227 

ing robust and relatively homogeneous signals ( Supplementary Fig. 13a ). In contrast, tissues including 228

ICePop uncovers nuanced disease-associated cell state changes within cell types 232 

We next investigated cases exhibiting substantial heterogeneity within cell types in TM FACS. We focused 233 

on scenarios involving subtler disease-associated cell state changes, allowing us to evaluate whether ICePop 234 

can detect nuanced within-cell-type variation. 235 

One illustrative example is the association between large intestine enterocytes and ulcerative colitis (UC). 236

In UC, dysregulated immune responses can damage gut epithelia including enterocytes, leading to immune-238 

stress-mediated damage such as epithelial erosion, ulceration, and increased intestinal permeability [23, 24, 239 

25]. However, epithelial damage is not spatially uniform, suggesting that not all enterocytes are equally 240 

vulnerable to immune stress [26]. 241 

Consistent with these facts, we observed that the association between enterocytes and UC was heterogeneous 242 

rather than being uniform across the cell type. To further characterize this pattern, we assigned individual 243 

cells to disease-associated or non-associated groups (see Methods). Associated and non-associated cells 244 

largely overlapped in UMAP space, indicating that the transcriptional differences were subtle and did not 245 

reflect discrete cell-type separation ( Fig. 4A ). This was further supported by broad expression of canonical 246 

enterocyte markers ( CDH1 [27], CA1 [28], KRT20 [29]) across both groups ( Supplementary Fig. 14b ), 247 

suggesting that the observed differences represent nuanced cell state variation within the cell type. 248 

Previous studies have shown that mature enterocytes are more vulnerable to apoptosis and immune stress 249 

than progenitor cells, particularly under endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress conditions [30, 31]. Based 250 

on this, we asked whether UC-associated enterocytes are more differentiated, whereas non-associated cells 251 

may be less differentiated and exhibit stronger cell homeostasis under stress. To answer this question, 252 

for each cell, we calculated the relative expression of genes annotated to sets of Gene Ontology Biological 253 

Processes, one representing differentiated enterocyte functions (e.g., ion transport and water absorption) 254 

and the other representing cellular homeostasis programs, including ER stress responses, apoptosis, and 255 

epithelial differentiation (see Methods). We found that UC-associated enterocytes exhibited higher relative 256 

expression of processes linked to differentiated epithelial functions. In contrast, non-associated cells showed 257 

higher expression of processes related to cellular homeostasis. Height, which has minimal association to 258 

enterocytes, did not show bias toward either processes ( Fig. 4B ). We further examined well-known markers 259 

of differentiated enterocytes. Ion transport genes such as SLC26A3 [32] and ATP12A [33], as well as 260 

the water channel gene AQP8 [34], were more highly expressed in UC-associated cells. In contrast, non-261 

associated cells expressed higher levels of genes related to cellular homeostasis and metabolic maintenance, 262 

including PRKG2 [35], ATP5IF1 [36], and RNF186 [37] ( Supplementary Fig. 14b and 15a-i ). Together, 263 

these results suggest that UC-associated enterocytes are enriched for mature epithelial functions, whereas 264 

non-associated cells maintain gene expression programs linked to cell differentiation and homeostasis. 265 

Another illustrative example is the association between lung capillary endothelial cells and FEV1/FVC 266 

ratio, a clinical measure of airway obstruction and lung function. Capillary endothelial dysfunction has been 267 

implicated in reduced lung function and progressive fibrosis [38]. In our analysis, we observed substantial 268 

within-cell type heterogeneity in capillary endothelial cells, with 56.8% of cells at significant levels. On 269 

UMAP, capillary endothelial cells are separated into two major populations corresponding to aerocytes 270 

(aCap) and general capillary endothelial cells (gCap), according to marker expression ( Supplementary Fig. 271 

16 ). While aCap cells were broadly disease-associated, the gCap population exhibited clear heterogeneity, 272 

with a subset showing weaker association signals ( Fig. 4C ). 273

ICePop revealed shared cellular mechanism among diseases 289 

Understanding shared mechanisms across diseases is essential for elucidating disease pathophysiology, im-290 

proving diagnosis, and guiding treatment design. While GWAS provides important insights, similarities 291 

based solely on shared genetic risk may be limited due to substantial genetic heterogeneity across complex 292 

traits [42]. Estimating similarities based on shared cellular programs instead offers a more nuanced, mecha-293 

nistic, and biologically interpretable view of relationships between diseases. We systematically characterized 294 

disease similarity using metacell-level disease association profiles and compared these patterns with gene-295 

based risk similarity. We further clustered the disease-disease similarity network to identify key differences 296 

between cellular association patterns and genetic risk similarity (see Methods). 297

# Discussion 371 

Thus far, disease-cell type association and within-cell-type heterogeneity have largely been treated as separate 372 

problems requiring distinct analytical frameworks. Methods such as scDRS [3] capture heterogeneity but 373 

often sacrifice statistical power, whereas approaches like seismic [5] maximize power at the cost of collapsing 374 

within-cell-type variation. In this study, we introduce ICePop, which resolves this trade-off by selecting an 375 

appropriate intermediate unit of analysis–metacells–rather than attempting to address both challenges at 376 

either the single-cell or cell-type level. 377 

Metacells have been shown to effectively mitigate the fundamental tension between resolution and statistical 378 

stability in single-cell analysis. While single-cell measurements are prone to dropout and noise, and cell-type-379 

level aggregation can obscure meaningful variation, metacells provide an intermediate representation that 380 

amplifies biological signal while preserving local structure [11]. This representation has proven compatible 381 

with a wide range of single-cell analyses, including clustering, differential expression, imputation, and RNA 382 

velocity [64, 65, 13]. Building on this foundation, we extend the metacell paradigm to disease-cell association 383 

analysis. By operating at a granularity that better matches the biological scale of disease processes, ICePop 384 

uncovers fine-grained heterogeneity within cell types, capturing continuous cellular state transitions that 385 

shape disease association patterns. 386 

As one of the first applications of ICePop, we used it to uncover the cellular basis of ulcerative colitis (UC). 387 

Our analysis shows that UC genetic risk is not uniformly distributed across epithelial cells, but is instead 388 

enriched in terminally differentiated states. While prior studies have established the central role of epithelial 389 

cells in UC pathogenesis [66, 67, 68, 69], they largely focused on broad cell types. Our results further refined 390 

this view by showing that genetic risk is strongly dependent on cellular state within the epithelial lineage, 391

Another application analyzing the association of FEV1/FVC ratio (measure of airway obstruction and lung 396

Our analysis of mouse colonic cells in the context of autism spectrum disorder (ASD) reveals that, although 403 

the association of enteric neurons with ASD is previously known [53], the risk is not uniformly distributed. We 404 

observe the strongest associations in sensory neurons. Within sensory neurons, mechanosensory PIEZO1 -405 

expressing neurons and intrinsic primary afferent neurons (IPANs) show stronger enrichment, consistent 406 

with known enteric nervous system (ENS) biology: IPANs initiate gut activity via chemical and mechani-407 

cal cues [58, 70], and their disruption impairs sensing and downstream motility [62], while loss of PIEZO1 408 

is linked to slowed peristalsis [59]. Regulatory circuits are also implicated, with enrichment in secreto-409 

motor/vasodilator neurons and a weaker but significant signal in inhibitory motor neurons. Secretomotor 410 

circuits regulate epithelial fluid secretion, while inhibitory motor neurons control muscle relaxation [71]; 411 

impairment in both may contribute to constipation in ASD. These results indicate that ASD risk primarily 412 

disrupts upstream sensory pathways, with additional effects on regulatory circuits for gut motility. Together, 413 

these findings provide a concrete mechanistic hypothesis for how ASD affects specific enteric neuron groups, 414 

facilitating further investigation into the mechanisms underlying ASD-associated gut dysfunction. 415 

A broad application of ICePop to a wide range of traits and diseases shows that, by integrating cellular 416 

context, clustering traits/diseases based on their perturbed metacells reveals distinct patterns compared to 417 

those solely based on genetic risk. For example, blood cell count traits cluster with immune diseases based 418 

on genetic risk due to shared immune genetics, but separate out based on metacell-based clustering because 419 

these traits reflect progenitor and vascular programs, whereas immune diseases are driven by mature effector 420 

states—consistent with hematopoietic differentiation trajectories [72]. Similarly, the clustering of pigmen-421 

tation traits illustrates that shared genetic architecture does not necessarily imply shared cellular etiology: 422 

hair pigmentation involves keratinocyte and epithelial programs involving hair pigment growth and reten-423 

tion, whereas melanoma and vitiligo do not. By capturing these distinctions, ICePop prioritizes downstream 424 

effector cell types over shared upstream genetic signals, revealing biologically meaningful differences that are 425 

not apparent from GWAS alone, thus improving disease stratification and mechanistic interpretation. 426 

The assumption that diseases manifest in a cell-type-specific manner underlies specificity-based methods such 427 

as ICePop and seismic. When gene risk of diseases is broadly distributed across genes widely expressed in 428 

the tissue and therefore having limited cell-type specificity, the association signal may be difficult to detect. 429 

In such cases, specificity-based methods may systematically underperform regardless of resolution, as no 430 

meaningful specificity can be captured. Under these conditions, reduced power should be interpreted as an 431 

absence of detectable cell-type-specific enrichment within the current dataset, rather than a failure of the 432 

method. In these scenarios, using datasets with greater cellular diversity or adopting alternative analytical 433 

strategies may be more appropriate. 434 

Metacell construction is a critical component of ICePop, as they define the resolution at which disease-cell 435 

associations are evaluated. While users can adopt different metacell construction methods, these approaches 436 

vary substantially in their properties, including the internal homogeneity of metacells, sensitivity to rare 437 

cell types, and computational efficiency. So, the choice of algorithm should be guided by the specific goals 438 

and constraints of the analysis. For example, Metacell-2 [73] applies stricter criteria during construction 439 

and can exclude low-quality or unassignable cells, resulting in more compact and homogeneous metacells. 440

single-cell atlases. 450 

The absence of a robust gold standard for disease-cell type associations is the field’s most constraining 451 

technical gap, impacting all methods including ICePop. Developing a rigorous and less biased benchmarking 452 

framework is necessary to fill this gap. Such a framework should go beyond easily detectable, well-known 453 

associations (e.g., neurons with neurological diseases or immune cells with immune disorders) and instead 454 

capture more subtle and heterogeneous disease-cell relationships. One potential strategy is to leverage case-455 

control single-cell datasets to identify cell populations showing differential expression or abundance, and 456 

use these as data-driven proxies for disease-associated cell types. Constructing such a resource should be 457 

a community priority, which would benefit not only ICePop but the entire class of GWAS-to-single-cell 458 

integration methods. 459 

Short of high-quality detailed gold-standards, this sub-field relies on simulations for model specification and 460 

evaluation. However, current simulation approaches for disease-cell type association do not provide a shared 461 

framework that generates data suitable for all classes of GWAS-to-single-cell integration methods. This 462 

includes not only methods based on summarized gene-level risk, but also SNP-level heritability enrichment 463 

approaches such as CELLECT [74] and sc-linker [75]. Developing a unified simulation framework that 464 

supports both gene- and SNP-level models would enable more consistent and fair comparisons across methods. 465 

Finally, current approaches identify disease-associated cell types and prioritize contributing genes but lack 466 

mechanistic interpretation of underlying regulatory programs. A key step forward is to map disease-467 

associated signals within relevant cell types to mechanism-aware frameworks such as PhenoPLIER [76], 468 

which can reveal coordinated gene programs and regulatory modules. In parallel, network-based approaches 469 

offer complementary insight by modeling interactions between disease and associated cell population, ei-470 

ther through mapping disease-cell association contributing genes onto functional interaction networks (e.g., 471 

STRING [77]) or building co-expression [78] or regulatory networks [79] from disease-associated cell popula-472 

tions. 473 

Overall, by building around metacells as the unit for detecting disease-associated cell populations, ICePop 474 

enables the investigation of pervasive heterogeneity in disease associations within cell types, opening the way 475 

for a more nuanced understanding of how diseases manifest in specific cellular contexts, and opportunities 476 

for early biomarker discovery and prevention-oriented research. 477

target number of metacells, which was defined as the total number of cells divided by the expected number 484 

of cells per metacell. We initially set the expected metacell size to 75 cells, a value empirically validated by 485

# Null and power analyses 587 

Preparing simulation data 588 

We randomly sampled 10,000 cells from the TM FACS dataset. Cell types represented by fewer than 10 cells 589 

were excluded, and genes expressed in fewer than 10 cells were removed. The resulting dataset was used 590 

as input for both the null and power analyses. Mouse gene expression was converted to human gene space 591 

using one-to-one orthologs obtained from NCBI Orthologs [80]. 592 

Conducting null simulation 593 

In null simulations, we preserved the overall distribution of MAGMA z-scores while breaking any true associ-594 

ation between cell type and disease by randomly shuffling MAGMA z-scores across genes. Model calibration 595 

is assessed by comparing the distribution of calculated p-values to the expected uniform distribution under 596 

the null. 597 

Conducting power analysis 598 

The goal of the power analysis was to evaluate whether methods can correctly identify an associated cell 599 

type when a true disease signal is present. In contrast to previous strategies used in scDRS and seismic, 600 

which simulate signal by perturbing gene expression profiles, we directly perturbed disease signals by syn-601 

thesizing MAGMA gene-level z-scores. The limitations of simulation through gene expression perturbation 602 

are discussed in Supplementary Note 4 .603 

We used the TM FACS dataset as the source for power simulations and preserve the original cell-type 604 

composition. In each simulation, one cell type is selected as the putative causal cell type. For each gene g,605 

we compute the log fold change (logFC g ) of its expression in the selected cell type compared to all other 606 

cell types. These log fold changes serve as the basis for simulated disease signals. A subset of genes is then 607 

designated as true causal genes. Gene selection probabilities determined by a softplus transformation of 608 

logFC g , which is further normalized to the sum of one. This design ensures that genes with higher logFC g609 

are more likely to be causal, while still allowing genes with near-zero or even negative logFC g to be selected. 610 

The proportion of sampled causal genes is controlled by the parameter causal fraction. For causal genes, 611 

signal strength is modulated by multiplying logFC g by a signal scaling factor, denoted as factor, and adding 612 

Gaussian noise εg , where εg ∼ N (0 , noise) . The parameter noise controls the strength of the stochasticity. 613 

For non-causal genes, the signal arises purely from noise with the same variance as in casual genes. Formally, 614 

the simulated disease signal is defined as 615

factor · logFC g + εg , if gene g is causal ,εg , otherwise , (15) In addition to controlling signal strength through causal fraction, factor, and noise, we introduced a setting 616 

in which only a subpopulation of cells within a cell type is associated, thereby modeling heterogeneous 617 

cell-type associations. Rather than sampling cells uniformly, we selected connected patches of cells within 618 

a cell type to preserve local transcriptional coherence. Specifically, we first randomly selected a seed cell 619 

located near the centroid of the target cell type to avoid sampling sparse outlier cells. Starting from this 620 

seed, we iteratively sampled adjacent cells until the desired number of cells was reached. The total number 621 

of sampled cells was determined by a sampling rate parameter, denoted sample rate. The logFC g values 622 

were then computed using only the sampled subpopulation. 623

Building disease-disease similarity graph 638 

To group diseases based on metacell association profiles, we used metacell-level association z-scores as features 639 

and applied principal component analysis (PCA) to account for correlations among metacells and reduce 640 

redundancy in the feature space. Pairwise Euclidean distances between diseases were then computed in the 641 

PCA space, and a k-nearest neighbor (KNN) graph was constructed, then edge weights were subsequently 642 

smoothed using a Gaussian kernel. Disease clusters were identified using Louvain community detection 643 

optimization [81]. 644 

For comparison, we performed an analogous analysis using gene-level genetic risk profiles derived from 645 

MAGMA z-scores. To ensure comparability across traits, MAGMA z-scores were quantile-normalized. Eu-646 

clidean distances were then calculated directly from the normalized gene-level profiles, followed by KNN 647 

graph construction and Louvain clustering using the same procedure as for metacell-level features. 648 

We chose the resolution parameter at 2.5 to get more granular clusters that better distinguish broad cate-649 

gories. 650

Collecting and processing GWAS dataset 676 

We collected GWAS summary statistics for 75 complex diseases from the set analyzed in the scDRS study [3]. 677 

These diseases span a broad range of categories, including immune, neurological, cardiovascular, liver-related, 678 

and lung-related disorders. In addition, we included ten additional traits to provide a more comprehensive 679 

representation of traits and phenotypes. Detailed information for all traits is provided in Supplementary 680 

Data 1. For autism spectrum disorder, we obtained GWAS summary statistics from Pedersen et al. [51]. 681 

GWAS summary statistics were aggregated to gene-level scores using MAGMA [2]. SNPs were mapped to 682 

genes using MAGMA’s default parameters, assigning SNPs within ±10 kb of each gene to that gene. 683 

Collecting and processing single cell data 684 

We processed the TM FACS dataset [86]. The count matrix was downloaded directly from the published 685 

resource. No additional cell filtering was applied because the original dataset had already removed cells with 686 

fewer than 500 detected genes (genes with non-zero UMI counts). We further filtered genes by removing 687 

those expressed in fewer than 10 cells. 688 

For the mouse colon dataset from Drokhlyansky et al. [48], the published data had already filtered cells 689 

with fewer than 1,000 detected genes. Therefore, we did not perform additional cell-level filtering. We 690 

removed genes expressed in fewer than 10 cells. For both datasets, we retained only genes with valid Entrez 691 

IDs. When multiple gene symbols mapped to the same Entrez ID, we kept the gene with the highest mean 692 

expression across cells. For visualization, we used the tSNE embedding provided in the original study rather 693 

than UMAP, as it showed clearer separation among cell groups. 694 

For MetaQ input, raw count data are required. However, to compute metacell-specific expression scores, we 695 

need normalized counts. We first normalized counts by sequencing depth (scaled to 10,000 counts per cell), 696 

and then applied natural log transformation with a pseudocount of 1. 697 

For mapping of gene from mouse to human, we used one-to-one orthologs from NCBI Orthologs [80]. 698 

Running benchmarked methods 699 

For seismic [5], we did not apply the default filtering parameters. Seismic’s strict filtering removes a sub-700 

stantial number of genes, resulting in a marked decrease in disease-cell-type associations ( Supplementary 701 

Fig. 22 ). For consistency across methods, we therefore did not filter any genes or cell types and discuss 702 

the problem of strict filtering in detail in Supplementary Note 2 . Log-normalized expression data were 703 

used as input, together with the cross-species conversion map based on one-to-one orthologs from NCBI 704 

Orthologs[80]. Cell types with FDR ≤ 0.1 were considered significant. 705 

For scDRS [3], we used default parameters (1,000 genes) with 1,000 Monte Carlo (MC) samples. The top 706 

5% quantile of trait scores across cells within each cell type was used as the test statistic. Prior to running 707 

scDRS, TM FACS mouse expression data were converted to human gene space using one-to-one orthologs 708 

from NCBI Orthologs [80] to ensure consistent gene mapping across methods. Disease-associated gene sets 709 

were selected from top 1,000 genes in the intersection between converted expression genes and genes present 710 

in the MAGMA z-score list. P-values were derived from MC z-scores, and cell types with FDR ≤ 0.1 were 711 

considered significant. 712
