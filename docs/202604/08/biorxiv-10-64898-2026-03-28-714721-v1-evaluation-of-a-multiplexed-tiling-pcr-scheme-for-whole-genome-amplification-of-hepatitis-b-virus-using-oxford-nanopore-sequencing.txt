# Abstract 15 

Here we evaluated the performance of a previously published tiling PCR primer scheme 16 

by Ringlander et al. (2022) for whole-genome amplification of Hepatitis B virus (HBV) in 17 

combination with Oxford Nanopore sequencing. The primer set originally developed for 18 

Ion Torrent sequencing was adapted by removing platform-specific adapters and tested 19 

using clinical serum or plasma samples submitted for routine HBV genotyping and 20 

resistance testing. Two multiplexing strategies were compared: a single PCR pool 21 

containing all primers and a two-pool strategy with non-overlapping amplicons. 22 

Sequencing reads were processed using a Nanopore analysis pipeline, and genome 23 

coverage and amplicon performance were compared across samples spanning a wide 24 

Ct range and representing HBV genotypes A–E. 25 

Across all samples, the median genome coverage was approximately 50%, although 26 

recovery varied widely, ranging from complete failure to nearly full genomes. Combining 27 

all primers into a single PCR reaction, or separating overlapping amplicons into different 28 

reactions, had little overall impact on genome recovery, and no consistent differences 29 

between the two pooling strategies were observed. In contrast, amplification efficiency 30 

differed markedly between individual amplicons. Amplicons 1 –5 generally produced 31 

higher sequencing depth, whereas amplicons 6–10 frequently showed low coverage and 32 

contributed to incomplete genome recovery. Genome coverage was strongly associated 33 

with Ct values, with higher coverage observed in samples with lower Ct values, while 34 

coverage was broadly similar across genotypes. 35 

These results demonstrate that the Ringlander et al. primer scheme can be adapted for 36 

multiplex PCR and Nanopore sequencing of HBV, but uneven amplicon performance 37 

limits consistent full-genome recovery and highlights the need for further optimization of 38 

HBV tiling PCR designs. 39

# Introduction 44 

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) remains a major global health concern, causing more than a 45 

million deaths annually, primarily due to liver cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma 46 

(WHO 2024). Although widespread vaccination programs have greatly reduced 47 

incidence in many countries, chronic HBV infection continues to represent a significant 48 

public health challenge, particularly among populations from high-prevalence regions. 49 

Chronic infection may be treated with antiviral therapy, yet long-term management is 50 

dependent on continued genomic monitoring of circulating strains to detect the 51 

emergence of novel mutations and drug resistance (Kim et al. 2014). 52 

Historically, genomic sequencing of HBV has relied on the amplification of partial 53 

genomic regions, most commonly the polymerase and surface genes, followed by 54 

Sanger sequencing (e.g., Kim et al. 2014; Ma et al. 2011; Naito et al. 2001). However, 55 

partial genome sequencing fails to capture variation across the entire genome and can 56 

miss clinically relevant mutations in other regions, such as the core and precore regions 57 

of the HBV e antigen. Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) provides a more 58 

comprehensive view of viral diversity and enables more accurate genotype or sub-59 

genotype assignment, while also facilitating detection of co-infections, recombination 60 

events, and resistance mutations (Rajoriya et al. 2017). In addition to supporting clinical 61 

characterization of individual infections, whole-genome data can also contribute to 62 

molecular surveillance by enabling detailed monitoring of circulating HBV lineages and 63 

emerging variants. 64 

The high specificity and sensitivity of PCR make it suitable for targeted enrichment of 65 

HBV DNA from clinical samples. However, because of the high genomic diversity among 66 

HBV strains, reliable primer design that can capture the full diversity is difficult (Chen et 67 

al. 2023; Rajoriya et al. 2017). Tiling PCR, where multiple overlapping amplicons 68 

spanning the genome are combined, has therefore become a widely used strategy for 69 

viral whole-genome sequencing. Each amplicon covers a portion of the genome, and 70 

together they provide redundancy: if one amplicon fails, the others can still recover 71 

much of the genome. This approach gained prominence during the Zika virus epidemic 72

Despite the widespread use of tiling PCR for viral whole-genome sequencing, its 75 

application to HBV remains limited. Only a small number of studies have proposed 76 

multi-amplicon schemes tailored to the HBV genome. Hebeler-Barbosa et al. (2020) 77 

designed three overlapping amplicons optimized for Illumina sequencing, whereas 78 

Ringlander et al. (2022) developed a set of ten overlapping amplicons of approximately 79 

400-500 bp that covered the entire HBV genome. More recently, Lumley et al. (2025) 80 

designed a scheme consisting of six longer amplicons (~600-700 bp), but with large 81 

primer redundancy at each site to better capture the HBV genetic diversity. 82 

Although the primer scheme designed by Ringlander et al. (2022) demonstrated 83 

successful and highly sensitive amplification across several HBV genotypes (A, B, C, and 84 

D), it was only sequenced using the Ion Torrent platform. Furthermore, the primers 85 

contained Ion Torrent-specific adapter sequences, limiting their compatibility with other 86 

sequencing technologies. Long-read sequencing technologies such as Oxford Nanopore 87 

offer substantial advantages for viral genomics , including the recovery of complete 88 

amplicons in single reads, which simplifi es genome assembly and improves haplotype 89 

reconstruction. Lumley et al. (2025) successfully applied Nanopore sequencing to 90 

sequence full-length amplicons from their tiling PCR scheme. Although the Ringlander 91 

et al. (2022) amplicons are somewhat shorter, adapting this primer set for Nanopore 92 

sequencing would still be advantageous. However, it remains uncertain whether these 93 

primers perform efficiently without the Ion Torrent adapters , or whether they can be 94 

multiplexed at all. 95 

In tiling PCR workflows , two key multiplexing strategies are commonly used. One 96 

approach pools all primers into a single PCR reaction, as done in Integrated DNA 97 

Technologies (IDT) protocols. This has the advantage that only a single PCR reaction is 98 

required per sample. But risk the amplification of primarily very short fragments from the 99 

overlapping amplicons. The other approach, as used in the ARTIC Network method 100 

protocols for example, divides primers into two pools of non-overlapping amplicons. 101 

While this requires two PCR reactions per sample and increases both the time of the 102

# Materials and Methods 111 

## Samples and DNA extraction 112 

Serum or plasma samples were submitted to the Norwegian Institute of Public Health 113 

(NIPH) mainly for routine HBV genotyping and resistance testing as part of the patient 114 

management. In selected cases, samples were also analyzed for detection of vaccine 115 

escape mutations or for characterization of rare, conflicting , or atypical diagnostic 116 

markers. 117 

The samples analyzed in this study had been previously sequenced for a small (~800bp) 118 

region where the P and S-genes overlap using PCR and Sanger sequencing. The resulting 119 

sequence was genotyped using Geno2Pheno (Beggel et al. 2009). Ct values were 120 

determined by quantitative PCR following the protocol described by (Pettersson et al. 121 

2019). We selected in total 84 different samples representing genotypes A (30 samples), 122 

B (19 samples), C (16 samples), D (37 samples – two of these were part of a EQA panel 123 

from QCMD), and E (18 samples) that were analyzed in this study (see Supplementary 124 

Table S1 for details). 36 of these samples were run in parallel to test the impact of 125 

different primer pooling approaches (described below), which makes for a total of 120 126 

different analyses. DNA was extracted from 200 μl plasma/serum using the Qiagen 127 

QIAamp DNA Mini Kit (Qiagen, Netherlands) on the QIACube (Qiagen, Netherlands) 128 

automated system following the manufacturers protocol or using the Abbott sp2000 129 

(Abbott, IL, US). Extracted DNA was eluted in 100 μL elution buffer and stored at −70°C 130 

prior to PCR amplification .131

## PCR amplification 166 

Amplification was performed using Platinum SuperFi II DNA Polymerase (Thermo Fisher 167 

Scientific , MA, US ) with the following cycling conditions: initial denaturation at 98°C for 168 

30 s; 35 cycles of 98°C for 10 s, 60°C for 30 s, and 72°C for 3 min; and a final extension at 169 

72°C for 5 min. Amplicons were visualized on TapeStation D5000 (Agilent, CA, US) and 170 

quantified by Qubit dsDNA HS assay (Thermo Fisher Scientific). 171

## Nanopore library preparation and sequencing 173 

Amplicons were prepared for sequencing using the Oxford Nanopore Technologies 174 

(ONT) Rapid Barcoding Kit (SQK-RBK114-96) and sequenced on GridION Mk1 using 175 

R10.4.1 flow cells (ONT, UK). In total , four different sequencing runs were performed. 176 

Two for the samples amplified with a single primer pool (named HBV9 and HBV10) and 177 

two for the samples amplified with two primer pools ( HBV11 and HBV12; see 178 

Supplementary Table S1). 179

## Read processing, mapping and generation of consensus sequences 181 

Basecalling and demultiplexing were performed using the MinKNOW software v24.02.16 182 

with Dorado v7.3.11 (ONT, UK) and the high accuracy 400 bps model. Reads were 183 

processed using version 1.8.5 of the ARTIC pipeline ( https://github.com/artic-184 

network/fieldbioinformatics ) which can handle libraries produced by the Nanopore 185 

Rapid kit . Reads were first demultiplexed and quality filtered using guppyplex (part of the 186 

ARTIC pipeline) with only reads between 100-4000 bp retained. Next, reference mapping 187 

and consensus sequence generation was done using minion as part of the ARTIC 188 

pipeline. In brief, reads were mapped using minimap2 (Li 2018) against the modified 189 

version of reference NC_003977.2 (see below), primer sequences were trimmed after 190 

mapping, and consensus sequences were created using Clair3 (Zheng et al. 2022) with 191 

the model r1041_e82_400bps_hac_v500 and a minimum read depth of 20 reads to call a 192 

consensus nucleotide. The minion pipeline also estimates the normalized abundance of 193 

each amplicon which were used to compare the performance of each amplicon. For the 194 

minion pipeline we used two different primer bed -files, one for each primer pooling 195

## Human read removal 215 

Prior to submission of the read files to the European Nucleotide Archive, all FASTQ files 216 

were screened for human-derived reads using an alignment-based approach. Reads 217

## Primer mismatch analysis 228 

Selected reference sequences representing each HBV genotype were obtained from the 229 

study by (McNaughton et al. 2020). To identify the theoretical primer-binding region in 230 

each reference sequence, a sliding-window matching approach was implemented in R 231 

v4.3.3. For each primer, each reference sequence was scanned using a window of size 232 

equal to the primer length, advancing one nucleotide at a time. At each position, the 233 

number of mismatches between the primer sequence and the corresponding reference 234 

subsequence was calculated by direct sequence comparison. Both the forward strand 235 

and the reverse complement of each reference sequence were evaluated, and the 236 

position with the lowest number of mismatches was retained as the best local match. A 237 

maximum of 10 mismatches were allowed. For the best local match, the number of 238 

mismatches against the entire primer was counted, in addition to the last 5 nucleotides 239 

to represent 3’-end mismatches. This approach does not take into account potential 240 

insertions or deletions. 241

## Sensitivity and specificity 337 

We examined the relationship between Ct value and genome coverage (Figure 5A). There 338 

was a moderate negative correlation between Ct value and genome coverage (r = -0.59, 339 

p < 0.001), with Ct values explaining approximately 33% of the variance in coverage (R² = 340 

0.34). Samples with low Ct values (high viral load) generally achieved higher genome 341 

coverage, with most samples below Ct 20 reaching 50–80% coverage. In contrast, 342 

samples with Ct values above 25 showed highly variable coverage ranging from 0% to 343 

60%, with many falling below 50%. 344 

A similar dependency was observed also for the individual amplicons (Supplementary 345 

Figure 1). Across all amplicons, normalized read depth decreased with increasing Ct 346 

value, consistent with progressive amplicon dropout at lower template concentrations. 347 

Although, the best performing amplicons still amplified well for Ct values above 30, 348 

overall there was a trend that the read depths were reduced with higher Ct. However, 349 

this effect was highly amplicon -specific. Amplicons 3 and 5 consistently achieved high 350 

read depth across a wide Ct range and remained detectable even at Ct values above 30, 351 

whereas the poorly performing amplicons, such as amplicons 9 and 10, showed low 352 

read depth across nearly all amounts of viral DNA. 353

and taxonomic classification of all sequenced reads (Figure 5B). The vast majority of 355 

reads were classified as HBV by Kraken2, demonstrating high specificity of the PCR 356 

amplification overall. Non-HBV reads represented only a small fraction of the total. The 357 

HBV-classified reads showed a length distribution centered around the expected 358

# Discussion 417 

In this study we evaluated the feasibility of adapting the (Ringlander et al. 2022) primer 418 

scheme, originally developed for singleplex Ion Torrent sequencing, to a multiplexed 419 

tiling PCR strategy compatible with Oxford Nanopore sequencing. Our findings 420 

demonstrate that while the primer set can be made to function under multiplexed 421 

conditions, independent of combining overlapping amplicons in a single reaction or not, 422 

per formance is limited by recurrent underamplification of specific genomic regions. 423 

Failures were concentrated in a reproducible set of genomic intervals and were not 424 

clearly linked to genotype or to the choice of primer pooling strategy. The specificity of 425 

the amplification reaction was high overall, with the vast majority of reads centered 426 

around expected amplicon sizes and classified as HBV, confirming that removal of Ion 427 

Torrent adapters did not introduce major off -target amplification. Likewise, human-428 

derived reads were present at low levels in most samples. Only in samples with high Ct 429 

values and very low total read counts did we observe substantial levels of human reads. 430 

However, these represented failed or poorly performing PCRs. 431 

Across the samples analyzed, genome recovery was modest, with a median of 432 

approximately half of the HBV genome represented, although with substantial variation 433 

ranging from >90% coverage to complete failure. A pronounced polarity in amplicon 434 

performance was consistently observed regardless of genotype, Ct-value or primer 435 

pooling strategy. Amplicons 1– 5 amplified reasonably well, while the remaining 436 

amplicons (perhaps except for amplicon 7) often failed to reach consensus-level depth. 437 

Even when the consensus depth cutoff was reduced from 20x to 10x. This asymmetric 438 

behavior explains the characteristic coverage plateau around 50% observed across 439 

samples. 440 

The decline in genome recovery with increasing Ct value reflects progressive dropout of 441 

individual amplicons rather than a uniform reduction in coverage across the genome. As 442 

Ct values increased, robust amplicons continued to amplify across a wide Ct range, 443 

while weaker amplicons dropped out early. The consistent amplification of amplicons 3 444 

and 5 across nearly the full Ct range explains why partial genomes were frequently 445 

recovered even at low levels of viral DNA. 446

The observed amplification pattern cannot be explained by genotype -specific effects. 447 

Across samples, multiple genotypes showed similar coverage profiles and the same 448 

subsets of amplicons repeatedly succeeded or failed. The polarity of amplification 449 

performance, together with the fact that all amplicons occasionally reached high 450 

depths, points to limitations in primer design and primer-template interaction. 451 

Primer mismatch analysis against the different genotypes did not reveal a consistent 452 

relationship between number of mismatches and amplification performance. Overall, 453 

mismatch counts were generally low across genotypes and showed no clear threshold 454 

beyond which amplification systematically failed. While some correspondence was 455 

observed for individual regions, for example the primers for amplicons 4, 8, and 9 456 

showed elevated mean mismatch counts and these amplicons were among those with 457 

the most unstable or frequently reduced coverage. This relationship was not 458 

generalizable and several poorly performing amplicons, such as amplicon 6 and 10, had 459 

few mismatches across genotypes. 460 

Thus, while primer– template mismatches likely contribute to reduced amplification 461 

efficiency in specific regions, mismatch burden alone does not predict amplicon 462 

performance. Nevertheless, there is room for optimalization of the primer sequences. In 463 

particular, improving consistently weak amplicons, rather than increasing the 464 

sequencing depth or relaxing the coverage thresholds, would be expected to greatly 465 

improve the sensitivity of the assay. 466 

The most consistently recovered genome region comprised the first ~800 nt of the 467 

reference, corresponding to amplicons 1–3. This region overlaps the polymerase 468 

reverse-transcriptase domain and the major surface antigen and is commonly used for 469 

HBV genotyping and clinical resistance testing. Thus, even when full-genome recovery is 470 

incomplete, the parts of the genome most relevant to surveillance and resistance 471 

monitoring are usually obtained. 472 

Importantly, multiplexing itself did not introduce major additional bias. Genome 473 

coverage and normalized amplicon depths were largely correlated between primer 474 

pooling strategies. Nevertheless, while avoiding amplifying overlapping amplicons in the 475 

same reaction yielded marginally higher coverage, neither approach overcame the 476

fundamental coverage instability imposed by weak amplicons. Given this, in high-477 

throughput laboratory workflows, such as in surveillance programs, pooling all primers 478 

into a single reaction may be an acceptable trade-off. It substantially reduces the 479 

workload and opportunities for error while yielding similar results as separating the non-480 

overlapping amplicons. 481 

Compared to more recent HBV whole-genome amplification strategies, the limitations 482 

of the Ringlander et al. (2022) scheme become particularly clear. Newer approaches, 483 

including the scheme described by Lumley et al. (2025), achieve substantially higher 484 

and more uniform genome recovery through redesigned tiling strategies specifically 485 

optimized for multiplex PCR and long-read sequencing. While Lumley at al. (2025) use 486 

fewer and longer amplicons than the Ringlander et al. primers, which thereby should be 487 

more sensitive to for example degraded template DNA, an important difference to their 488 

primer scheme is that they designed multiple overlapping or degraded primers per 489 

binding site. This in effect makes the primer scheme cover more of the potential HBV 490 

genomic diversity. 491 

Our study, together with other recent tiling amplicon amplifications of HBV, show s that 492 

multiplex tiling PCR amplification of entire HBV genomes is feasible and can be 493 

implemented as a cost-effective and rapid method in HBV genotyping and surveillance. 494
