# Abstract 

Phenotypic variation arises from the interplay of genetic, environmental, and stochastic developmental factors. Quantitative genetics predicts that reducing genetic variation through inbreeding or clonality should reduce phenotypic variation, an assumption that underlies the widespread use of inbred and clonal model organisms in biomedical research. Here, we test this assumption in the facultatively parthenogenic fly Drosophila mercatorum , in which parthenogenesis results in complete homozygosity and clonality after a single generation. Contrary to expectation, clonal parthenogenic flies showed broad shifts in trait means, altered interindividual variability, increased fluctuating asymmetry, and reduced behavioral and developmental canalization relative to sexually reproducing controls. Inbreeding reproduced substantial parts of this phenotype, whereas outcrossing restored robustness, identifying loss of heterozygosity as a major driver of the effect. Our findings show that extreme genetic uniformity can amplify rather than constrain stochastic phenotypic divergence, suggesting that controlled heterozygosity may, in some contexts, provide a more robust and reproducible experimental substrate than highly inbred, isogenic, or clonal animals.

# Introduction 

Phenotypic variation arises from the combined effects of genetic differences, environmental influences, and stochastic developmental processes (Fraser & Schadt, 2010). Reducing genetic variation is widely assumed to reduce phenotypic variation (Falconer, 1996). This assumption underlies the widespread use of inbred, isogenic, and clonal model organisms to improve reproducibility and statistical power (Crabbe et al., 1999; Festing, 1979; Tuttle et al., 2018; Würbel, 2000). Yet genetic uniformity does not necessarily produce phenotypic uniformity (Falconer, 1996). Developmental systems can buffer perturbations through canalization, but this buffering may itself depend on genetic context and can break down under conditions of stress or genetic load (Debat & David, 2001; Waddington, 1942). One widely used indicator of reduced developmental robustness is fluctuating asymmetry, the small random deviation of bilaterally symmetric traits from perfect symmetry (DONGEN, 2006; Palmer & Strobeck, 1986). More generally, breakdown of canalization can manifest as increased divergence among genetically similar individuals, including reduced behavioral consistency over time (Flatt, 2005; Gibson & Wagner, 2000). Such effects raise the possibility that extreme homozygosity may not simply reduce phenotypic variance but instead destabilize development, thereby altering both variance structure and robustness. This possibility is difficult to test in most model systems, but facultative parthenogenesis in 

Drosophila mercatorum provides an unusually direct way to do so. Facultative parthenogenesis in D. mercatorum provides an unusually strong test of how extreme homozygosity affects phenotypic robustness. In this species, rare parthenogenesis can occur in otherwise sexually reproducing populations, and experimentally derived parthenogenic lines have been established by selection for elevated parthenogenic rates (Templeton, Carson, et al., 1976). Because parthenogenesis in D. mercatorum proceeds through meiosis followed by pronuclear duplication, offspring become effectively homozygous after a single generation, producing a level of genetic uniformity even more extreme than

These findings suggest that extreme genetic uniformity and homozygosity can amplify, rather than constrain, stochastic phenotypic divergence by weakening developmental robustness.

# Materials and Methods 

## Fly stocks and rearing 

We used five Drosophila mercatorum strains. Two strains originated from Brazil and two from Hawaii: Brazil wild-type (NDSSC 15082-1521.25), Brazil parthenogenic (NDSSC 15082-1521.04), Hawaii wild-type (NDSSC 15082-1521.22), and Hawaii parthenogenic (NDSSC 15082-1525.05). In addition, a facultative parthenogenic strain (NDSSC 15082-1527.03) was included to control for the artificial selection procedures used to establish the parthenogenic stocks (Templeton, Carson, et al., 1976). To restore heterozygosity in the clonal parthenogenic population, parthenogenic females (NDSSC 15082-1521.04) were crossed to Brazil wild-type males (NDSSC 15082-1521.25). The resulting F 1 offspring were collected and analyzed for phenotypic means, interindividual variability, and canalization. This cross was designed to test whether phenotypes associated with clonality and extreme homozygosity could be mitigated by reintroducing genetic heterozygosity. All genotypes used in each main and supplementary figure are listed in Supplementary Table 1. Flies were maintained under standard Drosophila husbandry conditions on acornmeal-agar diet containing, per liter, 7.5 g agar, 64 g cornmeal, 16 g yeast, 85.5 ml sugar cane syrup, 8.5 ml ethanol, 0.51 g Nipagin, and 2.5 ml propionic acid (Linneweber et al., 2020). All stocks and experiments were maintained at 25 °C, 50% relative humidity, and a 12 h:12 h light-dark cycle in a Memmert HPPeco incubator. To minimize larval crowding, stocks were kept at low density with two parental females per vial, and food was changed at intervals of no more than 3 days. To reduce parental-age effects and further standardize developmental conditions, only offspring produced during the first 2 days of egg laying were used for experiments. Experimental cohorts were age-matched and reared under identical conditions.

Buridan’s paradigm experiments were performed as described previously (Colomb et al., 2012), with minor modifications. Flies were tested at 25 °C, 6-8 days after eclosion and at least 48 h after CO ₂ anesthesia. Wings were clipped before testing, as in the standard protocol; variation in clip length or shape did not measurably affect behavioral outcomes in our experiments. The assay consisted of a circular platform (117 mm diameter) surrounded by a water-filled moat and enclosed within a uniformly illuminated white cylinder (Fig. 1A). Illumination was provided by four circular fluorescent tubes (Osram L 40W, 640C, cool white) driven by an Osram Quicktronic QT-M 1×26-42 ballast. The tubes were positioned outside a cylindrical diffuser (DeBanier, Belgium; Kalk transparent, 180 g, white) at a distance of 147.5 mm from the arena center. Unless otherwise indicated, visual cues consisted of two opposing vertical black cardboard stripes, each 30 mm wide, attached to the inner surface of the diffuser. Depending on the position of the fly, the apparent retinal width of the stripes ranged from 8.4° to 19.6° (11.7° at the arena center). Fly trajectories were recorded and analyzed using CeTrAn (Colomb et al., 2012) and custom Python scripts (Linneweber et al., 2020). From each trajectory, we extracted behavioral parameters, including total distance traveled, median speed, meander, median turning angle, absolute angle deviation to the visual stripes, the number of complete stripe-to-stripe walks, centrophobism, and activity-related measures based on speed and time thresholds, including activity time, active bouts, pause duration, and number of pauses. Absolute angle deviation was defined as the smallest angle between the walking direction vector and the vector pointing towards either stripe, such that smaller values indicate stronger stripe fixation. Walks were defined as transitions beginning within 80% of the radius from one stripe and ending within 80% of the radius from the opposite stripe. Centrophobism was quantified as the time spent in the inner versus the outer zones of equal area. In addition, custom Python-derived metrics were calculated to capture directional and spatial aspects of visual orientation. These included directional angle deviation, stripe

## Locomotor activity and sleep measurements 

Locomotor activity and sleep were measured using the Drosophila Activity Monitor system (DAM; Trikinetics). Flies were collected at 3 days of age and loaded individually into glass tubes (65 mm length, 7 mm inner diameter) containing food at one end and a cotton plug at the other. Tubes were inserted into LAM7 monitors and maintained at 25 °C under a 12 h:12 h light-dark cycle for 10 consecutive days. Activity was recorded in 1-min bins throughout the experiment.

The first 48 h of recording were excluded to allow acclimation to the monitor environment, and the final 24 h were excluded to standardize the analyzed time window. Activity data were analyzed using SleepMat (SleepMat2024.1). Parameters were computed in two ways: first, across 5 consecutive days to quantify overall locomotor and sleep phenotypes; and second, as repeated 24 h summaries over 3 days to assess temporal behavioral consistency. Each fly was considered a biological replicate. A total of 21 locomotor and sleep parameters were extracted and grouped into three categories: anticipation, activity, and sleep. Anticipation parameters included morning and evening anticipation indices, defined as the ratio of activity during the 3 h preceding the light transition to activity during the preceding 6 h, as well as the phase of morning and evening activity onset. Activity parameters included total activity during the light and dark phases and the full day, as well as waking activity per minute during the light and dark phases. Sleep parameters included total sleep during the light and dark phases and the full day, sleep bout number during the light and dark phases, and average sleep bout length during the light and dark phases. Sleep was defined as at least 5 consecutive minutes without infrared beam crossings (Shaw et al., 2000). For canalization analyses, 24 h parameter values were compared across 3consecutive recording days for each individual fly. Day-to-day behavioral consistency was then quantified within individuals before group-level comparison. In the supplementary analyses, parameter-level temporal consistency was summarized using Pearson correlation coefficients after Fisher's z-transformation, and reanalysis was performed using the Mahalanobis distance, the MAD log-ratio, and the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC). Of the 21 extracted parameters, 20 were used in repeated-day canalization analyses.

Wings were prepared according to standard procedures (Houle et al., 2003). Briefly, left and right wings were dissected, mounted on microscope slides in a 1:1 mixture of glycerol and ethanol, covered with a coverslip, cleared of air bubbles, and fixed with Fixogum adhesive. Slides were stored at 4 °C until imaging. Wing morphology was imaged using a Leica M165 FC stereomicroscope equipped with a Leica K5C camera. Morphometric analysis was performed with Wings4, followed by landmark-based shape analysis in CPReader (Houle et al., 2003). Together, these procedures yielded the 27 wing parameters. Each fly was treated as one biological replicate, with left and right wings measured separately. Group differences in mean wing morphology were assessed using pairwise Wilcoxon rank-sum tests with Benjamini-Hochberg correction for multiple comparisons. Interindividual variability in wing morphology was assessed across wing traits using dispersion-based measures, including the median absolute deviation and Levene’s test. Developmental stability and canalization were assessed from bilateral correspondence between left and right wings within individuals. Because deviations between bilateral traits provide a standard measure of fluctuating asymmetry, reduced left-right correspondence was interpreted as increased developmental noise and reduced canalization. In supplementary reanalyses, wing data were additionally evaluated using the Mahalanobis distance, the MAD log-ratio, and the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC). 

## Thorax bristle anatomy 

Fly thoraxes were prepared by removing the head and abdomen and mounting the remaining thoracic cuticle on an insect pin, taking care not to damage the dorsal surface. Imaging was performed 1 day after dissection. To ensure consistent centering and orientation, the pin was secured in a micromanipulator before image acquisition. Thoraces were imaged on a Leica M165 FC stereomicroscope using z-stacks, which were subsequently flattened into extended depth-of-field images.

Flattened images were analyzed in ImageJ to manually extract the spatial coordinates of the bases of microchaete and macrochaete bristles. All annotations were performed by a single experimenter blinded to genotype during image analysis. Each thorax was treated as one biological replicate. The resulting two-dimensional point patterns were used to quantify bristle number, occupied area, bilateral asymmetry, and spatial regularity. Six parameters were extracted. Total thorax bristle number was defined as the total number of annotated bristles per fly. The thorax bristle area was defined as the area enclosed by a hull fitted over the outermost bristles. Bilateral asymmetry was quantified as the root-mean-square point-to-point distance between left and right bristle patterns after mirroring the right side and aligning it to the left side using an iterative closest point (ICP) algorithm. A greater mismatch was interpreted as reduced developmental stability. Local spatial regularity was assessed using three complementary measures. First, a robust spacing-irregularity metric was calculated from Voronoi tessellation by dividing the median absolute deviation of Voronoi edge lengths by their median; lower values indicate more regular spacing, whereas higher values indicate local clustering. Second, the variance-to-mean ratio (VMR) was calculated by dividing each bristle field into a 5 × 5 grid and dividing the variance of bristle counts across grid cells by the mean count within each grid cell. A VMR of 1 indicates spatial randomness, values below 1 indicate regular spacing, and values above 1 indicate clustering. Third, a regularity index was calculated as R = r_(obs) / r_(rand) , where 

r_(obs) is the observed mean nearest-neighbor distance and r_(rand) is the expected mean nearest-neighbor distance under complete spatial randomness. In two dimensions, r_(rand) = 1/2 sqrt(A/n) , where A is the bristle-field area, and n is the number of bristles. The bristle-field area was estimated using an alpha shape with alpha = 0.01. For comparison, regularity indices were also computed for two null models: random points sampled uniformly within the empirical alpha shape, and random points sampled uniformly within a square of equal area.

## Eye morphometry 

Adult compound eyes were imaged using a protocol adapted from (Keesey et al., 2019). Heads were detached from the body and immobilized by pinning through the cervical cavity with an insect pin. Each head was mounted on the confocal stage with one eye oriented towards the objective and imaged, then rotated by 180° to image the contralateral eye. Imaging was performed on a Leica SP8 white-light laser confocal microscope equipped with a 10× dry objective (NA 0.30). Ommatidial images were analyzed in Imaris 10 (Bitplane) using semi-automated segmentation and registration. Automated segmentations were manually checked and corrected where necessary before parameter extraction. From these reconstructions, six eye parameters were extracted: number of ommatidia per compound eye (nPoints), number of edges per compound eye (nEdges), number of faces per compound eye (nFaces), mean edge length (edgeLengthMean), mean face area (faceAreaMean), and the total number of ommatidia per animal (TotalN). Together, these parameters captured both eye size and local ommatidial lattice geometry. Both eyes were measured separately for each fly, and each fly was treated as one biological replicate. Group differences in mean morphology and interindividual variability were assessed across the six eye parameters in the summary and supplementary analyses. In addition, bilateral differences in ommatidial number were used to assess left-right asymmetry.

## Serotonergic neuron quantification 

Serotonergic neurons were quantified in Imaris 10 (Bitplane) using the Spots object-detection algorithm. Automated detections were manually checked and corrected where necessary before parameter extraction. Left and right measurements were extracted separately and compared within individuals for asymmetry analyses. Each fly was considered a biological replicate. ## Electroretinogram (ERG) 

Electroretinogram (ERG) recordings were performed according to standard procedures (Kohrs et al., 2021). Flies were immobilized by attaching the wings to a microscope slide with craft glue (UHU), allowing stable positioning during recording. A glass microelectrode was inserted into the compound eye, and a reference electrode was placed in the thorax. Each fly was recorded only once to avoid repeated-measure artifacts. Signals were acquired using Axoscope 10.6 (Molecular Devices). Light stimuli of 1 ms duration were delivered at 1% and 10% of maximum output. For each stimulus intensity, five responses were recorded per fly. From the ERG traces, two response components were quantified: the on-transient amplitude and the sustained depolarization. Responses were averaged per fly and per stimulus condition before statistical analysis. ERG measurements were used to assess visual responsiveness independently of behavioral orientation in Buridan’s paradigm. In the reported analyses, group comparisons were performed for on-transient amplitude and sustained depolarization at both 1% and 10% stimulus intensity.

## Statistical analysis 

All statistical analyses were performed in R. Throughout the manuscript, each fly was treated as a single biological replicate. Data distributions were assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test. For normally distributed data, group differences were analyzed by analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey’s post hoc test. For non-normally distributed data, pairwise comparisons were performed using Wilcoxon rank-sum tests. Homogeneity of variance was assessed using pairwise Levene’s tests. Paired data were analyzed using the paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test. Whenever multiple comparisons were performed, P-values were corrected using the Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) procedure. Correlations were quantified using either the Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient or the Spearman rank correlation coefficient, depending on data distribution. For repeated-day analyses, Pearson correlation coefficients were averaged after Fisher’s z transformation and back-transformation.

Pairwise comparisons between index distributions were performed using Wilcoxon rank-sum tests with Benjamini-Hochberg correction. ## Multivariate reanalysis of mean differences 

As a continuous alternative to the categorical mean index, multivariate phenotypic divergence between genotypes was quantified using a robust Mahalanobis distance relative to a reference genotype. Individual-level data were centered and, where appropriate, projected onto principal component axes to reduce dimensionality and collinearity. A robust covariance matrix was estimated using the Minimum Covariance Determinant method, and Mahalanobis distances between genotype mean vectors and the reference genotype were calculated in this multivariate space. Positive values indicate greater divergence from the reference genotype, whereas values near zero indicate high similarity. Uncertainty was estimated by nonparametric bootstrap resampling, and statistical significance was assessed by permutation testing with random reassignment of genotype labels. 

## Multivariate reanalysis of interindividual variability 

As a continuous alternative to the categorical variability index, phenotypic variability was quantified using a robust median-based log-variability metric (MAD log-ratio) relative to a reference genotype. For each parameter, within-genotype dispersion was estimated using the median absolute deviation. Log-ratios of MAD values were computed for each genotype relative to the reference genotype, and the mean across all parameters was used as a summary measure of relative variability. ## Quantification of canalization and repeatability 

As a continuous alternative to the categorical canalization index, behavioral or anatomical repeatability was quantified using the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC). For repeated behavioral measurements, ICC estimates the proportion of variance attributable to stable between-individual differences across days. For bilateral anatomical measurements, ICC estimates the degree of left-right correspondence within individuals. Higher ICC values indicate greater behavioral consistency or developmental stability, whereas lower ICC values indicate reduced canalization. Group differences in ICC-based canalization were evaluated by permutation testing with Benjamini-Hochberg correction.

# Results 

Drosophila mercatorum is a facultatively parthenogenic species in which rare parthenogenic events can occur in otherwise sexually reproducing populations (Carson, 1967; Stalker, 1954). Stable, fully parthenogenic lines have been generated experimentally by selecting for high rates of parthenogenesis followed by inbreeding (Templeton, Carson, et al., 1976). Because D. mercatorum reproduces parthenogenically by pronuclear duplication, offspring become fully homozygous and clonal after a single parthenogenic generation (Templeton, Sing, et al., 1976). To test how clonality and homozygosity affect phenotypic variance, we compared sexually reproducing and parthenogenic lines from two geographic origins, Brazil and Hawaii. We further analyzed offspring from facultatively parthenogenic mothers, inbred derivatives of wild-type lines, and F 1 rescue animals generated by crossing parthenogenic females with wild-type males. Together, these cohorts differ in both interindividual genetic variation and overall heterozygosity. Because monozygotic twins are typically more phenotypically similar than unrelated individuals (Boomsma et al., 2002; Johnson et al., 2009), we initially expected that clonality in 

D. mercatorum would reduce interindividual phenotypic variance. To test this prediction, we quantified behavioral and anatomical traits across multiple assays spanning locomotor behavior, sleep, visual physiology, and morphology. 

## 1. Clonal parthenogenic flies show increased behavioral variance and reduced behavioral consistency 

We first analyzed visually guided locomotor behavior in the two-stripe Buridan assay (Fig. 1A), in which flies walk freely on a circular platform relative to two opposing visual landmarks (Colomb et al., 2012; Linneweber et al., 2020). Compared with wild-type flies of both sexes, parthenogenic females showed markedly reduced stripe fixation and broad shifts in mean behavior across the 41 quantified parameters (Fig. 1B,C; Supplementary Fig.

Because parthenogenic flies showed weaker stripe fixation, we next asked whether this reflected gross visual impairment rather than altered visually guided locomotion. In a one-stripe version of Buridan’s paradigm, all groups, including parthenogenic flies, still showed a clear preference for the stripe. However, this preference was weaker in the parthenogenic animals (Supplementary Fig. S3). Likewise, electroretinogram recordings showed normal sustained depolarization amplitudes, whereas the on-transient was reduced in parthenogenic flies (Supplementary Fig. S4). These results argue against blindness and instead suggest a more subtle impairment in visual processing or sensorimotor transformation in the parthenogenic flies. We then asked whether clonality reduced behavioral variance, as predicted from reduced genetic diversity (Chai, 1960; Festing, 1976; Festing, 1979). It did not. Instead, parthenogenic flies showed more variable locomotor trajectories than controls (Fig. 1D), and this was confirmed quantitatively by increased interindividual variability across the 41 behavioral parameters, as measured by both the MAD-based index and MAD log-ratio reanalysis (Fig. 1E; Supplementary Figs. S1 and S2). The same overall pattern was observed in an independent Hawaii-derived dataset. Thus, genetically clonal populations displayed greater, not lower, interindividual behavioral variance. Increased variance can arise either from stable among-individual differences (Gosling, 2008; Réale et al., 2007) or from reduced within-individual consistency over time (Maloney et al., 2024). To distinguish between these possibilities, we retested flies on three consecutive days. Parthenogenic flies showed markedly reduced behavioral consistency across days (Fig. 1F,G; Supplementary Figs. S2 and S5), indicating that clonality does not increase behavioral predictability. Instead, clonal flies showed reduced behavioral canalization, with more variable and less consistent behavioral output over time. Importantly, reduced behavioral consistency

## 2. Activity monitoring reveals altered mean behavior and reduced temporal consistency 

To test whether the Buridan phenotype generalized to a second assay, we monitored locomotor activity and sleep in D. mercatorum using Trikinetics activity monitors over 2 weeks. Parthenogenic flies again differed from sexually reproducing controls in mean circadian activity and sleep phenotypes across 21 parameters (Fig. 2A,B; Supplementary Figs. S6 and S7). In contrast to the Buridan assay, however, interindividual variability was not significantly increased in the parthenogenic flies (Fig. 2C,D). Longitudinal analysis nevertheless revealed a marked reduction in within-individual consistency over time (Fig. 2E). Thus, across two independent behavioral paradigms, clonality robustly shifts mean behavior and reduces behavioral consistency, but does not uniformly increase interindividual variance. 

## 3. Clonality alters multiple anatomical traits and broadly reduces developmental robustness 

We next asked whether the phenotypic effects of clonality were specific to behavior or extended to morphology. We first examined the wings, a classic system for studying size variation, fluctuating asymmetry, and developmental canalization (Debat & David, 2001; Palmer & Strobeck, 1986). Parthenogenic flies had significantly larger wings than both sexually reproducing control groups, with consistent shifts across all 27 wing parameters (Fig. 3A; Supplementary Figs. S8 and S9). As expected, males had smaller wings than females. Changes in wing variance were not uniform. Parthenogenic flies showed increased variance in some traits and reduced variance in others, including elevated variability in specific wing veins such as L7 and L17. More strikingly, fluctuating asymmetry was markedly increased, most prominently in wing vein L7, resulting in reduced canalization across wing traits (Fig. 3A; Supplementary Figs. S8 and S9). ## 4. Loss of heterozygosity, rather than clonality alone, drives the major phenotypic effects of parthenogenesis 

Because parthenogenic reproduction in D. mercatorum produces nearly completely homozygous offspring, we next asked whether loss of heterozygosity, rather than clonality itself, drives the observed shifts in trait means, interindividual variability, and canalization. We first tested whether the increased variability in our parthenogenic populations could simply reflect the contribution of a large number of parthenogenic mothers. To address this, we analyzed behavioral and wing-morphological traits in offspring derived from a single parthenogenic mother. These flies closely matched the pooled parthenogenic populations in both Buridan behavior and wing anatomy (Supplementary Figs. S15 and S16), indicating that high numbers of mothers due to low fertility cannot account for the elevated variability observed in the parthenogenic lines. We next sought to separate the effects of homozygosity from those of clonal development itself by examining a facultative parthenogenic strain with elevated parthenogenic potential (Sperling et al., 2023; Templeton, Carson, et al., 1976). In this strain, reproduction is typically sexual, so parthenogenic offspring do not form a fully clonal population and can retain substantial interindividual genetic variation. We then tested directly whether reduced heterozygosity is sufficient to reproduce the clonal phenotype by generating inbred lines without selection. Even relatively mild inbreeding over five generations shifted mean behavioral phenotypes away from the wild-type state (Fig. 4A; Supplementary Figs. S22-S24). Effects on interindividual behavioral variability were already evident at this stage, although their direction depended on sex and population background. By contrast, reductions in behavioral consistency were more reproducible, with inbred flies often showing intermediate canalization between wild-type and parthenogenic animals. After 10 generations of inbreeding, these effects were often amplified, although in some cases they were partially reversed, indicating that progressive homozygosity has complex and nonlinear effects on behavioral means, variance structure, and repeatability. Wing anatomy showed a similarly partial recapitulation (Fig. 4B; Supplementary Figs. S25-S27). Inbred lines differed from wild type in mean wing morphology, with mild inbreeding often shifting wings toward smaller values and more extensive inbreeding shifting them back toward the enlarged phenotype observed in parthenogenic flies. As in the behavioral assays, effects on interindividual variance were heterogeneous across sexes, populations, and traits.

They further show that the consequences of extreme homozygosity are not limited to changes in trait means, but extend to interindividual variability and, most consistently, to reduced behavioral and developmental canalization.

# Discussion 

A central assumption in biology is that reducing genetic variation should reduce phenotypic variation. Our results challenge this view. In Drosophila mercatorum , populations of clonal parthenogenic flies did not become phenotypically uniform. Instead, relative to sexually reproducing controls, they showed broad shifts in trait means, altered interindividual variability, increased fluctuating asymmetry, and reduced behavioral and developmental canalization across multiple assays. These effects were partially reproduced by inbreeding and largely reversed by outcrossing, indicating that loss of heterozygosity is a major driver of these phenotypes. Together, our findings indicate that extreme genetic uniformity destabilizes developmental systems and thereby alters phenotypic means, variance structure, and canalization. However, our results do not suggest that reduced genetic variance due to homozygosity produces a single, universal variance signature. Changes in interindividual variance were trait-dependent: in some assays, variance increased; in others, it decreased; and in still others, it remained largely unchanged. By contrast, reduced robustness emerged as the most consistent effect. Across behavior and anatomy, parthenogenic flies repeatedly showed reduced repeatability, increased asymmetry, or weaker bilateral correspondence, indicating impaired canalization. Thus, clonality did not erase individuality, but altered its structure by combining persistent interindividual differences with reduced temporal stability. This distinction is important. Classical quantitative genetics predicts that reducing genetic variation should reduce phenotypic variance under many conditions (Falconer, 1996), and this expectation is not wrong in principle. However, our data show that when homozygosity becomes extreme, the destabilization of developmental buffering can offset or even outweigh the variance-reducing effects of genetic uniformity. The genetic comparisons in this study point to reduced heterozygosity, rather than clonality alone, as the principal driver of these effects. Facultative parthenogenic offspring likewise reproduced key aspects of the parthenogenic phenotype, despite not forming a clonal population, demonstrating that clonality is not required for these phenotypic changes. Outcrossing parthenogenic females to wild-type males largely restored wild-type-like behavior and morphology and, in several measures, even enhanced canalization beyond the wild-type state. This rescue strongly implicates heterozygosity itself as a stabilizing factor, consistent with classical genetic ideas of dominance, inbreeding depression (DAVID et al., 2007), and hybrid vigor (Lippman & Zamir, 2007). In this framework, extreme homozygosity may expose recessive deleterious alleles or destabilizing allele combinations that are normally buffered in more heterozygous genomes, thereby increasing sensitivity to developmental noise and weakening the robustness of trait formation. More broadly, our findings support the view that developmental robustness is itself a biologically variable property that depends on genetic context. Canalization is understood as a buffering process that stabilizes phenotypes against perturbation (Waddington, 1942), but our data show that this buffering can break down under extreme homozygosity. Importantly, this breakdown was evident across multiple levels of organization, including locomotor behavior, repeated behavioral consistency, bilateral wing symmetry, bristle patterning, eye morphology, and brain anatomy. The recurrent appearance of reduced repeatability and increased asymmetry across such diverse traits argues against a purely assay-specific explanation and instead suggests that homozygosity compromises general mechanisms of developmental stabilization. In this sense, the most consistent consequence of clonality in D. mercatorum was not simply a change in trait variance, but a loss of the robustness that normally constrains phenotypic divergence among genetically similar individuals. These findings have practical implications for the design and interpretation of experiments that rely on genetically uniform animals. Inbred and isogenic strains are widely used because they are expected to reduce variance (Festing, 1979; Tuttle et al., 2018; Würbel, 2000), thereby improving reproducibility and statistical power. Our results suggest that this expectation is not universally valid. Although reducing genetic diversity can in some cases

This may help explain why highly standardized animal models can still show substantial unexplained variability despite strong control of genotype and environment (Crabbe et al., 1999; Richter et al., 2009). More generally, our data raise the possibility that controlled heterozygosity (Mather, 1946), rather than maximal genetic uniformity, may in some contexts provide a more robust experimental substrate by preserving developmental buffering while still limiting uncontrolled genetic variation. Several limitations should be considered. First, our study focuses on a single facultatively parthenogenic species, and the specific route to clonality in D. mercatorum ,namely, pronuclear duplication after meiosis, is biologically highly unusual (Eisman &Kaufman, 2007; Templeton, Sing, et al., 1976). The extent to which the same relationships among homozygosity, variance, and canalization hold in other clonal or highly inbred systems remains to be tested. Second, the phenotypic effects we observed were not uniform across traits, sexes, or population backgrounds. Some traits showed increased interindividual variance, others showed reduced variance, and some were affected mainly at the level of canalization. This heterogeneity is informative in itself, but it also argues against overly simple generalizations. Thus, our data do not imply that homozygosity universally increases phenotypic variance. Rather, they show that extreme homozygosity can reproducibly erode developmental robustness, with consequences for trait means, variance structure, and reproducibility that depend on the biological context. Taken together, our results show that genetic uniformity does not necessarily produce phenotypic uniformity. In D. mercatorum , the transition to clonality and extreme homozygosity altered mean phenotypes broadly, changed interindividual variance in trait-dependent ways, and most consistently reduced behavioral and developmental canalization. Inbreeding partially reproduced these effects, whereas outcrossing restored robustness, identifying loss of heterozygosity as a major determinant of the phenotype.
