Abstract 34 

Recombination in polyploid genomes is generally constrained to homologous or 35 

homoeologous chromosomes; however, how chromosomal rearrangements influence 36 

recombination between chromosomes remains unclear. Here, we demonstrate that large-37 

scale chromosomal rearrangements in the wild relatives of wheat are associated with 38 

recombination involving non-homoeologous chromosomes or arms during alien gene 39 

introgression under conditions that permit homoeologous recombination mediated by 40 

ph1b . Using a wheat chromosome 6A monosomic-induced 6AS•6CL Robertsonian 41 

translocation combined with ph1b -mediated recombination, we generated 17 independent 42 

recombinants carrying a new stem rust resistance gene, Sr69 , from Aegilops 43 

caudata chromosome arm 6CL. Unexpectedly, 94.1% (16 of 17) of recombinants resulted 44 

from exchanges with wheat group-7 chromosomes rather than with the homoeologous 45 

group-6 chromosome. Comparative sequence- and marker-based analyses identified a 46 

67-Mb rearranged interval on Ae. caudata 6CL that corresponds to telomeric regions of 47 

the long arms of wheat group-7 chromosomes. Sequence similarity within this interval was 48 

quantitatively associated with recombination frequency, with higher similarity 49 

corresponding to more frequent translocations. Physical and optical mapping showed that 50 

recombination within the rearranged interval generated compensating 7A/6C, 7B/6C, and 51 

7D/6C translocations, whereas recombination outside this region produced non-52 

compensating 6A/6C exchanges. An independent case involving the powdery mildew 53 

resistance gene Pm7C showed a similar correspondence between a rearranged 7CL 54 

region and preferential introgression into wheat 7DS. Together, these results indicate that 55 

ph1b -mediated recombination involving structurally altered chromosomes is driven by 56 

local chromosomal structure and sequence similarity rather than strict homoeologous 57 

group identity. This provides a mechanistic basis for harnessing untapped beneficial 58 

genes from structurally rearranged alien genomes. 59 

Significance Statement 60 

Alien gene introgression is a powerful strategy for wheat improvement, typically relying on 61 

ph1b -mediated recombination between homoeologous chromosomes. The genomic basis 62 

and outcomes of introgression from structurally rearranged alien chromosomes remain 63 

unclear. Here, we show that ph1b -induced recombination can efficiently target wheat-64 

allosyntenic blocks in rearranged alien genomes, preferentially transferring genes from 65 

structurally altered alien segments into their syntenic regions on wheat chromosomes of 66 

different homoeologous groups. Crossover formation is governed by extended sequence 67 

similarity within corresponding intervals rather than strict collinearity across entire 68 

homoeologous chromosomes. As many wild species exhibit extensive genome 69 

rearrangement, these findings and methodologies expand access to underexploited 70 

genetic diversity embedded within highly rearranged wild genomes for wheat 71 

improvement. 72

Introgression from wild relatives in wheat has largely relied on inducing meiotic pairing 83 

and recombination between homoeologous chromosomes. The Ph1 (pairing 84 

homoeologous 1) locus on chromosome 5B ensures homologous synapsis (autosyndesis) 85 

while suppressing pairing between homoeologous chromosomes (allosyndesis) (6, 7). 86 

Ph1 spans a complex ~2.5-Mb region containing subtelomeric heterochromatin and cdc2 -87 

related genes (8). In the absence of Ph1 , as in ph1b or ph1c mutants, homoeologous 88 

recombination is permitted and has been widely used in pre-breeding to transfer genes 89 

from wild species into wheat (9–12). Most ph1b -mediated introgression efforts have 90 

focused on donor species with chromosomes broadly colinear with wheat, because 91 

collinearity facilitates homoeologous pairing and formation of stable translocations. 92 

In contrast, many Triticeae species harbor extensive chromosomal rearrangements 93 

that disrupt homoeologous collinearity with the wheat genome, including rye (13), Ae. 94 

caudata (14, 15), Ae. umbellulata (16, 17), and Ae. uniaristata (18). Although these 95 

species represent rich sources of genetic variation, their structural divergence raises two 96 

fundamental questions regarding recombination: Can genes within rearranged 97 

chromosomal segments be efficiently transferred into wheat, and what factors determine 98 

recombination patterns when homoeologous pairing is permitted (e.g., in ph1b mutants)? 99 

Thus, the mechanisms underlying recombination among structurally complex 100 

chromosomes remain a central and unresolved challenge in polyploid genome biology. 101 

Among structurally divergent wheat relatives, Ae. caudata (2n = 2x = 14, CC) is a 102 

cytogenetically well characterized system for examining the effects of structural 103 

rearrangement. Six disomic addition (DA) lines, each carrying individual Ae. caudata 104 

chromosomes from accession S740-69 in the wheat cultivar ‘Alcedo’, were previously 105 

developed (19, 20). Of these, five lines contain Ae. caudata chromosomes with 106 

translocations or inversions relative to wheat (14, 15, 21). Notably, the Ae. caudata 107 

chromosomes in DA lines AIII(D) and AIV(F) were assigned to 6C and 7C, respectively, 108 

but exhibit interchromosomal and intrachromosomal rearrangements. The telomeric 109 

region of the 6C long arm (6CL) is syntenic with the telomeric regions of the long arms of 110 

wheat group-7 chromosomes (group-7L), whereas the telomeric region of 7CL 111 

corresponds to the regions of the short arms of wheat group-7 chromosomes (group-7S) 112 

(14, 15). Diseases assays revealed that AIII(D) and AIV(F) confer resistance to multiple 113 

isolates of powdery mildew pathogen Blumeria graminis f. sp. tritici (Bgt ), and that AIII(D) 114 

also conferred resistance to diverse races of stem rust pathogen Puccinia graminis f. sp. 115 

tritici (Pgt ) (21), making them valuable resources for studying gene introgression from 116 

structurally complex genomes. 117 

Here, we used a chromosome engineering system to introgress a stem rust resistance 118 

(Sr ) gene from Aegilops caudata chromosome 6C into wheat under p h1b -mediated 119 

recombination. This system provides an opportunity to examine how recombination occurs 120 

between structurally rearranged chromosomal segments in a polyploid genome. We 121 

examined whether genes embedded in rearranged chromosomal regions can be 122 

efficiently transferred into wheat and what factors influence recombination when 123

To test whether this structural correspondence extends to the sequence level, we 265 

compared the rearranged 6CL region with wheat chromosomes 7A, 7B, and 7D using 266 

sliding-window analysis of aligned genomic blocks (Fig. 2B and SI Appendix , Fig. S9 and 267 

Table S7). At small window sizes (< 30 blocks), sequence identity distributions were broad 268 

and overlapping, indicating local heterogeneity. With increasing window size (30–200 269 

blocks), variance decreased and chromosome-level difference became apparent. 270 

Averaging ≥100 blocks revealed significant differences among 6CL/7AL, 6CL/7BL, and 271 

6CL/7DL (P < 0.0001) ( SI Appendix , Table S7). At 200-block resolution, sequence identity 272 

was highest between 6CL and 7AL (30.11%), followed by 7DL (29.30%) and 7BL (28.87%) 273 

(Fig. 2B and SI Appendix , Table S7), whereas identities with group-6 chromosomes were 274 

consistently lower (6AL, 26.54%; 6BL, 24.71%; 6DL, 24.53%). This gradient of sequence 275

a quantitative basis for the predominance of group-7/6C translocations. 279

crossover positions relative to the rearranged 6CL block, chromosomal rearrangement, 284 

and sequence similarity within the distal 6CL region explains the translocation patterns in 285

Sr69 introgression lines show broad stem rust resistance and variable powdery 307 

mildew resistance. All 17 Sr69 introgression lines were evaluated against 16 domestic 308 

and foreign Pgt races (Table 3 and SI Appendix , Table S1). All lines were resistant to 13 309 

races, producing infection types (ITs) of 0;–23, indicating that Sr69 confers broad-310 

spectrum resistance across diverse pathogen backgrounds. Reactions to race QTHJC 311 

were variable (ITs 2–34), whereas all lines were susceptible to TCMJC and TTRTF. The 312 

6-0142 (6A/6C) line, which lacks the terminal 6CL segment, showed high ITs (3–4) to 13 313 

races except for JCMNC (IT 23 –) and QCCJC (IT 2 +), confirming that resistance in the 314 

introgression lines is associated with the distal 6CL region carrying Sr69 . Although all 17 315 

introgression lines were susceptible to TTRTF, both S740-69 and AIII(D) were highly 316 

resistant (IT 1 +), indicating that chromosome 6C likely carries additional Sr gene(s) that 317 

were not captured in the introgressed segments (Table 3). 318 

To assess temperature effects, three lines (6-0015, 6-0283, and 6-0480) were tested 319 

with eight races under greenhouse (20–23 °C) and growth chamber (18 °C and 25 °C) 320 

conditions (Fig. 2D and SI Appendix , Table S9). The three lines maintained their 321 

resistance spectrums across temperatures. However, temperature influenced infection 322 

response: at 25 °C, reactions showed increased necrosis, whereas at 18 °C, infection 323 

types tended toward mesothetic. Notably, responses to QTHJC were higher in growth 324 

chambers at both 18 °C (IT 231X) and 25 °C (ITs 12N–32) than under greenhouse 325 

conditions (IT 12). 326 

Powdery mildew assays further revealed variation among introgression lines. Six lines 327 

(6-0281, 6-0480, 6-0676, 6-0724, 6-0809, and 6-1038) retained resistance from AIII(D), 328 

whereas the remaining 11 were susceptible (Fig. 2E and SI Appendix , Table S10 and 329 

S11). Because seed stocks from two to four BC ₂F₃–BC ₂F₄ families per line were tested, 330 

several lines segregated for mildew response. For example, lines 6-0724 and 6-0725— 331 

previously classified as resistant and susceptible, respectively, both segregated in 332

shorter introgressed 6CL segments than eight of the 11 susceptible lines (Fig. 1C), thereby 334 

preventing precise localization of the Pm gene(s). Because 6CL segment variation 335 

occurred mainly at proximal breakpoints, the Pm locus is likely located distal to Sr69 . Small 336 

telomeric deletions or exchanges, which are difficult to detect by GISH and markers, may 337

Discussion 340 

341 

Wild relatives are major sources of genetic variation for improving modern wheat but 342 

structural divergence between wild donors and wheat has long been considered a barrier 343 

to efficient and predictable recombination (26, 27). However, successful transfer of Pm7C 344 

(22), Sr69 , and a potentially unmapped Pm gene from structurally rearranged Ae. caudata 345 

chromosomes demonstrate that extensive chromosomal divergence does not preclude 346 

precise, stable introgression. Instead, recombination outcomes appear to be governed 347 

primarily by local structural context and sequence similarity rather than classical 348 

homoeologous group relationships. 349

Here, we engineered a chromosome constitution containing a single wheat 350 

chromosome 6A and a single 6AS•6CL Robertsonian translocation to promote ph1b -351 

mediated recombination between 6AL and 6CL. Unexpectedly, the rearranged 6CL 352 

segment preferentially recombined with its allosyntenic regions on chromosomes 7A, 7B, 353 

and 7D, despite these group-7 chromosomes being present as homologous pairs. This 354 

observation challenges the assumption that recombination in ph1b backgrounds primarily 355 

follows homoeologous group relationships and instead highlights the dominant role of 356 

chromosome-scale structural collinearity. The strong predominance of 7A/6C and 7D/6C 357 

translocations (16 lines), contrasted with the rarity of canonical 6A/6C recombination (one 358 

line), indicates that recombination can deviate substantially from expected homoeologous 359 

paring relationships. 360 

Comparative genomic analyses provide a structural explanation for this pattern. The 361 

terminal ~67 Mb of 6CL is syntenic with wheat group-7 telomeric regions rather than 362 

group-6 chromosomes (15), and recombination breakpoints are concentrated within this 363 

region. The close correspondence between rearranged chromosome structure and 364 

recombination outcomes indicates that recombination preferentially occurs within regions 365 

that share extended structural alignment, even when they reside on non-homoeologous 366 

chromosomes (Table 2). Together, these results provide clear evidence that ph1b -induced 367 

allosyndetic recombination can track syntenic regions among non-homoeologous 368 

chromosomes and can often be anticipated once chromosome-scale assemblies are 369 

available. 370 

Sequence similarity analyses across genomic scales further clarify constraints on 371 

recombination partner choice. The approximately 4 percentage point increase in 372 

sequence similarity between 6CL and group-7 chromosomes, relative to group-6, likely 373 

represents a threshold that influences partner selection and the frequency of 374 

homoeologous recombination when Ph1 -mediated constraints are removed (Fig. 2B). A 375 

similar pattern governs the ph1b -facilitated transfer of Pm7C from 7CL to 7DS (22), where 376 

sequence mismatches are accommodated at a rate commensurate with the overall 377 

divergence between chromosome pairs, conferring a recombination advantage on more 378 

closely related sequences (28). This principle likely also explains the preferential 379 

introgression of Sr68 from a Thinopyrum junceum group-4 chromosome to wheat 1BS 380 

(11); such a transfer suggests the gene resides within a rearranged Th. junceum segment 381 

that is allosyntenic with 1BS. 382 

Collectively, this study demonstrates that alien chromosomes with complex structures 383 

can be utilized predictably when their structural organization is well characterized, 384 

highlighting the importance of integrating structural genomics into chromosome 385 

engineering strategy. As genome assemblies for numerous wild relatives of wheat and 386 

other crops continue to expand, the framework established here expands access to 387 

previously underutilized diversity embedded in highly rearranged genomes. These 388 

findings support a genome structure–guided strategy for more efficient alien gene 389 

introgression for crop improvement. 390

Transmissions of Ae. caudata 6CL segments were evaluated in BC 2F2 populations and 461

# SI Appendix, Methods 

SI Appendix, Method S1. Production of Robertsonian translocations and allosyndetic recombinants 

Allosyndetic recombinants carrying stem rust resistance gene Sr69 from wheat ‘Alcedo’-

Aegilops caudata S740-69 disomic addition line AIII(D) were developed using the method as previously described (1). To generate a Robertsonian translocation, ‘Chinese Spring’ (CS) monosomic 6A (CS M6A) was crossed as the female parent with AIII(D) (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). The F 2 plants derived from double monosomic F ₁ plants (2 n = 42, 20 ″ + 1 ′6A + 1 ′6C ) were evaluated for reaction to Pgt race TMLKC and resistant plants with infection types (IT) ≤ 23 were subjected to genomic in situ hybridization (GISH) analysis following Mandel et al (1) except that root tips were collected after stem rust phenotyping. An F 2 plant (CS M6A/AIII(D)) carrying a 6AS•6CL Robertsonian translocation chromosome was used as the female parent in a cross with the CS ph1b mutant. The resulting F ₁ plants were evaluated with Pgt race TMLKC, and resistant plants were backcrossed to the CS ph1b mutant. BC ₁F₁ plants were again tested for stem rust resistance using race TMLKC. Leaf tissues were collected from resistant BC ₁F₁

plants for DNA extraction as described below. Samples were analyzed using multiplex PCR with markers AWJL3 , Psr574 , and Psr128 . AWJL3 served as a positive control, whereas Psr574 and 

Psr128 were used to detect the absence of the 5BL segment carrying Ph1 (2). Rust-resistant BC 1F1 plants lacking Ph1 were crossed as male to CS to generate a large BC 2F1 population for selection of allosyndetic recombinants carrying shortened alien chromatin segments. The pedigree of the selection population was CS/4/CS ph1b /3/CS M6A 6A/AIII(D)//CS ph1b . The allosyndetic recombinants carrying Sr69 on the short Ae. caudata chromosome segments were identified from the large BC 2F1 population based on stem rust test, marker genotyping, and GISH analysis.
