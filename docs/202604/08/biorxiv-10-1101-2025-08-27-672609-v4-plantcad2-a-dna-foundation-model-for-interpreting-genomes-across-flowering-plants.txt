# Abstract 16 

Understanding how DNA sequence encodes biological function remains a fundamental challenge 17 in biology. Flowering plants (angiosperms), the dominant terrestrial clade, exhibit maximal 18 biochemical complexity, extraordinary species diversity (over 100,000 species), relatively recent 19 origins (~160 million years), ~200-fold variation in genome size and relative compact coding 20 regions compared with other eukaryotes. These features present both a unique challenge and 21 opportunity for pre-training DNA language models to understand plant-specific evolutionary 22 conservation, regulatory architectures and genomic functions. Here, we introduce PlantCAD2, an 23 extended context, plant-specific DNA language model with single-nucleotide resolution, pre-24 trained on 65 angiosperm genomes, together with a series of public benchmarks for evaluation. 25 Comprehensive zero-shot testing shows that PlantCAD2 (676 million parameters) efficiently 26 captures evolutionary conservation, surpassing the 7-billion-parameter Evo2 model in 10 of 12 27 tasks. With parameter-efficient fine-tuning, PlantCAD2 also outperforms the 1-billion-parameter 28 AgroNT across seven cross-species tasks including chromatin accessible region, gene 29 expression and protein translation. Moreover, its 8,192bp context window substantially improves 30 accessible chromatin prediction in large genomes such as maize (AUPRC increasing from 0.587 31 to 0.711), underscoring the importance of long-range context for modeling distal regulation. 32 Together, these results establish PlantCAD2 as a powerful, efficient, and versatile foundation 33 model for plant genomics, enabling accurate genome annotation across diverse species. 34

# Introduction 35 

Deciphering how DNA sequence encodes molecular functions, phenotypes and fitness remains 36 a fundamental goal in biology. The rapid decline in sequencing costs has enabled large-scale 37 initiatives such as the Darwin Tree of Life project 1, the Earth BioGenome Project 2, the Vertebrate 38 Genomes Project 3, and the 10KP Plant Genome Project 4, which collectively aim to sequence 39 tens of thousands of species across the tree of life, with plants alone contributing over a thousand 40 assembled genomes 5. While genomic data accumulates exponentially, functional annotations lag 41 far behind, particularly in plants where labeled data exists for only a few model species and crops 42 

> 6

. Flowering plants (angiosperms) 7, the dominant terrestrial clade 8, underpin global food security 43 and ecosystem function. They exhibit maximal biochemical complexity, extraordinary species 44 diversity (over 300,000 species), wide genome size variation, and relatively compact coding 45 regions compared with other eukaryotes—features that present both a unique challenge and an 46 opportunity for computational approaches. This widening gap between available sequences and 47 functional knowledge highlights the critical need for models that can learn from raw sequences 48 alone and transfer knowledge across plant species. 49 

50 

Foundation models 9, large neural networks pre-trained on vast collections of unlabeled data using 51 self-supervised objectives have emerged as a powerful approach to this challenge. Rather than 52 requiring large, labeled datasets for each task, these models learn general representations that 53 can be adapted to specific applications with minimal supervision. This paradigm has achieved 54 significant success in protein science, where models such as ESM 10–12 , ProtTrans 13 , and 55 ProBERT 14 have demonstrated strong performance in predicting protein function 15 , structure 16 ,56 and variant effect 17 .57 58 In contrast, genomic LMs are still rapidly evolving, with recent developments spanning DNA 18–27 ,59 RNA 28–30 and transcriptomes 31–33 . Early work such as the DNABERT 20 pre-trained BERT 34 model 60 on the human genome showed improved regulatory element prediction. Multi-species pre-training 61 soon proved critical for learning evolutionary conservation 21,22,25,35 , a principle first demonstrated 62 in plants by GPN 22 , which was pre-trained on eight Brassicales genomes and achieved strong 63 variant effect prediction. Broader multi-species models followed, including Nucleotide 64 Transformer 25 (850 genomes excluding plants) and Evo 18 (prokaryotic and phage genomes). 65 Within plants, AgroNT 36 extended pre-training to 48 genomes with longer context windows but 66 sacrificed single-nucleotide resolution through non-overlapping k-mer tokenization. # Results 106 

## PlantCAD2: an extended-context DNA language model for angiosperms 107 

PlantCAD2 builds on the original PlantCAD 27 DNA language model, preserving its single-108 nucleotide tokenization and masked language modeling objective, while introducing four major 109 improvements: architectural efficiency, context length, parameter scale, and phylogenetic breadth 110 (Figure 1A ). First, PlantCAD2 retains the Caduceus 26 architecture with its bidirectional, reverse-111 complement-equivariant design, but replaces the original Mamba 37 blocks with Mamba2 blocks 112 

> 42

. Mamba2 has substantial advantages over Mamba1, leveraging structured state space duality 113 for more efficient parallel training and simplifying recurrence computations to reduce memory 114 usage (see Methods). Compared to traditional transformer architectures 43,44 , PlantCAD2 model 115 architecture shows a much slower increase in inference time than modernBERT models under 116 the same input and output dimensions ( Figure S1A ), due to the inherent efficiency of state space 117 models in handling long sequences 37,45 . Exploiting this efficiency, PlantCAD2 takes 8,192 base 118 pair (bp) windows, which is a 16-fold increase over the 512-bp windows used in PlantCAD. 119 Second, to evaluate the effect of model sizes on performance, we trained a series of depth-scaled 120 PlantCAD2 models of 88M, 311M and 694M parameters ( Figure 1A-1B ), which we named 121 PlantCAD2-S, PlantCAD2-M, and PlantCAD2-L respectively. As expected, following pre-training, 122 the largest model (PlantCAD2-L) demonstrated the best masked token prediction accuracy 123 (0.657), followed by PlantCAD2-M (0.641) and PlantCAD2-S (0.598), when evaluated on hold-124 out test set by randomly masking 15% of nucleotides per sequence ( Figure S1B ). However, the 125 largest model also shows slowest inference speed at ~32,238 nucleotides per second on a 126 NVIDIA H100 80 GB PCIe GPU ( Figure 1C ) and uses ~27G GPU memory with a of 32 ( Table 3 ), 127 reflecting the typical trade-off between accuracy and computational efficiency. The smallest 128 PlantCAD2 model, PlantCAD2-S, achieves a 4.5-fold ( Figure 1C ) higher inference throughput 129 while requiring considerably less GPU memory at equivalent batch sizes ( Table 3 ), highlighting 130 its practicality for high-throughput analyses. Despite differences in model size, the three models 131 showed high correlation in their per-species prediction accuracies (r > 0.97), suggesting 132 consistent learning patterns across scales ( Figure S1C; Supplemental Table 1 ). Third, to assess 133 the effect of input length on pretraining accuracy, we varied the context window size from 512bp 134 to 8,192bp and evaluated performance by masking the central token. All three models showed 135 improved masked token prediction accuracy with longer contexts, underscoring the importance 136 of extended context for modeling kilobase-scale genomic dependencies ( Figure 1D ). ## PlantCAD2 accurately predicts evolutionary conservation with a zero-shot 167 

## strategy 168 

Evolutionary conservation, commonly estimated from multiple sequence alignment (MSA), is 169 widely used to identify deleterious mutations that may reduce organismal fitness 47–50 . However, 170 estimating a genome-wide MSA is particularly challenging in plants due to extensive transposable 171 element (TE) insertions and their high turnover rate, which obscure orthologous relationships 172 outside of conserved coding regions 51 . This limitation highlights the need for alignment-free 173

Given that PlantCAD2 is pre-174 trained on 65 evolutionary distant species, we hypothesize that PlantCAD2 can be used to predict 175 evolutionary conservation without an MSA. We first evaluated how accurate PlantCAD2 is to 176 distinguish highly conserved sites versus less conserved sites using a zero-shot strategy. As 177 illustrated in Figure 2A, we used the masked nucleotide/token prediction accuracy from the frozen 178 model to represent per-base conservation, which means highly conserved bases would receive 179 higher predicted probabilities for the reference allele, whereas less conserved bases would yield 180 lower confidence scores. We benchmarked the performance of PlantCAD2 against three 181 baselines: its predecessor PlantCAD 27 , GPN 22 (a plant specific DNA LM trained on Brassicales 182 genomes), and Evo2 19 , a general-purpose DNA language model pre-trained using a causal 183 language modeling (CLM) objective, also known as next-token prediction. Unlike masked 184 language modeling, which enables access to both upstream and downstream context, CLM 185 imposes a strict left-to-right constraint, meaning Evo2 is inherently unidirectional. Therefore, we 186 input the entire sequences without masking for Evo2 and use the likelihood of the model to 187 represent conservation. Notably, Evo2 was trained at a substantially greater scale, with 7 billion 188 parameters and 9.3 trillion nucleotides, which is over 310 times more training data than used for 189 PlantCAD2, therefore providing a rigorous benchmark for assessing the efficiency and 190 representational power of our models. We excluded AgroNT from zero-shot evaluation as its non-191 overlapping k-mer tokenization strategy prevents single-nucleotide resolution tasks, and we 192 previously demonstrated its limited zero-shot capabilities 27 .193 194 We assessed this strategy in three independent tasks. First, we performed cross-species 195 alignments of 34 Andropogoneae genomes 27 to the sorghum reference genome, and identified 196 highly conserved and less conserved sites based on alignment coverage and identity (see 197 Methods). PlantCAD2 consistently outperformed PlantCAD in distinguishing highly conserved 198 from less conserved sites in the sorghum genome, with the largest PlantCAD2 achieving the 199 highest AUROC ( Figure 2B; Supplemental Table 2 ). Notably, PlantCAD2-M achieved slightly 200 better performance than Evo2 (AUROC 0.708 vs 0.691) despite being ~22-fold smaller (311M vs 201 7B parameters), while PlantCAD2-L, being ~11-fold smaller (694M parameters), further improved 202 to 0.73. This demonstrates that our PlantCAD2 models can match or exceed Evo2's performance 203 with far fewer parameters. Given that PlantCAD2 is pre-trained with a context window of 8192 bp, 204 we also examined the effect of context length on conservation prediction. AUROC scores 205 increased with longer sequence contexts, plateauing at 4096 bp for all PlantCAD2 models ( Figure 206 

S2A; Supplemental Table 2 ). These findings indicate that evolutionary constraint signals benefit 207

Given Evo2’s unidirectional nature, we 216 hypothesized it might struggle with features requiring bidirectional context, particularly translation 217 initiation sites (TIS), where both upstream regulatory motifs in the 5′ UTR and downstream coding 218 sequence context critically influence start codon recognition and conservation 54 . To test this 219 hypothesis, we separately evaluated performance on TIS versus non-TIS positions within coding 220 sequences. For non-TIS sites, PlantCAD2 models showed lower performance compared to Evo2 221 (Figure 2C; Supplemental Table 2 ), potentially because Evo2's training data included mature 222 mRNA sequences while PlantCAD2 was trained exclusively on genomic DNA, giving Evo2 an 223 advantage in coding sequence conservation tasks. We also note that the original PlantCAD 224 slightly outperforms all PlantCAD2 models on this specific task ( Figure 2C ). This likely reflects 225 differences in input–pretraining alignment: PlantCAD was pre-trained on short 512 bp windows 226 enriched for coding regions, and the CDS-centered inputs used here closely match that training 227 setting. In contrast, PlantCAD2 was pre-trained on 8 kb genomic windows that systematically 228 include intronic and intergenic contexts. Consequently, restricting the input to CDS-centered 229 sequences removes intronic context and deviates from PlantCAD2’s pre-training setting, 230 attenuating the advantages conferred by its long receptive field. Nevertheless, despite this 231 mismatch, PlantCAD2 models still achieve competitive performance, indicating that their learned 232 representations remain informative even under constrained input conditions. Interestingly, when 233 evaluating TIS conservation, we observed a strong bias in Evo2: its AUROC dropped drastically 234 to 0.534, barely above random. In contrast, PlantCAD2 maintained robust performance (AUROC: 235 0.632–0.670; Figure 2D; Supplemental Table 2 ). This TIS-specific weakness in 238 Evo2 validates our hypothesis: without access to coding sequence contexts that are more 239 evolutionary constrained, Evo2 cannot properly assess the conservation patterns at translation 240 start sites. ## PlantCAD2 accurately predicts within-species conserved transcriptional and 276 

## translational junction sites with zero-shot strategy 277 

We next quantified how well PlantCAD2 captured the sequence context that defines key 278 transcriptional and translational junctions. Using a similar zero-shot strategy, we designed four 279 tasks to recapitulate motifs ( Figure 3 ). Instead of masking one base pair, for each annotated 280 junction, we replaced the annotated motif with consecutive [MASK] tokens. Given the non-281 canonical motifs do exist in plants 57 , we retained all annotated sites without filtering for canonical 282 signals. Consequently, the evaluation included both canonical and non-canonical variants as 283 defined in the reference annotation. For example, translation initiation sites included both 284 canonical ATG and non-canonical start codons such as GTG and TTG. We then extracted a fixed 285 8,192-bp window centered on the masked motif and presented the entire masked sequence to 286 the model without fine-tuning. A prediction was considered correct if the model’s top-1 287 reconstruction exactly matched the ground truth motif found in the genome. 288 289 As above, we benchmarked the performance of PlantCAD2 against PlantCAD, GPN and Evo2. 290 Given what we observed in Figure 2D that Evo2 is limited with its poor TIS conservation prediction, 291 we evaluated it using two configurations: (1) forward sequences (Evo2-fwd), where the model 292 uses upstream context to predict the junction, and (2) reverse-complement sequences (Evo2-rc), 293 where the model uses downstream context (reverse complemented) to predict the junction. We next investigated whether PlantCAD2 learned chromatin states by assessing its performance 408 in predicting genome-wide chromatin accessibility across multiple plant species. We formulated 409 this as a binary classification task, in which the model predicts whether a given genomic region 410 corresponds to an accessible chromatin region, as defined by ATAC-seq ( Methods ). We used 411 recently published ATAC-seq data including 11 diverse plant species, including both dicots and 412 monocots 67 . # Discussion 621 

In this work, we present PlantCAD2, an extended-context window DNA language model that 622 substantially advances the sequence-to-function modeling in plant genomics. Building on the 623 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 3, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.08.27.672609 doi: bioRxiv preprint

foundation laid by PlantCAD, PlantCAD2 features a model architecture that is three times larger, 624 a 16-fold longer context window (8,192bp vs. 512bp), and a pre-training dataset that is evenly 625 distributed across angiosperm orders to better capture phylogenetic diversity. Through 626 comprehensive zero-shot and fine-tuned evaluations, we demonstrate that PlantCAD2 not only 627 exhibits strong cross-species generalization, but also achieves superior performance across a 628 wide range of sequence-to-function tasks, including evolutionary conservation prediction, 629 functional important junction sites prediction involved in both transcription and translation, variant 630 (including indels) effect estimation, cis-regulatory activity, gene expression, and protein 631 translation. 632 633 A key design consideration for PlantCAD2 is the careful curation of 65 angiosperm species to 634 maximize phylogenetic diversity while minimizing data bias. Rather than using all publicly 635 available plant genomes, we selected a single representative genome per genus. This balanced 636 strategy avoids two major issues. First, pre-training on all available genomes would dramatically 637 increase computational cost. As shown by scaling laws in large language models, increasing 638 dataset size alone while keeping model size fixed leads to diminishing returns unless model 639 capacity and training compute are expanded proportionally 80 . In practice, simply adding more 640 genomes would not necessarily improve performance unless the model were enlarged 641 substantially, which would reduce its efficiency and complicate deployment. Second, public 642 genome resources are heavily skewed toward a few lineages such as grasses and Brassicaceae 643 

> 81

. Such skewed distributions have been widely documented across NLP 82,83 and protein 644 language models 84 . These studies consistently show that large models tend to learn 645 disproportionately from overrepresented groups in the training data, resulting in biased 646 representations and reduced generalization to underrepresented categories. # Methods 726 

## Preparing pre-training genomes 727 

A total of 65 genomes were selected for pre-training from the Phytozome database. To ensure 728 taxonomic relevance and minimize redundancy, we applied a series of manual filtering steps. First, 729 non-angiosperm species were excluded. For each remaining species, we retained only the most 730 recent genome assembly version. In cases where two haplotypes were available for a species, 731 we selected the haplotype with the higher N50 value; if N50 values were comparable, we retained 732 the assembly with fewer scaffolds to prioritize less fragmented genomes. Taxonomic information, 733 including order, family, and genus, was appended to each genome to facilitate downstream 734 analyses, and their relationships were visualized using a published time-callibrated phylogeny 91 .735 For each selected genome, we extracted genomic sequences centered on each annotated gene, 736 extending 5 kilobases (kb) upstream and 5 kb downstream from the gene center. These ±5 kb 737 gene-centered regions were then segmented into overlapping windows of 8,192 bp with a step 738 size of 4,096 bp, ensuring comprehensive coverage of regulatory and genic features while 739 maintaining continuity across sequence boundaries. These windows served as input sequences 740 for model pre-training. 741 

## PlantCAD2 model architecture and pre-training 742 

PlantCAD2 builds upon the Caduceus architecture 26 used in PlantCAD 27 , retaining its key design 743 principles while incorporating architectural improvements. Like PlantCAD1, PlantCAD2 maintains 744 three core features: (1) bidirectional sequence processing, where sequences are processed both 745 forward and reverse with outputs summed together; (2) reverse-complement (RC) equivariance, 746 ensuring the model commutes with RC operations; and (3) parameter-efficient bidirectional 747 implementation through shared linear projections between forward and reverse passes. 748 749 The primary architectural improvement in PlantCAD2 is the replacement of Mamba1 blocks 37 

750 with Mamba2 blocks 42 . Mamba2 introduces a structured state space duality that recasts the 751 selective state space computation into an equivalent convolutional form using structured matrices, 752 improving parallelism and hardware efficiency. This dual representation enables significantly 753 faster training (up to 2–4× in some scenarios) while retaining the input-dependent selection 754 mechanism that allows the model to dynamically modulate state updates based on sequence 755

758 For the pre-training of PlantCAD2, each model was trained for 240,000 steps using a Decoupled 759 AdamW optimizer 92 with the global batch size of 2,048. The learning rate is 2E-4 with a cosine 760 decay scheduler, and 6% of the training duration was dedicated to warm up. The learning rate 761 decayed to 4E-6 by the end of training. The default BERT 34 masking recipe was used with a 762 masking probability of 15%. For each masked token: i) there is an 80% probability it will be 763 replaced by a special token ([MASK]), ii) a 10% probability it will be replaced by a random token, 764 and iii) a 10% probability it will remain unchanged. Unless otherwise specified, all models were 765 trained using a sequence length of 8192 base pairs. A weight decay of 1E-5 was applied 766 throughout the training process. Pre-training of the 676M-parameter PlantCAD2-L model required 767 approximately 28 days on 64 NVIDIA H100 GPUs, while the 88M-parameter model completed 768 training in approximately 3 days under the same hardware configuration. 769 770

## Accessible chromatin region prediction 835 

To evaluate the capability of PlantCAD2 to capture regulatory sequence features, fine-tuning 836 experiments were performed using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) 68 on two accessible chromatin 837 prediction tasks: (1) cross-species accessible regions (ACRs) prediction, and (2) cell-type-specific 838 ACR prediction. 839 840 For the cross-species task, ATAC-seq peak regions from 12 plant species 67 were downloaded 841 from NCBI (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE128434). We followed the 842 data processing pipeline as described by Wrightsman et al 100 . For each species, peak regions 843 were processed by extracting the midpoint of each peak and symmetrically extending it by half 844 the target input window size (300bp, 600bp and 1,000bp) in both directions to generate positive 845 observations. To reflect the real-world scenario in which most of the genome is inaccessible, the 846 rest of the genome was used as negative examples, ensuring no overlap with known peaks. 847 848 For the cell-type-specific task, we used the single-cell ATAC-Seq 70 and used a similar 849 preprocessing pipeline, but each genomic region could be associated with accessibility across 92 850 cell types. ## Gene expression prediction in leaf 854 

To evaluate the models’ ability to predict gene expression, we designed two tasks: (1) leaf 855 absolute expression and (2) leaf on/off expression classification. The training dataset was derived 856 from 15 Andropogoneae species 71 . For validation, we held out two species closest to Zea mays —857 

Tripsacum zopilotense and Zea diploperennis —both members of the Tripsacinae subtribe, which 858 diverged from maize approximately 0.6 to 4 million years ago. This setup enabled evaluation of 859 the models’ cross-species generalization to closely related taxa. For the leaf absolute expression 860 task, the log 10 TPM values were used as regression targets during fine-tuning. For the on/off 861 expression task, genes with TPM > 1 were labeled as expressed (positive), and those with TPM 862

## Leaf protein abundance prediction task 865 

To evaluate the models’ ability to predict protein abundance, we designed two tasks analogous 866 to the gene expression analysis: absolute abundance and on/off classification. Ribo-Seq data 867 were obtained from Arabidopsis 101 and Z. mays 102 . Raw reads were downloaded from NCBI, and 868 Trimmomatic 103 was used to trim adapters and filter low-quality reads. Cleaned reads were first 869 aligned to rRNA reference sequences using Bowtie 104 to remove contaminating rRNA. The 870 remaining reads were then mapped to the reference genomes of Arabidopsis and maize using 871 STAR 105 . Gene-level translation abundance was quantified using StringTie 106 based on uniquely 872 mapped reads. We then designed two tasks analogous to the expression prediction setup: (1) 873 absolute protein abundance, where the log10-transformed Ribo-Seq expression values were 874 used as regression targets, and (2) on/off classification, where genes with TPM > 1 were 875 considered expressed (positive) and those with TPM ≤ 1 were labeled as non-expressed 876 (negative). 877

## Fine-tuning PlantCAD2 878 

To adapt the pre-trained PlantCAD2 model to downstream tasks, we employed Low-Rank 879 Adaptation (LoRA) 68 , a parameter-efficient fine-tuning strategy that inserts trainable low-rank 880 matrices into the attention layers of the transformer. This approach enables effective adaptation 881 while keeping the vast majority of model parameters frozen. Fine-tuning was performed using the 882 PEFT library 107 with LoRA rank = 8, α = 32, and dropout = 0.1, targeting the "x_proj", "in_proj", and 883 

"out_proj" modules. Models were trained using the Hugging Face Trainer with a learning rate of 884 

1e−4, a global batch size of 128, and one training epoch. BF16 precision and linear learning rate 885 

scheduling with 50 warm-up steps were used. Over 98% of the model parameters remained 886 

frozen, enabling efficient and scalable fine-tuning across tasks. All tasks were fine-tuned for a 887 single epoch without hyperparameter tuning to ensure stability and consistency across 888 experiments. Fine-tuning objectives for all models were a binary cross entropy loss for 889 classification tasks and a mean squared error loss for regression tasks. 890
