Abstract 13 

Understanding how mutations evolve on Y chromosomes is central to ex-14 

plaining the origin, diversity and persistence of sex chromosomes. Mutations 15 

occurring on the Y chromosome in sexual populations experience selective dy-16 

namics that differ markedly from those on autosomes, due to a reduced effective 17 

population size and the presence of large non-recombining regions containing 18 

alleles maintained in a permanently heterozygous state. These specific features 19 

alter gene transmission in the Y chromosome population compared to autosomes, 20 

even within the same pedigree. Here, we provide a two-sex diploid Wright-Fisher 21 

model that explicitly incorporates both sex chromosomes and autosomes within 22 

a unified population framework, in order to capture the influence of these spe-23 

cificities on the fate of mutations, not only considering fixation probabilities but 24 

also segregation times. We use diffusion approximations and provide analytical 25 

and numerical tools to compute these quantities across a wide range of paramet-26 

ers and selection regimes. We recover classical results on fixation probabilities 27 

in various scenarios, including purely beneficial, deleterious or overdominant 28 

mutations, and extend them in the light of mean segregation time, a key but 29 

often overlooked determinant of evolutionary outcomes over finite timescales. 30 

In particular, our analyses show that overdominant mutations are overall more 31 

likely to fix in observable time windows on the Y chromosome than on auto-32 

somes. Individual-based simulations corroborate our approximations and high-33 

light parameter regimes where the theoretical approach is particularly useful, 34 

especially for parameter values inducing long segregation times or small fixa-35 

tion probabilities, for which simulations are impractical. Our results provide a 36 

comprehensive and tractable framework for clarifying how chromosome-specific 37

# 1 Introduction 41 

Sex chromosomes play a central role in the evolution of many species. They are 42 

frequently involved in speciation processes, contribute to the development and main-43 

tenance of sexual dimorphism, and harbor numerous mutations causing sex-linked 44 

disorders, such as red–green color blindness in humans. A major explanation for 45 

their particular evolutionary impact is that Y (or W) chromosomes are often non-46 

recombining over most of their length and permanently heterozygous. This has im-47 

portant consequences, including the progressive degeneration of the Y (or W) chro-48 

mosome due to the reduced efficacy of selection in non-recombining regions. Below, 49 

we focus on Y chromosomes in XY systems for simplicity, but a similar reasoning 50 

holds for W chromosomes in ZW systems ( e.g. , in birds). Despite their importance, 51 

the consequences of recombination arrest and permanent heterozygosity for the evol-52 

utionary trajectories of these regions, as well as for processes such as adaptation, 53 

speciation, and genomic conflicts are still poorly known. Similarly, the evolutionary 54 

mechanisms driving recombination suppression on Y chromosomes are still debated 55 

(Charlesworth, 1978; Saunders and Muyle, 2024; Charlesworth and Olito, 2024; Jay 56 

et al., 2025). Moreover, although the rise in genome sequence availability has revealed 57 

a striking variation in the genomic architecture, levels of degeneration, patterns of di-58 

versity, and evolutionary histories of Y chromosomes across the tree of life, we still 59 

lack a clear understanding of the evolutionary forces that have generated and shaped 60 

this diversity. For instance, it remains unclear what fraction of the differences in 61 

evolutionary trajectories observed between autosomes and sex chromosomes can be 62 

attributed to distinct selective regimes affecting these genomic compartments (for ex-63 

ample, the occurrence of sex-specific selection), to differences in their recombination 64 

landscapes that modulate the strength of selective interference, or to the permanent 65 

heterozygosity of the Y chromosome, which influences both the effective population 66 

size and the dominance effects of mutations. 67 

These challenges arise in part because we lack a unified framework for rigorously in-68 

ferring the evolutionary trajectories of mutations on Y chromosomes and for directly 69 

comparing these dynamics to those on autosomes, which currently limits our ability 70 

to meaningfully interpret the patterns of genetic diversity observed in natural pop-71 

ulations. Two key quantities allow a precise description of the fate of a mutation: 72 

its fixation or extinction probability and its segregation time, i.e. , the time during 73 

which the mutation is neither extinct nor fixed. The latter is often neglected, while 74 

it plays a central role in the understanding of the mutation dynamics, most of muta-75 

tions observed in genomes being still segregating rather than fixed. Moreover, some 76 

mutations may have segregation times so long that, even when their fixation prob-77 

ability is substantial, fixation may effectively never occur on observable timescales. 78 

For example, strong balancing selection can maintain allele frequencies far from 0 or 79 

1 for extremely long periods, until the stochasticity in reproduction eventually gen-80 

erates a sufficiently large fluctuation to drive the mutation frequency towards one of 81 

these absorbing states. This observation raises questions about our ability to directly 82 

observe the fixation of new alleles, even in cases where models would predict their 83 

fixation with significant probability. Although this issue is well known, it has not 84

The fixation probability and segregation time of mutations depend on multiple evolu-86 

tionary forces, including natural selection and genetic drift. The foundations for un-87 

derstanding fixation and extinction probabilities were established in textbook works 88 

by Fisher (1923) and Wright (1931). The Wright-Fisher model assumes that the 89 

population size is finite and constant through time and the stochastic nature of the 90 

reproduction dynamics is explicitly encoded. This leads to a binary eventual outcome, 91 

in which the mutation is either fixed or lost with probability 1. Using a branching 92 

process approximation, Haldane proved that the fixation probability of a beneficial 93 

autosomal mutation in a Wright-Fisher model could be approximated by a simple 94 

quantity equal to twice the selection coefficient (Haldane, 1927). Kimura later exten-95 

ded these results to varying population sizes and strengths of selection, studying the 96 

fixation probability as a function of the initial frequency of the mutation (Kimura, 97 

1955, 1962). Although most mutations are known to be deleterious, beneficial muta-98 

tions have long been considered as key drivers of adaptation to changing environments, 99 

and they have been the focus of many models (see Ewens 1967; Patwa and Wahl 2008 100 

and references therein). Deleterious mutations have also been extensively investig-101 

ated, and shown to accumulate in asexual or selfing populations, for example through 102 

Muller’s ratchet (Muller, 1964; Waxman, 2011). They can accumulate in small pop-103 

ulations too, even with sexual reproduction, possibly leading to a species extinction 104 

(Lynch et al., 1995). In this work, we aim to cover a wide range of selection regimes, 105 

including most of the above. 106 

A key element distinguishing the fates of a mutation carried by an autosome and by a 107 

non-recombining Y chromosome in a diploid population is the asymmetry between the 108 

X and Y chromosomes, present in different proportions in the population. Indeed, 109 

in a population with 1:1 sex ratio, only one fourth of the sex chromosomes are Y 110 

chromosomes. Although sex chromosomes and autosomes both follow the rules of 111 

Mendelian inheritance, a mutation carried by the X or the Y chromosome in the 112 

non-recombining region ( i.e. , in the sex-linked region) will not be transmitted to 113 

males and females in the same way. Consequently, the variations in allele frequencies 114 

within these two subpopulations will be different, and will also be different from the 115 

variations in allele frequencies experienced by a mutation carried by an autosome. 116 

These differences are expected to affect the fixation probability and the segregation 117 

time of Y-linked mutations compared to autosomal ones. 118 

By using Haldane’s branching approximations for beneficial mutations and Kimura’s 119 

diffusion for deleterious mutations, Charlesworth et al. (1987) compared the substitu-120 

tion rate of these mutations on sex chromosomes and autosomes. They also compared 121 

the substitution rate of underdominant mutations on X chromosomes and autosomes. 122 

Considering that the substitution rate is equal to the mutation rate times the fixation 123 

probability, they found that loci with partially or fully recessive beneficial muta-124 

tions should show higher substitution rates on Y chromosomes than on autosomes. 125 

They also showed that deleterious mutations should fix more frequently on Y chro-126 

mosomes than on autosomes. However, there has been no such analysis considering 127 

overdominant or underdominant mutations on Y chromosomes, i.e. , mutations with 128 

an heterozygote advantage or disadvantage compared to both homozygotes. Yet, re-129 

cent empirical and theoretical works have shown that chromosomal rearrangements, 130 

which are abundant on sex chromosomes, may often be overdominant (Berdan et al., 131 

2023). Furthermore, the substitution rate does not account for the differences in se-132 

gregation times between different mutation types, or between different chromosome 133 

types (autosomes versus Y-like sex chromosomes), despite the fact that they condition 134

Here, we aim to extend the work of Charlesworth et al. (1987) by deriving expressions 136

the evolution of sex chromosomes ( e.g. , sexually antagonistic selection, Flintham and 139

properties, i.e. , its lower effective population size and its forced heterozygosity, on 141 

the fixation probability and segregation time of a mutation that it carries. We do 142 

not consider mutations appearing on the X chromosome, as the mutation frequency 143 

then differs in the male and female subpopulations, and its dynamics in the whole 144 

population cannot be satisfactorily described by the same type of one-dimensional 145 

Markov processes as those we study here. More precisely, in this case the frequency 146 

in the male and female subpopulations must be tracked separately in order to express 147 

the law of future changes in mutation frequency, and our methodology based on 148 

one-dimensional diffusions does not apply (similar formulae are not available for two-149 

dimensional diffusions). 150 

In order to compare the invasion, fixation and extinction of mutations on autosomes 151 

vs. on Y chromosomes, we must consider a diploid, biparental two-sex model. Möhle 152 

(1994) introduced a Wright-Fisher biparental two-sex model and studied the probab-153 

ilities of extinction of the family descending from a given individual at some time in 154 

the future. Although one could consider using this result to track the lineage of the 155 

original carrier of a particular mutation (and deduce for instance the extinction prob-156 

ability of this mutation), two features make it unsuitable for addressing the question 157 

we are interested in with regard to mutation transmission. First, males and females 158 

play symmetric roles in this model, preventing the incorporation of key specific fea-159 

tures of sex chromosomes. Second, because the genetic transmission tree differs from 160 

the biparental pedigree (or family tree, which is the object of interest in Möhle 1994), 161 

a family lineage may persist even after a given mutation has been lost. The former 162 

limitation also arises in the model proposed in (Möhle and Sagitov, 2003). 163 

To be able to incorporate these specificities in our analysis, we introduce a diploid, 164 

biparental, two-sex Wright-Fisher model, in which the two haploid genomes of each 165 

individual are picked in exactly one male and one female, accounting for selection and 166 

the particular features of heterogametic sex chromosomes. This allows us to finely 167 

incorporate the key characteristics distinguishing the allelic transmission for a gene 168 

located on the Y chromosome versus an autosome along a biparental pedigree. We de-169 

rive Wright-Fisher diffusion approximations for the allele frequencies of autosomal and 170 

Y-linked genes using the same scaling of time. Indeed, the allele frequencies emerge 171 

from this model as discrete-time Markov chains, with complex transition probabilit-172 

ies that render a direct analysis practically impossible, hence the need for diffusion 173 

approximations. Using precisely the same timescales in the autosomal and Y-linked 174 

contexts allows a direct comparison of the mean segregation times between the two 175 

cases. The approach consisting of taking diffusion approximations is the same as 176 

Kimura (1962)’s approach and the resulting objects are standard (Durrett, 2008), 177 

but we detail how they can be jointly obtained from the same population dynamics. 178 

Indeed, the transmission dynamics of both sex chromosomes and autosomes are em-179 

bedded in the model, building the population pedigree in the same way, and keeping 180 

the same selection coefficients, thereby ensuring a legitimate comparison between the 181 

fixation probabilities and mean segregating times on the two types of chromosomes. 182 

This unified framework also provides numerical and analytical tools to quickly com-183 

pute these quantities given the selection parameters and the initial frequencies. 184

contrast, the diffusion drift of (pt)t≥0 is more complex and can change sign depending 333 

on the mutation frequency. 334 

One-dimensional diffusions are particularly suitable for our study, as many theoretical 335 

results have been developped to analyse their stochastic behaviour (see, e.g. , Chapter 7 336 

in Durrett 2008 or Chapter 3 in Etheridge 2011). In the cases we consider, it is possible 337 

to find explicit expressions for the probability of absorption in 0 or 1, corresponding 338 

to the purging and the fixation of the mutation, respectively. We also know that one 339 

of these two events will happen in finite time with probability 1 (Ikeda and Watanabe, 340 

1989), and we can compute the average time for the mutation to be fixed or removed 341 

from the population, i.e. , the mutation mean segregation time . It is important to note 342 

that we obtain our limiting diffusions on precisely the same timescale (N t, t ≥ 0) 343 

in both chromosomal contexts (autosomes and Y chromosomes), which will allow us 344 

to compare the mean segregation times on autosomes and on Y chromosomes in a 345 

straightforward way. 346 

Diffusions of the form taken by (pt)t≥0 and (qt)t≥0 have been known and studied for a 347 

long time. Diffusion approximations for the trajectories of allele frequencies trace back 348 

to Kimura (1962), who first computed the fixation probability of a favourable allele in 349 

a haploid Wright-Fisher model with selection using a diffusion similar to (qt)t≥0. The 350 

process (pt)t≥0 emerges from asexual diploid Wright-Fisher models (Durrett 2008, 351 

chap. 7; Etheridge 2011, chap. 5), and was also used in Takahata (1990) in the 352 

special case of balancing selection ( h < 0, s0 < 0). 353 

In our framework, both (pt)t≥0 and (qt)t≥0 are derived from the same population 354 

dynamics, the same selective coefficients and on the same time scale. The differences 355 

between them come from the structural effects of the heterogametic Y chromosome 356 

as opposed to the autosomes, namely the set of feasible genotypes and the smaller 357 

population size inducing stronger genetic drift. We will therefore be able to study 358 

how these differences affect the mutation fixation probability and mean segregation 359 

time by directly comparing (pt)t≥0 and (qt)t≥0.360

## 3.3 Comparing the mutation dynamics on autosomes and on 548 

## Y chromosomes 549 

We now turn to a direct comparison of the mutation fixation probabilities and mean 550 

segregation times on the Y chromosome and on autosomes. We perform these com-551 

parisons using identical sets of parameters, covering all the cases exposed in Table 1. 552 

This allows us to isolate the effects of the specific transmission of alleles through the Y 553 

chromosome, comparatively to autosomes. In particular, since both processes (pt)t≥0554 

and (qt)t≥0 evolve on the same time scale, we can directly contrast the mean times 555 

of interest. 556 

To enable meaningful comparisons between the autosome and Y chromosome con-557 

text, it is essential to account for the fact that autosomes experience an effective 558 

population size four times larger than Y chromosomes. Since our focus is on the 559 

fate of a mutation after it has arisen (rather than on its appearance rate), we use 560 

initial allele frequencies that scale accordingly: p0 = 1 /(2 N ) for the autosome and 561 

q0 = 2 /N for the Y chromosome. These differences in initial frequencies influence 562 

both the fixation probabilities and the mean segregation times in ways that depend 563 

on the values of s0 and h. Importantly, this framework does not capture the fact that, 564 

in a diploid population with balanced sex ratio, mutations arise more frequently on 565 

autosomes than on the Y chromosome; our results therefore reflect fixation dynamics 566 

rather than substitution rates. Nevertheless, the approach is flexible and could read-567 

ily accommodate alternative assumptions about the initial allele frequency, including 568 

equal starting frequencies across genomic locations or scenarios where the mutation 569 

has already reached a substantial frequency. 570

analysis across the entire parameter space. 600 

First, advantageous mutations ( h > 0, s0 > 0) can have a higher probability of 601 

fixation on autosomes than on Y chromosomes but only when they are beneficial 602 

and not overdominant, according to the classification in Table 1 (see, e.g. , the case 603 

s0 = 10 , h = 0 .1 in Figure 4 b. This is expected, as beneficial mutations with a 604 

moderate selective advantage are more likely to fix when genetic drift is weaker, as 605 

the latter counteracts selection. This effect is particularly pronounced when h is close 606 

to 0, as recessive mutations have a reduced selective advantage (and therefore a lower 607 

fixation probability) on the permanently heterozygous Y chromosome. Consistent 608 

with the nearly neutral theory (Ohta, 1973), slightly beneficial mutations ( 0 < s 0 ≤ 1,609 

0 < h < 1) exhibit a higher fixation probability on the Y chromosome, as their nearly 610 

neutral behavior makes them more likely to fix in smaller populations (see Figure 4). 611 

Fixation is also more likely on Y chomosomes when the strength of selection is strong 612 

(s0 ≥ 100 ), as genetic drift becomes negligible and the mutation is again more likely 613 

to fix in the smaller Y chromosome population. 614 

When the mutation is underdominant and advantageous at homozygote state ( h < 0615 

and s0 > 0, see Table 1), fixation probabilities on autosomes can also exceed those 616 

on the Y chromosome ( e.g. when h = −1 and s0 = 100 ). This occurs because these 617 

mutations are always deleterious on the Y chromosome, unlike on autosomes where 618 

homozygous individuals aa benefit from a selective advantage. The smaller effect-619 

ive population size of the Y chromosome may also compensate for this difference at 620 

smaller parameter values ( e.g. , in the case h = −0.1 and s0 = 10 ), leading to roughly 621 

similar fixation probabilities on autosomes and on Y chromosomes. Underdominant 622 

mutations tend to segregate slightly longer on autosomes on average, especially as 623 

more mutations manage to avoid an early purge. However, the mean segregation 624 

time remains orders of magnitude smaller than in the overdominant scenarios. In-625 

deed, underdominant mutations are either rapidly purged from the population or, 626 

through genetic drift, reach sufficiently high frequencies for homozygotes aa to be-627 

come common, which subsequently facilitates their relatively rapid fixation. 628 

A somewhat opposite pattern emerges in the overdominant scenario ( h < 0, s0 < 0629 

or h > 1, s0 > 0). On the Y chromosome, the mutation maintains a constant se-630 

lective advantage, whereas on the autosomes its advantage decreases as its frequency 631 

increases due to the rising proportion of homozygotes. As discussed above, such con-632

# 4 Discussion 649 

The importance of a unified framework for studying the evolutionary tra-650 

jectories of autosomal and Y-linked mutations 651 

Understanding how mutation fixation probabilities differ between Y-like chromosomes 652 

and autosomes is essential for explaining sex chromosome differentiation and its evolu-653 

tionary consequences, including the build-up of genetic incompatibilities contributing 654 

to speciation and the accumulation of sex-linked deleterious mutations underlying 655 

many heritable diseases. Y-chromosome specificities in gene transmission, due to 656 

their lower effective population size as well as their forced heterozygosity, among 657 

other mechanisms, greatly impact the fixation probabilities and mean segregation 658 

times of mutations. To compute these key quantities, we used diffusion approxim-659 

ations derived from a biparental two-sex diploid Wright-Fisher model encompassing 660 

X and Y sex chromosomes and autosomes. We provided analytical and numerical 661 

tools to derive the mutation fixation probability and mean segregation time from this 662 

unified framework, for mutations evolving under different kinds of selective regimes. 663 

The fixation probabilities and mean segregation times of mutations depend on the 664 

interplay between selection and genetic drift, and their role can be directly estab-665 

lished through the coefficients of our diffusion approximations. In the Y chromosome 666 

context, no individuals with genotype aa are formed and the fate of the mutation 667 

only depends on the product hs 0 and on its initial frequency. For example, if hs 0 > 0,668 

selection favours the mutation regardless of its frequency, as the diffusion drift term 669 

remains positive for any value of qt ∈ [0 , 1] . Genetic drift counteracts the increase 670 

in mutation frequency driven by selection, especially when the mutation is still rare 671 

in the population. By contrast, the role of genetic drift in the autosomal context 672 

is more complex. While it can also lead to the early loss of the mutation when it 673 

fails to reach relatively high frequencies in the first generations, genetic drift can also 674 

override selection by driving one allele to fixation even in regimes where selection 675 

tends to maintain polymorphism, particularly under strong balancing selection. The 676 

framework we provided allows for direct comparisons of fixation probabilities and of 677 

mean segregation times between autosomal and Y-linked mutations, as time is scaled 678

Recovering classical results in population genetics 683 

Our approach retrieves classical results in population genetics. For instance, deleter-684

to autosomes, solely due to the increased genetic drift experienced by the Y chromo-686

s0 ≥ 10 ). We also show that purely beneficial mutations ( e.g. h = 0 .1, s0 = 100 )689 

can reach similar fixation probabilities in both the autosomal and Y-linked contexts 690 

(Patwa and Wahl, 2008). Given that mutations arise more frequently on autosomes 691 

due to their larger population size, one might expect a higher overall number of fix-692

light of segregation times. Indeed, segregation times are often overlooked, despite 694 

their strong influence on the number of fixations actually occurring in a finite time 695 

window. In particular, they are not taken into account by the classical substitution 696 

rate - yet it is essential for properly interpreting the fixation probability results. 697 

The importance of the mean segregation time 698 

These time considerations play a particularly important role in the overdominant 699 

scenario. Indeed, when h > 1 and s0 > 0, the mutation is still beneficial in the homo-700 

zygous state but heterozygotes become advantaged comparatively to homozygotes. 701 

This has no impact in the Y chromosome context, but greatly modifies the behaviour 702 

of the mutation on autosomes, as it becomes disadvantaged once it reaches a high 703 

enough frequency to often appear in a homozygous state. This cutoff frequency is 704 

a metastable state towards which the process (pt)t≥0 is attracted, as the associated 705 

diffusion drift term is positive when the mutation is rare and negative when it is fre-706 

quent. Such a metastable state also arises in another overdominant scenario ( h < 0707 

s0 < 0), which explains why we jointly studied these two cases although they looked 708 

different at first sight (the mutant homozygotes being fitter than the wild-type homo-709 

zygotes in the former case and less fit in the latter case). We do observe substantial 710 

differences between fixation probabilities in the two cases. These arise from the nature 711 

of diffusion processes, which, through rare stochastic excursions, will eventually reach 712 

one of the two absorbing states 0 or 1. What actually distinguishes the two scenarios 713 

is the range within which the metastable state is found: between 1/2 and 1 in the 714 

first case ( h > 1, s0 > 0) and between 0 and 1/2 in the second ( h < 0, s0 < 0). 715 

Stochastic excursions away from the metastable state, driven by genetic drift, are 716 

therefore more likely to bring the mutation to fixation in the first case and to extinc-717 

tion in the second, explaining the discrepancy in fixation probabilities. However, such 718 

excursions are unlikely to be observed in a meaningful timescale. Indeed, we observe 719 

a very large mean segregation time in both cases ( e.g. an order of 10 69 generations for 720 

h = −2 and s0 = −100 ). This leads to a rather similar behaviour in the two cases : 721 

most mutations are quickly purged from the population, while a small fraction reaches 722 

the metastable frequency around which they can oscillate virtually indefinitely. Our 723 

framework for deriving the mean segregation time is therefore crucial in such cases for 724

Comparison of the Wright-Fisher framework to the individual-based sim-726

A consequence of long segregation times is that the theoretical fixation probabilit-728 

ies sometimes deviate from those obtained through individual based-simulations A.1. 729 

This is expected, as our simulations are capped at 100,000 generations. Therefore, 730 

in cases where the mutation is still segregating at that point, the observed number 731 

of actual fixations is 0. Consequently, our theoretical approach can be used in a first 732 

instance to determine whether performing time-consuming simulations is worthwhile 733 

for a given range of parameters. In addition to scenarios leading to long segregation 734 

times, the other situation where our theoretical results differ from simulations is when 735 

fixation probabilities are small but not negligible. For example, if the fixation probab-736 

ility is 10 −5 (which is the case, e.g. , for N = 100 , h = 0 .1, s0 = 100 ), the probability 737 

to actually observe a fixation in 10000 simulations is only 10%, and our simulations 738 

indeed ended up without any fixation in this case. This highlights another advant-739 

age of our theoretical approach: it enables the estimation of small but non-negligible 740 

fixation probabilities in cases where simulations would be prohibitively costly. 741 

Application to the sheltering effect on Y-like chromosomes 742 

Our analyses show that overdominant mutations are overall more likely to fix in ob-743 

servable time windows on the Y chromosome than on autosomes. This is particularly 744 

true in the case where h < 0 and s0 < 0, because the permanent heterozygosity of the 745 

Y chromosome prevents the appearance of the disadvantaged homozygotes aa . Such 746 

protection from homozygous disadvantage by being kept at the heterozygous state 747 

due to linkage to a permanently heterozygous locus (or a locus under strong balan-748 

cing selection) is often called a sheltering effect (Antonovics and Abrams, 2004; Jay 749 

et al., 2024). Although single point mutations are unlikely to exhibit true overdomin-750 

ant behavior, both theoretical and empirical studies indicate that large chromosomal 751 

rearrangements that suppress recombination are prone to behave as overdominant 752 

(Berdan et al., 2023). Indeed, large rearrangements such as inversions tend to cap-753 

ture multiple partially recessive deleterious mutations, which generate a fitness cost 754 

when they are homozygous. If these rearrangements confer a fitness advantage when 755 

heterozygous due to a lower number of captured recessive deleterious mutations, they 756 

can behave as overdominant with resulting negative h and s0. In agreement with pre-757 

vious studies, our results show that this type of chromosomal rearrangements follows 758 

distinct evolutionary dynamics on the Y chromosome and on autosomes, with notably 759 

higher fixation probabilities on the Y chromosome over observable timescales, which 760 

may contribute to the progressive cessation of recombination on the Y chromosome 761 

(Hartmann et al., 2021; Tezenas et al., 2023; Jay et al., 2024, 2025). 762

Our model focuses on the evolutionary dynamics of one particular mutation (or gen-764 

omic region) and does not account for the interactions it may have with other parts of 765 

the genome. For instance, Y-chromosome degeneration through the accumulation of 766 

deleterious mutations involves complex mutation interactions, such as Hill-Robertson 767

# 5 Conclusion 793 

Overall, our study provides a comprehensive and tractable framework for studying 794 

the evolutionary fate of mutations on Y chromosomes and for establishing relevant 795 

comparisons with the trajectories of autosomal mutations. Beyond simply estimating 796 

fixation probabilities, our results illustrate how chromosome-specific features, such as 797 

permanent heterozygosity and population size effects, interact to shape the dynamics 798 

of mutation segregation and accumulation. Our study underlines the pivotal role of 799 

mean segregation time, which can exceed evolutionary timescales in the overdominant 800 

scenario, causing mutations to become trapped at intermediate frequencies. This 801 

approach offers a versatile tool for improving our understanding of Y chromosome 802 

evolution and lays the groundwork for future studies aiming to dissect the complex 803 

forces governing genome evolution. 804

## A.1 Comparison with the results of Charlesworth et al. (1987) 962 

Charlesworth et al. (1987) provided tools to compare the fate of a mutation appearing 963 

on an autosome or a sex chromosome. In particular, they compared the autosomal and 964 

Y chromosome contexts in the beneficial and deleterious scenarios using two different 965 

approximations. In this section, we contrast our results with those obtained in Char-966 

lesworth et al. (1987) and discuss the parameter space in which their approximations 967 

provide results comparable to ours. 968 

Actually, Charlesworth et al. (1987) studied substitution rates , computed as the rate of 969 

apparition of a new mutation times the probability that it eventually reaches fixation. 970 

These rates are written KA = 2 N νp Ah,s 0 and KY = N νp Yh,s 0 /2 for autosomes and Y 971 

chromosomes respectively, with pAh,s 0 and pYh,s 0 representing the fixation probabilities 972 

in the two cases for parameters h and s0. As the rate of apparition of a mutation ν is 973 

assumed to be the same in all chromosomes, the total rate at which a mutation appears 974 

on an autosome ( 2N ν ) is thus four times higher than the rate on Y chromosomes 975 

(N ν/ 2). The main quantity studied in Charlesworth et al. (1987) is the ratio R =976 

KA/K Y = 4 pAh,s 0 /p Yh,s 0 . Note also that the selection coefficients they consider are 977 

slightly different for Y chromosomes as the heterozygotes have a fitness given by 978 

1 + s, instead of 1 + hs as in our model. To account for this difference, we will 979 

compare the fixation probabilities for given sets of parameters (h, s 0) by fixing h = 1 980 

in the Y chromosome context, that is studying the ratio given by pAh,s 0 /p Y
