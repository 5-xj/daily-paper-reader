Abstract 35 

36 

New mutations arise in germ cells and must preserve fertility while altering somatic phenotype to 37 

confer a fitness advantage. To understand how whole organisms balance these constraints, we 38 

generated mice with humanized noncoding promoter sequence at Traf6 , an NF-kB pathway 39 

component with recently diverged chromatin state in mouse germ cells. The humanized allele 40 

(Traf6 h) reinstated human-like expression in germ and immune cells, which we traced to a 13 41 

base pair deletion that impairs CTCF binding. Traf6 h had no effect on fertility but induced a 42 

sensitized innate immune response to endotoxin. Other NF-kB pathway genes followed a similar 43 

pattern, suggesting that their expression has evolved in tandem and consistent with known 44 

mouse-human differences in inflammatory response. Our results reveal distinct roles for germ 45 

and immune cells in shaping gene regulatory evolution, where immune cells quickly adjust 46 

phenotype, enabling selection, while germ cells robustly maintain fertility despite changing 47 

expression levels, enabling inheritance. 48

Introduction 50 

51 

Divergent gene regulation is a major source of evolutionary change 1,2 . Regulatory divergence is 52 

caused by changes in noncoding sequence and enacted through assembly of alternative 53 

chromatin domains at regulatory elements, including enhancers and promoters 3–10 . Importantly, 54 

evolutionary divergence in regulatory sequence impacts chromatin state and gene regulation 55 

differently in different cell types. While genomic sequence is the same in each cell of an 56 

organism, chromatin state induced at a given sequence differs from cell to cell, enabling diversity 57 

of cellular form and function 11 . Therefore, selection for function in one tissue can unpredictably 58 

alter expression in other tissues, resulting in pleiotropic effects. This pleiotropy requires that 59 

multiple tissues coordinate phenotypic responses to the same change in regulatory sequence and 60 

complicates the relationship between evolution of regulatory sequence, chromatin state, and 61 

phenotype, making the outcomes of regulatory divergence difficult to predict 12–15 .62 

63 

The pleiotropy problem is especially acute for broadly expressed genes, which usually have 64 

important roles in multiple cell types. When expression of these genes is altered by an acquired 65 

regulatory mutation, the impact could be positive, negative, or neutral in different tissues, and the 66 

interplay of these effects ultimately determines the fitness of the organism and the likelihood that 67 

the mutation will persist. The necessity of balancing competing effects across tissues likely 68 

contributes to the longstanding observation that genes with broad expression patterns evolve 69 

more slowly than tissue-specific genes 16–18 .70 

71 

Among the many cell types in multicellular organisms, gametes (sperm and eggs) have a unique 72 

and central function in propagating the species and therefore represent a critical barrier in the 73 

evolution of broadly expressed genes. New mutations absolutely must be compatible with 74 

gamete development and fertility in order to persist. At the same time, male germ cells are 75 

transcriptionally promiscuous: spermatogenic cells express most of the genome at low levels, so 76 

mutations that impact expression of almost any gene have the potential to impair gamete function 77 

and fertility 19–23 . How evolutionary change at broadly expressed genes occurs despite this 78 

germline barrier remains a fundamental question. 79 

80 

In mammalian gametes and gamete precursors, genes important for somatic development are 81 

marked by a specialized chromatin state called bivalency 24–27 . Bivalency is defined by 82 

simultaneous enrichment of activation- and repression-associated histone modifications at the 83 

same site: H3 lysine 4 trimethylation (H3K4me3) and H3K27 trimethylation (H3K27me3), 84 

respectively 24–26,28–30 . Bivalency in germ cells evolves in parallel with expression of the 85 

associated gene in somatic tissues, linking gamete chromatin state to regulatory divergence 86 

across different somatic tissues 31,32 .87 

88 

Here, we used the bivalent state in gametogenic cells as an entry point to ask how divergence in 89 

regulatory state occurs at broadly expressed genes in mammalian evolution. Genes that recently 90 

gained bivalency in gametes during evolution are commonly broadly expressed, while genes 91 

with deeply conserved bivalency are tissue-specific, suggesting that recent gain of bivalency 92 

marks broadly-expressed genes with the potential to evolve tissue-restricted expression. We 93 

selected Traf6 (TNF receptor associated factor 6), a representative broadly-expressed gene that 94 

recently gained bivalency in murine germ cells, to interrogate the cross-tissue effects of 95

Results 111

The murine Traf6 promoter acquired H3K27me3 to gain bivalency 113 

We first sought to identify broadly-expressed genes that have undergone recent regulatory 114 

change during evolution. We mined our multi-species ChIP-seq dataset from male germline cells 115 31 for loci marked by bivalency in germ cells, and assessed tissue specificity using a previously 116 

reported metric (tau 33,34 ) and multi-tissue expression data from ENCODE 35,36 . We found that in 117 

general, genes marked by bivalency are more tissue-specific than the genome average ( Figure 118 

1A, Table S1 ). Interestingly, this association with expression breadth was dependent on the 119 

evolutionary age of the bivalent state. Genes with deeply conserved bivalency were strongly 120 

tissue-specific (median tau 0.8607 and 0.8043 in mouse and human, respectively), while genes 121 

bivalent only in a single species were even more broadly expressed than the genome average 122 

(median tau 0.6352 and 0.6006 in mouse and human) ( Figure 1B ). Thus, acquisition of 123 

bivalency in germ cells marks evolutionarily recent regulatory change at genes with broad 124 

expression patterns in somatic tissue. 125 

126 

We next selected a representative locus to experimentally interrogate the factors that guide this 127 

regulatory evolution. We identified the Traf6 promoter as broadly expressed (tau = 0.4768-128 

0.6895) and enriched only for H3K4me3 in most mammalian species from primates to 129 

marsupials, but enriched for both H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 in mouse germ cells, indicating a 130 

switch from an active to a bivalent state in the murine lineage ( Figure 1C, Table S2 ). The 131 

species difference in chromatin marks was constant across meiotic and post-meiotic development 132 

in male germ cells and persisted into epididymal sperm ( Figure 1D, S1A ). As expected for 133 

conversion from an active, H3K4me3-only to a bivalent state, mouse germ cells had low levels 134 

of Traf6 transcript relative to other species ( Figure 1E ). We confirmed that the overlapping 135 

H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 ChIP-seq signals reflect true bivalency using sequential ChIP in 136 

mouse testis tissue ( Figure 1F ). 137

Humanized Traf6 regulation supports normal spermatogenic development and fertility 167 

Having validated that the humanized Traf6 h promoter allele recapitulates a human-like chromatin 168 

and expression state in germ cells, we next asked how the humanized Traf6 promoter impacts 169 

phenotype. Since our GFP insertion interferes with regulation of the Traf6 gene ( Figure 2E ), we 170 

generated a Traf6 h allele with a humanized promoter sequence but no Egfp insertion, and 171 

homozygosed this allele (Traf6 h/h ) to better study phenotype ( Figure 3A ). Traf6 h/h mice were 172 

viable and healthy with no apparent morphological or physiologic abnormalities in either sex. 173 

174 

We first assessed the impact of Traf6 h on the primary function of the germline, fertility, under 175 

standard laboratory conditions. We confirmed that Traf6 transcript and protein expression are 176 

elevated in Traf6 h/h testis tissue, reflecting the GFP expression we observed with the reporter 177 

allele ( Figure 3B-C ). Histological examination of Traf6 h/h testis tissue revealed normal 178 

spermatogenesis, with all spermatogenic cell types present in the seminiferous epithelium and 179 

normal-appearing sperm in the tubule lumen ( Figure 3D ). Consistent with normal 180 

spermatogenesis, there was no detectable defect in fertility as Traf6 h/h male mice generated 181 

normal numbers of pups ( Figure 3E-F ), and we observed that fertility of female Traf6 h/h breeders 182 

was likewise unremarkable. We conclude that humanization of the Traf6 promoter robustly 183 

humanizes regulatory state and activates expression in testis, but has no biologically significant 184 

effect on testicular phenotype or fertility. 185

Humanized Traf6 regulation alters immune expression and inflammatory phenotypes 188 

Since there was no strong effect on germ cell phenotype or fertility, we predicted that any 189 

functional effects of Traf6 regulatory divergence would be found in somatic tissue. TRAF6 is an 190 

E3 ubiquitin ligase that is broadly expressed across tissues in mice and humans and has a range 191 

of functions, most prominently as a positive mediator of canonical NF-kB signaling 37–40 . It is 192 

important for development and function of both adaptive and innate immune cells as well as 193 

organogenesis of the thymus and lymph node 37,39–45 . To determine if humanized Traf6 regulation 194 

alters immune function, we focused on the well-characterized role of TRAF6 as a regulator of 195 

Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) signaling in myeloid cells to enact a systemic inflammatory response 196 

(Figure 4A ). Upon activation of TLR4, TRAF6 serves as an essential adaptor protein, 197 

coordinating the nuclear translocation of NF-kB and leading to the production of pro-198 

inflammatory cytokines including TNF a and IL-6 46–50 . To examine how humanized regulation 199 

of Traf6 impacts this process, we utilized lipopolysaccharide (LPS, endotoxin). LPS is a well-200 

established ligand of TLR4, and LPS challenge is a commonly used mouse model of sepsis, 201 

wherein mortality results entirely from the ensuing systemic inflammatory response. Upon 202 

intraperitoneal challenge with LPS, Traf6 h/h mice were found to exhibit a dramatic increase in 203 

endotoxin sensitivity, resulting in complete (100%) mortality in response to the same LD50 dose 204 

given to controls ( Figure 4B ). Furthermore, Traf6 h/h mice had significantly elevated levels of 205 

circulating pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-6 and TNFα throughout challenge ( Figure 4C-D ), 206 

consistent with the role of TRAF6 as a positive regulator in the production of these cytokines, as 207 

well as the role of systemic inflammation as the cause of death in this model. Thus, humanization 208 

of Traf6 regulation results in heightened responsiveness and mortality during systemic pro-209 

inflammatory challenge. 210 

211 

Sensitization to LPS challenge could be due to increased TRAF6 expression in effector cells, 212 

changes in relative proportions of different immune cell types, or both. To test for immune 213 

lineage bias, we first evaluated immune populations in several tissues and organs. We found no 214 

or minimal change in proportions of immune cell types in either myeloid or lymphoid lineages in 215 

spleen, bone marrow, lung, or liver tissue in Traf6 h/h mice compared to wild type controls 216 

(Figure S3, S4A-D ). 217 

218 

We next asked if levels of Traf6 expression were elevated within immune cells. We dissociated 219 

cells from liver, spleen, lung, intestine, and bone marrow isolated from mice carrying the Traf6 h-220 

GFP and Traf6 m-GFP reporter alleles and assessed GFP expression in populations of circulating 221 

and tissue-resident immune cells by flow cytometry. Expression of GFP under the humanized 222 

promoter allele was higher than under the endogenous mouse promoter in nearly all immune 223 

populations ( Figure 4E-H, S4E-F) . Reporter expression was increased in both myeloid and 224 

lymphoid lineages but the effect was consistently stronger in myeloid populations (fold change 225 

over Traf6 m: 1.39-2.45 lymphoid, 2.09-4.41 myeloid, Figure 4I ). We further confirmed elevated 226 

expression of Traf6 h-GFP compared to Traf6 m-GFP by immunofluorescence in small intestine, 227 

where GFP expression was significantly higher in immune as well as epithelial cells in Traf6 h-228 

GFP animals ( Figure 4J-K, S4G-H ). Together, these results indicate that the Traf6 h promoter 229 

regulates immune phenotypes by increasing expression of Traf6 within immune cells rather than 230 

altering composition of the immune compartment. 231

We next asked if the increased expression driven by the Traf6 h promoter was largely selective for 233 

immune cells or if it reflected a nonspecific increase in promoter activity driving global 234 

upregulation. We assessed Traf6 transcript levels in multiple somatic tissues and found that 235 

substantially increased levels of Traf6 transcript were detected only in testis (8-19x relative to 236 

control, Figure S4I ). Mild but statistically significant increases in transcript levels were also 237 

detected in lung, heart, and small intestine, consistent with modestly increased GFP expression 238 

seen in intestinal epithelial cells in addition to immune cells in our immunofluorescence data 239 

(Figure S4G-H ). In lung and heart, this result could also indicate selective upregulation of Traf6 240 

in immune populations that is partially masked by non-immune cells at the bulk tissue level. We 241 

conclude that the humanized Traf6 promoter drives elevated expression primarily in immune and 242 

germ cells. 243

Given the role of myeloid cells as first responders to immune stimulus and the finding that the 245 

Traf6 h promoter drives expression more strongly in myeloid populations, we then used a 246 

common in vitro model to functionally validate a stronger myeloid response to stimulus in 247 

Traf6 h/h immune cells. We generated bone marrow derived macrophages (BMDMs) from Traf6 h/h 

248 

animals and littermate controls and challenged these cells with LPS in vitro. In response to LPS 249 

stimulation, Traf6 h/h BMDMs produced higher levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-6 and 250 

TNF a relative to controls ( Figure 4L-M ), further confirming that the elevated response to LPS 251 

in Traf6 h/h mice is mediated by increased Traf6 expression in immune cells. 252

Loss of CTCF binding permits gain of H3K27me3 at the Traf6 promoter 254 

We next sought to determine how sequence divergence at the Traf6 promoter mediates altered 255 

expression and chromatin state. We interrogated the 1475 bp sequence constituting the 256 

humanized promoter transgene for features that differentiate it from the orthologous sequence in 257 

mouse. GC content is known to contribute to recruitment of Polycomb and resultant enrichment 258 

of H3K27me3 in mouse ESCs (mESCs) 51–53 , but the GC content of the two sequences is similar 259 

(human: 60.3%; mouse: 59.4%). Additionally, while transposable element insertions are a 260 

common source of regulatory change in evolution 54–58 , we did not detect transposable element 261 

sequences in either the human or mouse Traf6 promoter sequences. We noted that a predicted 262 

binding site for CTCF in primate Traf6 promoter sequences (human, chimpanzee, and rhesus 263 

macaque) overlapped a natural deletion in the mouse genome ( Figure 5A -C). Indeed, we found 264 

that the Traf6 h sequence recruits CTCF more strongly than the endogenous mouse sequence in 265 

testis at the predicted CTCF site using allele-specific ChIP-qPCR in our internally controlled 266 

heterozygous Traf6 h-GFP mouse line ( Figure 5D ). 267 

268 

CTCF plays many roles in genome regulation, including mediating three-dimensional contacts 269 

and promotion of transcriptional activity 59 . Inspection of micro-C data from mouse and human 270 

ESCs did not reveal meaningful species differences in local chromatin contacts near Traf6 ,271 

indicating that changes in 3D contacts likely do not explain the observed differences in gene 272 

regulation ( Figure S5 ). CTCF has also been found to directly antagonize H3K27me3 deposition, 273 

suggesting a mechanism where loss of CTCF binding permits gain of H3K27me3 deposition at 274 

the Traf6 promoter 60,61 . Consistent with an antagonistic relationship, genome-wide CTCF 275 

binding sites are enriched for H3K4me3 but depleted for H3K27me3 in both mouse and human 276 

testis ( Figure S6A-B ). 277

A preexisting H3K27me3 domain expanded into the Traf6 promoter following loss of CTCF 292 

binding in the rodent lineage 293 

Finally, we asked if the CTCF-positive, H3K27me3-negative configuration represents the 294 

ancestral state of the Traf6 promoter, suggested by the absence of H3K27me3 at Traf6 in other 295 

mammalian species ( Figure 1C-D ). To trace the evolutionary history at this locus, we collected 296 

H3K4me3, H3K27me3, and CTCF ChIP data from testis tissue of an additional rodent species, 297 

rat ( Rattus norvegicus , 11.6 million years diverged from mouse) as well as rabbit ( Oryctolagus 298 

cuniculus , 79 million years diverged from mouse and rat) ( Figure 6A, Table S4, Table S5 ) 62 .299 

The rat Traf6 promoter sequence resembles the mouse and carries the same CTCF binding site 300 

deletion. In the rabbit genome, the predicted CTCF site is intact but diverges from primate at 301 

several individual base pairs, making predictions of CTCF binding based on sequence in the 302 

rabbit ambiguous ( Figure 5B ). We interpret these sequence differences as resulting from a 303 

deletion that occurred in the rodent common ancestor after its split from the rabbit lineage, 304 

although we note that further analysis would be needed to conclusively determine the ancestral 305 

state. 306 

307 

By ChIP-qPCR, both rat and rabbit promoters were enriched for H3K4me3, while only rat was 308 

enriched for H3K27me3 ( Figure 6B-C ). At the same time, the rabbit but not the rat promoter 309 

was enriched for CTCF ( Figure 6D ), supporting a history where CTCF binding was lost and 310 

H3K27me3 enrichment gained after the split between rabbits and true rodents. Interestingly, 311 

examination of the surrounding region using ChIP-seq revealed an adjacent H3K27me3 domain 312 

in the rabbit genome that is absent in other species and ends at the TRAF6 promoter immediately 313 

adjacent to the site of CTCF enrichment ( Figure 6D ). These data imply that gain of H3K27me3 314 

in the region of the TRAF6 locus occurred in the common ancestor of rabbits and rodents, 315 

followed by loss of CTCF in the true rodent lineage that allowed extension of the H3K27me3 316 

domain into the TRAF6 promoter. 317

make them especially sensitive to Traf6 regulatory mutations, and predict that other genes 320 

marked by newly emerging bivalent chromatin in germ cells may also have evolving immune 321 

functions. Indeed, we found that genes that recently gained species-specific germline bivalency, 322 

but not those with deeply conserved bivalency, are enriched for immune-biased inflammatory 323 

pathways such as NF-kB and TRIF/TLR4 signaling ( Figure 6E-F ) 31 . Both newly-bivalent genes 324

Discussion 336 

337 

Here, we uncovered a functional species difference in inflammatory gene expression by 338 

examining germline bivalency across multiple mammalian species. Replacing a short interval of 339 

noncoding promoter sequence in mice with orthologous human sequence was sufficient to fully 340 

humanize Traf6 chromatin state in the testis without impacting spermatogenic development or 341 

fertility. Instead, the humanized promoter induced a strong immune phenotype, including 342 

humanization of the inflammatory response and increased cytokine production in macrophages. 343 

Together, our results point to immune and germ cell populations as sensitive detectors of 344 

regulatory sequence evolution at broadly-expressed genes with differing phenotypic 345 

requirements, such that tolerance of gene expression variation in germ cells balances selection 346 

for optimized immune function. 347 

348 

Our findings highlight an important fitness filter, where any regulatory change resulting in an 349 

advantageous somatic phenotype must also preserve gamete development. Thus, the pool of 350 

genes undergoing evolutionary shifts from broad to tissue-restricted expression is constrained by 351 

a requirement that their expression level in germ cells is not strongly linked to fertility. In this 352 

context, it is notable that spermatogenic cells are transcriptionally promiscuous, expressing most 353 

of the genome at some point in their development and therefore giving most new mutations an 354 

opportunity to affect germ cell function 19–21,65 . However, many of these testis-expressed genes 355 

do not have a detectable impact on fertility when knocked out 19–22,65 . Our results suggest that 356 

spermatogenic development may be optimized to tolerate variable levels of expression for most 357 

genes, allowing them to transmit mutations that confer an advantage to the organism as a whole. 358 

However, we note that our assessment of baseline fertility does not rule out potential germline 359 

vulnerabilities that might become apparent under conditions of physiological stress, aging, or 360 

environmental challenge. 361 

362 

In germ cells, evolution of Traf6 regulation involved a tradeoff between CTCF binding and 363 

H3K27me3 deposition, where loss of CTCF binding permitted expansion of adjacent 364 

H3K27me3, generating a new bivalent domain and reducing expression. CTCF appears to act 365 

primarily as an activating transcription factor, rather than a looping factor or insulator, at the 366 

Traf6 locus. In mESCs and germ cells, GC content and CpG island density are strong correlates 367 

of the bivalent state 51–53 , but are antagonized by the presence of activating sequence 25,66,67 .368 

Notably, both inactive developmental genes in mESCs and inactive LPS-inducible genes in 369 

unstimulated macrophages have promoter chromatin features likely to make them 370

Humans are known to be much more sensitive to endotoxin challenge than mice, requiring 500-379 

fold less LPS per kilogram required to induce a similar level of cytokine response 63,64,82 .380 

Remarkably, a single small noncoding change was sufficient to recapitulate a portion of this 381 

phenotypic difference in our study. We also found evidence for reduced expression of other 382 

components of the NF-kB signaling pathway in the mouse lineage, suggesting that the species 383 

difference in endotoxin sensitivity is linked to cumulative regulatory changes at multiple genes in 384 

this pathway, with Traf6 as a representative member of this set. In general, our analysis suggests 385 

an enrichment for immune function, and specifically for TLR and NF-kB signaling, among genes 386 

that have recently acquired germline bivalency. 387 

388 

Together, our results highlight the germ line as a critical filter in shaping regulatory evolution for 389 

broadly expressed genes, and indicate that divergence in germline bivalency can be used as a 390 

marker to identify genes with important and evolving roles in immunity. TRAF6 is also involved 391 

in other non-immune functions, including bone formation, tooth morphology, and development 392 

of hair follicles, sweat glands, and sebaceous glands 46,72–78 , and has been implicated in 393 

tumorigenesis 79–81 . Whether differential regulation of Traf6 and other NF-kB pathway members 394 

impacts these functions, and the full effects of its differential role in mouse and human immune 395 

cells, remain open questions. By expanding our set of target loci and further comparing our 396 

humanized mice to additional human phenotypes, our model can uncover fundamental species 397 

differences in inflammatory responses and serve as an important system for studying human 398 

disease. 399

temperature for 10 minutes, then quenched with 0.114 M Glycine. Fixed cells were resuspended 722 

in 300 uL High Salt Lysis/Sonication Buffer (800 mM NaCl, 25 mM Tris pH 7.5, 5 mM EDTA, 723 

1% Triton X-100, 0.1% SDS, 0.5% sodium deoxycholate, 1 x protease inhibitor cocktail) and 724 

frozen in -80°C. 20 uL Dynabeads Protein G (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was first incubated with 725 

1 μL of CTCF antibody (Active Motif, 61932) in 20 uL Chromatin Dilution Buffer (25 mM Tris 726 

pH 7.5, 5 mM EDTA, 1% Triton X-100, 0.1% SDS, 1 x protease inhibitor cocktail) at room 727 

temperature for 3 hours with rotation. Fixed cells were transferred to a Bioruptor Pico Microtube 728 

(Diagenode) and sonicated for 10 cycles (30 seconds on/off) in Bioruptor (Diagenode) at 4°C. 1 729 

mL Chromatin Dilution Buffer was added to sonicated cells, and samples were spun at 13600 x g 730 

for 30 minutes at 4°C to pellet insoluble material. 65 uL soluble chromatin was set aside as input. 731 

Dynabead/antibody mix was added to the soluble chromatin and incubated at 4°C overnight with 732 

rotation. Then, beads were washed with 1 mL Wash Buffer A (140 mM NaCl, 50 mM HEPES pH 733 

7.9, 1 mM EDTA, 1% Triton X-100, 0.1% SDS, 0.1% sodium deoxycholate), Wash Buffer B 734 

(500 mM NaCl, 50 mM HEPES pH 7.9, 1 mM EDTA, 1% Triton X-100, 0.1% SDS, 0.1% 735 

sodium deoxycholate), and Wash Buffer C (20 mM Tris pH 7.5, 1 mM EDTA, 250 mM LiCl, 736 

0.5% NP-40 Alternative, 0.5% sodium deoxycholate) once each, and TE Buffer (10 mM Tris pH 737 

7.5, 1 mM EDTA) twice. Bound DNA was eluted twice with 100 uL Elution Buffer (10 mM Tris 738 

pH 7.5, 1 mM EDTA, 1% SDS) at 65°C for 5 min. Eluted DNA and input samples were 739 

incubated with 4 ug RNase A (Millipore Sigma) at 65°C overnight to reverse cross-linking and 740 

digest any contaminating RNA. ChIP and input samples were further digested with 40 ug 741
