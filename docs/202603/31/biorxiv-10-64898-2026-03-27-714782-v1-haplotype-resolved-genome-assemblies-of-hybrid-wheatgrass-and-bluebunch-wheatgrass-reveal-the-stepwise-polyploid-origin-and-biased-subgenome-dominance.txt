Abstract 19 

Concerns over climate change have intensified the demand for stress resistant crops like hybrid 20 

wheatgrass (HWG; Elymus hoffmannii , StStStStHH), a perennial forage species known for its 21 

exceptional salt and drought tolerance. However, hexaploidy and high heterozygosity have 22 

complicated efforts to resolve its genomic structure and evolutionary history. Here, we present 23 

high-quality, haplotype-resolved, chromosome-level genome assemblies for HWG (CDC 24 

Saltking) and its putative progenitor, bluebunch wheatgrass ( Pseudoroegneria spicata ). By 25 

integrating PacBio HiFi and ultra-long Oxford Nanopore sequencing with Hi-C scaffolding, we 26 

assembled the 10.7 Gb HWG genome into 21 pseudochromosomes per haplotype. Our 27 

phylogenomic analysis redefines the origin of the H subgenome, positioning it as an intermediate 28 

between Old-World Hordeum marinum (sea barley) and Hordeum brevisubulatum . Notably, we 29 

identified significant chromosomal rearrangements, including a unique duplication on St 30 

chromosome 4. Transcriptome analysis across multiple tissues revealed a pronounced expression 31 

dominance of the H subgenome. This dominance was not associated with reduced LTR density, 32 

suggesting that selective pressures for rapid adaptation of the latest subgenome entrant may drive 33 

its dominance. Finally, using the f-branch statistic, population genomic analysis of 189 34 

accessions representing eight Elymus and Pseudoroegneria species revealed extensive reticulate 35

Introduction 59 

Escalating climate change have driven an increased demand for resilient crop varieties with high 60 

adaptability to barren soils and abiotic stress conditions such as drought and salinity 1-3 . Hybrid 61 

wheatgrass ( Elymus hoffmannii , StStStStHH, HWG), is a perennial forage grass recognized for 62 

its strong tolerance to salinity and drought, offering a sustainable solution for livestock 63 

production while reducing soil erosion 4-6 . Although various breeding strategies have been 64 

proposed to improve the yield and quality of forage wheatgrasses 7,8 , achieving stable yield under 65 

highly variable, resource constrained environments remains a major challenge. In this context, 66 

generating a high-quality genome sequence of HWG and deciphering its genomic architecture 67 

and evolutionary history have become urgent priorities for making a breakthrough in genetic 68 

improvement. 69 

70 

One of the primary challenges in assembling polyploid plant genomes is their inherent genomic 71 

complexity, which largely arises from recurrent polyploidization and interspecific hybridization 72 

events 9-11 . This challenge is particularly pronounced in HWG, an outcrossing hexaploid grass 73 

that exhibits high levels of heterozygosity. To date, four cultivated varieties have been developed 74

share the same StStStStHH genomic constitution 15 .76 

77 

Polyploid species are generally classified as auto- or allopolyploid based on their origin. 78 

Autopolyploids arise through genome duplication within a single diploid species, while 79 

allopolyploids result from genome doubling following the hybridization between two distinct 80 

diploid progenitors 16 . Due to the absence of high-quality genome sequences for HWG and its 81 

diploid or tetraploid relatives, the evolutionary origin of the two St subgenomes remains 82 

unresolved. In particular, it is unclear whether HWG represents an autopolyploid or an 83 

allopolyploid with respect to its St subgenome composition. Because polyploidization and 84 

hybridization are major evolutionary forces underlying the emergence of agronomically 85 

important traits and adaptation to diverse environments 17,18 , elucidating the history of these 86 

events among the three subgenomes of HWG will offer valuable insights into its genetic 87 

potential for forage crop improvement and facilitate the introgression of desirable traits into 88 

related crops such as wheat. 89 

90 

In this study, we report two high-quality, haplotype-resolved genome assemblies: one for HWG 91 

(Elymus hoffmannii , cultivar CDC Salt king) and one for its putative progenitor, 92 

Pseudoroegneria spicata (PI635993). By comparing these two newly assembled genomes with 93 

other publicly available genomic resources, we aim to: (1) resolve the evolutionary position of 94 

HWG within the Poaceae family; (2) uncover its polyploidization and hybridization history; (3) 95 

identify the closest extant relatives of its diploid progenitors; (4) characterize patterns of 96 

subgenome dominance and their genetic basis; and (5) examine population structure and 97 

introgression events within Elymus and Pseudoroegneria species. 98

Chromosome Origins and Subgenome Assignment in HWG 143 

Among the four varieties, AC Saltlander is believed to derived from a natural crossing between 144 

quackgrass ( Elymus repens , 2n = 42, StStStStHH) and a Pseudoroegneria species carrying the St 145 

genome 12 , whereas, Newhy, RS hybrid and CDC Saltking are synthetic hybrids generated 146 

through crosses involving quackgrass or AC Saltlander and P. spicata 4,5,13,14 . Despite their 147 

distinct backgrounds, all four varieties share key phenotypic improvements over their quackgrass 148 

ancestor, including enhanced tolerance to salinity and reduced rhizome 12-15 . Because the H 149 

genome is directly inherited from E. repens without additional introgression, the observed 150 

phenotypic differences are more likely driven by divergence within the St genomes. Although 151 

previous cytological evidence suggests a high similarity between the two St genomes 15 . The 152

Evolutionary Placement of HWG Subgenomes Among Cereal Crops and Its 207 

Polyploidization History 208 

Similar to the H subgenome in barley, the St subgenome in Pseudoroegneria species also 209 

proposed to be evolutionarily ancient 28 . This genomic composition makes HWG particularly 210 

informative for subgenome evolution within Triticeae. To clarify the evolutionary positions of 211 

the HWG and P. spicata subgenomes and their relationship to other sequenced Triticeae 212 

genomes, we conducted a gene family analysis across 16 subgenomes in 11 species from the 213 

Poaceae family identifying 975 single-copy orthologs (Methods). Phylogenetic relationships 214 

inferred from sequence alignments of these orthologs using a maximum likelihood method 215 

(Figure 3a) showed that the H subgenome in HWG and E. sibiricus diverged approximately 8.2 216 

Mya and are more closely related to H. marinum than to H. vulgare within the Hordeum clade. 217 

The St subgenomes diversified around 17.6 Mya —slightly later than Hordeum (19.75 Mya) but 218 

earlier than the Secale cereale (15.18 Mya) and Thinopyrum elongatum (12.66 Mya). The 219 

divergence between St 1 and St 2 genomes was estimated at approximately 14.23 Mya, with P. 220 

spicata showing closer genetic affinity to St 2. These divergence times and genomic relationships 221 

between the H and St lineages were further supported by synonymous substitutions ( Ks) rate 222 

analyses of orthologous coding genes (Figure 3c). 223 

To further identify the donors of H and St subgenomes, we analyzed a combination of publicly 224 

available whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data and our GBS datasets, including 10 lineages 225 

that represent diverse genomic subtypes (Methods). In total, 286.45 Gb of sequencing data were 226 

mapped to the H, St 1 and St 2 subgenomes, and unrooted phylogenetic trees were reconstructed 227 

using SNPs variants. These analyses showed that the H subgenome is more closely related to H. 228

To further explore subgenome-specific evolutionary patterns, we constructed phylogenetic trees 231 

using single-copy genes grouped by chromosomes. Most chromosomes supported the genome-232

relative to its St 1 counterpart (Supplementary Figure 10), making it the only chromosome that 234 

deviated from the overall pattern. In addition, we examined syntenic relationships among Elymus 235

P. spicata ). Overall, synteny was largely conserved among the St subgenomes, with only a few 237 

large-scale structural rearrangements in Chr 3, 4, and 6. In contrast, a large region of 238 

translocation between Chr4 and Chr6 was detected in the H subgenome of E. sibiricus but was 239 

absent in HWG, indicating an independent evolutionary history of HWG (Figure 3e). 240 

Given the wide distribution of tetraploid wheatgrass such as E. sibiricus (StStHH) and 241 

Pseudoroegneria species (StStStSt), an important question is whether HWG originated from an 242 

initial tetraploid of Pseudoroegneria species or from an allotetraploid StStHH ancestor. To 243 

address this aspect of HWG polyploidization history, we focus on major gene tree conflicts 244 

involving the H, St 1 and St 2 subgenomes. The predominant topology, ((St 1,St 2), H), accounted 245 

for 57.29% of gene trees, whereas the alternative topologies —((St 2, H), St 1) and ((H,St 1), St 2) —246 

were observed at much lower and nearly equal frequencies (21.72% and 20.99%, respectively 247 

Figure 3b). The prevalence of ((St 1,St 2), H) topology (>50%) supports a stepwise polyploid 248 

origin, where St 1 and St 2 initially hybridized, followed by a second hybridization with the H 249 

genome. Consistent with expectations under the incomplete lineage sorting (ILS) model, the two 250 

alternative topologies occur at similar frequencies (~21%), suggesting a short interval between 251 

divergence of the St lineages and their subsequent hybridization with the H genome. The lack of 252 

frequency bias between these minority topologies further suggests that the observed phylogenetic 253 

discordance is more likely due to the stochastic sorting of ancestral polymorphisms rather than 254 

asymmetric gene flow 29 . To independently assess the order of hybridization events, we examined 255 

LTR insertion times across subgenomes and found that LTRs in the H subgenome are 256 

consistently younger than those in the St subgenomes, providing further support to this 257 

evolutionary scenario (Supplementary Figure 12). 258 

Furthermore, the plastid phylogeny was reconstructed using whole-chloroplast genome 259 

alignment from 10 species. The results showed clear maternal inheritance of St genome during 260 

the second polyploidization event (Supplementary Figure 11). Based on these results, we propose 261 

a refined model for the polyploidization history of HWG (Figure 3d), in which two diverged St 262 

genomes first hybridized to form an allotetraploid (St 1St 1St 2St 2). This tetraploid species 263 

subsequently acted as the maternal donor in a second hybridization with H genomes, giving rise 264 

to hexaploid HWG (St 1St 1St 2St 2HH). 265

Population Structure and Asymmetric Gene Flow in the Wheatgrass Complex 310 

HWG, originally identified in Central Europe 15 , is an outcrossing polyploid species that has 311 

likely undergone extensive reticulate evolution driven by persistent gene flow. Understanding the 312 

evolutionary relationships among its close relatives—particularly within the Elymus and 313 

Pseudoroegneria genera—is therefore essential for clarifying how genetic variation is exchanged 314 

across their global distributions. However, previous studies have often struggled to clearly 315 

delineate the genetic boundaries within and between these groups 33 .316 

To characterize population structure and resolve these genetic boundaries, we performed GBS on 317 

189 globally sourced accessions, including 71 E. repens , 115 Pseudoroegneria , 1 E. dahuricus ,318 

and 2 HWG accessions (Figure 5a). By mapping GBS reads to the HWG reference genome, we 319 

identified 50,118 high-quality SNPs (Methods). Principal component analysis (Figure 5c) and 320 

phylogenetic reconstruction (Figure 5d) consistently resolved three major clades corresponding 321 

to E. repens , P. spicata , and European Pseudoroegneria species, in close agreement with their 322 

established evolutionary relationships. 323 

Admixture analysis supported K=5 as the optimal number of genetic clusters (lowest cross-324 

validation error: 0.35187). These clusters clearly distinguished P. spicata , P. geniculata , and P. 325 

tauri , while subdividing E. repens into two distinct subclusters (Figure 5b). Both HWG 326 

accessions grouped within the E. repens group. Notably, one intermediate cluster spanning P. 327 

geniculata and P. tauri consisted of multiple European Pseudoroegneria species (including P. 328 

stipifolia , P. strigosa , P. tauri , and P. cognata ) together with E. dahuricus , indicating a shared, 329 

highly admixed or hybrid genetic background among these lineages. 330 

To quantify the extent and direction of introgression underlying these patterns, we conducted an 331 

f-branch ( f b) test across the phylogeny. This analysis revealed strong evidence of gene flow, most 332 

prominently between E. repens and P. strigosa (f b = 0.464), with weaker but detectable signals 333 

between the European clade and the E. repens , HWG, and P. spicata group (G1) (Figure 5e). A 334 

striking feature of the f-branch statistic results was the asymmetric role of P. spicata . Although it 335 

showed little evidence of receiving introgression, P. spicata emerged as a major genetic donor to 336 

several lineages including HWG ( f b = 0.121), P. cognata (f b = 0.165), P. strigosa (f b = 0.036), 337 

and P. libanotica (f b = 0.096) (Figure 5e). This pronounced unidirectional gene flow suggests 338 

that P. spicata may have served as a key ancestral genetic source during the diversification of 339 

these species. Collectively, these results suggest that introgression from P. spicata has played a 340 

substantial role in shaping the genomic architecture and speciation of European Pseudoroegneria 341 

lineages. 342

Discussion 343 

Polyploid grass genomes are exceptionally large and complex, requiring extensive effort to 344 

resolve their genomic structure and evolutionary trajectories 34 . Although chromosome-scale 345 

assemblies have become increasingly available across the grass family, particularly for 346 

allopolyploids such as oat and wheat 10,35,36 , near-complete assembly of partially autopolyploid 347 

species remains rare due to the high sequence similarity among homologous chromosomes. In 348 

this study, we generated chromosome-level, haplotype-resolved genome assemblies for CDC 349 

Saltking and its diploid ancestors P. spicata . These assemblies provide a robust foundation for 350 

dissecting subgenome-specific architecture, interspecific variation and evolutionary history 351 

within Triticeae. 352 

For decades, the St subgenomes of hexaploid HWG were interpreted as autopolyploid 353 

derivatives based on their high sequence similarity and the assumption of genome duplication 354 

within a single lineage 15 . Our assembly challenges this simplified view and unveils a more 355 

dynamic evolutionary history. While three St 1 chromosomes are nearly indistinguishable from a 356 

canonical St genome, the remaining four chromosomes coalesce into a distinct cluster, indicating 357 

unrecognized subgenome differentiation. Most strikingly, Chr4 of the St 2 subgenome exhibits a 358 

unique duplication divergent from St 1 counterparts in both collinearity and phylogeny (Figure 359 

1d). Comparative genomic analyses demonstrate that this chromosome represents a complex 360 

rearrangement incorporating segments from both P. spicata and E. repens (Figure 1e), implying 361 

a large-scale chromosomal recombination or fusion event. Such chromosomal restructuring 362 

events are major drivers of karyotype evolution and speciation 37,38 .363 

Our phylogenomic analyses further refine the origin of the H subgenome. Rather than clustering 364 

with barley, the H subgenome of HWG is positioned phylogenetically as an intermediate 365 

between H. marinum and H. brevisubulatum , two species associated with Old-World Hordeum 366 

clades 39,40 . This finding both corroborates and extends prior observations from E. repens , where 367 

the H genome was shown to affiliate with H. brevisubulatum and H. bogdanii , highlighting 368 

repeated contributions from ecologically distinct Old-World Hordeum lineages 41,42 . Tracing the 369 

H genome’s ancestry offers a strategic blueprint for accelerating the development of salt-tolerant 370 

crops. Given the close phylogenetic relationship of H genome of HWG with H. marinum and H. 371 

brevisubulatum , the pronounced salt tolerance observed in HWG likely reflects the retention or 372 

parallel evolution of stress-adaptive traits characteristic of H. marinum 39 and H. 373 

brevisubulatum 40 , two species well known for their ability to thrive under high salinity. This 374 

evolutionary proximity strengthens the biological significance of HWG’s salt tolerance, 375 

highlighting its role of a valuable reservoir of salinity-adaptive alleles within the Triticeae, with 376 

potential relevance for improving salt tolerance in related cereal and forage crops. 377 

Moreover, the functional importance of the H subgenome is further evidenced by its clear 378 

expression dominance. Interestingly, this dominance is not accompanied by reduced LTR 379

Interspecific introgression is a recognized driver of adaptive evolution in forage grasses, 384 

including perennial ryegrass ( Lolium perenne L. )43 , turtle grass ( Thalassia testudinum )44,45 , and 385 

turfgrass ( Poa trivialis L. )46 . Recent phylogenetic studies have proposed that P. spicata is 386

including HWG, P. cognata , P. strigosa , and P. libanotica . These findings reinforce the 390 

hypothesis that P. spicata acted as an ancestral source of gene flow that facilitated the speciation 391 

of European lineages. Furthermore, the robust signal of introgression between E. repens and P. 392 

strigosa —when compared to the gene flow between HWG and P. spicata —suggests that 393 

differential introgression events are the primary drivers of the genomic divergence observed 394 

between HWG and E. repens .395

rearrangement, together with evidence for an Old-World Hordeum origin of the H subgenome, 397 

fundamentally revises prevailing models of hexaploid HWG evolution. These findings 398 

underscore the importance of high-resolution, haplotype-resolved assemblies for disentangling 399 

complex polyploid histories. Future research integrating high-resolution cytogenetics, 400 

transcriptomics, and functional genomics will be instrumental for fully elucidating the 401 

evolutionary and agronomic significance of these genomic innovations. 402

Centromeric region identification 457

diploid P. libanotica . Notably, STlib_96 and STlib_98 are specific to the St genome and absent 465 

in wheat 63 . The density of each marker was calculated across the genome using a 1Mb sliding 466 

windows, with centromeric regions defined by the co-localization and significant enrichment of 467

Homoeologous gene triplets were initially identified based on the syntenic blocks detected using 556 

JCVI (v.1.5.6). Protein sequences within each homoeologous group were aligned using Clustalw 557 

(v.1.83) to verify sequence similarity and confirm homology. A total of 6742 gene triplets 558 

(triads) in hexaploid HWG were ultimately selected for differential homoeologous gene 559 

expression analysis. To cluster the homoeologous genes, TPM values of triplets across all tissues 560 

were transformed using the common logarithm. A Euclidean distance matrix was computed from 561 

normalized data, followed by two-dimensional hierarchical clustering to group homoeologous 562 

genes with similar expression profiles. To evaluate the relative expression of homoeologous gene 563 

pairs in different tissues, the relative expression between two subgenomes was calculated as the 564 

log base 2 of the ratio between the TPM value of each subgenomes with applying a pseudocount 565 

to avoid division by zero (log 2(Sub 1TPM + 0.1/Sub 2TPM + 0.1). 566 

To analyze the subgenome bias expression in HWG, we normalized the absolute TPM for each 567 

gene within a homoeologous triad by calculating its normalized expression as the proportion of 568 

its TPM value divided by the sum of TPM values from all three subgenomes. To improve the 569 

reliability of subgenome bias analysis and avoid artificial bias, the triplets with the sum of TPM 570 

value across the three subgenomes less than 0.5 were removed. Based on the normalized 571 

expression proportions of the three homoeologs within each triad, genes were classified into 572 

expression bias categories. Triads with proportions close to 1:1:1 were considered balanced. A 573 

proportion close to 1:0:0 indicated H-subgenome dominance, while 0:1:0 and 0:0:1 represented 574 

St 1 -subgenome dominance and St 2-subgenome dominance, respectively. Conversely, proportions 575 

such as 0:0.5:0.5 indicated H-subgenome suppression, whereas 0.5:0:0.5 and 0.5:0.5:0 indicated 576 

St 1 - and St 2-subgenome suppression, respectively. The normalized expression analysis was 577 

performed for each tissue as well as the average across all tissues. 578
