Abstract 23 

Understanding how and why genetic variation is maintained under sustained selection remains a 24 

central question in evolutionary genetics. Experimental evolution shows that adaptation in 25 

sexually reproducing populations is often highly polygenic, proceeding through coordinated, 26 

genome -wide allele frequency shifts from standing variation rather than classic hard sweeps. 27 

Recent explanations emphasize highly polygenic architectures, optimizing -selection, and genetic 28 

redundancy, which can slow fixation by distributing selection across many loci during adaptation. 29 

However, observations from long -term selection experiments reveal a pattern these frameworks 30 

do not fully explain: substantial genetic variation persists after hundreds of generations of intense 31 

directional selection in constant environments. Here, we use long -term experimental evolution in 32 

Drosophila melanogaster to test whether balancing -selection actively maintains genetic variation 33 

under strong life -history selection and preserves evolutionary reversibility. Longstanding 34 

populations selected for accelerated or delayed reproduction were shifted to the opposing regime, 35 

imposing age -structured fitness trade -offs. Notably, selection for early reproduction is associated 36 

with substantial loss of genetic variation, providing a stringent test of whether standing variation 37 

is truly depleted. Following reciprocal shifts, populations showed rapid phenotypic convergence 38 

toward the target regime. At the genomic level, allele -frequency trajectories were strongly 39 

antiparallel and highly repeatable across replicates, revealing coordinated polygenic responses. 40 

Relaxing long -standing early -life selection produced a pronounced rebound in genome -wide 41 

heterozygosity. Deep sequencing uncovered ultra -rare alleles at sites appearing fixed under 42 

standard coverage, indicating low -frequency functional variation persists below detection 43 

thresholds. These results suggest that substantial genetic variation can persist under intense 44 

directional selection and be rapidly redeployed when selection reverses, consistent with 45 

widespread balancing -selection. 46

Introduction 48 

Understanding how genetic variation is shaped and maintained in populations has long been a 49 

central question with in evolutionary genetics ( Lewontin 1974 ). A major puzzle is why substantial 50 

genetic variation often persists within sexually reproducing populations despite long -term, intense 51 

selection, under which classical models predict rapid fixation and loss of diversity. Evolution 52 

experiments in sexually reproducing organisms have repeatedly shown that responses to 53 

selection are often highly polygenic, fueled by standing genetic variation, and characterized by 54 

modest coordinated shifts in allele frequencies rather than classic hard sweeps in which selected 55 

alleles and linked sites fix ( Burke 2012 ; Barghi and Schlötterer 2020 ). In mesocosm experiments 56 

mimicking natural conditions, the absence of sweeps and persistence of genetic variation is often 57 

attributed to fluctuating selection and adaptive tracking ( Rudman et al. 2022 , Bitter et al. 2024 ). 58 

However, we know that this explanation cannot be applied to laboratory based experimental 59 

evolution studies where environments and selection pressures are typically constant. 60 

Experimental evolution combined with whole -genome sequencing (“E&R”) is an established 61 

approach for studying the genetic basis of adaptation and complex trait variation ( Long et al. 2015 ;62 

Schlötterer et al. 2015 ). Across the Drosophila studies that comprise most of this work, the shift 63 

versus sweep dynamic is widespread (e.g. Burke et al. 2010 ; Tobler et al. 2014 ; Graves et al. 64 

2017 ; Barghi et al. 2019 , 2020 ). It is even observed in outcrossing yeast studies , where large 65 

population sizes and longer selection durations allow for a greater possibility of adaptation from 66 

de novo mutations ( Burke et al. 2014 ; Phillips et al. 2020 ; Linder et al. 2022 ). Recent explanations 67 

for the “shifts not sweeps” pattern emphasize highly polygenic architectures, optimizing selection, 68 

and genetic redundancy ( Chevin and Hospital 2008 ; Barghi et al. 2019 ; Hayward and Sella 2022 ). 69 

However, while this framework is compelling, it does not resolve a puzzling pattern that emerges 70 

in the case of long -term selection experiments, where populations experience sustained selection 71 

for many hundreds of generations. Although genetic redundancy can slow fixation by distributing 72 

selection across many loci during ongoing adaptation, it does not provide a mechanism for 73 

actively maintaining polymorphisms once populations approach a phenotypic optimum, at which 74 

point drift is expected to erode variation. 75 

Evidence for this phenomenon comes from our own long -term Drosophila experimental evolution 76 

system, in which populations maintained under intense selection for over 1,000 generations retain 77 

substantially more genetic variation than expected under neutral drift and under forward 78 

simulations incorporating both directional and optimizing selection (Phillips et al. 2016 ). Why so 79 

much genetic variation persists after hundreds of generations of intense selection remains an 80

In previous work, we raised the possibility that the long -term persistence of genetic variation in 84 

these populations reflects pervasive balancing selection acting within populations rather than 85 

passive retention (Graves et al. 2017 ). This idea was motivated by a key result from that study: 86 

despite many generations of selection on reproductive timing, populations recently derived from 87 

the same ancestral stock rapidly converged phenotypically and genotypically with long -standing 88 

selected lines. This repeatability implies that adaptive alleles are retained in the base populations 89 

far longer than expected, given that hundreds of generations of selection and drift should 90 

otherwise erode standing variation. Antagonistic pleiotropy provides a plausible mechanism: 91 

alleles that enhance fitness through one life -history character may impose costs upon other 92 

characters, constraining fixation, and in some cases, sustaining genetic polymorphism over time 93 

(Rose 1982 ; Rose 1985 ).94 

Here we aim to empirically test whether widespread balancing selection contributes to the long -95 

term maintenance of genetic variation in Drosophila experimental evolution. We focus on 96 

populations that have been under intense selection for early or late reproduction for hundreds of 97 

generations and assess whether they retain the capacity to evolve back toward ancestral 98 

phenotypic and genomic states when selection is reversed. 99 

The early -reproduction populations, termed the A -types, are maintained on a 10 -day generation 100 

cycle, whereas the late -reproductive populations , the C -types, are maintained on a 28 -day cycle 101 

(Table S1) . Both sets were ultimately descended from the same ancestral Ives base population 102 

(Ives 1970 ; Rose et al. 2004 ). A -type selection produces accelerated development, reduced adult 103 

lifespan, reduced stress resistance, increased early -life fecundity, and distinct metabolic profiles 104 

(Burke et al. 2016 ; Kezos et al. 2023 ; Hubert et al. 2025 ). Genomic characterization further 105 

indicates that these phenotypes are underlain by widespread genomic responses to selection, 106 

including the apparent fixation of some polymorphic sites and regions of the genome where 107 

variation is almost entirely purged . (At present, the long est -standing A -type populations have 108 

experience d ~1,200 generations of selection for early reproduction , and the more recently derived 109 

A-types ~650 ). Given that population sizes are on the order of a thousand, beneficial de novo 110 

mutations are unlikely to be a factor driving adaptation and populations should have reached 111 

mutation -selection -drift equilibrium under their respective selection regimes. Under standard 112 

expectations when antagonistic pleiotropy is absent, most segregating variation should be 113

To assess the possible conclusion described above, we use antiparallel selection shifts between 118 

the A - and C -type regimes and follow two trajectories: A→C (A -types placed under C -type 119 

conditions) and C→A (C -types placed under A -type conditions) illustrated in Figure 1 . In doing so ,120 

we both relax the intense early -reproduction selection experienced by A -types and impose this 121 

same regime on C-types. Starting from these contrasting genetic backgrounds, we track 122 

phenotypic and genomic changes over several dozen generations to evaluate how populations 123 

respond to their new life -history cycles. If widespread balancing selection is maintaining functional 124 

variation in this system, we expect that: (1) evolutionary history will not impose strong, persistent 125 

constraints, and populations will converge genomically and phenotypically on long -standing 126 

counterparts , where under our design, this should translate into inverted genomic trajectories for 127 

the A→C and C→A given they are ultimately derived from the same ancestral population; (2)128 

shifting A -types to C -type conditions will result in a widespread rebound in genetic variation, 129 

revealing low -frequency alleles that persisted despite the apparent loss of polymorphisms in past 130 

work , and (3) replicate populations within each trajectory will show repeatable genomic 131 

responses, consistent with access to shared reservoirs of selectively maintained variation .132

Adult Life -History Evolution: Mortality Trajectories 135 

Founder populations exhibited the expected, long -established divergence in age -specific mortality 136 

between A -type and C -type selection regimes (Fig. S1, S2; Tables S2 -10), providing internal 137 

baselines for evaluating phenotypic change along reciprocal trajectories. At the time of the first 138 

mortality assay, C→A populations had experienced approximately 3 3 generations of early -139 

reproduction selection, whereas A→C populations had experienced approximately 12140 

generations of delayed -reproduction selection. At this stage, both trajectory types differed 141 

significantly from their respective founders but exhibited asymmetric convergence toward the 142 

target selection regime consistent with differences in total number of generations under selection 143 

(Fig. S3A). C→A populations showed strong convergence with A -type founders across much of 144 

the adult lifespan, particularly at early adult ages, whereas A→C populations remained 145 

substantially divergent from C -type founders, showing early phase mortality profiles (Tables S11 -146 

14). 147 

At the next timepoint in the evolutionary trajectory, C→A populations had experienced 148 

approximately 143 generations of early -reproduction selection, whereas A→C populations had 149 

experienced approximately 33 generations of delayed -reproduction selection. Here we see both 150 

trajectory types have converged on the adult mortality phenotypes characteristic of long -term 151 

populations maintained under their respective selection regimes (Fig. S3B). Neither A→C nor 152 

C→A populations differed significantly from long -established populations of the same regime 153 

across adult life -history intervals, and both remained distinguishable from their original founders 154 

(Table S15 -18). 155 

To visualize systematic changes in adult mortality following reciprocal shifts in selection regime, 156 

we summarized mortality trajectories as the difference in mean age -specific mortality between the 157 

evolved populations and their regime founder counterparts, plotted as a function of age from egg. 158 

In both reciprocal selection treatments, differences in age -specific mortality relative to the founder 159 

populations exhibit a smooth, wave -like pattern across the adult lifespan (Fig. 2). For A→C 160 

populations, there is convergence on mortality at early ages until a breakpoint day, at which point, 161 

mortality differences begin to increase gradually at successive ages. This breakpoint is pushed 162 

towards later ages with more selection, indicating age -structured convergence toward C -type 163 

mortality profiles rather than uniform shifts across the lifespan. 164

Developmental Timing Responses 166 

To determine whether developmental timing responded concordantly to reciprocal life -history 167 

selection, we assayed pupation timing along the same evolutionary trajectories. For the first 168 

assay, A→C populations (~6 generations of delayed -reproduction selection) and C→A 169 

populations (~15 generations of early -reproduction selection) exhibited pupation timing 170 

intermediate relative to their respective founders (Figs. S3, S4; Table S19). At the next timepoint, 171 

A→C (~64 generations) C→A (~182 generations), pupation timing in both trajectory types 172 

converged on the phenotypes characteristic of long -term populations maintained under their 173 

current selection regimes (Fig.S5; Table S20). Thus, developmental timing shows a consistent 174 

and reversible response to reciprocal life -history selection, corroborating patterns observed for 175 

adult mortality. 176 

Antiparallel Genomic Trajectories 177 

To characterize genome -wide shifts associated with reciprocal selection, we performed a principal 178 

component analysis (PCA) on SNP frequencies from samples taken over the course of the 179 

experiment (Fig. 3). The first two components captured most of the variance, with PC1 explaining 180 

60.4% and PC2 explaining 11.0% of genome -wide variation; all higher -order PCs each explained 181 

<2%. 182 

Projection onto the first two PCs revealed strongly antiparallel genomic trajectories. A→C 183 

populations shifted toward the cluster of long -established C -type populations, whereas C→A 184 

populations moved toward A -type populations along opposing directions. Dispersion among 185 

replicates was minimal at the final timepoints collected, whereas greater spread was observed 186 

among A→C populations at earlier stages relative to C→A populations, consistent with fewer 187 

generations having elapsed under delayed -reproduction selection. 188 

To identify genomic axes that differ in their temporal response to selection, we analyzed the first 189 

ten principal components (PCs) using linear mixed -effects models with selection treatment (A→C 190 

vs. C→A) and generation as fixed effects and population as a random effect. The treatment × 191 

generation interaction tests whether PC scores change through time differently between 192 

treatments. Significant interactions were detected for PC1 (Table S21: estimate = 7.74, 193 

Bonferroni -adjusted p = 6.26 × 10 ⁻⁵) and PC2 ( Table S21: estimate = −12.47, Bonferroni -adjusted 194 

p = 2.0 × 10 ⁻⁵), indicating opposing, generation -dependent shifts in these components between 195 

A→C and C →A populations. No significant interactions were detected for higher -order PCs, each 196

To focus on locus -specific responses, we quantified allele -frequency dynamics at individual SNPs 199 

using beta -binomial generalized linear mixed models applied independently to 1, 389,963 loci. In 200 

these models, allele -frequency change was modeled as a function of treatment (A→C vs. C→A), 201 

generation, and their interaction, with population included as a random effect . Under this 202 

framework, significant treatment × generation effects identify SNPs exhibiting reciprocal 203 

evolutionary responses (i.e. allele frequencies shifting in opposite directions between the groups) .204 

After Bonferroni correction, we detected a widespread and highly polygenic genomic response to 205 

reciprocal selection (Fig. 4). The majority of significant signal was attributable to treatment × 206 

generation interactions, with 1 34 ,7 09 SNPs exhibiting divergent allele -frequency trajectories 207 

between A→C and C→A populations. By comparison, 39 ,666 SNPs showed a significant main 208 

effect of generation with interaction , and only 1,164 SNPs exhibited a significant main effect of 209 

generation alone. Similarly, only 387 SNPs were significant for just treatment. Overlap among 210 

significant SNP sets was limited (Fig. 5), with only 204 SNPs significant for all three terms. Most 211 

SNPs exhibiting significant interaction effects lacked independent treatment effects, indicating 212 

that genomic differentiation between trajectories arises primarily through opposing evolutionary 213 

change over time rather than static differences between selection regimes . Together, these results 214 

demonstrate that reciprocal selection regimes produce extensive, genome -wide, and largely non -215 

overlapping allele frequency changes, consistent with anti -parallel evolutionary trajectories 216 

shaped by selection history. 217 

Relaxing Early -Life Selection Recovers Hidden Genetic Variation 218 

Mean genome -wide heterozygosity changed in opposite directions along the two reciprocal 219 

trajectories (Fig. 6). A→C populations, which originate from low -heterozygosity A -type founders, 220 

showed a significant increase in heterozygosity over evolutionary time, whereas C→A 221 

populations, derived from high -heterozygosity C -type founders, exhibited a significant decline. 222 

These trends were consistent across replicate populations (n = 10 per trajectory) and were 223 

already evident at earlier generations. Comparisons between founder and endpoint populations 224 

confirmed highly significant changes in both trajectories (Welch two -sample t -tests, p < 10 ⁻¹¹ ). 225 

Together, these results demonstrate divergent genome -wide responses to reciprocal selection, 226 

with relaxation of early -life selection permitting recovery of genetic variation, while renewed early -227 

life selection reduces heterozygosity. 228

Many sites in the initial generation of the A→C trajectory appeared fixed under standard 229 

sequencing depth (90 ×), yet the genome -wide rebound observed following selection reversal 230 

suggested that this apparent fixation might instead reflect alleles present at frequencies too low 231 

to be reliably detected. To test whether these populations harbored hidden variation, we compared 232 

allele frequencies from a single founding population (A -type1 , Timepoint 1 ) sequenced at higher 233 

depth (800× coverage) against sites appearing fixed in the standard -depth dataset. Specifically, 234 

we focused on sites that were classified as significant in the beta -binomial GLM analysis and that 235 

showed either zero or full minor -allele counts under standard sequencing depth (Bonferroni -236 

corrected GLM, p < 0.005). Of the SNPs identified as statistically significant following Bonferroni 237 

correction, 524,873 sites appeared fixed in the A -type1 population. Deep sequencing data were 238 

available for 151,329 of these sites, and subsequent analysis was performed on this subset. 239 

Using the deep -sequencing subset, we examined the distribution of minor allele frequencies at 240 

these sites and compared it to a random subset of equal size drawn from the same deep -241 

sequencing dataset (Fig. 7). While the random subset exhibited a broad range of allele 242 

frequencies (mean minor allele frequency = 0.174), sites that appeared fixed under standard 243 

coverage were strongly skewed toward very low -frequency alleles, with a mean minor allele 244 

frequency of 0.0063. A permutation test (10,000 iterations) confirmed that this mean was 245 

significantly lower than expected under random sampling (Z = −450.79, p < 1 × 10 ⁻⁴). Together, 246 

these results demonstrate that many sites appearing fixed at the start of the A →C trajectory are 247 

in fact segregating at low frequencies, indicating that apparent fixation largely reflects limited 248 

sequencing depth rather than true loss of genetic variation. 249

Discussion 333 

Long term experimental evolution studies have repeatedly shown that adaptation in sexually 334 

reproducing populations is dominated by modest, coordinated allele frequency shifts across many 335 

loci rather than fixation at single large -effect variants (Burke 2012 ; Barghi et al. 2019 ; Barghi and 336 

Schlötterer 2020 ). While this “shifts not sweeps” pattern is now well established, deeper questions 337 

emerge regarding the mechanisms responsible for maintaining such extensive variation, 338 

especially in cases with sustained and intense selection. In the absence of frequent de novo 339 

mutation s or environmental fluctuation s, classical theories predict that drift and directional 340 

selection should steadily erode standing genetic variation as populations converge on a new 341 

optimum. In this study we demonstrate that genetic variation persists and remains evolutionarily 342 

accessible even after hundreds of generations of intense life -history selection, even from 343 

populations associated with major loss of variation ( Graves et al. 2017 ). These results provide 344 

strong evidence for the active maintenance of polymorphisms through balancing selection. 345 

Reciprocal selection reveals active maintenance of genetic variation 346 

A central result of this study is the rapid phenotypic reversibility of populations with opposing 347 

selection regimes . Adult mortality and developmental timing assays demonstrated clear 348 

responses to selection , with populations evolving toward the characteristic phenotypes of long -349 

established A - and C -type populations. Crucially, this reversibility was observed even in A -type 350 

populations, which are characterized by substantial genome -wide reductions in genetic variation. 351 

These results suggest that alleles contributing to delayed reproduction, extended lifespan, and 352 

slower development were actively maintained at low frequencies in the A -type populations in 353 

some manner. 354 

Importantly, phenotypic reversibility was mirrored at the genomic level. Principal component 355 

analysis reveals a global anti -parallel response with A→C and C→A populations moving in 356 

opposing directions toward their respective selection state. When we examined locus -specific 357 

responses using beta -binomial generalized linear mixed models, the interaction term accounted 358 

for the largest number of significant SNPs suggesting global antiparallel trajectories were not 359 

diffuse or idiosyncratic. Rather, they appear to be driven by repeatable and parallel shifts in target 360 

site s and linked regions. In total, these results are consistent with widespread balancing selection 361 

ensuring populations consistently had access to the same subsets of standing variation when 362 

selection was reversed. These results build on those of Graves et al. (2017 ), showing that in this 363

Conclusion 432 

Broadly, these findings contribute to a growing body of evidence that adaptive evolution in sexually 433 

reproducing populations frequently proceeds through coordinated, genome -wide shifts in allele 434 

frequencies rather than classic hard sweeps (Burke 2012 ; Barghi et al. 2019 ; Barghi and 435 

Schlötterer 2020 ). In our reciprocal selection framework, the largely anti parallel responses across 436 

replicate populations and chromosome arms, together with the capacity for directional reversal 437 

under altered age -specific selection, indicate that substantial genetic variation is maintained over 438 

long evolutionary timescales. Rather than being exhausted by prolonged selection, this standing 439 

variation remains available to fuel rapid and repeatable responses when the selective regime 440 

changes. Such patterns serve as strong signatures of balancing processes operating across 441 

many loci, maintaining polymorphism through time and enabling predictable yet reversible 442 

genomic trajectories. Viewed in this light, our results contribute to the growing body of literature 443 

on the importance of considering balancing selection in evolutionary genetics, not as a marginal 444 

phenomenon, but as a major driver of observed patterns of genetic variation ( Kawecki et al. 2021 ,445 

Zwoinska et al. 2026 ). 446

Materials and Methods 448 

Experimental Populations 449 

All Drosophila melanogaster populations used in this study derive and branch from the outbred 450 

population established by P. T. Ives in 1970 and reared on standard banana agar media. From 451 

this founding population multiple experimental evolution lines have been derived, typically with 452 

five independently replicated populations and maintained and subjected to distinct selection 453 

regimes. Of these treatments, we have cultured ten early -reproducing A-types and ten late -454 

reproducing C-types , achieving hundreds of generations of sustained selection ( Rose et al. 1992 ;455 

Chippindale et al. 1997 ; Burke et al. 2016), demonstrating strong convergence across life -history 456 

characteristics and genomics (Graves et al. 2017). Given this strong convergence within each 457 

regime, we will refer to these two sets of 10 populations as the founder A-types and the founder 458 

C-types respectively. 459 

Building upon this work, we derived 20 new populations from the progenitor A-types and C -types. 460 

This was accomplished by imposing an A -type selection on 10 populations derived from the 461 

progenitor C -type populations to create the C→A -types . In tandem, C -type selection was imposed 462 

on 10 lines derived from the progenitor A -type populations to create the A→C-types. 463 

C→A -type populations were created by collecting eggs from the 10 founder C-types and only 464 

permitting the first ~20% developing flies to contribute to the next generation. This was 465 

accomplished by rearing eggs from each generation in carousels, which feature food caps housed 466 

in a dome to contain the earliest developing flies within until enough individual flies have eclosed 467 

to create a new outbred population. The earliest derived flies contained in the carousel were then 468 

gassed with CO 2 and pooled into Plexiglass cages to lay eggs for the next generation. Multiple 469 

carousels were utilized per population to yield large cohort sizes to prevent inbreeding depression 470 

from the intense selection regime. With each vial containing 80+ eggs, 80 vials each in each 471 

carousel, and ~3 carousels per population, ~19,200 total eggs were obtained each early 472 

generation, with approximately ~3840 young adults constituting the top 20% of early developers 473 

permitted to create the next generation. With each successive generation of directional selection, 474 

average development time decreased, reducing the need for additional carousels and thereby 475 

minimizing the risk of large population bottlenecks and cohorts necessary for each population as 476 

the experiment continued. Once the C→A -type populations were reliably reproducing on Day 10 477 

without complications, they were maintained like the ir progenitor A-type counterparts with only 478 

vials and cages, and no carousels. 479
