Abstract: 26 

Crossing over during meiosis drives genetic diversity and ensures the accurate segregation of 27 

homologous chromosomes. Variation in the rate of crossing over has been linked to evolutionary 28 

divergence and environmental adaptability, shaping fitness and responses to selective pressures. 29 

Despit e its significance, the molecular mechanisms underlying this variation remain poorly 30 

understood. Crossover sites are selected from a large pool of potential sites initiated by programmed 31 

DNA double -strand breaks. Post -translational modification by SUMO (Sm all Ubiquitin -like 32 

Modifier) has been implicated in this process . Here, we show that crossover rate, chromosome 33 

length, and abundance of chromosome -associated SUMO are positively correlated across a range 34 

of vertebrate species , including mouse, chicken, pi g, cattle , sheep, and goat . Crossover variation 35 

between goat breeds across the Indian subcontinent was also positively correlated with 36 

chromosomal SUMO level. Furthermore, modulating SUMO levels in cultured goat spermatocytes 37 

alter ed crossover frequenc y. Cumulatively, these observations point to a central role for SUMO in 38 

mediating crossover variation both between and within vertebrate species. 39

Introduction: 57 

Meiosis produce s haploid gametes during sexual reproduction 1. In meiotic prophase I, crossing 58 

over between homologous chromosomes promotes accurate segregation and genetic variation 2.59 

Crossover rates vary between individuals , sexes , and species 3. A four -locus model suggests that 60 

crossover variation is beneficial for environmental adaptability 4. Notably, a population with a 61 

higher recombination rate shows a more ro bust response to natural and artificial selection 62 

pressures 5-7. This is manifest in domesticated plant and livestock species , which have higher 63 

crossover rate s than their predecessors or wild counterparts 8,9 . Although crossover rates vary 64 

between individuals and species, the distribution of crossovers is controlled to ensure that at least 65 

one crossover forms between each chromosome pair 10 . The se obligate crossovers are required to 66 

ensure accurate disjunction of homologous chromosomes at the first meiotic divisio n10 .67 

> 68

Crossing over is one outcome of homologous recombination , which is initiated during meiosis by 69 

SPO11 -catalyzed DNA double -strand break s (DSB s)11 . DSB ends are resected to form long 3' 70 

single -stranded tails that assemble filaments of the RAD51 and DMC1 recombinases to catalyze 71 

homology search and DNA strand invasion 2,12 . Nascent strand invas ion intermediates are stabilized 72 

by a conserved set of meiosis -specific ZMM factors that facilitate chromosome pairing and synapsis 73 

and enable the differentiation of crossover sites. I nitially defined in budding yeast, the ZMMs 74 

include Zip1 Sycp1 , Zip2 Shoc1 , Spo16 , Zip3 Rnf212 , Zip4 Tex11 , Mer3 Hfm1 , and Msh4 -Msh5 (names of 75 

mammalian counterparts are superscript )2,13,14 . Among these, the RING -family E3 ligase RNF212 76 

plays a key role in crossover designation by localizin g along the synaptonemal complex (SC) and 77 

becoming selectively enriched at a subset of recombination sites, where it stabilizes recombination 78 

intermediates. In parallel, post -translational modification by SUMO (Small Ubiquitin -like 79 

Modifier) acts in concer t with RNF212 to regulate recombination progression. SUMO localizes as 80 

discrete foci along chromosome axes during early prophase I, particularly during zygonema, in 81 

association with the SC, and this axis -associated SUMO is largely dependent on RNF212. Toge ther, 82 

RNF212 and SUMO are thought to stabilize recombination intermediates and promote their 83 

differentiation into crossovers 15,16 . At designated crossover sites, ZMMs and crossover -specific 84 

factors such as MutL  (MLH1 -MLH3) facilitate the formation and resolution of double Holliday 85 

junction (dHJ) intermediates specifically into crossovers 17,18 . At other recombination sites , the 86 

invad ing strand is extended by DNA synthesi s before being dissociated and annealing to the other 87 

DSB end to produce a non -crossover , without exchange of chromosome arms 2,18 . 16 Genetic, 88 

epigenetic, and environmental variation can modulate the rate of crossing over 19 -21 . The rapid 89

Discuss ion: 262 

> 263

Crossover rates vary significantly across species, sexes, and even individuals 26,50,51 . Such variation 264 

is thought to be shaped by evolutionary pressures, environmental factors, and genetic makeup 26,51,52 .265 

However, the molecular mechanisms governing crossover variation remain elusive. This study 266 

provides evidence that SUMO plays a role in modulating crossover rates both between and within 267 

vertebrate species. 268 

> 269

Chromosom e axis length as a conserved scaling parameter 270 

Our comparative analysis shows that the positive correlation between chromosome axis length and 271 

crossover frequency , previously reported for mouse and human, can be extend ed across livestock 272 

vertebrate species (Fig. 1). Moreover, t he quasi -fixed crossover density per unit of chromosome 273 

axis length observed in these species points to a conserved regulatory mechanism that ensures a 274 

consistent recombination rate proportional to chromosome length (Fig. 1). A likely mechanism is 275 

crossover interference, the one dimensional patterning process that reduces the occurrence of 276 

closely -spaced crossovers and results in a relatively even spacing of adjacent crossovers 53 . Our 277 

crossover density data (MLH1 foci per μm axis) imply that interference distances (MLH1 inter -278 

focus distances) average 6.7 –9.1 μm in the species stud ies. In this context, the chromosome axis 279 

can be viewed not merely as a scaffold but as an active regulator that spatially constrains 280 

recombination events. 281 

However, the case of chicken highlights deviation in this model. The elevated crossover density 282 

obse rved in chicken is largely explained by the presence of numerous micro -chromosomes, each of 283 

which must acquire at least one crossover. This indicates that obligate crossover requirements can 284 

locally override global scaling rules, demonstrating how structur al constraints and regulatory 285 

mechanisms interact to shape recombination landscapes. 286

SUMO regulates chro mosome axis length and crossover rate 305 

> 306

One of the most notable finding is that recombination rate is not a rigid property of a species, but a 307 

plastic trait that can be tuned through changes in chromosome architecture. The strong covariation 308 

we observe betw een chromosome axis length, SUMO levels, and crossover frequency, both across 309 

species and within goat breeds, suggests that relatively subtle shifts in how chromosomes are 310 

organized during meiosis can produce meaningful differences in recombination output (Fig. 4 and 311 

5). This provides a simple and elegant solution to a long -standing problem: how crossover rates can 312 

evolve without disrupting the essential requirement for at least one crossover per chromosome pair. 313 

A key feature of this model is that it decou ples recombination rate from genome size and, to a large 314 

extent, from DSB formation. Instead of requiring large -scale genomic changes, recombination can 315 

be adjusted through modulation of axis length or the SUMO -dependent environment that governs 316 

recombinat ion intermediate stability. Because the SUMO pathway targets a wide range of 317 

chromosomal proteins including cohesin, condensin, and axis components , small changes in SUMO 318 

dynamics or target specificity could alter chromosome structure in ways that scale cr ossover 319 

number 54,55 . In this sense, the SUMO machinery represents a broad and flexible regulatory layer 320 

capable of fine -tuning recombination. 321 

This framework also provides a plausible explanation for the rapid evolu tion of recombination 322 

landscapes observed across vertebrates 56 . Changes in axis organization or SUMO regulation could 323 

shift crossover rates over relatively short evolutionary time scales, without requiring alterations to 324 

the core recombination machinery. The variation we observe among goat breeds reinforces this 325 

idea, demonstrating that such changes can arise within a single species and may contribute to 326 

differences in fertility, ad aptation, or response to selection. 327

were collected from the slaughterhouse. The slaughterhouse is government -licensed, and samples 338 

were collected from healthy mature animals and ready f or reproduction. Three independent 339

this study. The different goat breed testes were collected from the herd in diverse geographical areas 341 

based on phenotypic characteristics in consultation with the herd's goat herder. However, we did 342 

not get any parental history. In addition, we have confirmed the breed by phylogenetic analysis 343 

using mitochondrial hypervariable region (D -loop sequencing) . Except for chicken te stes, all 344 

samples were transported immediately to the lab on ice and processed by chopping seminiferous 345 

tubules for surface spreading. The leftover testes were frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at -80 C 346 

for further use. Chicken ( Gallus gallus domesticus ) testes samples were collected in 1X PBS from 347

Phylogenetic analysis of the meiosis -specific gene sequences was done using MEGA X 58 . Initially, 437
