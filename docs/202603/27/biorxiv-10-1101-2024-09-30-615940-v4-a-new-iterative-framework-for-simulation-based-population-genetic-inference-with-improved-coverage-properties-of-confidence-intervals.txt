# Abstract 17 

Simulation-based methods such as approximate Bayesian computation (ABC) are 18 

widely used to infer the evolutionary history of populations from molecular genetic 19 

data. We describe and evaluate a new iterative method of statistical inference about 20 

model parameters, which revisits the idea of inferring a likelihood surface using simu-21 

lation when the likelihood function cannot be evaluated. It is based on combining the 22 

random forest machine learning method, and multivariate Gaussian mixture (MGM) 23 

models, in an effective inference workflow, here used to fit models with up to 15 24 

variable parameters. In addition to the traditional assessment of precision in terms 25 

of bias and mean square error, we also evaluate the coverage of confidence intervals. 26 

The method is compared with approximate Bayesian computation using random 27 

forests (ABC-RF), a non-iterative method sharing some technical features with the 28 

proposed approach, across scenarios of historical demographic inference from pop-29 

ulation genetic data. It is also compared to another iterative method, sequential 30 

neural likelihood estimation (SNLE). These comparisons highlight the importance 31 

of an iterative workflow for exploring the parameter space efficiently. For equivalent 32 

simulation effort of the data-generating process, the new summary-likelihood method 33 

provides intervals whose coverage is better controlled than the marginal coverage of 34 

intervals provided by ABC with random forests, and than generally reported for ABC 35 

methods. The iterative workflow can also yield greater improvements in estimator 36 

precision when larger datasets are used. 37

# Introduction 38 

The likelihood function is a classical component of efficient statistical inference 39 

methodologies. In instances where the likelihood function cannot be computed in 40 

a reasonable time frame, alternative statistical methods can be employed. These 41 

include moment-matching techniques when the analytical results for moments of a 42 

response variable are known, or approaches based on the simulation of the putative 43 

data-generating process. Among the latter simulation-based methods, approximate 44 

Bayesian computation (ABC) has been particularly developed, notably for applica-45 

tions in population genetics (Beaumont, 2010; Beaumont et al. , 2002; Bertorelle et 46 

al. , 2010; Tavar´ e et al. , 1997), but also in diverse other fields of biology (Sch¨ alte and 47 

Hasenauer, 2020) and beyond (e.g., Akeret et al. , 2015; Sisson et al. , 2019). In molec-48 

ular evolutionary studies, ABC has been widely used to infer the past history of mi-49 

gration, founding events, invasion routes and introgressions among populations (e.g., 50 

Fraimout et al. , 2017), but also selection pressures (e.g., Nakagome et al. , 2015) or 51 

genomic rearrangement rates (e.g., Moshe et al. , 2022). 52 

The idea of estimating the likelihood function by simulation is less developed, 53 

although it goes back at least to Diggle and Gratton (1984). Some recent machine-54 

learning methods, such as sequential neural likelihood estimation (SNLE, Papa-55 

makarios et al. , 2019), incorporate this step, although they perform inference via 56 

the posterior distribution rather than the likelihood function. Diggle and Gratton’s 57 

more strictly likelihood-based approach has had limited follow-up in the form of 58 

practically implemented likelihood methods for more complex models, particularly 59 

when the data are represented by many descriptive statistics (i.e., “summary statis-60 

tics”). The “synthetic-likelihood” method (Wood, 2010) may be seen as derived from 61 

Diggle and Gratton (1984), except that it assumes the summary statistics to have a 62 

multivariate normal distribution for each parameter value. An extension of Diggle 63 

and Gratton’s approach not making this assumption has been described by Rousset 64 

et al. (2017). As this is a likelihood inference using the information retained in the 65 

summary statistics, we refer to this method as “summary-likelihood (SL) inference”. 66 

Summary likelihood is not full-data likelihood but is still a form of likelihood, which 67 

one can evaluate if the full data have been thrown away and only some summary 68 

statistics have been retained. When the number of summary statistics is large, infer-69 

ring the likelihood surface becomes more tractable by applying dimension-reduction 70 

techniques. Various machine learning methods can be employed for this purpose; 71 

here, we adopt the random forest approach (Breiman, 2001; Geurts et al. , 2006). 72 

This method is also used in ABC-RF (Collin et al. , 2021; Pudlo et al. , 2016; Raynal 73 

et al. , 2019). 74

The aforementioned methods all address the issue of inferring the parameters θ75 

of a hypothetical data-generating process, utilizing simulations of this process for 76 

varying values of the vector θ. In Rousset et al. (2017) as in Diggle and Gratton 77 

(1984) and Wood (2010), values of θ are drawn and for each draw, the distribu-78 

tion of summary statistics is estimated using a moderately large number of simu-79 

lations of the data-generating process. In this study, we present a more efficient 80 

summary-likelihood inference workflow that requires significantly fewer simulations 81 

to provide accurate inferences. This iterative workflow facilitates the inference of 82 

more parameter-rich models, even when simulating observations is computationally 83 

expensive. The new workflow employs a single simulated observation for each drawn 84 

value of θ. This approach aligns with the general methodology of ABC methods, 85 

where each drawn θ and the corresponding summary statistics for each simulated 86 

observation form one line of a table commonly known as the reference table. The 87 

new iterative method has already been used to fit a simple two-parameter model of 88 

evolution of experimental populations by Laugier et al. (2025). 89 

The idea of estimating the likelihood function for summary statistics from an 90 

ABC reference table already appears in the ABC literature (e.g., Fan et al. , 2013; 91 

Papamakarios et al. , 2019; Rubio and Johansen, 2013), but this has not yet led to 92 

accessible and validated software implementations of summary-likelihood inference. 93 

Indeed, a potentially substantial drawback of this idea is that inferring the likelihood 94 

surface in high dimensions is a complex task. For inference of likelihoods or joint full 95 

posterior distributions, the importance of iterative (or “sequential”) methods of con-96 

struction of the reference table has been repeatedly stressed (e.g. Blum and Fran¸ cois, 97 

2010; Cranmer et al. , 2019; Del Moral et al. , 2006; Lueckmann et al. , 2021; Toni et 98 

al. , 2009). Modern variants employ neural networks for density estimation (Blum 99 

and Fran¸ cois, 2010; Greenberg et al. , 2019; Lueckmann et al. , 2017; Papamakarios 100 

and Murray, 2016; Papamakarios et al. , 2019). However, in practice, non-iterative 101 

ABC methods (e.g., Beaumont et al. , 2002; Blum and Fran¸ cois, 2010; Pudlo et al. ,102 

2016) remain widely used. Such methods can, in principle, enable amortized infer-103 

ence, whereby a single reference table (or even a single trained neural network) can 104 

be used to analyze many datasets. This can yield substantial computational savings 105 

when data are repeatedly generated under the same design (e.g., Zammit-Mangion 106 

et al. , 2025). However, in many real-world applications of the non-iterative ABC 107 

methods described above, datasets differ enough in design or characteristics that 108 

such amortization is not feasible. 109 

ABC methods are employed for the purpose of inferring posterior distributions for 110 

parameters, given a prior distribution for θ. Subsequently, credible intervals can be 111 

derived from the posterior distribution. As an alternative approach, likelihood-ratio 112

The intervals returned by the different methods can be compared in terms of cover-114

for different perspectives on these two concepts (credibility and confidence inter-119

appears that a significant number of ABC methods are unable to effectively con-121

for 95% nominal probability. Additionally, they found that the coverage of other 127 

ABC methods was contingent upon a required rejection threshold, which is challeng-128 

ing to accurately set a priori. Consequently, they also generated anti-conservative 129 

intervals for certain threshold values. As observed by Hermans et al. (2022), the 130 

issue of coverage control has been seldom addressed in earlier machine learning lit-131 

erature. The deep learning-based methods they examined were all found to produce 132 

anti-conservative intervals, indicating a clear need for improvement in the coverage 133 

of intervals in simulation-based inference methods. Consistent with this, coverage 134 

of credibility intervals has received increasing attention in recent work (e.g., Frazier 135 

et al. , 2024), with some studies explicitly distinguishing the coverage properties of 136 

Bayesian credible intervals from those of frequentist confidence intervals (Dalmasso 137 

et al. , 2024). 138 

In this paper, we present an evaluation of the performance of our new summary-139 

likelihood workflow using simulation scenarios of inference of demographic history by 140 

analysis of population genetic data. A toy simulation scenario completes the tests 141 

of the method. Compared with the non-iterative ABC-RF method, our results un-142 

derscore the value of an iterative workflow for improving inference when accurate 143 

exploration of the parameter space is critical. We show that the new workflow allows 144 

more uniform control of the coverage of intervals than previously reported for ABC 145 

methods. The main deviations from ideal control appear due to lack of information 146 

about parameters in the data, a common feature of demo-genetic inferences, and 147 

of other fields of application of simulation-based inference (e.g., Auger-M´ eth´ e et al. ,148 

2021; Daly et al. , 2018; Fan et al. , 2019). We also provide some comparison with the 149 

SNLE workflow implemented in the sbi package (Tejero-Cantero et al. , 2020). This 150

# Material and Methods 157 

The inference workflow 158 

Starting from a reference table built from a limited number of simulations, an ini-159 

tial estimation of the summary-likelihood surface is derived. Then, new parameter 160 

points are sampled with greater probability in regions of high inferred likelihood, 161 

with the objective of more accurately inferring the likelihood surface in such regions. 162 

These sampling and likelihood-surface estimation steps are repeated iteratively to 163 

augment the reference table and to obtain progressively more precise inferences of 164 

the likelihood surface. 165 

In this Section we provide a first description of these steps, introducing terminol-166 

ogy and notation. For clarity, the simulation results included in the reference table 167 

are called “samples” to distinguish them from the “data” to be analyzed, whether the 168 

latter are real data or simulated ones. In the same way, we distinguish the data-169 

generating parameters values (denoted θ†), which are not information used by the 170 

inference workflow, from the sample-generating parameters, which are essential in-171 

formation included in the reference table. Following well-established notation that 172 

we will repeatedly use below, probability densities will be denoted as P, with indices 173 

denoting the random variables whose values are the arguments of the function. In 174 

particular, P Y ;Θ denotes the density of Y values (where these values may represent 175 

the data, or some simulated sample) as function of parameter values Θ, and P Y, Θ176 

denotes the joint density of Y and Θ, under the assumption that Θ is sampled from 177 

some distribution. PY ;Θ(S; θ) is thus the probability (or probability density) of a 178 

sample S for a given parameter vector θ.179 

Inferring the likelihood from a reference table 180 

The likelihood L(θ; D) of θ given observed data D is generally defined, up to a 181 

constant factor, as P Y ;Θ(D; θ), viewed as a function of the parameters for fixed data. 182

From raw statistics to projected statistics 204 

Here, as in most applications of ABC, the information provided to the inference 205 

method is typically a vector of statistics u(D), summarizing higher-dimensional ob-206 

served data D which is the information available to the analyst. For example, the 207 

largest genetic datasets considered in our simulation study involve genetic informa-208 

tion at 10,000 genetic markers from 120 individuals, which is summarized by 130 209 

statistics; but in some applications u(D) may just be a vectorial representation of 210 

the full D. The simulated samples for each drawn θ must then also be expressed as 211 

a vector of summary statistics u(S). As with ABC, it is up to the user to provide 212 

statistics that are informative for a given inference problem. 213

scribing the data or the simulated samples in the reference table (up to 130 summary 215 

statistics in our simulations), is typically reduced by a projection step to a smaller 216 

number of projected summary statistics p[u(D)], in order to reduce the dimension of 217 

the joint distribution, of parameters and of statistics, which is estimated. “Projec-218

Refinements of likelihood surface inference through iterations 292 

In each iteration, new parameter values are drawn, simulations of the process are 293 

performed and added to the reference table, and the above steps of the workflow 294 

are repeated on the incremented reference table. The sampling of parameters should 295 

still permit further exploration to prevent entrapment in the initial high-likelihood 296 

region, which may be disjoint from the final one (as will be illustrated by the re-297 

sults on the 7-parameter human admixture scenario). The implemented sampling 298 

procedure further allows exploration of the parameter space beyond the previously 299 

sampled ranges, and users can specify “absolute” bounds that should not be exceeded 300 

during such exploration. The software also allows users to specify not only ranges 301 

for each parameter, but also arbitrary additional constraints on any combination of 302 

parameters (this is used in some of our simulations). 303 

The rules for drawing ni+1 new parameter vectors after iteration i are detailed in 304 

the Supplementary Information (Section S.1.1). They are defined to preferentially 305 

fill the region of parameters with a given minimum likelihood ratio lmin relative to 306 

the current summary-MLEs. In practice, lmin was set to the 95% threshold for two-307 

dimensional confidence regions. The sampling also allows exploration beyond the 308 

boundaries of this “top” region. The sampling step within the “top” aims to fill it 309 

uniformly rather than in proportion to the likelihood ratio. 310 

The iterative workflow allows the projections to be re-computed. However, random-311 

forest computations on large tables take time, so some elaborations have been im-312 

plemented to shorten them. First, for large reference tables, they use a subset of the 313 

reference table, mostly defined from points identified as belonging to the top of the 314 

likelihood surface (see Supplementary Material for details). Second, projections are 315 

not re-computed when more than 90% of the selected points were already used in the 316 

previous computation of the projections. The easiest way to accelerate inferences in 317 

parameter-rich models without substantially compromising performance may be to 318 

reduce this threshold so that projections are re-computed less often. 319

Control of inference workflow 325 

Our workflow potentially depends on many control parameters, but we set default 326 

values for all of them, which were used for all simulations in this paper, unless 327 

mentioned otherwise. These values, as described here and further detailed in Sup-328 

plementary Section S.1, are automatically selected by our implemented procedures 329 

unless users specifically request different values. 330 

In particular, for fitting np parameters to a given dataset, a final number of 331 

1000(3 np − 1) sample simulations are run. Supplementary Table S.1 illustrates com-332 

putations times for single datasets for different simulation designs, and Supplemen-333 

tary Section S.1.6 provides further details on how the number of samples added at 334 

each iteration is controlled. Smaller references tables appear to lead to degraded per-335 

formance (one example being given in Supplementary Table S.15) and are therefore 336 

not recommended. Larger tables may require substantial increases in computation 337 

times, which may be a small concern when only one dataset is analyzed, but would 338 

be unpractical when inferences are performed on series of hundreds of simulated 339 

datasets. 340 

While the final size of the reference tables was predefined in our simulation study, 341 

alternative and more adaptive stopping rules could be used, based in particular on 342 

two criteria implemented in the R package. The first criterion is the estimated 343 

precision of the log profile likelihood ratio statistics at the bounds of the inferred 344 

confidence intervals. A bootstrap procedure is implemented to evaluate root mean 345 

square errors (RMSEs) of prediction of these log-ratio statistics. It is thus possible 346 

to request termination of iterations when these RMSEs, averaged over the different 347 

interval bounds, or when all such RMSEs, are below a certain threshold. A second 348 

criterion is the comparison of the distribution of samples simulated in two ways: 349 

samples from the simulator of the process being inferred, versus samples from the in-350 

ferred distribution. These distributions can be compared as in a classifier two-sample 351 

test (Lopez-Paz and Oquab, 2017), by training a classifier to assign samples to either 352 

distribution: the better the inferred distribution, the lower the classification perfor-353 

mance. This has previously been used to compare different methods of inference of 354 

posterior distributions (Lueckmann et al. , 2021), but can be used here to compare 355 

the distributions of samples given specific values of the inferred parameters, such as 356 

their MSLEs. 357

To perform SNLE inference, we used the Python package sbi version 0.25.0, with 359 

default controls for training the MAF neural density estimator. In particular, the 360 

flow consisted of five autoregressive transformations, each parameterized by a Masked 361 

Autoencoder for Distribution Estimation (MADE)-type neural network (Germain et 362 

al. , 2015) with hidden layers of size 50 and two blocks. Dropout and batch normal-363 

ization were disabled. The autoregressive networks used ReLU activation functions. 364 

Between successive transforms, random permutations were applied to increase flexi-365 

bility of the flow. 366 

Posterior samples were obtained using the default Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) 367 

sampler implemented in sbi . This corresponds to slice sampling with multiple par-368 

allel chains. The default configuration uses 20 chains, with 100 warmup steps per 369 

chain and thinning factor equal to one. 370 

Final reference tables had the same size as in our default summary-inference work-371 

flow, and we ran ten rounds of the sequential procedure with an equal number of 372 

simulated samples per round (Lueckmann et al. , 2021, Appendix Section A.4). 373

Assessment of confidence and credible intervals 438 

The concept of confidence interval is based on the control of coverage whatever 439 

the data-generating parameter values θ†, rather than only on average over a prior 440 

distribution, so we assess coverage for given θ† values, with only a few exceptions. 441 

For fixed prior distributions, credibility intervals may become asymptotically 442 

equivalent to confidence intervals for “large samples”, i.e., when the information 443 

contained in the parameters increases indefinitely with sample size (Lehmann and 444 

Casella, 1998, Section 6.8). Credibility intervals and confidence intervals may then 445 

be seen as asymptotic approximations to each other. But this asymptotic equivalence 446 

may fail when (i) coverage is evaluated conditionally for a parameter at the bound-447 

ary of the prior distribution and (ii) for some definition of posterior intervals. In 448 

particular, intervals based on central quantiles of the posterior distribution (“central 449 

posterior intervals”) will never cover the θ† value when it is at a boundary. 450 

This boundary effect should not be overlooked: (i) it may be widely ignored by 451 

practitioners (reports of performance focusing on good marginal coverage encour-452 

aging such ignorance), and (ii) it is a real concern in practice insofar as software 453 

(including ABC-RF) often report central posterior intervals, which leads to the ex-454 

clusion of parameter values at range bounds from reported intervals, even when there 455 

is actually not enough information in the data to actually reject such values. For the 456 

following comparisons, we implemented the computation of highest posterior den-457 

sity (HPD) intervals from the ABC-RF output, which are expected to have better 458 

coverage at the boundaries. For unimodal posterior distributions, these are also the 459 

shortest credible intervals. 460

accommodate. Addressing these constraints within the sbi framework would require 716 

at least a non-trivial reparameterization of the parameters. In the remaining scenar-717 

ios, point-estimate performance (bias and RMSE) was broadly similar between the 718 

two methods, in particular when contrasted to ABC-RF. In contrast, interval estima-719 

tion yielded more heterogeneous results (Table 5). In the 15-parameter toy example, 720 

SNLE posterior intervals achieved good coverage, comparable to summary-likelihood, 721 

but were consistently slightly wider (width ratio 1.02–1.16 across parameters; mean 722 

1.07). By contrast, in the 7-parameter admixture scenario, SNLE intervals were 723 

mostly too narrow and exhibited poorly calibrated coverage. Results for the lady-724 

bird invasion scenario were intermediate: coverage was, on average, improved relative 725 

to uncorrected profile likelihood-ratio intervals, but showed greater variability across 726 

parameters (0.912–1). 727 

# Discussion 728 

In this study, we present and assess the performance of an automated workflow for 729 

summary-likelihood, a method of simulation-based inference based on inferring a like-730 

lihood surface for summaries of the data. The efficiency of the method is contingent 731 

upon its iterative procedure of exploration of the parameter space. In a 15-parameter 732 

toy example, the coverage of confidence intervals provided by summary-likelihood is 733 

nearly optimal. However, in cases where some parameters are not practically identi-734 

fiable, the results are inherently more complex. In these cases, the intervals derived 735 

from the likelihood profiles for parameters with low information content are, as ex-736 

pected, too large. Nevertheless, bootstrap procedures appear to be a viable solution 737 

for obtaining better coverage. Our simulations suggest that the “bootLR” bootstrap 738 

intervals may be systematically used to improve the coverage of intervals. These 739 

intervals are defined by the profile-likelihood ratio threshold value determined as the 740 

q-quantile of the bootstrap distribution of the likelihood ratio, where q is the in-741 

tended coverage. The Bartlett-corrected intervals often performed similarly to these 742

Beyond documenting the general performance of the summary-likelihood method, 756 

our simulations provide a comparison with the ABC-RF method. Raynal et al. (2019) 757 

compared their ABC-RF method to some iterative (or “sequential”) ABC workflows 758 

(ABC-PMC, Beaumont et al. , 2009; Prangle, 2017; SMC-ABC, Del Moral et al. ,759

ever, they did not extend their comparisons to their population genetics example. 762 

By contrast, our results highlight the benefits of an iterative workflow for the explo-763 

ration of parameter space, and are thus consistent with the premises of sequential 764 

ABC methods. 765 

There are further differences between the summary-likelihood and ABC-RF meth-766 

ods, beyond the nature of the intervals sought, and whether the workflow is iterative 767 

or not. In particular, ABC-RF is currently constrained to infer marginal posterior 768 

distributions for each parameter separately, which makes it more difficult to identify 769 

sets of parameters that can be estimated only in combination with each other. By 770 

contrast, the likelihood surface inherently retains information about the joint effect 771 

of parameters on the likelihood of the data. It is also possible to extend ABC-RF for 772 

the inference of multivariate parameters, using distributional random forests (Dinh 773 

et al. , 2024). 774 

The comparisons with ABC-RF are based on previously considered simulation 775 

scenarios. Our findings indicate that ABC-RF is capable of producing estimates 776 

with favorable bias and RMSE characteristics, comparable to those of summary-777 

likelihood. However, we also identified instances where its estimation performance 778 

diverged significantly, which can be attributed, at least in part, to the imperfect 779 

exploration of parameter space by non-iterative methods. Further evidence of this 780

In our simulations, the size of the reference table was determined only by the 819 

number of parameters. Our implementation allows alternative and more adaptive 820 

stopping criterion to be used, based on the estimated precision of the profile likelihood 821 

ratios at the bounds of the inferred confidence intervals. A bootstrap procedure is 822 

implemented in our R package to evaluate mean square errors of prediction of these 823 

ratios. It is thus possible to request termination of iterations when the average RMSE 824 

for the different bounds, or when all RMSEs, are below a certain threshold. However, 825 

such a termination condition is necessary rather than sufficient, because likelihood 826 

surfaces may be inferred with high precision but low accuracy, in particular in cases 827 

where large reference tables are built from few iterations, whereas many iterations 828 

would be needed to identify narrow parameter regions with high likelihood. 829 

The fixed reference-table sizes in our simulations were deliberately kept small 830 

enough to allow performance evaluation over hundreds of samples, but larger tables 831 

may be required to achieve accurate inference in many applications. In particular, 832 

the linear scaling with the number of parameters used here may become increasingly 833 

optimistic as dimensionality grows. Further, even with few parameters, much larger 834 

numbers of simulations may be required when the distribution of the data is heavy-835 

tailed, as illustrated by the g-and-k distribution (Frazier et al. , 2024). 836 

Further iterative ABC methods have been developed, notably sequential neural 837 

likelihood estimation (SNLE, Papamakarios et al. , 2019) and related workflows based 838 

on deep-learning methods that learn probability distributions and related functions 839 

(e.g., Sharrock et al. , 2024 and references therein). In our comparison of summary-840 

likelihood with SNLE, we found that the relative performance of the two methods 841 

strongly depended on the simulation scenario. SNLE showed excellent performance 842 

in one case but more variable interval coverage in the other two, particularly in the 843 

7-parameter admixture scenario. As currently implemented, the main limitation of 844 

summary-likelihood is its rapidly increasing runtime with the number of parameters, 845 

whereas SNLE’s iterative likelihood learning becomes comparatively more efficient 846 

as dimensionality grows. This leaves open the possibility that a workflow based on 847 

iterative MAF training to learn the likelihood surface, as in SNLE, could ultimately 848 

deliver consistently better-calibrated intervals at more moderate computational costs. 849 

# Conclusion 850 

In this study, we have implemented and evaluated summary-likelihood inference, an 851 

iterative simulation-based method for approximate likelihood inference. In addition 852 

to documenting the method’s favorable performance, including in regard to confi-853 

dence interval inference, we have conducted comparisons with the ABC-RF method. 854

These comparisons demonstrate that constructing a reference table of simulations 855 

based on pre-specified priors may result in the omission of the most pertinent param-856 

eter regions. In contrast, an iterative method of exploring the parameter space may 857 

prove more effective in identifying these regions. Using summary-likelihood inference, 858 

we found that the full inference for any given dataset required a moderate number of 859 

simulations, of the order of 3000 times the number of estimated parameters, showing 860 

that approximate likelihood inference is a practically achievable objective even when 861 

intensive simulation of genomic datasets is required. 862 

While iterative ABC methods have the potential to outperform non-iterative 863 

simulation-based approaches such as standard rejection ABC or ABC-RF, they are 864 

less commonly used by non-experts. This may be due to factors such as simplicity, ro-865 

bustness, computational requirements and familiarity with non-iterative approaches. 866 

However, as the field advances and tools become more user-friendly, there should be 867 

a shift towards iterative methods, particularly for studies involving high-dimensional 868 

parameter spaces and where computational efficiency is critical. The SNLE iterative 869 

method, which uses MAF training to learn the likelihood surface, already enables 870 

fast and efficient inference, but the intervals it provides do not always appear to 871 

be well-calibrated. Our method can yield better intervals within a reasonable time-872 

frame for models with a moderate number of parameters, but our results leave open 873 

the possibility that improved use of iterative MAF training may also provide better 874 

intervals at even lower computational costs. 875

S.1.1 Iterative sampling of parameter points 14 

The aim of the iterative procedure is to fill a target “top” space defined as the space 15 

of parameters with a given minimum likelihood ratio lmin relative to the summary-16 

MLE. As a first approach, a uniform sampling of the currently predicted top space, 17 

together with sampling out of it with decreasing density for decreasing likelihood, 18 

may seem appropriate to identify iteratively the boundaries of the target space and 19 

to infer the likelihood surface within it. An approximately uniform sampling of the 20 

target space is obtained by a simple rejection sampling method, where parameter 21 

vectors are first sampled from a given proposal distribution qΘ covering the target 22 

space, and the following rejection step preferentially retains sampled points with low 23 

qΘ density, with probability in inverse proportion of the qΘ density relative to its 24 

maximum among the sampled points. The current estimate ˆ ıΘ of the instrumental 25 

density is used as proposal distribution, except as specified below. To explore the 26 

boundaries, distinct rejection probabilities are also computed for points outside the 27

Another problem that was apparent, at least in the early versions of the work-41

boundaries. In the current workflow, performance may be practically insensitive to 44

of outside points sampled from ˆ ıΘ has been implemented so that the proportion of 52 

outside points among retained ones can increase to a given value q. All simulations 53 

used q = 1 /4. The rejection rule for points sampled from the instrumental posterior 54

of the top space. 56

S.1.3 Control of random-forest methods 87 

Default values are defined for control settings of random-forest methods in Infusion 88 

and abcrf , which were used in all simulations reported below. 1000 trees were used 89 

in all cases for both methods. The other default settings differ between the two 90 

methods, as those from Infusion combine features of Breiman’s original random-91 

forest method and of the extremely-randomized trees method (Geurts et al. , 2006). 92 

The latter method does not provide out-of-bag predictions, but the combined options 93 

used as defaults by Infusion do provide such predictions. This combination is made 94 

possible by the ranger R package (Wright and Ziegler, 2017), and the combined 95 

defaults ( splitrule="extratrees" , mtry set to the number of predictor variables, 96 

replace set to FALSE , and sample.fraction set to 0.632) were chosen in light of the 97 

results of an independent simulation study of the effects of the settings on prediction 98 

performance (Appleton et al. , 2022, Appendix), giving some slight advantage to these 99 

combined settings over the basic random-forest method. 100

S.1.7 Simplified bootstrap procedure 195 

We used the parametric bootstrap to simulate the distribution of the summary-196 

likelihood ratio statistic and to deduce from it a corrected p-value. We used it also 197 

to compute bootstrap confidence intervals. In its canonical version, the parametric 198 

bootstrap would be too time-consuming, as it would imply repeating the full inference 199 

workflow on many simulated datasets. However, in the present case, we used two 200 

approximations. First, it is possible to obtain the parametric bootstrap samples 201 

directly as a vector of projected summary statistics, denoted t∗, since the distribution 202 

of such samples, given the parameter estimates as in standard parametric bootstrap, 203 

is a MGM distribution easily deduced from the representation of the joint distribution 204 

stored in the fit object. Second, we did not reconstruct references tables for each of 205 

these bootstrap samples. Instead, inferences are performed on t∗, using the available 206 

estimated joint and marginal distributions to evaluate the terms in the expression 207 

for the summary-likelihood of the bootstrap sample, L(θ; t∗) = P T, Θ(t∗, θ)/PΘ(θ). 208 

Only 199 bootstrap samples were used for each bootstrap computations. It is 209 

advised to use more samples when analyzing a given dataset. However, such a low 210 

number is enough to appreciate the correction brought by the bootstrap procedure, 211

We have used an automatic rule to select θ† values in a relatively simple way that is 217 

both unaffected by our knowledge of the position of θ† within the prior distributions 218

# S.5 Human admixture scenario 372 

S.5.1 Details of simulation scenario 373 

S.5.1.1 The demo-genetic model 374 

The Human admixture scenarios considered in our study are sub-models of a scenario 375 

with 17 variable parameters studied by Raynal et al. (2019) and Collin et al. (2021, 376 

scenario 2) to illustrate the ABC-RF method. This scenario includes (i) an ancient 377 

demographic change in the ancestral African population, (ii) a single out-of-Africa 378 

colonization event giving an ancestral out-of-Africa population which secondarily 379 

split into one European and one East Asian population, and (iii) a recent genetic 380 

admixture of the Americans of African Ancestry in SW-USA between their African 381 

ancestors and individuals of European origin. The original 17-parameter scenario is 382 

detailed in Figure 1 (right panel) of the Main Text. In this highly parameterized 383 

scenario, very few original parameters can be estimated. For this reason, we have 384 

chosen to derive sub-models in which we have fixed the number of variable parameters 385 

to 7 or 13, by fixing 10 or 4 parameters respectively, as detailed below. 386 

Sample simulations for this scenario have been run using diyabc as in Raynal et 387 

al. (2019) and as described for the previous scenario. Notable differences compared to 388 

Raynal et al. (2019) lie in the way by which the event times have been expressed and 389 

in the way the diyabc software is used. In its basic use, the diyabc software draws 390 

values from parameter prior distributions until they satisfy a given set of constraints 391 

on the parameters (such as t1 < t 2 < t 3 < t 4 in our scenario). A non-standard 392 

use of the diyabc simulator (based on the -o option in the online command of the 393 

executable file) is to provide it with a table of parameter values that do not depend 394 

on its rejection sampling and are therefore fully controlled by the user, possibly with 395 

different rules than the rejection sampling described above. Further, one can re-396 

parametrize the model according to differences between split or merge time events 397 

t12 = t2 − t1, t23 = t3 − t2, t34 = t4 − t3, and sample these new parameters only 398 

under the constraint that they are positive. This allows more transparent control of 399 

the distribution of sampled parameter points by the inference workflow. Hence this 400 

reparametrization was used for SL inferences. 401 

In the parametrization of the 13-parameter Human admixture scenario by bot-402 

tleneck parameters, b3 = d3/N bn3 , b4 = d4/N bn4 , and b34 = d34 /N bn34 , their possible 403 

values are subject to further logical constraints with respect to the times of events 404 

in the demographic scenario, which can be represented as follows 405 

d3 = ⌊b3Nbn3 ⌋ ≥ 0, d4 = ⌊b4Nbn4 ⌋ ≥ 0, d34 = ⌊b4Nbn4 ⌋ ≥ 0,d3 ≤ t2, d34 ≤ t23 , d4 ≤ t12 , and t3 ≤ t4. (S.3) The choice of either sampling rules (rejection versus re-parametrization) has dif-406 

ferent implications for the two inference methods: rejection sampling under the con-407 

straint that t1 < t 2 < t 3 < t 4 means that the sampled prior distributions of param-408 

eters are not uniform and have relatively low density near the boundaries defined 409

S.5.2.4 Results with synthetic reference table 470 

The fit of the simulated joint distribution for parameters and summary statistics in 471 

a reference table allows, in principle, the likelihood to be evaluated following eq. 3 472 

for any dataset simulated under the same model as the reference table. Thus, a 473 

single such fit could in principle be used to perform inference for different real or 474 

simulated datasets D. However, in practice, attempts to reuse reference tables built 475 

for other datasets may not be successful, if the likelihood evaluation is precise only 476 

after several iterations where the sampling of new parameter points is driven by the 477 

analyzed dataset. 478 

In order to check whether this occurs, we built a single synthetic reference table 479 

of 20000 samples, by drawing 100 samples randomly from each of the 20000-sample 480 

reference tables (containing raw summary statistics) for each of the first 200 simulated 481 

datasets. This table was used to infer new projections and likelihood surfaces for 482 

the same 200 simulated datasets. Although the biases of resulting estimators are 483 

not necessarily large, the biases and RMSEs are uniformly larger than those of the 484 

original inferences, as can be seen in Table S.13 and Fig. S.5, and coverage is far 485 

from controlled. Thus, the inference workflow is best run essentially independently 486 

for each new dataset. 487

The RMSEs of summary ML estimates is lower or higher than that of ABC-RF 493 

estimates, depending on the parameter considered (Main Text Table 4). As empha-494

data-generating values θ† relative to prior means, so such comparisons of RMSEs are 496 

not sufficient to characterize the relative estimation performance of the two meth-497 

ods. The RMSEs of ABC-RF estimators are also possibly affected by other effects 498 

illustrated in the main Text. Although we did not try to tell apart these different 499 

effects for all parameters, we can look for the distinctive pattern observed in the 500 

previous simulations, and interpreted as resulting for poor exploration of parameter 501 

space by the non-iterative workflow. This pattern, where for a given parameter the 502 

ABC-RF estimates are out of the range defined by the data-generating value and 503 

the prior mean, and have a variance much lower than that of summary-MLEs, is 504 

again observed for log(1 + t23 ), but also for log( t12 ), Nbn3 , log( Nbn4 ), b34 , all with 505 

substantially lower variance than the MSLE [all ratios of variances < 0.28 except 506 

0.43 for log( t12 )]. Each of these ABC-RF estimators is weakly correlated with the 507 

MSLE one for the same parameter [correlation < 0.5 except 0.57 for log( t12 )]. 508 

Although log( Na) appears relatively well estimated by both methods, its ABC-RF 509 

estimator also meets these diagnostic criteria, except that it has higher correlation 510 

with the MSLE (0.75). 511

b4 appear well inferred by both methods. However, the ABC-RF estimator of log( N2)513 

has a suspiciously low variance ratio (0.33, versus between 0.61 and 1.31 for the three 514 

other parameters), suggesting either a strong effect of the prior mean on estimates, 515 

or an artifact of poor exploration of parameter space. The deficiency of low p-values 516

analysis). 519
