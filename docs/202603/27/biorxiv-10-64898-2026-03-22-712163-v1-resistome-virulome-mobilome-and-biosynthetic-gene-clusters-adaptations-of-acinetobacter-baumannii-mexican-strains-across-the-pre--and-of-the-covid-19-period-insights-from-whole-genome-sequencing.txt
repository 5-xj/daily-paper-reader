Abstract 24 

Background: Acinetobacter baumannii is a critical multidrug-resistant pathogen whose 25 genomic landscape in Mexico has been reshaped by the COVID-19 pandemic. While global 26 studies have highlighted distinctive sequence type distributions, systematic analyses in Mexico 27 remain limited. 28 

Methods: We analyzed 194 genomes, including 47 newly sequenced post-COVID isolates 29 (MIQ), alongside 147 publicly available genomes (HPG). Whole-genome sequencing was 30 combined with phylogenetic reconstruction, resistome and virulome profiling based on gene 31 presence and absence, mobilome analysis, and biosynthetic gene cluster (BGC) characterization. 32 

Results: Two major clades dominated by Oxford STs 758, 208, 417, and 369 were 33 identified. Resistome profiling uncovered 128 distinct resistome profiles (combinations of genes) 34 and 44 emerging antimicrobial resistance genes (ARGs), with an increased number of resistance 35 genes in the strains obtained during the pandemic. Virulome analysis revealed enrichment of 36 metabolic adaptation genes ( argG , carA , ilvC ) in MIQ strains. Mobilome profiling demonstrated 37 enrichment of ISAbA1 and ISAbA3 elements, known to mobilize carbapenemase genes. BGC 38 analysis showed conserved siderophores involved in virulence, alongside diversification of the 39 secondary metabolite repertoires in MIQ genomes. Additional observations included geographic 40 mixing of clades across Jalisco, Aguascalientes, and Mexico City and referral bias toward 41 carbapenemase-positive isolates. 42

Conclusion: The genomic landscape of A. baumannii in Mexico has diversified post-43 COVID, with evidence of inter-regional transmission, referral bias, virulome expansion, 44 mobilome-driven ARG dissemination, and metabolic adaptation. These findings underscore the 45 urgent need for coordinated genomic surveillance, functional and clinical validation of 46 adaptation signals, and regionally integrated infection control strategies to mitigate resistance 47 trajectories. 48 

Keywords: antimicrobial resistance AMR), genomic surveillance, healthcare associated 49 infections (HAI), horizontal gene transfer (HGT), whole genome sequencing (WGS) analysis. 50 

Introduction 51 

Widespread empirical antibiotic use in COVID-19 patients has led to a significant increase 52 in secondary infections caused by resistant bacteria (Castillo-Bejarano et al., 2023). Among 53 these, Acinetobacter baumannii coinfections in ICU settings have surged, frequently exhibiting 54 extensive drug resistance (XDR) or pan-resistance, coupled with high virulence potential, 55 presenting geo-economical specificities (Barrios-Camacho et al., 2025). 56 Recently, global studies indicated that in the pre-pandemic period, Mexico presented 57 sequence type (ST) patterns that differ from neighboring countries (Fernández-Vázquez et al., 58 2023). Understanding the genomic traits that underpin these phenotypes is essential for effective 59 infection control and the development of therapeutic strategies. 60 The persistence and pathogenicity of A. baumannii are largely attributed to its remarkable 61 ability to evolve and acquire resistance to multiple drugs through diverse mechanisms, including 62 the production of extended-spectrum β-lactamases (ESBLs), carbapenemases, efflux pumps, and 63 reduced membrane permeability (Gomes Chagas et al., 2024; Manobanda Nata and Jaramillo 64 Ruales, 2023). Beyond the resistome, the virulome encompasses genes responsible for 65

The mobilome, consisting of plasmids, transposons, and integrative conjugative 67 elements, plays a pivotal role in the horizontal gene transfer (HGT) of resistance and virulence 68 determinants (Fernández-Vázquez et al., 2023). Additionally, the specialized metabolome, which 69 includes biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) responsible for specialized metabolites, may 70 influence bacterial fitness, interspecies competition, and interactions with the host and 71 microbiota (Djahanschiri et al., 2022; Djahanschiri et al., 2024). 72 Collectively, these components highlight the complexity of A. baumannii adaptability and 73 underscore the urgent need for comprehensive genomic studies to inform effective 74 countermeasures. 75 In this study, we conducted a comprehensive genomic investigation using whole-genome 76 sequencing (WGS) and in silico analyses of clinical isolates collected from Aguascalientes, 77 Mexico City, and neighboring states in Central Mexico during the COVID-19 pandemic and 78 post-pandemic periods (2020–2023). To provide robust regional and temporal comparisons, we 79 integrated all publicly available genomes characterized across Mexico from the pre-COVID 80 period. This approach provides an integrative and current view of the A. baumannii genomic 81 landscape in Mexico, shedding light on the evolution of resistance patterns, virulence factors, 82 mobile genetic elements, and specialized metabolite profiles over time. Our findings aim to 83 support the development of targeted therapies and antimicrobial stewardship programs tailored to 84 the Mexican context. 85

Selection of strains for sequencing 115

Whole Genome Sequencing 120 

Forty-seven clinical strains from patients with lung disease in Central Mexico were cultured 121 on Columbia agar and confirmed by MALDI Biotyper®. Genomic DNA was extracted using the 122 NEB Monarch® kit according to manufacturer’s instructions. Libraries were prepared with the 123 Illumina MiSeq™ DNA Prep kit v3 and sequenced on the Illumina MiniSeq platform using 124 paired-end 2×150 bp reads. 125 Comparative genomic analyses of Acinetobacter baumannii strains were conducted using a 126 standardized bioinformatics workflow designed to ensure reproducibility and robustness. Raw 127 sequencing reads were first subjected to quality control with FastQC v0.11.9, followed by 128 adapter and low‑quality base trimming using Trimmomatic v0.39. High‑quality reads were 129 assembled de novo with SPAdes v3.9.0, and assemblies were annotated using Prokka v1.14.6 130 and the RAST toolkit implemented in the BV‑BRC v3.30.19 platform. 131

Phylogenetic Analysis 141 

Phylogenetic reconstruction was performed using a combined dataset of newly sequenced 142 MIQ isolates and historic published genomes (HPG) retrieved from GenBank (Supplementary 143 Table 1), thus forming a complete HPG-MIQ dataset used for the following analyses. 144 Core genome alignments were generated using Roary v3.13.0, and phylogenetic trees were 145 reconstructed with IQ-TREE v2.1.2 under the maximum likelihood framework with 1,000 146 bootstrap replicates. Assemblies were uniformly annotated with Prokka v1.14.6, and STs were 147 assigned using the PubMLST database. Metadata (hospital, state, year, Pasteur and Oxford ST 148 schemes) were incorporated into the tree visualization in iTOL v6, allowing contextual 149 interpretation of lineage distribution across clinical and environmental sources. 150

Resistome and Virulome Analysis 159 

For resistome profiling, raw assemblies were screened with the BV‑BRC K‑mer pipeline, 160 complemented with BLAST searches against the Comprehensive Antibiotic Resistance Database 161 (CARD) and the National Database of Antibiotic Resistant Organisms (NDARO). In addition, 162 we applied SraX, a pipeline optimized for CARD‑based annotation. Following annotation, 163 redundant entries were removed and homologous genes were grouped to generate a 164 non‑redundant resistome dataset suitable for comparative analyses. 165 Resistome diversity was quantified using Bray–Curtis dissimilarity indices, which measure 166 compositional differences in gene content between isolates. The resulting distance matrices were 167 visualized through Principal Coordinates Analysis (PCoA) to identify clustering patterns. To 168 further assess group separation, k‑means clustering was applied, and statistical robustness was 169 validated using PERMANOVA (permutational multivariate analysis of variance). 170 

Mobilome Analysis 171 

Mobile genetic elements (MGEs) were systematically characterized for each genome using 172 VRprofile2. For each isolate, we quantified the number and diversity of MGEs. Descriptive 173 statistics were applied to compare mobilome composition between pre‑COVID and post‑COVID 174 isolates, including median counts per genome and prevalence of specific replicon or transposon 175 families. 176 To visualize mobilome dynamics, we employed SankeyMATIC to generate Sankey 177 diagrams illustrating gene flow between chromosomes, plasmids, and MGEs. 178

Results 184 

Phylogenetic Landscape 185 

The HPG set included A. baumannii strains collected between 2011 and 2018 from diverse 186 Mexican hospitals and institutions (e.g., Hospital Civil de Guadalajara, Instituto Nacional de 187 Cancerología, Hospital Infantil de México Federico Gómez, ISSSTE, and Hospital Universitario 188 de Nuevo León), spanning multiple sequence types (ST1, ST2, ST156, ST422, among others). 189 This integration provided temporal and geographic breadth, enabling direct comparison of 190 post-COVID MIQ isolates with pre-COVID epidemic lineages. 191 Our genomic survey revealed two major clades of A. baumannii circulating in Mexico 192 (HPG-MIQ), cutting across geography and time (Figure 1 A and B). The phylogenetic trees show 193 that these dominant lineages are not confined to single states but circulate across Jalisco, 194 Aguascalientes, and Mexico City. 195 Based on the canonical gdhB allele, the most frequent Oxford STs in our dataset (n=47 MIQ 196 strains) were ST208 (24.0%), ST369 (11.3%), ST417 (19.1%), ST758 (26.0%), and ST136 197 (3.4%). Post-COVID isolates introduced rare and previously unreported sequence types, 198 signaling new epidemiological trajectories (Figure 1). Within our MIQ collection, 6.3% could 199 not be typed, and rare types such as STs 1321, 490, 227, 934, 1942, 690 and 225 appeared. 200

Virulence profiling revealed 19 distinct virulome patterns (Figure 3), with six exclusive to 268 MIQ strains, eight exclusive to previously characterized strains, and five shared. Mobilome Composition 285

Specialized Metabolism 322 

Secondary metabolite analysis revealed conserved siderophore biosynthetic clusters across 323 all clades, underscoring iron acquisition as a central survival strategy. Post-COVID isolates also 324 carried novel biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) with antimicrobial potential, suggesting 325 unexplored metabolic capacities that may influence interspecies competition and host 326 interactions. 327 Using antiSMASH 7.0, we identified 1,637 genomic regions linked to specialized metabolite 328 production across 204 genomes, with an average of eight BGCs per genome; strain 329 Ab-s.SEDENA6 carried 11. Our integrative genomic analysis demonstrates that the COVID-19 pandemic reshaped the 341 genomic architecture of multidrug-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii in Mexico. By analyzing 342 194 genomes, including 47 newly sequenced post-pandemic isolates (MIQ), we identified two 343 major clades dominated by Oxford STs 758, 208, 417, and 369. Conclusion 395 

This study provides the first integrative genomic portrait of Acinetobacter baumannii 396 circulating in Mexico across the pre- and post-COVID-19 periods. By analyzing 194 genomes, 397 including 47 newly sequenced isolates, we revealed how resistance determinants, virulence 398 factors, mobile genetic elements, and biosynthetic gene clusters converged to shape novel 399 pathogenic lineages. The dominance of Oxford STs 758, 208, 417, and 369 highlights the 400 persistence of internationally recognized clones, while the emergence of rare sequence types 401 signals ongoing local diversification. 402 Beyond these core findings, several additional observations carry important public health 403 relevance. Phylogenetic analyses demonstrated geographic mixing of clades across Jalisco, 404 Aguascalientes, and Mexico City, suggesting inter-hospital or inter-state transmission routes that 405 demand coordinated regional surveillance rather than isolated hospital-level monitoring. 406 Resistome profiling revealed referral bias toward carbapenemase-positive isolates, underscoring 407 the need to complement hospital-based genomic surveillance with community-level sampling to 408 avoid overestimating resistance prevalence. Virulome analysis showed enrichment of metabolic 409 adaptation genes in MIQ strains, pointing to enhanced resilience that may facilitate persistence in 410 hospital environments and increase outbreak risk. Mobilome profiling identified enrichment of 411 ISAbA1 and ISAbA3 elements in post-COVID isolates, highlighting ongoing plasmid-mediated 412
