Abstract 14

Background: Conventional models of human ribosomal DNA (rDNA) array organization have historically 15

depended on transcription-centric boundaries, partitioning the unit into a ~13 kb rDNA transcription region 16

and a monolithic ~31 kb intergenic spacer (IGS). While our previous identification of Duplication Segment 17

Units (DSUs) mapped these arrays based on an intuitive analysis of the microsatellite density landscape of 18

the complete reference human genome, our present deep mining of this landscape has revealed a more 19

accurate rDNA Gene Unit Pattern. 20

Methods & Results: In this study, we conducted a deep mining analysis of our previously established 21

microsatellite density landscape of the T2T-CHM13 assembly, focusing specifically on nucleolar organizing 22

regions (NORs). We suggest a more accurate rDNA Gene Unit Pattern containing a (CTTT)n microsatellite 23

aggregation ahead of the rDNA gene and a (CT)n microsatellite aggregation behind the gene, rather than a 24

1. Introduction 31

The organization and stability of the human genome are profoundly influenced by the structure and 32

dynamics of ribosomal DNA (rDNA) arrays, which rank among the most transcriptionally active and 33

repetitive loci in the genome. Located in nucleolar organizing regions across the short arms of five 34

acrocentric chromosomes, these loci are composed of hundreds of tandem repeats (Henderson et al., 1972; 35

Sylvester et al., 1986). The active transcription of rRNA genes forms the structural and functional core of the 36

nucleolus, a multifunctional nuclear body central to cell cycle progression, cellular homeostasis, and genome 37

integrity (Pederson, 2011; Grummt, 2013). However, the highly repetitive nature of rDNA, particularly 38

within the intergenic spacer (IGS), has historically posed significant challenges for genome assembly and 39

bioinformatic analysis. This technical barrier resulted in fragmented representations in standard references, 40

hindering comprehensive studies of sequence variation, recombination, and the functional roles of repetitive 41

elements (Treangen & Salzberg, 2012). Consequently, early characterizations of the rDNA repeat unit relied 42

on idealized consensus models or context specific sequences from isolated clones, providing a refined yet 43

fundamentally incomplete picture of the locus. 44

Overcoming these barriers is critical because the IGS is far from a mere structural void. It is a known 45

hotspot for recombination and structural variation, harboring a diverse array of repetitive elements including 46

microsatellites, minisatellites, and transposable elements (Wahls et al., 1990; Stults et al., 2008; Bendich, 47

n.d., 2023). Beyond its role in driving rapid genomic evolution and potential instability (Smirnov et al., 48

2021), the IGS contains essential regulatory sequences, protein binding sites, and noncoding RNAs that 49

actively influence rDNA transcription, nucleolar organization, and cellular stress responses (Vacík et al., 50

2019). Among these diverse components, microsatellites or short tandem repeats have emerged as uniquely 51

potent functional elements. Rather than acting as neutral genomic filler, these sequences directly modulate 52

gene regulation, chromatin folding, and three dimensional architectural organization (Kumar et al., 2013). 53

Furthermore, their highly mutable nature makes them powerful drivers of sequence heterogeneity (Kashi & 54

King, 2006). This functional versatility strongly suggests that the microsatellite distribution within the IGS 55

serves as a critical architectural code necessary for fine tuning rDNA activity and maintaining genomic 56

stability. 57

The completion of the telomere to telomere T2T CHM13 human genome assembly fundamentally 58

transformed this research landscape by providing the first contiguous, gapless reference for these highly 59

complex regions (Antonarakis, 2022). This milestone enabled the precise mapping of the microsatellite 60

landscape at single nucleotide resolution. Yet, despite this comprehensive reference, much of the subsequent 61

research has remained conceptually fragmented, frequently isolating specific sequence elements or localized 62

regions rather than adopting a whole genome perspective. Because genomic regulation operates through 63

continuous spatial networks, adopting a comprehensive structural perspective is essential to systematically 64

compare repeat characteristics and distinguish functionally conserved motifs from mere accumulative 65

variations across diverse genomic contexts. 66

Applying this global perspective, our previous mapping of the whole genome microsatellite distribution 67

uncovered a striking architectural feature: pronounced palisading clusters of CT repeats specifically 68

localized to the short arms of all five acrocentric chromosomes (Xia et al., 2024). This genomic pattern 69

indicates that despite their inherent length variability, the sheer accumulation of these repeats may serve a 70

profound structural purpose. Rather than relying on a precise sequence length, the clustering itself could 71

facilitate the formation of specific higher-order configurations, such as distinct double-stranded structures. 72

Consequently, these formations can act as physical boundaries that partition functional genomic domains to 73

regulate regional gene expression. However, while our initial mapping successfully identified this 74

genome-wide microsatellite accumulation, the precise internal topology of the rDNA arrays and the exact 75

spatial relationship between the rRNA coding sequences and their surrounding IGS regulatory domains 76

required a more targeted investigation. 77

In this study, we employ an in silico approach to comprehensively delineate the micro-architecture of 78

microsatellite clustering specifically within the rDNA arrays of the T2T-CHM13 assembly. By quantitatively 79

mapping the distribution of these motifs across all rDNA repeat units, we identified two distinct types of 80

microsatellite clustering peaks that stably and preferentially localize within the intergenic spacer (IGS). 81

Rather than randomly accumulating, these specific aggregations display a strict spatial orientation relative to 82

the coding regions. The precise localization of these distinct microsatellite clusters challenges the traditional 83

view of the IGS as a monolithic spacer, suggesting they potentially serve as critical architectural boundaries 84

2. Materials and methods 88

2.1. Deep mining the microsatellite density landscape 89

We performed a deep mining of the microsatellite density landscape based on our previous work (Xia et al., 90

2024).This analysis was conducted across all chromosomes, with a particular focus on the Nucleolar 91

Organizing Regions (NORs). 92

2.2. Genome sequences 93

The complete set of 24 chromosome sequences from the human telomere-to-telomere (T2T) reference 94

genome CHM13v2.0 was obtained from GenBank. NORs coordinates were defined using the CenSat 95

annotation track available through the UCSC Genome Browse. 96

2.3. Detection of a Conserved (CTTT)n-Rich Regulatory Head at the Physical Start of rDNA Arrays 97

To delineate the true physical origin of human ribosomal DNA (rDNA) repeating units, we first defined the 98

boundaries of NORs on the five acrocentric chromosomes (13, 14, 15, 21, and 22) using the CenSat 99

annotation track from the UCSC Genome Browser. For each chromosome, we extracted a reference window 00

extending from the annotated NOR start coordinate to the transcription start site (TSS) of the first 45S rDNA 01

gene and further expanded it by 10 kb upstream, yielding a ~14 kb region that fully encompasses the 02

transition between pericentromeric heterochromatin and the rDNA array. 03

This reference window served as the search space for identifying the conserved “regulatory head.” For each 04

of the 219 rDNA repeat units, we extracted the terminal ~5 kb of its intergenic spacer (IGS)—corresponding 05

to the segment immediately upstream of the 45S coding sequence—and aligned it against the 06

chromosome-matched reference window using Clustal Omega (v1.2.4) with default parameters for 07

nucleotide sequences. The subregion within the reference window showing the highest local sequence 08

similarity to the IGS terminus was designated as the candidate regulatory head for that unit. 09

To assess the structural features of these candidates, we integrated the alignment results with the 10

microsatellite density landscape. We examined whether each candidate overlapped a high- or 11

medium-density microsatellite peak of the (CTTT)n motif [H/MMDP-(CTTT)n], a common feature of 12

2.4. Characterization of NOR architecture using microsatellite density landscapes and sequence 20

alignment 21

Following the identification of the (CTTT)n-rich regulatory head at the physical start of each rDNA array, 22

we systematically delineated the full structural architecture of NORs in the T2T-CHM13 genome. 23

Using the microsatellite density landscape maps generated in our prior study (Xia et al., 2024) as a structural 24

scaffold, we annotated all rDNA-containing segments on chromosomes 13, 14, 15, 21, and 22. 25

For each of the 219 validated rDNA genomic units (rDNA-GUs), we defined three consecutive structural 26

domains: 27

(i) an upstream (CTTT)n microsatellite density peak region (MDP-R), corresponding to the conserved 28

regulatory head identified in Section 2.3; 29

(ii) the core rDNA transcriptional unit, encompassing the 45S pre-rRNA coding sequence (including 18S, 30

5.8S, and 28S rRNA genes along with internal transcribed spacers); 31

(iii) a downstream (CT)n MDP cluster region (MDP-CR), characterized by a dense array of (CT)n 32

microsatellite peaks. 33

To assess structural conservation across the array, we performed multiple sequence alignments of all 34

rDNA-GUs using Clustal Omega (v1.2.4) with default parameters for nucleotide sequences. 35

high-density microsatellite peaks (HMDPs) within non-coding regions, 89.25% in intergenic spacers (IGS) 86

demonstrate that the IGS is not a uniform spacer but comprises distinct upstream and downstream regulatory 98

domains, necessitating a revision of the DSU boundary to reflect the true physical organization of the rDNA 99

3.2. Revising the rDNA Unit Model: Beyond Transcription-Centric Boundaries 38

This discovery underscores the fundamental limitations of prevailing rDNA structural models. Historically, 39

the definition of the rDNA unit was constrained by fragmented, cloned sequences, engendering a 40

"transcription-centric" perspective. This paradigm rested on a coarse binary distinction between functional 41

coding domains and inert non-coding regions, thereby obscuring the structural complexity of the intergenic 42

intervals, particularly the architectural significance of repetitive elements. Consequently, this approach 43

erroneously designated the Transcription Start Site (TSS) as the unit’s physical origin. While the 44

T2T-CHM13 genome now enables the delineation of NOR boundaries via sequence similarity (Nurk et al., 45

2022), it lacks the requisite resolution to decode the internal structural logic. 46

Our microsatellite density landscape provides this missing resolution. Our previous DSU model, though an 47

improvement, focused primarily on internal repeat patterns and failed to account for the boundary signals 48

that define the true origin of the unit. The current findings allow us to refine this concept: 49

1.Reconceptualization of the IGS: The conventional IGS should no longer be viewed as a monolithic 50

"spacer." Instead, it is partitioned into two distinct subdomains: one located downstream of the gene cluster 51

and another positioned upstream. 52

2.Structural Revision: The traditional "gene-spacer" dichotomy oversimplifies the rDNA array as a series of 53

functional genes separated by non-functional gaps. Our model supports a more nuanced architecture where 54

the (CTTT)n-rich "head" acts as a conserved upstream regulatory reservoir. 55

By integrating these boundary markers with internal MDP patterns, we provide a structural foundation that 56

more accurately reflects the physical and regulatory reality of the NOR. This revised model suggests that the 57

coordinated transcription of rDNA is governed by a bidirectional regulatory logic, with the "head" segment 58

playing a potentially crucial role in organizing the chromatin landscape of the array. 59

3.3. The rDNA Gene Unit (rDNA-GU): A Tripartite Structural Model 60

To identify the most stable structural components within this new framework, we generated a consensus 61

pattern across all 219 rDNA-GUs in the T2T-CHM13 genome (Figure 2). We filtered for peaks present in at 62

least 10% of the rDNA-GUs to ensure robustness against microsatellite instability. This systematic analysis 63

revealed a highly conserved, tripartite architecture underlying the ribosomal array. 64

Based on this distinct landscape, we propose the rDNA Gene Unit (rDNA-GU) model to more accurately 65

capture the complete physical and functional architecture of the repeating unit. Unlike the traditional 66

gene-spacer dichotomy, the rDNA-GU is defined by its structural landmarks and comprises three 67

consecutive domains: 68

1．The Upstream (CTTT)n MDPs-R: Initiated by a H/MMDP−(CTTT)n peak, this region spans an average 69

of 3,864 bp. It exhibits remarkable sequence conservation across the array (mean identity = 0.962) and 70

serves as the structural "head" of the unit. 71

The H/MMDP-(CTTT)n peak at the start of unit serves as the second most consistent structural landmark in 72

our study, appearing in 97.72% of all units (Table 1). Following this anchor, the region encompasses several 73

additional peaks that correspond to the known enhancer repeat elements. While these intermediate peaks 74

exhibit greater positional variability than the initial (CTTT)n landmark, they collectively maintain a coherent 75

compositional gradient. Notably, as the sequence approaches the 18S gene boundary, we observe a 76

characteristic shift in nucleotide composition: a gradual decrease in Thymine accompanied by an increase in 77

Cytosine content. This gradient culminates in a conserved M/LMDP-(CCCT)n peak (present in 66.67% of 78

units), located just upstream of the transcription start site (TSS), marking the precise transition into the 79

gene-coding region. 80

2．The Core Transcription Region: Comprising the 18S, 5.8S, and 28S rRNA gene clusters. 81

As anticipated, the gene-coding regions showed a scarcity of repeat enrichment. Merely four M/LMDPs 82

3.4. Genomic Specificity and Architectural Significance of (CT)n and (CTTT)n 25

To determine whether the predominance of (CT)n peak clusters and (CTTT)n solitary peaks is a feature 26

specific to the NOR or a widespread genomic one, we mapped the global distribution of microsatellite 27

density peaks (MDPs) across the entire T2T-CHM13 genome. This analysis revealed a distinct, 28

chromosome-specific landscape (Figure 3A, Supplementary Figure4). Generally, centromeric regions across 29

all chromosomes are depleted of microsatellite peaks, likely due to the dominance of long-range repeats 30

such as α-satellite (Altemose et al., 2022). On the five acrocentric chromosomes, however, the short arms 31

exhibit a unique, tripartite zonal architecture (Figure 3B). The distal region (closest to the telomere) is 32

characterized predominantly by Low-Density Peaks (LMDPs). This transitions into the central NOR region, 33

which is defined by a highly regular, “periodic” arrangement of High-Density Peaks (HMDPs). Following 34

the NOR, the proximal region (approaching the centromere) initially displays a segment of irregular mixed 35

density peaks before tapering off into a final low-density zone similar to the distal end. 36

Within this structured landscape, (CT)n and (CTTT)n motifs emerge as the specific drivers of the stable, 37

high-density clustering observed exclusively in the NORs. The quantitative disparity between the NORs and 38

the rest of the genome is striking (Supplementary Table 2). Globally, we identified 905 (CT)n HMDPs, of 39

which an astounding 87.1% are sequestered within the NORs. Similarly, 54.9% of the 215 global (CTTT)n 40

HMDPs reside within these regions. When normalized by sequence length, the spatial density of (CT)n and 41

(CTTT)n HMDPs in the NORs is 2,093-fold and 378-fold higher, respectively, than in the non-NOR genome. 42

This overwhelming concentration confirms that these motifs are not merely repetitive noise but are 43

fundamental architectural components specific to the nucleolus organizer regions. 44

The exclusivity of these motifs to the rDNA array is further underscored by the identification of extensive 45

genomic intervals devoid of such signals. We cataloged Common Zero (CT/CTTT)n Microsatellite Density 46

4. Discussion 87

4.1. The Upstream (CTTT)n-Rich "Head" Operates as an Extended Cis-Regulatory Region 88

A central conclusion of our rDNA-GU model is the reclassification of the ~4,000 bp (CTTT)n-rich segment, 89

which has been traditionally viewed as the distal tail of the Intergenic Spacer (IGS), into the physical and 90

functional "head" of the rDNA repeat unit. This architectural realignment resolves a long-standing spatial 91

disconnect in rDNA biology: the presence of critical, interacting regulatory elements scattered across what 92

was previously considered an unstructured spacer. By defining the start of the unit at the (CTTT)n MMDP-R, 93

the rDNA-GU structurally encapsulates the complete network of cis-regulatory elements required for rRNA 94

transcription into a single, logical upstream domain. 95

Extensive functional evidence supports the classification of this upstream region as a highly active 96

regulatory hub. Previous studies have identified a spacer promoter located ~2 kb upstream of the core 97

promoter. Transcripts originating from this site, known as promoter RNA (pRNA), are essential for 98

recruiting the Nucleolar Remodeling Complex (NoRC) to establish the repressive chromatin states that 99

enhancer repeats situated between this spacer promoter and the core promoter actively drive transcription 01

through long-range chromatin interactions. (De Winter & Moss, 1986; Tower et al., 1989; Kuhn et al., 1990; 02

Paalman et al., 1995; Caudy & Pikaard, 2002). Although this classic model of repeat-driven enhancement is 03

primarily derived from model organisms like Xenopus and mouse, human rDNA achieves analogous 04

transcriptional enhancement through a distinct topological mechanism. Specifically, a05

CTCF/cohesin-mediated "enhancer boundary complex" localizes near the human spacer promoter (Herdman 06

et al., 2017; Mars et al., 2018). Rather than relying on repetitive sequences, this complex drives localized 3D 07

chromatin looping, physically bridging the upstream regulatory elements with the core promoter to actively 08

stimulate transcription. 09

Building upon this topological framework, our microsatellite landscape analysis reveals a critical new 10

dimension to the human rDNA architecture. Strikingly, the most conserved (CTTT)n accumulation peak is 11

not located at the ~2 kb spacer promoter, but rather at the very beginning of the (CTTT)n MMDP-R, 12

approximately 4 kb upstream of the TSS. This prominent 4 kb peak likely serves as the master structural 13

boundary or "insulator-promoter anchor" of the cis-regulatory domain. We therefore propose a hierarchical 14

control model: the 4 kb (CTTT)n peak acts as a rigid upstream boundary that protects the unit's regulatory 15

environment from bleeding into the preceding sequence, while the 2 kb region and its localized (CTTT)n 16

tandem clusters act as the proximal "engine" for orchestrating both activation and silencing. 17

Thus, the (CTTT)n MMDP-R does not merely act as a structural bookend; it serves as the foundational 18

anchor for a comprehensive regulatory cascade. By starting the unit here, our model correctly positions the 19

distal enhancers, the spacer promoter, and the tandem repeats as a contiguous upstream regulatory apparatus 20

that governs the activation and silencing of the adjacent ribosomal genes. The heavy concentration of 21

(CTTT)n repeats in this region likely plays a role in facilitating the unique chromatin architecture or 22

4.2. The Downstream (CT)n MDP-CR Functions as a Structural Boundary and Genomic Insulator 25

While the upstream (CTTT)n MDP-R serves as the regulatory command center for the rDNA-GU, our 26

model designates the downstream (CT)n MDP-CR as its essential structural bookend. It is important to note 27

that this distal boundary is not a monolithic block of simple repeats; rather, it is a massive domain spanning 28

approximately 27,892 bp. Its defining architecture is characterized by three prominent, high-density (CT)n 29

accumulation clusters localized in the middle of the segment, interspersed with less stable microsatellite 30

peaks. Defining the unit's terminus precisely at this clustered (CT)n landscape clarifies the biophysical 31

mechanics required to maintain stability in a highly transcribed tandem array. 32

Genomic regions containing concentrated (CT)n repeats—often referred to as polypyrimidine/polypurine 33

(Y/R) tracts—are well documented for their ability to adopt non-B DNA conformations, such as 34

intramolecular triplexes (H-DNA). Foundational biophysical studies have demonstrated that long 35

polypyrimidine tracts readily transition into rigid triplex DNA structures when subjected to high negative 36

supercoiling, even at a neutral pH and physiological ionic strength in the presence of Mg²   (Sakamoto et al., 37

1996). 38

Beyond triplex formation, these downstream sequence landscapes are heavily implicated in the formation of 39

scheduled, regulatory R-loops (complexes comprising a DNA:RNA hybrid and displaced single-stranded 40

DNA) (Smirnov et al., 2021). Recent single-molecule footprinting has revealed that R-loops are 41

non-randomly distributed across human rDNA and play a critical role in nucleolar organization. Strikingly, 42

recent data indicate that antisense transcription by RNA polymerase II generates a protective R-loop shield 43

within the intergenic regions. This shield physically protects nucleolar organization by suppressing 44

disruptive, unscheduled non-coding RNAs transcribed by RNA polymerase I. 45

Therefore, the presence of these three massive (CT)n clusters at the distal boundary of the rDNA-GU is 46

highly functional. In the context of the nucleolus, the (CT)n MDP-CR likely operates as an inducible, 47

multi-layered biophysical barrier. By facilitating the formation of both supercoiling-induced triplexes and 48

regulatory "R-loop shields," these clusters provide the exact structural roadblocks needed to stall replication 49

forks, prevent transcriptional read-through from the preceding core, and physically insulate adjacent rDNA 50

units. By resolving the former intergenic spacer into distinct regulatory (upstream) and structural 51

(downstream) compartments, the rDNA-GU model accurately reflects the biophysical realities of nucleolar 52

organization. 53

4.3. Distinct Biophysical Signatures of Microsatellites Drive 3D Regulatory Architecture 54

The distinct spatial distributions of (CT)n and (CTTT)n tracts within the human rDNA intergenic spacer 55

strongly suggest that their regulatory roles are fundamentally rooted in their divergent biophysical properties. 56

While both are pyrimidine-rich, it is crucial to distinguish the thermodynamic and mechanical differences 57

driven by their varying GC contents. Standard dinucleotide (CT)n arrays maintain a 50% GC content and are 58

well-characterized for their structural rigidity and propensity to adopt non-B DNA conformations, such as 59

H-DNA (intramolecular triplexes), under physiological supercoiling. In stark contrast, the tetranucleotide 60

(CTTT)n motif possesses a significantly lower GC content of 25%. This substantial reduction in G-C base 61

pairing fundamentally alters the double helix's mechanical properties. The T-rich nature of the (CTTT)n tract 62

lowers the local melting temperature, thereby increasing DNA "breathability" (the propensity for localized 63

unwinding) and imparting a distinct intrinsic helical flexibility and curvature. 64

We hypothesize that these precise topological features, dictated by the specific GC-to-AT ratio, act as a 65

structural fingerprint for the master boundary. Rather than functioning merely as sequence-specific binding 66

sites, the localized DNA curvature and breathability provided by the prominent 4 kb (CTTT)n peak likely 67

furnish the exact physical topology required to exclude nucleosomes or efficiently anchor architectural 68
