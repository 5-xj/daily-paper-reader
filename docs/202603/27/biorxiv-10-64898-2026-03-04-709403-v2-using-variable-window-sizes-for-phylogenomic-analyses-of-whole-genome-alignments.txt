ABSTRACT 11 

Many phylogenomic studies used non-overlapping windows to address gene tree discordance 12 

across a set of aligned genomes. Recently, Ivan et al. (2025) proposed an information 13 

theoretic approach to choose an optimal window size given the alignment. However, this 14 

approach selects only a single fixed window size per chromosome, which is a useful first step 15 

but fails to account for variation in the size of non-recombining regions along each 16 

chromosome. Such variation is expected to occur due to the stochastic nature of 17 

recombination as well as the variation in recombination rates along chromosomes. In this 18 

study, we extend the approach of Ivan et al. (2025) to allow window sizes to vary across the 19 

chromosome, using a splitting-and-merging strategy that allows for each window to be of an 20 

arbitrary length. We showed that the new method outperformed the fixed-window approach 21 

in recovering gene tree topologies on a wide range of simulated datasets. Applying the new 22 

method on the genomes of seven Heliconius butterflies, we found that the average window 23 

sizes for the group ranged between 538-808bp, but with a very similar distribution of gene 24 

tree topologies compared to previous studies that used fixed window sizes. For the genomes 25

INTRODUCTION 34 

Many phylogenomic studies of closely-related taxa begin by aligning the genomes of species 35 

or individuals, and then use those alignments to infer evolutionary events such as speciation, 36 

introgression, and demographic changes (e.g., Pease et al. 2016; Morales-Briones et al. 2021; 37 

Lescroart et al. 2023; Herrig et al. 2024). As different sites in the alignments may reflect 38 

different evolutionary histories (Maddison 1997; Degnan and Rosenberg 2009; Mallet et al. 39 

2016; Hibbins et al. 2023; Steenwyk et al. 2023), methods that account for this variation 40 

become important. For example, Thawornwattana et al. (2018, 2022) extracted 100-locus 41 

blocks (where a locus was defined as a coding or non-coding region with a minimum length 42 

of 100bp and spaced at least 2kb apart) and inferred the species tree for each block using BPP 43 

(Flouri et al. 2018). This approach is useful for identifying introgressed regions, but it 44 

excludes most parts of the genomes. To infer the complete histories of whole genome 45 

alignments – which is important for analyses such as PhyloGWAS (Pease et al. 2016) – 46 

methods that use Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) and Ancestral Recombination Graphs 47 

(ARGs) are powerful for inferring site-specific histories by considering the similarities 48 

between neighbouring sites, potentially under a population genetic model of evolutionary 49 

change. However, these approaches have their own limitations, including accuracy and 50

to partition the genome alignment into non-overlapping blocks (or windows) and infer a 52 

single gene tree for each window. This approach is commonly-used in phylogenomic studies 53 

to assess various evolutionary processes, such as incomplete lineage sorting (ILS) and 54 

introgression (e.g., Fontaine et al. 2015; Pease et al. 2016; Edelman et al. 2019; Meleshko et 55 

al. 2021; Yang et al. 2021; Feng et al. 2023; Lescroart et al. 2023; Herrig et al. 2024). 56 

However, one of the main challenges for this approach is to select a window size that best 57 

reflects the recombination breakpoints of given alignment, with a trade-off between 58 

concatenation preferring the primary tree topology (when the window size is too long, thus 59 

recombination is ignored) and gene tree estimation error (when the window size is too short, 60 

thus limiting the information available to estimate a tree for each window). Most previous 61 

studies have selected a single window size somewhat arbitrarily, in an attempt to balance 62 

these tradeoffs. However, the selection of window size can have significant effects on 63 

downstream inferences. For example, Ivan et al. (2025) showed that in the genomes of great 64 

apes (i.e., human, chimpanzee, gorilla, orangutan), only 50% of gene trees grouped human 65 

and chimpanzee together when using 250bp non-overlapping windows across all 66 

chromosomes. However, this proportion increased to almost 100% when the window size 67 

was 64kb. This highlights the importance of carefully selecting a window size in such 68 

analyses. 69 

70 

Recently, Ivan et al. (2025) proposed that the AIC (Akaike Information Criteraion; Akaike 71 

1974) – which reflects how the substitution models and the trees fit the observed alignment 72 

data – could be used to select a window size in phylogenomic non-overlapping window 73 

analyses. They showed that the AIC was a good predictor of the accuracy of different 74 

window sizes in recovering the true histories of individual sites along simulated 75

chimpanzee, and gorilla to be ~109bp, but see also Edwards et al. (2016)). While the AIC 84 

provides a less arbitrary way to choose a window size for non-overlapping window analysis, 85 

the method proposed by Ivan et al. (2025) has two key limitations: it was evaluated using 86 

simulated chromosomes with constant recombination rates, and it selects a single fixed 87 

window size for each chromosome. A single fixed window size is problematic as some non-88 

recombining blocks will be much longer than others even under constant recombination rate, 89 

due to the stochastic nature of recombination. And since recombination rates vary along the 90 

chromosomes for a variety of reasons (Jensen-Seaman et al. 2004; McVean et al. 2004; Chan 91 

et al. 2012; Edelman et al. 2019), the best window size may also vary systematically along 92 

chromosomes. 93 

94 

In this study, we extend the method proposed in Ivan et al. (2025) to allowing the window 95 

size to vary along chromosomes via a splitting-and-merging approach (Fig. 1). We do this by 96 

first partitioning the alignment into non-overlapping windows of a fixed size. Then, we split 97 

each window into two sub-windows and build gene trees for both the initial window and the 98 

two sub-windows. If the total AIC of the two sub-windows is better than the AIC of the initial 99 

window, we keep the split; otherwise, we retain the initial window. Once no further AIC 100

instead of a genetic algorithm to locate the putative genomic breakpoints, which allows our 113 

method to scale to whole genome alignments in reasonable amount of time. 114

Overview 141 

Our method works by first partitioning the genome alignment into non-overlapping windows 142 

of a fixed size. We then iteratively refine this partitioning by either splitting each window 143 

into two smaller sub-windows or merging two neighbouring windows together, keeping the 144 

split and/or merge only if it improves the AIC of the gene trees. This approach generates a 145 

partitioning scheme in which the window size can be longer in some regions due to merging, 146 

and shorter in other regions due to splitting. In order to evaluate if using variable window 147 

sizes improves the accuracy of non-overlapping windows to recover the true histories from 148 

the alignment (as compared to using only one fixed window size), we ran our splitting-and-149

Simulating Chromosomes with Varying Recombination Rates 157 

We designed seven scenarios to simulate 10Mb alignments: four with homogeneous (i.e., 158 

constant) recombination rates and three with heterogeneous recombination rates (Fig. 2). We 159 

used ms (Hudson 2002) to simulate the gene trees and AliSim (Ly-Trong et al. 2022) to 160 

simulate the alignments in every scenario. The four scenarios with homogeneous 161 

recombination rates (Fig. 2) were taken directly from Ivan et al. (2025). The three scenarios 162 

with heterogenous recombination rates comprise: (i) Scenario 1 (Fig. 2), a simple artificial 163 

scenario in which the first half of the 10Mb chromosome was simulated with no 164 

recombination, and the second half was simulated with an intermediate recombination rate of 165 

𝜌 =100; (ii) Scenario 2 (Fig. 2) crudely mimics a pattern of recombination often seen in 166 

empirical datasets where the recombination rates are higher towards the ends of the 167 

chromosomes and lower towards the center, in which 2.5Mb at each end of the chromosome 168 

was simulated with 𝜌 =50, and the central 5Mb region was simulated with 𝜌 =0; (iii) Scenario 169 

3 (Fig. 2) mimics the same scenario, but with a 1Mb region at each end of the chromosome 170 

was simulated with 𝜌 =200, the next 2.5Mb region at each end simulated with 𝜌 =50, and the 171 

central 3Mb of the chromosome simulated with 𝜌 =0. 172

Running Non-overlapping Window Analyses with Fixed Window Sizes 192 

We ran non-overlapping window analysis on each simulated chromosome with 16 different 193 

fixed window sizes, ranging from 100bp to 10Mb. Then, we built the gene tree for each 194 

window using IQ-TREE v2.4.0 (Minh et al. 2020) with the following command: iqtree2 -195 

S alndir -m JC -blmin 1/window_size , where -S alndir refers to the directory 196 

storing all window alignments, -m JC sets the substitution model to be JC (Jukes and 197 

Cantor 1969), and -blmin 1/window_size sets the minimum branch length for all trees 198 

to represent at least one substitution per branch, penalising gene trees that were inferred from 199 

windows with limited phylogenetic signal (Ivan et al. 2025). 200 

201 

For each simulated chromosome, we then calculated the accuracy of each window size using 202 

site accuracy (i.e., the proportion of sites from non-overlapping window analysis that 203 

correctly recover the true simulated topology) and the RMSE of the gene tree distribution 204 

from the non-overlapping window analysis compared to the simulated truth (Ivan et al. 2025). 205 

As Ivan et al. (2025) proposed that the AIC is a good predictor of both accuracy metrics, we 206 

also retrieved the AIC of each window size from each simulation and calculated the loss of 207 

accuracy if we always chose the window size with the lowest AIC score (compared to the 208 

window sizes with the highest site accuracy and/or lowest RMSE). To avoid unnecessary 209 

computation, we retrieved the results for the 40 simulated chromosomes with homogenous 210 

recombination rates from Ivan et al. (2025) and did not redo these analyses. 211

Once a starting window size has been selected, the splitting-and-merging approach proceeds 223 

as follows for each simulated chromosome. Every initial window is divided into two sub-224 

windows using three size ratios: 0.25:0.75, 0.50:0.50, and 0.75:0.25 (e.g., if the initial 225 

window size is 1kb and the ratio is 0.25:0.75, then the first sub-window would be 250bp 226 

while the second sub-window would be 750bp). If the initial window was not divisible by the 227 

given ratio, the length of the first sub-window was rounded up (e.g., if the initial window size 228 

is 250bp and the ratio is 0.25:0.75, then the first sub-window would be 63bp while the second 229 

sub-window would be 187bp). Next, we inferred the gene trees for the original window and 230 

each of the three split ratios using IQ-TREE2 with the following command: iqtree2 -s 231 

file_fasta -blmin 1/window_size , where -s specifies the alignment file and -232 

blmin sets the minimum branch length based on the window size. We also set the minimum 233 

number of parsimony informative sites per window to be equal to the total number of 234 

branches on the (unrooted) tree, ensuring, in principle, that each branch is supported by at 235 

least one informative site. If either of the sub-windows from a split could not be analysed 236 

(e.g., due to missing data or lack of parsimony informative sites), we skipped that splitting 237 

configuration. We then compared the AIC scores of the resulting four partitioning schemes 238 

(i.e., the original unsplit window and up to three split configurations) for each window and 239 

kept the scheme with the lowest AIC for the next iteration. After applying this approach to 240

Following this, we used the AIC to guide a procedure in which neighbouring windows are 244 

iteratively merged. For each pair of neighbouring windows in the chromosome, we first 245 

calculated the change in AIC that would result from merging them into a single window. To 246 

do this, we concatenated their alignments using SeqKit v2.4.0 (Shen et al. 2016) and inferred 247 

the gene tree of the newly-merged window using IQ-TREE2 with the same parameters as 248 

above. We then extracted the AIC of the merged window and compared it with the total AIC 249 

of the two original (unmerged) windows. This procedure provided a list of neighbouring 250 

windows whose merging would improve the AIC score. However, not all these merging 251 

events were necessarily mutually compatible, because the AIC could be improved by 252 

merging the same window with the neighbouring window to the left and to the right of it. To 253 

resolve these conflicts, we used a greedy algorithm that prioritises merges with the biggest 254 

AIC improvements. The algorithm proceeded by merging the pair of windows with the 255 

largest AIC improvement, then removing the pair and all conflicting merges from the list of 256 

merges that could improve the AIC score. This procedure was iterated until all possible 257 

merges that improved the AIC score were exhausted. We then iteratively repeated the entire 258 

merging step until we could find no pair of neighbouring windows whose merging would 259 

improve the AIC score. 260

Erato-sara clade of Heliconius butterflies 270 

We downloaded the genome alignments from six species of erato-sara clade of Heliconius 271 

butterflies (with H. melpomene outgroup) from Edelman et al. (2019) following the steps 272

accuracy (see Results). Thus, we ran the splitting-and-merging approach on each of the 21 276

window size. 278

RESULTS 298

Variable window sizes outperform fixed window sizes on simulated datasets 300 

For simulated chromosomes with zero recombination rate, non-overlapping window analyses 301 

using either fixed window sizes (from Ivan et al. 2025) or variable window sizes (from the 302 

splitting-and-merging approach proposed in this study) always prefer full concatenation, as 303 

expected (Fig. 3-4). However, as variable window sizes consistently outperform fixed 304 

window sizes in any other scenario (see below), we recommend to always use variable 305 

window sizes when running non-overlapping window approach. For simulations with non-306

accuracy of non-overlapping window method by on average 3.0-4.7% across scenarios 308

average 0.05-0.35% across scenarios (except for 𝜌 = 2000 with on average 0.14% RMSE 310 

increase; Fig. 4). For simulated chromosomes with heterogeneous recombination rates, the 311 

variable window size approach also outperforms the fixed window size method. Using 312

DISCUSSION 450 

The non-overlapping window method is a common approach to infer gene trees across a set 451 

of aligned chromosomes. While the selection of window size is often arbitrary, a recent study 452 

by Ivan et al. (2025) showed that the total AIC score of the gene trees is a good indicator of 453 

the ability of different window sizes to recover the ‘true’ topology of each site in the aligned 454 

chromosomes. In this study, we extended this approach by allowing the window size to vary 455 

across the chromosomes. We achieved this by iteratively splitting each window and merging 456 

neighbouring windows until the total AIC of the whole alignment stops improving from any 457 

new split and/or merge (Fig. 1). Our results showed that using variable window sizes from 458 

the splitting-and-merging algorithm had better accuracy in recovering the ‘true’ gene tree 459 

topology from each site of simulated datasets compared to using fixed window sizes. The 460 

fixed window sizes selected by the AIC had at least 70% site accuracy across simulated 461 

chromosomes with homogeneous and heterogenous recombination rates (Fig. 3-4). Using 462 

variable window sizes consistently increased this accuracy further by on average 3-8% across 463 

scenarios (Fig. 3), reflecting that variable window sizes better fit the size variation of non-464 

recombining loci across the chromosomes (Fig. S3-S5). While the different starting window 465 

sizes tested in this study did not significantly affect the accuracy of the splitting-and-merging 466 

approach (Fig. 3-4), we proposed future phylogenomic analyses to start with full 467 

concatenation compared to the best window size from the AIC (Ivan et al. 2025) because: (i) 468 

we found one simulation with low accuracy when starting with short window size (Fig. 3), 469 

which might be caused by initial over-splitting of the chromosome alignment that makes it 470 

harder to improve the AIC via merging (i.e., if a non-recombining locus is split into multiple 471 

windows, merging two neighbouring windows might not improve the AIC due to lack of 472 

phylogenetic signal, as opposed to merging all windows together); (ii) it better captured the 473 

long non-recombining loci from the simulated chromosomes as splitting these regions would 474

not have to test and compare the AIC of various fixed window sizes for the given alignment. 476

When we ran the splitting-and-merging algorithm on the six species from erato-sara clade of 478 

Heliconius butterflies (with H. melpomene outgroup), we found that the window sizes varied 479 

substantially across the chromosomes (Fig. S6-S7). For instance, the window sizes on 480 

chromosome 1 ranged from 29bp to 70kb with an average of 581+11bp (95% CI), while the 481 

best fixed window size from the stepwise non-overlapping window method (Ivan et al. 2025) 482 

was 250bp (Table S1). This best fixed window size was ~10x longer than the smallest 483 

window – which prompted the concatenation of multiple windows during gene tree inference 484

during gene tree inference. As both concatenation and gene tree estimation error might bias 486 

tree inferences (Ivan et al. 2025), this result further highlights the importance of using 487 

variable window sizes when running non-overlapping window method on chromosome 488 

alignments. Unfortunately, we cannot compare the AIC between the two methods, because 489 

the stepwise non-overlapping window method (Ivan et al. 2025) excluded windows that 490 

cannot be analysed while the splitting-and-merging approach keep them in the analysis. 491

note that the Heliconius chromosomes from Edelman et al. (2019) were not phased. Huang et 496

differences in the estimates. First, both window-based and gene-based inferences are likely to 527 

be affected by trade-offs between concatenation and gene tree estimation error (Ivan et al. 528 

2025). While our method should more accurately reflect chromosomal recombination 529 

breakpoints – and thus provides a more reliable estimate of topology distribution than gene-530 

based or fixed-window approaches – it is not entirely free from these competing factors. For 531 

instance, we did not split a window if it cannot be analysed due to missing data or an 532 

insufficient number of parsimony informative sites (as clearly shown for chromosome Y; 533

topology that is supported by the majority of sites. In contrast, methods like MAST assign the 535 

tree for individual sites, allowing weaker incongruent signals to be accounted in the final 536 

estimates. Second, our analyses were based on whole genome alignments that include both 537 

coding and non-coding regions. In great apes, coding regions were shown to undergo stronger 538 

selection than non-coding regions (Cagan et al. 2016). Under the Hill-Robertson effect (Hill 539 

and Robertson 1966), regions with stronger selection tend to have reduced effective 540 

population size ( 𝑁 ") (Comeron et al. 2008), which correlates with shortened coalescent time 541 

and increased gene tree discordance. However, we cannot fully evaluate this explanation 542 

because we did not explicitly differentiate the estimates between the coding and non-coding 543 

regions. Third, Reddy et al. (2017) proposed that the current substitution models might be 544 

inadequate to explain the evolution of coding regions, and potentially better-fit the non-545 

coding regions. As model violations have been shown to affect phylogenetic analysis (Naser-546 

Khdour et al. 2019), it is possible that analyses of coding regions – on which most previous 547 

estimates were based – might underestimate the proportion of the major topology due to 548 

higher level of discordance caused by model misspecification. In order to assess the relative 549

Overall, our AIC-based splitting-and-merging approach provides a method that uses variable 580 

window sizes to infer gene trees from whole genome alignments. The windows (i.e., loci) 581 

selected by our method should better reflect recombination breakpoints and thus serve as 582 

better approximations of c-genes than simply choosing a fixed window size, or selecting loci 583 

based on known genomic boundaries such as exons and introns. These loci can then be used 584 

in a number of downstream applications. For example, if the aim is to reconstruct different 585 

species trees across a genome alignment, loci from our method could be used as the starting 586 

point for coalescent-based approaches such as those used by Thawornwattana et al. (2018, 587 

2022). If the aim is to infer the evolutionary history of every site in the genome (as may be 588 

required for applications such as PhyloGWAS (Pease et al. 2016)), then gene trees could be 589 

estimated for each locus. If stochastic error is a concern, gene trees inferred from these loci 590 

could be further refined using more complex substitution models that better-explain empirical 591 

datasets (e.g., GHOST (Crotty et al. 2020) that models heterotachy, MixtureFinder (Ren et al. 592 

2025) that models variation of transition rates among sites), and/or more sophisticated 593 

methods that estimate the trees for individual sites (e.g., MAST (Wong et al. 2024)) or refine 594 

the gene trees in relation to each other (e.g., Espalier (Rasmussen and Guo 2023), HMMs 595 

(Siepel and Haussler 2005)). In all these cases, more accurate approximations of c-genes can 596 

benefit downstream analyses, and have the potential to improve our understanding of 597 

complex genomic histories. 598
