# Abstract 39 Recent advances in long-read sequencing have enabled near telomere-to-telomere (T2T) 40 assemblies across diverse taxa. However, avian genomes remain challenging due to 41 numerous microchromosomes, small, typically < 20Mb, elements that are gene-, GC-, and 42 repeat-rich. As a consequence, microchromosomes are often missing from genome 43 assemblies. Here, we present a chromosome-level, haplotype-resolved genome assembly 44 for the Western barn owl ( Tyto alba ). Using a trio-binning strategy with Illumina parental 45 reads combined with PacBio HiFi and Oxford Nanopore Technologies data, we generated 46 two phased contig sets. These were scaffolded into 40 linkage groups using a linkage map. 47 Comparative analyses identified unplaced HiFi scaffolds corresponding to 48 microchromosomes, which we integrated into six additional microchromosomes using long 49 reads information. The two assemblies present 46 chromosomes, matching the karyotype of 50 the species. They exhibit strong synteny between parental haplotypes, except for a ~38 Mb 51 complex region on chromosome 7 containing nested inversions. This high-quality reference 52 provides the first haplotype-resolved and chromosome-level genome for Strigiformes ,53 enabling fine-scale studies of structural variation and avian genome evolution. 54 55

# Background 60 In recent years, genomics has entered the telomere-to-telomere (T2T) era. This shift began 61 with the first complete human genome (T2T-CHM13) [1] and quickly expanded to other 62 species, from crops [2,3] to livestock [4] and primates [5]. These milestones were achieved 63 thanks to the advent of long-read sequencing technologies, mostly led by Pacific 64 Biosciences (PacBio) and Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT), combined with 65 chromosome conformation contact Hi-C sequencing [6]. With this resolution, assemblies 66 now expose complex regions, such as the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) or the 67 Killer-cell immunoglobulin receptors (KIRs) locus, that were previously poorly characterised 68 due to extreme polymorphism and repetitive structure [7–11]. 69 Although these recent advances have transformed genome assembly, significant challenges 70 remain. One difficulty of genome assembly is reconstructing both parental haplotypes in 71 diploid species rather than a mosaic consensus. Common strategies include trio binning with 72 parental short reads [12,13], Hi-C phasing coupled to long, accurate reads [14,15], and 73 Strand-seq for chromosome length phasing [16,17]. When successful, haplotype-resolved 74 assemblies can improve the discovery of complex structural variants [12,18–20]. Still, 75 phasing can be limited by repeat- and GC-rich regions that promote switches or mis-joins 76 [21], by genomes with large size, high heterozygosity or polyploidy (common in plants) [22], 77 and by practical constraints such as the need for parental samples (trio binning) and the 78 cost/complexity of specialised libraries. Consequently, fully phased references are still less 79 common than haploid assembly in many taxa [11,22]. 80 The genome reconstruction also varies widely across taxa. In vertebrates, bird genomes are 81 difficult to assemble due to the presence of numerous microchromosomes [23,24]. These 82 microchromosomes share features that hamper their reconstruction: they are small, gene-83 rich, GC-rich and contain a high proportion of repetitive elements [24,25]. Because of these 84 properties, many avian genome assemblies contain fewer chromosomes than the actual 85 karyotype, with microchromosomes frequently missing [24]. Recently, the introduction of 86

5Oxford Nanopore ultra-long reads has improved their recovery, leading to more complete 87 assemblies in species such as chicken [26], bustard [27], and mallard [25,28]. Still, adoption 88 of these approaches has been uneven across birds and achieving full and accurate 89 resolution of avian microchromosomes remains a major challenge. 90 Among owls ( Strigiformes) , genome assembly poses extra difficulties. Their karyotypes 91 feature many microchromosomes and rearrangements [29], making synteny with well-92 assembled model species less informative [30]. The Common barn owl ( Tyto alba ) illustrates 93 this difficulty. The species has 46 pairs of chromosomes [31] and a complete genome 94 assembly has yet to be achieved. The current assembly generated using PacBio long reads 95 and Bionano optical mapping consists of 70 “pseudo-haplotypes” where phase information is 96 not conserved, (N50 = 36.03 Mb) [32]. This was a major improvement from the previous 97 version, which contained 21,509 contigs with a N50 of 4.62 Mb [33]. Nevertheless, additional 98 work is needed to reach a chromosome-level assembly that matches the 46 pairs observed 99 in the barn owl karyotype. 100 In this study, we aim to produce a chromosome-level, haplotype-resolved reference genome 101 for the barn owl ( Tyto alba ). We achieve accurate chromosome phasing through trio-binning 102 using Illumina parental reads. We construct unitigs from HiFi PacBio and ONT data, which 103 we then scaffold into chromosomes using linkage map information [34]. Finally, we leverage 104 the gene-rich nature of microchromosomes to manually curate unplaced unitigs, based on 105 the presence of genes typically found on microchromosomes in other species. With this 106 integrated approach, we deliver a high-quality genomic resource, add a chromosome-level 107 reference of an owl, and resolve previously uncharacterised complex structures. 108 109

# Results 110 

Chromosome-scale, haplotype resolved assemblies 111 Using PacBio HiFi reads (150.8 Gb, ~ 119x of paternal and ~117x of maternal expected 112 coverage, Supplementary Table 1), Oxford Nanopore reads (40.2 Gb, ~32x of paternal and 113 ~31x of maternal expected coverage, Supplementary Table 1), and linkage information, we 114 generated two chromosome-level, haplotype-resolved assemblies of the barn owl ( Tyto 115 

alba ). Each haplotype comprised 46 chromosomes, matching the expected karyotype 116 (Figure 1; Table 1; Supplementary Table 2). The paternal assembly spanned 1.27 Gbp (N50 117 = 36.7 Mb; N90 = 15.5 Mb, Figure 1A), and the maternal assembly spanned 1.29 Gbp (N50 118 = 38.3 Mb; N90 = 15.1 Mb, Figure 1B). Both haplomes showed a slight increase in size 119 compared to previous assembly (GCF_018691265.1: 1.25 Gbp, Table 1). Hi-C contact maps 120 showed strong, chromosome-scale diagonals with no spurious trans-chromosomal signals, 121 consistent with high contiguity and correct scaffolding (Figure 1C-D). 122 We quantified phasing accuracy by leveraging k  mers unique to each parental read set. For 123 each haplotype assembly, we computed the switch error rate as the proportion of 124 parental  specific k  mers incorrectly assigned to the opposite haplotype relative to the total 125 number of parental  specific k mers detected across both assemblies. This analysis 126 indicated that 7.8% of paternal  specific k mers were misplaced into the maternal 127 haplotype, while 7.5% of maternal  specific k mers were incorrectly assigned to the 128 paternal haplotype. 129 As the assembly was constructed from a homogametic male (ZZ), identification of the sexual 130 chromosome could not be based on identification of an heterochromatide unique in each 131 assembly. Instead, to identify the Z chromosome, we used a protein-based homology with 132 other avian species. Species such as chicken ( Gallus gallus ) and zebra finch ( Taeniopygia 133 

guttata ) showed that proteins encoded on their Z chromosome mapped predominantly to our 134 largest assembled chromosome (Supplementary Figure 1 and 2). This largest chromosome 135

Repeat and gene annotation 155 Custom repeat annotation revealed that repeats constituted 13.68% (paternal) and 14.83% 156 (maternal) of assembled sequence. LINEs were the predominant class (47.81% paternal and 157 47.07% maternal of total repeats), followed by LTR elements (32.23% paternal and 32.45% 158 maternal, Supplementary Figure 3). Repeats were enriched toward chromosomal ends and 159 in the six smaller chromosomes. On the metacentric Z chromosome, repeat density peaked 160 around the centromere (Figure 2A). The unassembled W chromosome shows intense FISH 161 signals for both telomeric and centromeric probes, indicating a marked enrichment of these 162 repeats (Figure 2B). 163

Recovery and comparison of microchromosomes 171 To recover microchromosomes that were missing from the long-reads-based unitigs, we 172 used MicroFinder [24]. This strategy allowed us to re-assign unplaced long-reads-based 173 unitigs to specific microchromosomes and integrate them into the final haplotype 174 assemblies. 175 We screened 116 unplaced unitigs in the paternal assembly and 119 in the maternal 176 assembly. Based on conserved gene content, we identified 14 paternal and 18 maternal 177 candidates using MicroFinder. We further assembled them in 6 new microchromosomes, 178 that were absent from previous versions (RefSeq: GCF_902150015.1 and 179 GCF_018691265.1). 180 In contrast to chromosomes already present in the previous assembly, the six newly 181 recovered chromosomes are characterized by distinct genomic properties and separate 182 clearly from all remaining chromosomes in the PCA built from chromosomal features 183 (Supplementary Figure 4). The recovered microchromosomes showed elevated GC content 184 and most were extremely repeat-rich relative to larger chromosomes (Figure 3A, Figure 3B). 185 They contained few genes in absolute terms and remained gene-sparse even after 186 accounting for their size (Figure 3C, Figure 3E). Despite their sparse gene content, most of 187 their annotated genes were absent from the prior assembly (GCF_018691265.1, Figure 3D, 188 Figure 3E). 189

Haplotype synteny and a complex rearrangement on chromosome 7 190 Whole-genome alignment between haplotypes indicated extensive collinearity punctuated by 191 a single, large and complex rearrangement on chromosome 7 (Figure 2A). Nearly the entire 192 paternal chromosome 7 aligned in the opposite orientation to the maternal chromosome 7, 193 with nested structure: a large (~20 Mb) inversion interleaved with two shorter inverted 194 segments and separated by two collinear blocks, all translocated relative to each other 195 (Figure 4A). Repeat density increased near inferred breakpoints in both haplotypes, 196 supporting the potential evolution of successive rearrangements (Figure 4a) [55]. 197 198

# Discussion 206 The telomere  to  telomere (T2T) era has transformed genome assembly, making it possible 207 to reconstruct complete chromosomes across an increasing range of species [1–5,8,11]. Yet 208 birds remain among the most challenging vertebrates to assemble owing to their numerous, 209 small, GC-rich and repeat  dense microchromosomes, which have historically been 210 under  represented or mis  assembled in reference genomes [7,23,24]. Here, we present the 211 first haplotype  resolved, chromosome  level assemblies for an owl, the Western barn owl 212 (Tyto alba ), each comprising 46 chromosomes as expected from the karyotype [31,56] and 213 substantially improving upon previous barn owl references [32,33]. These two haplotype-214 resolved assemblies exhibit high contiguity and completeness (with few gaps and BUSCO 215 scores above 99%) and recover six chromosomes that were absent from earlier assemblies, 216 providing the first near  complete representation of the species’ chromosomal assembly. 217 

A multi-layered assembly strategy 218 No single sequencing technology was sufficient to resolve the barn owl genome. Instead, the 219 assembly succeeded because multiple complementary resources were integrated, with each 220 compensating for the others’ blind spots. First, we phased the haplomes using Illumina reads 221 from the parents, partitioning offspring long reads with trio aware k-mers before assembly to 222 reduce switches and produce cleaner haplotype-specific contigs for downstream scaffolding. 223 Then, HiFi contigs formed the structural backbone of the assembly, providing the accuracy 224 and read length necessary to produce long, high  confidence unitigs. However, Hi-Fi reads 225 alone cannot reliably span extremely GC  rich or repeat  dense regions, features abundant 226 on avian micro- and dot chromosomes [57]. ONT ultra  long reads filled this gap, traversing 227 repetitive sequences and bridging discontinuities between HiFi contigs, consistent with 228 earlier reports demonstrating their unique value for difficult regions in bird genomes [25,26]. 229 For chromosomes represented in the linkage map [34], population  based marker order 230 provided a scaffold for organising HiFi / ONT contigs into linkage groups, after which Hi  C231 contact data confirmed chromosome  scale structure for the larger elements. However, Hi  C232

16 proved uninformative for microchromosomes: their short length, high repeat content, and 233 poor mappability limit reliable detection of meaningful contact patterns, a difficulty also 234 encountered in other avian assemblies [24]. To identify and assemble these highly 235 challenging chromosomes, we instead relied on conserved microchromosome gene  set 236 signature [24]. This allowed to pinpoint unplaced unitigs enriched for 237 microchromosome  specific genes. These fragments were then manually curated and 238 stitched together, guided by ONT long  read alignments that provided the only long  range 239 continuity through repetitive landscapes. 240 Altogether, the assembly demonstrates that the integration of all the available information 241 was critical to construct a robust, near  complete chromosomal assembly. Our 242 recommendation to facilitate good future assemblies would be to collect enough material to 243 generate several HiFi libraries of different fragment sizes and collect enough ONT 244 sequences, preferably as long as possible, before starting the assembly. Filtering of 245 sequences containing high number of internal repeats is also essential to prevent assembly 246 artefacts. 247 

Recovery and characteristics of the missing chromosomes 248 Bird genomes remain difficult to assemble fully, and only a handful of avian references 249 currently include the entire set of microchromosomes [23,25,26,28]. However, 250 synteny  based scaffolding is often insufficient for microchromosomes because they show 251 rapid evolution and poor conservation of gene order across species [23,24]. 252 We assembled six previously missing chromosomes for the barn owl, displaying hallmark 253 features of micro / dot chromosomes (elevated GC content and enriched repeats) consistent 254 with other avian genomes [25,26,28]. However, in contrast with the micro-chromosomes 255 present in the previous assembly, they remain gene  sparse relative to expectations based 256 on the classic view of microchromosomes as strongly gene  dense [23,24]. This pattern may 257 reflect genuine biological variation in the barn owl lineage but also residual annotation 258 challenges in small repeat  rich elements now referred to as dot chromosomes [58]. 259

17 Based on our feature  based analysis, the distinction between macro  , micro  , and 260 dot  chromosomes is not straightforward, and no single variable (e.g., length, GC content, or 261 gene content) provides clear boundaries between these chromosomal categories. Future 262 work should examine the functional significance of genes located on these newly identified 263 chromosomes and explore whether particular gene families (such as immunity   or 264 sensory  related genes) exhibit micro   / dot  chromosome–specific organization in owls. 265 

Phased assemblies reveal complex structural variation 266 Fully phased haplotypes allow the precise characterisation of complex structural variation. 267 Structural variants (SVs), such as large inversions, are increasingly recognised as important 268 drivers of adaptation [59], phenotypic evolution [60], and recombination landscape 269 heterogeneity [61], but their detection and interpretation require high  quality, often phased 270 reference genomes [7,8,55,62]. In barn owls, previous indirect evidence suggested the 271 presence of a large inversion linked to climate [63], but its structure and extent could not be 272 determined with pseudo  haploid or fragmented references [32,33]. Our phased assemblies 273 reveal a ~38 Mb complex inversion on chromosome 7, comprising multiple nested inverted 274 and collinear segments. Yet the evolutionary history and potential phenotypic consequences 275 of this inversion remain unknown. An important next step will be to examine its population 276 frequency, heterozygosity patterns, and possible associations with traits such as coloration, 277 dispersal, or behaviour traits that have shown geographic structure in barn owls [32]. 278 

Conclusions and perspectives 279 Together, our phased, chromosome  level assemblies provide the most complete and 280 accurate genomic resource to date for the barn owl and, more broadly, for the order 281 Strigiformes [30,32,33]. They reveal previously unknown chromosomes, expose the 282 architecture of a complex chromosomal inversion, and shed new light on microchromosome 283 organisation in a lineage with a complex karyotype. These resources create a strong 284 foundation for investigating structural variation, recombination dynamics, and the genetic 285 basis of phenotypic divergence in barn owls. Allele sorting of HiFi reads 381 With the final intention to build haplome specific assembly, we sorted HiFi reads according to 382 the parent of origin using a k-mer based approach that identifies parent-specific kmers in 383 each read. 384 We first build parent-specific sets of k-mers from parental Illumina reads. To balance uneven 385 parental sequencing depths (Father 172’731’951 reads vs Mother 93’360’939 reads) and 386 sampling bias of kmers during sequencing, we randomly drew non-overlapping subsets of 387 46,680,469 Illumina paired-end reads, 3 for the Father and 2 for the Mother. In each of these 388 five sets, we counted 49-mers (length chosen to avoid palindromes and reduce 389 homopolymer errors; Supplementary Table 4), free of Ns. Finally, we defined nine parent 390 specific k-mer libraries containing 49-mers present ≥ 2x within a parental subset and absent 391 from pairs of subsets of the other parent (see Supplementary Table 5). These parent specific 392 k-mer libraries allowed to account for sampling bias and statistical testing during the read 393 classification procedure described in the next paragraph. This comparison between parental 394 sets was done using a modified jellyfish v2.3.0 to emit k-mer “bin” files unique to one library 395 and absent from a given set of other libraries (patch provided at: 396

Gene annotation 519 We annotated both haplomes with NCBI Eukaryotic Genome Annotation Pipeline (EGAPx) 520 [48]. Genome mappability masking 541 Genome-wide mappability was quantified using the seqbility/SNPable pipeline (available at 542 https://github.com/lh3/misc/tree/master/seq/seqbility). The reference genome was first 543 decomposed into all possible k-mers using the splitfa program, with the k-mer size of 150bp, 544 matching common read length. For this step several non  default stringency thresholds were applied (-r 553 0.50, 0.90, 0.95, and 0.99). These thresholds represent the minimum fraction of overlapping 554 k-mers that must map uniquely for a genomic position to be considered mappable. Lower 555 thresholds generate more permissive callable regions, whereas higher thresholds identify 556 only the most confidently unique regions. 557
