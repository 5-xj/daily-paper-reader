ABSTRACT 19 

Background 20 

Accurate single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) annotation is central to genomic research yet widely used 21 tools and gene models often yield divergent results. Prior studies have shown such discrepancies in small 22 datasets, but the extent of genome-wide variation and its impact on downstream pathway analysis remain 23 unclear. 24 

Results 25 

We conducted a comprehensive comparison of three commonly used SNP annotation tools, ANNOVAR, 26 SnpEff, and VEP, using both Ensembl and RefSeq gene models to evaluate more than 40 million SNPs from 27 the Haplotype Reference Consortium. At the protein level, annotation output differed significantly across tools 28 and gene models (p-adj < 0.001), with discrepancies present in both genic and intergenic regions. RefSeq 29 produced broader annotation coverage, particularly for intergenic SNPs, while Ensembl showed greater 30 internal consistency. SnpEff provided the most complete coverage overall, whereas no single tool or model 31 configuration achieved full annotation recovery of the union reference. Integration across tools and models 32 maximized coverage and reduced annotation loss. In a case study of 204 colorectal cancer–associated SNPs 33 from the FIGI GWAS, pathway enrichment results varied depending on annotation strategy. The fully 34 integrated approach identified all four significant pathways, whereas several single-tool or single-model 35 strategies missed one or more. 36 

Conclusion 37 

SNP annotation outcomes are influenced by both the tool and gene model used, and relying on a single 38 approach may result in incomplete coverage. A multi-tool, multi-model strategy provides the most 39 comprehensive annotation and preserves enriched pathways, supporting more robust and reproducible 40 genomic interpretation. 41 42

BACKGROUND 45 

Single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), the most prevalent form of genetic variation in the human genome, 46 play a central role in understanding disease susceptibility 1 , gene regulation 2 , and phenotypic diversity 3 .47 Interpreting SNPs accurately depends on gene annotation tools that map each variant to one or more genes 48 and predict how those variants might affect gene or protein function, which is an essential step in studies of 49 complex traits 4 , pharmacogenomics 5 , and disease etiology 6,7 .50 

A common application of SNP annotation is biological pathway analysis 8 , which allows researchers to assess 51 whether coding genes (or their protein products), linked to phenotype-associated SNPs, such as those 52 identified in case-control or genome-wide association studies, are disproportionately represented in specific 53 biological pathways 9 . This approach involves mapping SNPs to protein-coding genes, which are then 54 compared against curated pathway databases like Reactome 10 and PANTHER 11,12 using pathway enrichment 55 methods 8,13,14 . Because these analyses depend on the accuracy of SNP-to-protein mapping, pathway results 56 can be strongly influenced by the annotation tools 15,16 or gene models 17,18 .57 

A wide range of tools have been developed for SNP annotation, including ANNOVAR 19 , SnpEff 20 , and the 58 Variant Effect Predictor (VEP) 21 . These tools rely on reference gene models, curated definitions of gene and 59 transcript boundaries, such as Ensembl 22 and RefSeq 23 , which differ in how genes and transcripts are defined 60 and annotated. Ensembl includes more alternative transcripts and regulatory features, while RefSeq 61 emphasizes curated gene models 17 . These structural differences can yield divergent interpretations of the 62 same SNPs. Previous studies have noted such discrepancies using focused datasets or transcriptomic 63 contexts, but to our knowledge, none have quantified this variation across the entire genome using 64 standardized inputs and outputs 15–18 .65 

To address this gap, we conducted a comprehensive, genome-wide assessment of annotation consistency 66 across ANNOVAR, SnpEff, and VEP, using both Ensembl and RefSeq gene models. Leveraging over 40 67 million SNPs from the Haplotype Reference Consortium (HRC) 24 , we compared annotations across tools and 68 gene models in both genic regions, within or near genes, and intergenic regions, between genes. Although 69 intergenic SNPs do not alter coding sequence, they frequently map to regulatory regions or nearest genes and 70 are routinely included in post-GWAS functional analysis 25,26 .71 We used two complementary approaches to evaluate agreement at the SNP level: (1) a qualitative analysis 72 

of annotation overlap patterns for each SNP, such as whether annotations were tool-specific or shared 73 across tools, and (2) a quantitative analysis measuring the proportion of protein-coding annotations 74 

captured by each tool per SNP , relative to a unified reference set. 75 

Furthermore, to evaluate these downstream effects, we applied our annotation framework to a pathway 76 enrichment case study using 204 SNPs from the Functionally Informed Gene–Environment Interaction (FIGI) 77 colorectal cancer GWAS 27,28 . Our findings indicate that the choice of annotation tool and gene model 78

Benchmarking Impact on Pathway Interpretation: Case Study Using Colorectal Cancer SNPs 283 

To determine whether these annotation discrepancies influence biological interpretation, we next conducted a 284 pathway enrichment analysis using PANTHER on 204 colorectal cancer–associated SNPs from the FIGI 285 GWAS. We tested different combinations of annotation tools and gene models. When using individual tools 286 with the Ensembl gene model, up to three pathways reached FDR significance: TGF-beta signaling, 287 Alzheimer’s disease–presenilin, and gonadotropin-releasing hormone receptor. In contrast, RefSeq-based 288 annotations enabled the detection of a fourth significant pathway, cadherin signaling, for some tool 289 configurations. 290 

While TGF-beta signaling was consistently enriched across all tools and gene models, results for Alzheimer’s 291 disease–presenilin and cadherin signaling varied. Cadherin signaling was not significant with any tool using 292 Ensembl (FDR ≥ 0.59) and was also missed by VEP when used with RefSeq (FDR = 1.0). Similarly, 293 Alzheimer’s disease–presenilin did not reach significance with VEP and RefSeq (FDR = 0.19). In contrast, both 294 pathways were detected when using RefSeq annotations from ANNOVAR or SnpEff (Additional File 4A). 295 

When all three tools were combined using Ensembl, the same three pathways were detected. Combining the 296 tools with RefSeq identified all four (Additional File 4B). Finally, integrating all three tools and both gene 297 models also captured all four pathways (Additional File 4C). 298 Although this fully combined strategy did not produce lower FDR values than combining all tools with RefSeq 299 alone, it preserved the complete set of enriched pathways while maximizing annotation coverage. Differences 300 in FDR-adjusted p-values across annotation strategies reflect changes in both the size and composition of the 301 mapped protein sets submitted to PANTHER. In this case study, union-based integration generally produced 302 intermediate significance estimates across pathways, although TGF-beta signaling showed the highest (yet still 303 significant) FDR under full integration. Together, these results suggest that integrating tools and gene models 304 provides a more robust basis for downstream interpretation by prioritizing pathway stability over minimizing 305 FDR values. 306 

Practical Guidance for Users 307 

For practical use, our results suggest two defensible annotation strategies. Regardless of approach, studies 308 should explicitly report the annotation tool(s) and version(s), the gene model(s) and release(s), and the 309 pathway enrichment resource used (e.g., database name and version) to ensure reproducibility. First, 310 investigators can make an informed single-configuration choice by learning the behavior of their selected 311 annotation tool and gene model, recognizing that these choices can materially change downstream protein 312 sets and enrichment results. DISCUSSION 323 

This study reveals substantial variability in SNP-to-protein annotations driven by both the annotation tools and 324 the reference gene model used. No single tool–gene model combination achieved complete recovery of the 325 union reference, and our genome-wide evaluation underscores how these discrepancies propagate through to 326 the protein level. By systematically applying both a qualitative analysis of annotation overlap patterns and a 327 quantitative analysis of annotation completeness, we highlight not only where tools diverge but also how those 328 differences influence downstream biological interpretation. 329 

One of the most striking findings was the large discrepancy in protein annotation output between RefSeq and 330 Ensembl: RefSeq mapped ~32.6% more SNPs to at least one protein than Ensembl (34,349,476 vs 331 25,905,898) and produced ~53.3% more SNP-to-protein annotation events (44,870,279 vs 29,268,457; Figure 332 1). This pattern held across both genic and intergenic regions and remained consistent even after region 333 definitions were standardized to those of Ensembl. While Ensembl exhibited greater internal agreement among 334 tools, RefSeq consistently annotated more proteins, particularly in intergenic regions, suggesting a broader, 335 more permissive annotation scope (Table 1). 336 

Annotation tool coverage patterns varied depending on genomic context. SnpEff demonstrated high and 337 consistent coverage across all configurations, making it the most robust performer in our comparison. VEP, in 338 contrast, performed well in genic regions but showed steep drop-offs in intergenic coverage, particularly when 339 used with RefSeq (Figures 4 - 6). ANNOVAR showed intermediate performance across most conditions. 340 

The practical impact of these tools and gene model differences is substantial. As shown in Table 2, genome-341 wide protein coverage ranged from as low as 40.5% (VEP with RefSeq) to as high as 94.1% (SnpEff with 342 RefSeq). Even top-performing combinations differed by several hundred thousand annotations. While using 343 SnpEff across both gene models achieved 99.2% coverage, this still left over 352,529 proteins uncaptured, 344 reinforcing the additive value of full integration. When tools were combined within a single gene model, 345 coverage improved (Figure 7A-C), but combining tools across gene models consistently yielded significantly 346 greater coverage than any within-model configuration (Figure 7D-F, Table 2). 347

These annotation differences had meaningful downstream consequences in our colorectal cancer case study. 348 When using individual tools with the Ensembl gene model, up to three pathways reached statistical 349 significance: TGF-beta signaling, Alzheimer’s disease–presenilin, and gonadotropin-releasing hormone 350 receptor. RefSeq-based annotations enabled the detection of a fourth pathway, cadherin signaling, depending 351 on the tool used. However, both cadherin signaling and Alzheimer’s disease–presenilin were missed when 352 using VEP with RefSeq (FDR = 1.0 and 0.19, respectively), highlighting the variability introduced by annotation 353 choices. In contrast, both pathways were successfully detected when using RefSeq annotations from 354 ANNOVAR or SnpEff. 355 

Combining all three tools with Ensembl recovered the same three pathways, while combining tools with 356 RefSeq yielded all four. The fully integrated approach, merging all tools and both gene models, also detected 357 all four pathways. Although this strategy did not improve statistical significance beyond combining all tools with 358 RefSeq and in some cases produced slightly higher but still significant FDR values, it ensured the broadest 359 annotation coverage and preserved all enriched pathways. These results underscore the importance of using 360 both multiple tools and multiple gene models to support more comprehensive and reliable downstream 361 interpretation. 362 Importantly, differences in pathway significance across annotation strategies do not reflect uncertainty in the 363 pathway enrichment test itself, which is well defined statistically, but rather uncertainty in how SNPs are 364 mapped to genes prior to enrichment analysis. Because there is no gold standard for selecting or prioritizing 365 annotation tools or gene models, different strategies yield distinct but internally valid gene sets that define 366 different enrichment hypotheses. Our results suggest that integrating annotations across tools and gene 367 models, or explicitly evaluating pathway stability across strategies, provides a more robust framework for 368 interpretation than relying on any single annotation configuration. 369 In practice, we suggest using an integrated, combined annotation strategy as a default, or explicitly evaluating 370 pathway stability across tools and gene models when a single configuration is used. 371 372 

CONCLUSION 373 

This genome-wide assessment of SNP annotation consistency demonstrates that both tool and gene model 374 selection substantially influence protein-level outputs. RefSeq produced more total annotations, particularly in 375 intergenic regions, while Ensembl showed stronger agreement across tools. SnpEff consistently delivered high 376 coverage across all contexts, whereas VEP’s performance was more variable, especially in intergenic regions. 377 Coverage varied widely across tool–gene model combinations, with Table 2 illustrating how these choices can 378 shift annotation outcomes by millions of SNP-to-protein annotations. 379

While no single combination was sufficient, the fully integrated approach, 382 merging all tools and both gene models, captured the broadest set of annotations and recovered all significant 383 biological pathways. METHODS 392 

Because SNP annotation can yield multiple gene and protein IDs per variant, we first describe how outputs 393 were standardized and then how agreement was evaluated. 394 

Data Sources and Annotation Tools 395 This study was conducted on the AnnoQ platform 29 (version 1.11) and used single-nucleotide polymorphism 396 (SNP) data from the Haplotype Reference Consortium (HRC.r1-1) dataset; annotations were generated within 397 the AnnoQ environment (WGSA v0.95) 30 . All analyses were performed on hg38/GRCh38 coordinates with HRC 398 variants processed in hg38 coordinates within the AnnoQ/WGSA workflow. Gene-model annotations were 399 generated using Ensembl release 107 (consistent with GENCODE v41) and the RefSeq gene model bundled 400 within the AnnoQ/WGSA v0.95 environment. Annotations were retrieved from three commonly used tools, 401 ANNOVAR (2020-06-07), SnpEff version 4.3t, and VEP version 107, through the AnnoQ interface 402 (https://annoq.org). AnnoQ executes each tool with its standard, tool-recommended configuration for the 403 specified gene model; we did not impose cross-tool harmonization of gene boundary definitions, upstream or 404 downstream distance thresholds, or intergenic assignment rules. As a result, differences observed across tools 405 reflect tool-native behavior rather than post hoc parameter tuning. PANTHER 19.0 was used for pathway 406 enrichment analyses. All analyses were conducted using custom Python 31 (version 3.12) and R 32 (version 407 4.4.1) scripts and data available at https://github.com/USCbiostats/SNP-Annotation-Agreement-and-408 Downstream-Impact-Analysis. 409 

Region Classification: Genic and Intergenic SNPs 410 SNPs were categorized as genic or intergenic using ANNOVAR functional annotation outputs under Ensembl 411 and RefSeq gene models. This stratification was used only for context-specific reporting and statistical 412

Genic regions were defined as overlapping exons, introns, or untranslated regions 414 (UTRs), while intergenic SNPs were those located outside annotated gene boundaries and linked to the 415 nearest gene. ANNOVAR was selected for this step because its output clearly distinguishes between the two 416 categories: genic annotations are listed under the “ANNOVAR Gene ID” field, and intergenic ones under the 417 “ANNOVAR Closest Gene ID” field. ID Mapping 423 To enable consistent protein-level comparisons across tools and gene models, we mapped gene-level 424 annotations to UniProt 33 identifiers. Depending on the gene model, we used gene symbols and Entrez IDs 34 for 425 RefSeq-based annotations, and Ensembl gene IDs for Ensembl-based annotations, consistent with how each 426 gene model defines its identifiers. 427 Because PANTHER enrichment operates on identifiers that map to PANTHER protein records, we restricted 428 analyses to protein-coding genes. Pseudogenes and non-protein-coding loci were excluded because they do 429 not consistently map to PANTHER protein entries and therefore are not reliably testable in this enrichment 430 framework. 431 

To standardize identifiers across tools and gene models, we used the HUGO Gene Nomenclature Committee 432 (HGNC) 35 approved gene dataset 36 , which links gene symbols, Entrez IDs, and Ensembl gene IDs to UniProt 433 IDs. This file was used to enforce a single gene-to-UniProt mapping (one UniProt ID per gene) to avoid 434 inflation from transcript isoforms. Applying this standardized mapping step ensured comparability across 435 annotations and supported downstream pathway analysis. 436 

To verify that the mapping process did not introduce discrepancies, we applied it uniformly across all tools and 437 gene models. During this process, we identified a number of outdated Ensembl gene IDs missing from the 438 HGNC file. To confirm these were deprecated and not mistakenly excluded, we manually checked a subset in 439 Ensembl and verified that the missing IDs had been superseded. 440

This reference set served as 447 the basis for both qualitative and quantitative analyses. For example, if ANNOVAR returns proteins {A, B}, 448 SnpEff returns {A, C}, and VEP returns {A, B, C} for the same SNP, the reference set is {A, B, C}. A tool is 449 considered in full agreement only if it matches {A, B, C}, and in partial agreement if it matches a subset. 450 

First, we conducted a qualitative analysis to characterize patterns of annotation agreement. For each 451 SNP, each tool or gene model was assessed relative to the reference set and classified as exhibiting full 452 agreement if it recovered the complete reference set, partial agreement if it recovered a subset of 453 reference proteins, or no agreement if it recovered none. SNPs were then assigned to categorical 454 classes based on the combination of agreement outcomes observed across tools or gene models. 455 At the tool level, categories included full agreement across all three tools, tool-specific matches (e.g., 456 only SnpEff fully recovering the reference set), pairwise agreement cases in which two tools fully 457 recovered the reference set while the third did not, nested subset cases in which two tools fully 458 recovered the reference set while the third recovered only a subset, and fully disjoint cases in which no 459 tool fully recovered the reference set. In total, 17 distinct tool-level agreement categories were observed 460 (Additional File 2). 461 At the gene model level, SNPs were classified into seven categories by comparing RefSeq- and Ensembl-462 derived UniProt identifiers relative to the SNP-specific reference set. For each SNP, each gene model was 463 evaluated according to whether it fully recovered the reference set, recovered only a subset of reference 464 proteins, or contributed no unique annotations. Based on these outcomes, SNPs were assigned to one of 465 seven categories, including Exact Match (both gene models fully recovering the reference set), RefSeq with 466 Ensembl Subset, Ensembl with RefSeq Subset, partial overlap between gene models, fully disjoint 467 annotations, and gene-model-specific cases in which only RefSeq or only Ensembl contributed annotations 468 (Additional File 3). 469 Second, we performed a quantitative analysis to assess annotation completeness per SNP. For each tool, 470 we recorded the number of UniProt IDs it captured out of the total in the SNP’s reference set. These 471 numerator-denominator pairs were retained for two distinct purposes: the numerators were used to perform 472 pairwise statistical comparisons across tools and gene models, while the denominators were used to calculate 473 coverage percentages (Tables 1 & 2). 474 

In a complementary analysis, we also evaluated how annotation output varied when holding the tool constant 475 and switching gene models. This comparison allowed us to directly measure the effect of gene model selection 476 independently from tool behavior and served as an intermediate step between tool-based and fully integrated 477 analyses. 478

Statistical Analysis 482 To compare total protein annotation output across tools, we aggregated annotation counts by chromosome. 483 For each chromosome (1–22 and X), we recorded the number of protein-level annotations produced by each 484 tool based on SNPs located on that chromosome. 485 Although annotations were evaluated at the SNP level to characterize agreement patterns, formal statistical 486 testing was performed on chromosome-level aggregates rather than individual SNPs to avoid SNP-level 487 hypothesis testing that would incorrectly treat linkage disequilibrium-correlated variants as independent 488 samples and to limit inflation of statistical significance. 489 

We used paired t-tests to compare annotation counts between tools, treating chromosomes as the 490 matched units. Bonferroni correction was applied for pairwise comparisons (ANNOVAR vs. SnpEff, 491 ANNOVAR vs. VEP, and SnpEff vs. VEP). These tests were performed separately for genome-wide, 492 genic, and intergenic regions. For comparisons between gene models (RefSeq vs. Ensembl), Bonferroni 493 correction was also applied. Box plots and bar plots were used to visualize annotation counts and tool 494 comparisons (Figures 4-7). 495 Proportion-based tests were not used because the true number of expected protein annotations per SNP 496 is unknown. Chromosomes were treated as matched units (n = 23) because annotation pipelines were 497 applied uniformly across all chromosomes, enabling consistent aggregation and comparison of 498 annotation outputs across tools, gene models, and their combinations. 499 Counts represent SNP-to-protein annotation events (i.e., a SNP annotated to multiple proteins 500 contributes multiple annotations). 501
