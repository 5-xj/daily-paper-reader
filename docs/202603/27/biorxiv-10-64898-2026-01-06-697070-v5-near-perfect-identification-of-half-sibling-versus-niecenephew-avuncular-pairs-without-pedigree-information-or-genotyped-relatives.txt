## Abstract 

Motivation: Large-scale genomic biobanks contain thousands of second-degree relatives with missing pedigree metadata. Accurately distinguishing half-sibling (H-S) from niece/nephew–avuncular (N/N-A) pairs—both sharing approximately 25% of the genome—remains a significant challenge. Current SNP-based methods rely on aggregate Identical-By-Descent (IBD) segment counts and age differences, but substantial distributional overlap leads to high misclassification rates. There is a critical need for a scalable, genotype-only method that can resolve these “half-degree” ambiguities without requiring observed pedigrees or extensive relative information. 

Results: We present a novel computational framework that achieves near-complete separation of H-S and N/N-A pairs using haplotype-level sharing features derived from across-chromosome phasing. By modeling these features with a multivariate Gaussian mixture model (GMM), we demonstrate exceptional classification performance in biobank-scale data. Ground-truth validation labels were established through a multi-step inference process within a family graph constructed from high-confidence first-degree rela-tionships. To overcome the scarcity of genotyped half-sibling parents, we structurally validated H-S pairs by identifying specific pedigree configurations involving first cousins that are logically incompatible with an avuncular relationship. Our method achieves a sensitivity of 96.9% and a specificity of 99.7% on these structurally-validated pairs. Furthermore, we show that these identified relationships serve as superior phase anchors, that measurably improve the accuracy of across-chromosome homologue assignment. This method provides a robust, scalable solution for pedigree reconstruction and the control of cryptic relatedness in large-scale genomic studies.

## 1 Introduction 

Genome-wide datasets routinely contain pairs of individuals more closely related than expected under random population sampling, often in the ab-sence of explicit pedigree records [4]. The accurate inference of these relationships has become a cornerstone of modern human genetics, partic-ularly as sample sizes in biobank-scale resources have scaled to hundreds of thousands of individuals [5]. Robust relationship subtyping is es-sential for controlling cryptic relatedness in genome-wide association studies (GWAS), refining demographic models, and enhancing forensic and genealogical investigations [6, 8]. A persistent challenge in relatedness inference is the ambiguity between “half-degree” relationships. While first-degree relatives (parent– child and full siblings) share approximately 50% of their genome identical-by-descent (IBD), second-degree relatives share approximately 25%. However, several distinct pedigree configurations result in this same 25% sharing, most notably half-siblings (H-S) and niece/nephew–avuncular (N/N-A) pairs [21]. Distinguishing between these configurations is compu-tationally non-trivial because the genome-wide proportion of IBD sharing is stochastically similar across these classes [4]. Accurately distinguishing H-S from N/N-A relationships is critical for several domains of genomic research. First, in heritability and pheno-typic analysis, misclassification can lead to biased estimates; half-siblings typically share a more similar childhood environment and possible mater-nal effects compared to pairs of avuncular and niece/nephew, making it essential to partition these components correctly when modeling shared variance [19, 20].

Second, in forensic genetics and missing persons cases, resolving this ambiguity is a primary challenge, as the two relationships imply different pedigree structures for lineage reconstruction [21, 22]. Third, for compu-tational phasing, H-S pairs serve as superior "phase anchors." Because H-S pairs share a parent directly, they provide long-range structural constraints that link haplotypes across chromosomes more effectively than N/N-A sharing, which is fragmented by an additional generation of recombina-tion [23]. Finally, in clinical diagnostics, identifying the exact relationship is necessary for accurate risk assessment and the identification of carriers in recessive disease pedigrees [24]. Current methodologies primarily rely on the distribution of IBD seg-ments; half-siblings theoretically inherit sharing from a single shared parent, whereas niece/nephew–avuncular pairs inherit through an addi-tional meiosis, typically leading to a higher count of shorter segments [5]. Despite these theoretical differences, empirical distributions of segment length and count exhibit substantial overlap, exacerbated by the stochastic nature of recombination [18]. Furthermore, while demographic meta-data such as age differences can provide heuristic support, they are often insufficient for high-confidence classification in cohorts with restricted recruitment windows or non-traditional family structures. In this work, we move beyond these aggregate IBD metrics to propose a novel computational framework that utilizes across-chromosome phas-ing to resolve second-degree relationships. By deriving a 4-dimensional feature vector, we capture the specific inheritance logic of parental homo-logues. We demonstrate that modeling these features with a multivariate Gaussian mixture model (GMM), fitted via the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm [25], provides near-perfect separation between H-S and N/N-A pairs. Finally, we show that these identified relationships can be utilized as phase anchors, establishing a feedback loop that improves the structural integrity of across-chromosome phasing. This approach offers a scalable, genotype-only solution that eliminates the dependency on explicit pedigree metadata. 

## 2 Methods 

2.1 UK Biobank sample and ancestry definition 

The method was developed and evaluated using data from the UK Biobank [11], focusing on individuals of European descent. This choice was motivated by the altered performance of relatedness estimation in mixed-ancestry samples and by the fact that individuals of Euro-pean descent constitute the overwhelming majority of the cohort [11]. Genetic principal components (PCs) were computed on a linkage-disequilibrium–pruned set of common autosomal SNPs [5]. The smallest 4-dimensional hypercube in PC space containing all individuals with self-described white British ancestry was then identified, and all individuals lying within this hypersphere, regardless of self-described ancestry, were retained as the eligible sample (435,187 individuals). Next, we selected biallelic autosomal SNPs that were present on both the UK BiLEVE and UK Biobank Axiom arrays, had a minor allele fre-quency (MAF) of at least 5%, and had a Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium chi-square test p-value greater than 0.0005. This resulted in a final dataset of 327,744 SNPs for relatedness estimation and downstream analyses [11].

2.4 Across-chromosome phasing 

All UK Biobank individuals were initially phased within chromosomes using Shapeit2 [11]. To resolve the phase across different chromosomes, we utilized a novel across-chromosome phasing algorithm that employs a window-based SNP-similarity metric as described in ? ]. This method iden-tifies which haplotypes on different chromosomes originate from the same parent by analyzing correlations of SNP-similarity values across genomic windows defined by recombination hotspots. For each window, the algo-rithm compares each of the focal individual’s haplotypes to both haplotypes of every non-focal individual in a sample of the UK Biobank defined in Sec-tion 2.1, selecting the maximum similarity to mitigate within-chromosome switch errors in the reference panel. By iteratively merging windows based on a consolidated measure of across-window correlation, the method es-tablishes a consistent parental homologue ( p1 or p2) across the entire autosomal genome without requiring explicit relative data or IBD segment calling.

2.6 Multivariate GMM and Posterior Probability P (H-S )

To classify pairs, we fit a two-component multivariate normal mixture model to the 4-D feature vectors of the labeled ground-truth set using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm (Implemented in C and R, available at [26]). The joint density for a pair with feature vector x is modeled as: 

f (x|Θ) = πN (x|μ1, Σ1) + (1 − π)N (x|μ2, Σ2) (3) where π represents the mixing proportion, and N (x|μk , Σk ) is the 4-dimensional multivariate normal distribution for component k ∈{H-S, N/N-A }. For every pair with a complete 4-D feature vector, we compute the posterior probability P (H-S ) as the probability of the H-S component given the observed vector x:

P (H-S |x) = πϕ (x|μH-S , ΣH-S )

πϕ (x|μH-S , ΣH-S ) + (1 − π)ϕ(x|μN/N-A , ΣN/N-A ) (4) This value is clamped to the range [0 , 1] and serves as the primary metric for relationship subtyping and the identification of high-confidence phase anchors. 

## 3 Results 

3.1 Unsupervised cluster identification and validation 

The classification of second-degree relatives was performed using an unsu-pervised framework to minimize bias and avoid circularity in validation. The mixture model EM algorithm identifies two distinct clusters within the 459 ground-truth (GT) pairs based solely on the 4-D feature vector; critically, the relationship labels (H-S or N/N-A) are not utilized by the algorithm during the cluster discovery phase [25]. Following the unsupervised estimation of cluster parameters, the GT labels are utilized to assign the biological identity of each component (e.g., identifying which cluster corresponds to the H-S class) and to estimate the sensitivity and specificity of the approach on this labeled set. This methodology ensures that the performance metrics—showing a sensitivity of 96.9% and a specificity of 99.7%—reflect the model’s ability to recover known biological structures without over-fitting to pre-defined labels. The 4-D multivariate GMM demonstrates near-perfect classification performance when applied to the full UK Biobank candidate set. As shown in Figure 4, the distribution of the posterior probability P (H − S) ex-hibits sharp bimodal peaks at 0 and 1, reflecting high model certainty in distinguishing between the two pedigree configurations. Using a stringent classification threshold of P (H − S) > 0.999995 

to ensure the highest confidence for long-range phasing anchors, the model achieves exceptional separation (Figure 4). Of the 395 ground-truth N/N-A pairs, 394 were correctly classified ( T N = 394 ), with only a single pair exceeding the threshold ( F P = 1 ). For the ground-truth H-S pairs, 62 out of 64 were correctly identified ( T P = 62 ), while two pairs fell below the threshold ( F N = 2 ). These results correspond to a sensitivity of 96.9% and a specificity of 99.7% on ground-truth data. Beyond the ground-truth set, the model identified 800 previously unla-beled pairs in the highest probability bin ( P (H − S) > 0.999995 ), while 5,657 unlabeled pairs were predicted as N/N-A (Figure 4). This demon-strates the power of the multivariate approach to recover a substantial number of high-confidence phase anchors from an unlabeled biobank-scale population. 

3.2 Impact of across-chromosome phasing on misclassification 

The high classification accuracy of the multivariate framework suggests that haplotype-level features are robust discriminators; however, the re-maining edge cases highlight the method’s dependence on the quality of across-chromosome phasing. Our analysis indicates that misclassifications are primarily driven by noise in the across-chromosome phasing process rather than a failure of the multivariate model or within-chromosome switch errors. For the single false positive (FP), a validated N/N-A pair yielded an extremely high posterior probability of P (H − S) = 0 .9999966 . This occurs when across-chromosome phasing noise incorrectly assigns shared IBD segments from different chromosomes to the same parental homo-logue. For an N/N-A pair, independent assortment typically distributes sharing across both parental homologues of the focal individual. How-ever, if the across-chromosome phasing erroneously groups these disjoint shared segments into a single parental strand, the resulting 4-D feature vector will stochastically mimic the single-parent inheritance pattern of a half-sibling, leading to an artificially inflated P (H − S).Similarly, noise in the across-chromosome phasing is the likely cause for the two false negatives (FN). These validated H-S pairs (with P (H −S)

values of 0.999918 and 0.977821 ) fell just below our stringent 0.999995 

threshold. In these instances, the across-chromosome phasing failed to maintain the continuity of the truly shared parental haplotype, effectively distributing the sharing across both homologues in the focal individual. This fragmentation reduces the observed x1 (ˆπMax ) while increasing the values of the remaining features, thereby deflating the posterior probability of the H-S component. These results underscore that while the multivariate GMM is highly effective at resolving the biological logic of inheritance, its precision is ultimately capped by the performance of the across-chromosome phas-ing algorithm. Improving the stability of across-chromosome homologue assignment remains a key area for further refinement in biobank-scale pedigree reconstruction. 

3.3 Resolution limits and third-degree relationships 

To evaluate the generalizability of the haplotype-aware framework, we applied the ˆπhh Max metric to pairs sharing approximately 12.5% of their genome (third-degree relatives). This degree of relatedness encompasses first-degree cousins (FDC), who share a pair of grandparents, and half niece/nephew–avuncular (Half N/N-A) pairs, who share a single grand-parent (i.e., a parent of the half-avuncular is a grandparent of the half-niece/nephew). Similar to the second-degree case, these configurations are often indistinguishable using diploid IBD measures alone. ## 4 Application to Phasing 

Beyond subtyping second-degree relationships, the inferred H-S and N/N-A pairs can also be used to improve both within- and across-chromosome phasing. In particular, different second-degree configurations contribute unequal information about parental origin: some effectively mix maternal and paternal lineages, whereas others constrain sharing to a single par-ent and therefore act as strong phase anchors. Using relationship labels derived from haplotype-level sharing, phasing algorithms can incorporate additional long-range constraints that link homologous segments across individuals and chromosomes. Niece/nephew–avuncular pairs offer little direct information for re-fining the avuncular individual’s phase, because the niece or nephew is related to both parents of the avuncular individual and the shared genome can originate from either parental lineage. As a result, shared regions be-tween a niece or nephew and an avuncular relative cannot, on their own, be cleanly attributed to one of the avuncular individual’s parents. ## 5 Conclusion 

This study demonstrates that a multivariate Gaussian mixture model (GMM) applied to haplotype-level sharing features provides a robust so-lution for distinguishing half-sibling (H-S) from niece/nephew–avuncular (N/N-A) pairs. By utilizing a 4-dimensional sharing vector derived from across-chromosome phasing, the method effectively leverages the struc-tural inheritance logic of parental homologues to resolve ambiguities that diploid SNP-based methods cannot. Our results in the UK Biobank show that the model achieves excep-tional specificity (99.7%), identifying 800 high-confidence half-sibling pairs and 5,657 high-confidence niece/nephew–avuncular pairs that were previously unlabeled. These results not only clarify the pedigree struc-ture of large-scale biobanks but also provide superior phase anchors that measurably improve the accuracy of across-chromosome genomic reconstruction. While the current framework provides a scalable, genotype-only solution for large cohortslike the UK biobank. To confirm the gener-alizability and robustness of the P (H − S) decision boundary, future work should include validation on independent datasets with diverse ances-try and different recombination landscapes. Furthermore, extending this haplotype-aware logic to resolve third-degree relatives remains a critical direction for refining pedigree reconstruction in biobank-scale research. This study is a direct application of across chromosome phasing that have direct implications for downstream analyses that require accurate relationship subtyping, including the construction of refined pedigrees, the control of cryptic relatedness in association studies, and the interpretation of shared genetic and environmental variance in large cohorts.
