ABSTRACT 32 

Extrachromosomal DNA (ecDNA) is common in human cancers and is associated with poor 33 

clinical outcomes, yet how ecDNA -driven genetic heterogeneity is translated into functional 34 

heterogeneity remains unclear. Using single -cell multiomics sequencing and multiplexed IF -FISH, 35 

we show that asymmetric inheritance of ecDNA generates copy number heterogeneity that 36 

propagates to gene expression programs , including oncogenic signal ing and cellular stress 37 

responses . Transgenerational live -cell lineage trac king directly shows that ecDNA heterogeneity 38 

arises within only a few cell divisions and modulates daughter cell division timing in a copy 39 

number -dependent manner, a property not observed for evenly inherited chromosomal 40 

amplicons. We identify an optimal middle ecDNA copy number range that maximizes proliferative 41 

fitness at baseline , while drug selection pressure induced by low -dose CHK1 inhibition selects for 42 

cells with a new optimal range at low ecDNA copy numbers. These low ecDNA copy number cells 43 

pre -exist in the population and can be generated de novo, driving copy number shifts promoting 44 

drug resistance. In vivo experiments further demonstrate that shifts toward ecDNA copy numbers 45 

that are optimal under the tumo ur microenvironment enhance tumo urigenicity. Together, these 46 

findings establish ecDNA copy number plasticity as a central driver of tumour evolution. 47

INTRODUCTION 49 

Extrachromosomal DNA s (ecDNA s) are circular DNA elements that are found in 50 

approximately 17% of human cancers 1, more than half of all tumour types 2, and are strongly 51 

associated with shortened patient survival 3. EcDNA s are typically hundreds of kilobases to 52 

megabases in length 4–6 and frequently encode oncogenes at high copy number 2,3,7 –9. Their 53 

circular architecture supports an open chromatin configuration that enhances chromatin 54 

accessibility and drives exceptional transcriptional output 4,10 . Furthermore, EcDNAs have been 55 

shown to cluster into transcriptionally active hubs that further amplify oncogenic signaling 11 . A56 

defining hallmark of ecDNA is their asymmetric segregation during mitosis due to the lack of 57 

centromeres 12 –14 , generat ing pronounced copy number heterogeneity within tumour s that is 58 

associated with rapid adaptation and resistance to targeted therapies 2,14,15 ,16,17 .59 

Although ecDNA s are now recognized as active drivers of cancer evolution rather than 60 

passive byproducts of chromosomal instability, several key questions remain unresolved. It is still 61 

unclear what governs ecDNA abundance within individual cells, why cells do not continuously 62 

amplify ecDNA to maximize oncogenic potential, and why shifts in ecDNA copy number promote 63 

adaptation to different treatments or in different environments . Given the strong links between 64 

ecDNA copy number variability, cellular adaptation, and survival under drug treatment, clarifying 65 

how ecDNA dosage shapes cell fitness and fate is essential for improving our ability to predict 66 

and counteract ecDNA -driven cancer progression and drug resistance. 67 

In this study, we investigated how ecDNA copy number status shapes single -cell behavior 68 

and phenotypic plasticity across ecDNA -positive cancers. We integrated single -cell multiomics 69 

analyses, live -cell imaging, computational modeling, and both in vitro and in vivo experimentation 70 

to define the functional consequences of increased oncogenic ecDNA copy number heterogeneity 71 

at single -cell resolution. Notably, we employed long -term live -cell imaging in an isogenic system 72 

to directly track the impacts of ecDNA abundance on cell survival and lineage dynamics in real 73 

time over multiple generations, representing the most direct assessment of how uneven 74 

inheritance of oncogenic amplification s influences cancer cell function and adaptation .75

Live -cell imaging captures rapid emergence of ecDNA copy number heterogeneity 150 

To track ecDNA dynamics and cell fitness, we engineered a near -isogenic pair of cell lines 151 

that allows real -time and persistent visualization of ecDNA and HSRs in living cells ( Fig. 2a ). 152 

Using a similar amplicon labelling strategy 14 , we used CRISPR -Cas9 to knock in a tetO array 153 

(96x) into the intergenic region between MYC and PCAT1 in an isogenic colorectal cancer cell 154 

line pair: COLO 320DM and COLO 320HSR. The COLO 320HSR line exhibits Mendelian 155 

inheritance of the MYC oncogene and thus serves as a control for the uneven MYC inheritance 156 

observed in COLO 320DM 18 . We then visualized MYC ecDNA and HSRs with the expression of 157 

a Tet repressor fused to an mNeonGreen fluorescent protein (TetR -mNeonGreen) that bound to 158 

the tetO array insertion ( Extended Data Fig . 2a ). We named the ecDNA cell line TG19 ec and 159 

the HSR cell line TG19 HSR , by abbreviating MYC -tetO TetR -mNeon Green to “TG ” and “19” 160 

corresponding to the single clone number . DNA FISH on metaphase spreads confirmed robust 161 

tetO labeling efficiency in both TG19 ec and TG19 HSR ( Extended Data Fig . 2b ). The 162 

relationship between tetO area and MYC area is significantly linear and positively correlated in 163 

both clones , suggesting that TetR -mNeonGreen signal is a fair proxy for MYC copy number 164 

(Extended Data Fig . 2c ). Overlap fraction between tetO and MYC foci was above 0.5 in 70% of 165

Cell fitness is determined by inherited ecDNA content 191 

In addition to quantifying MYC copy number, we also measured the time to the next 192 

division (T2D) as a proxy for fitness of each cell ( Fig. 2d -e, Extended Data Fig . 3a -b). Most 193 

strikingly , TG19 ec sister cells arising from the same progenitor exhibited greater variance in both 194 

inherited MYC copy number and T2D compared to TG19 HSR sister cells (TG19 ec copy number 195 

variance = 7.20, T2D variance = 12.7 vs. TG19 HSR copy number variance = 0.0232, T2D 196 

variance = 2.02) (Fig . 2f -g). We only recorded T2D in cells from Generation 1 to 2 to avoid bias es 197 

in division tim e arising from di fferences in cell cycle stage at the start of image acquisition or due 198 

to finite imaging duration . The relationship between ecDNA copy number and daughter cell T2D 199 

was best described by a quadratic model (Fig. 2h ). This conclusion was supported by 200 

comparisons with nested linear models (horizontal vs. linear vs. quadratic) using ANOVA and 201 

non -nested nonlinear models (sigmoid vs. quadratic) using Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), 202 

where lower AIC values indicate a better model. The sigmoidal model, commonly used to capture 203 

dose -response relationships and here intended to model a plateau in T2D beyond a copy number 204 

threshold , provided a substantially poorer fit than the quadratic model (AIC_sigmoid = 813.7 vs. 205 

AIC_quadratic = -5.1) . These analyses indicate that daughter cell fitness is maximized at a middle 206 

ecDNA copy number range (minimum near 9.13 μm 2 of TetR -mNeonGreen area in this dataset) 207 

rather than increasing or saturating monotonically with ecDNA abundance. We therefore defined 208 

the ecDNA copy number range associated with the shortest T2D as the optimal copy number 209

Dynamic optimal ecDNA copy numbers drive cell adaptation 251 

To understand how the relationship between ecDNA copy number and T2D can inform 252 

cancer cell evolution , we first simulated population growth from a range of initial copy numbers 253 

using three models: (1) a horizontal model, in which T2D is independent of copy number 254 

(Extended Data Fig . 6a); (2) a sigmoid -plateau model, in which T2D decreases and then plateaus 255 

as copy number rises (Extended Data Fig . 6 b); and (3) a quadratic model derived from live -cell 256 

imaging, in which T2D is minimized at a middle copy number range (Extended Data Fig . 6c). 257 

Under the quadratic model, the mean ecDNA copy number of the population rapidly converged 258 

to an optimal range as the population expanded (Extended Data Fig . 6f), whereas no 259 

convergence was observed under either the horizontal or sigmoid -plateau models (Extended 260 

Data Fig . 6d -e). Combining with our optimal copy number model , we modeled the uneven 261 

inheritance of ecDNA and even inheritance of HSR to predic t changes in copy number 262 

distributions over time across populations initialized with different copy numbers . EcDNA(+) cells 263

DISCUSSION 381 

Recent advancements in sequencing technologies have shed light on the widespread 382 

prevalence of ecDNA in cancer 1,2 and its contribution to escalating malignant potential via 383 

increased intratumoural heterogeneity 14,34 . Despite this progress, the specific impact of ecDNA 384 

copy number on shaping cancer cellular phenotypes remains a central and unresolved question. 385 

Here, using single -cell -resolved approaches, we identify key determinants of ecDNA copy number 386 

dynamics. Single -cell multiomics analyses show that ecDNA abundance is constrained by 387 

opposing selection pressures , including enhanced oncogenic signaling and elevated cellular 388 

stress , which are conserved across diverse ecDNA(+) cancers. Transgenerational live -cell 389 

imaging demonstrates that asymmetric inheritance of ecDNA directly generates heterogeneity in 390 

copy number and proliferative capacity. Moreover, we show that cancer cells dynamically shift 391 

ecDNA copy number toward new optimal ranges in response to environmental change, thereby 392 

enhancing tumourigenicity and driving tumour evolution. 393 

A key strength of this study is the application of live -cell imaging to directly interrogate 394 

ecDNA dynamics in living cells. By establishing a near -isogenic pair of fluorescently labeled 395 

ecDNA and chromosomal amplicon (HSR) cell lines, we were able to track ecDNA copy number 396 

and time to division across multiple generations . Long -term live -cell imaging provides insight into 397 

the kinetics at which ecDNA -conferred copy number heterogeneity develops, demonstrating that 398 

a single ecDNA(+) progenitor can give rise to a very diverse population of cells within few cell 399 

divisions, accelerating subclone development. We have shown that there is immense 400 

heterogeneity of growth patterns within a single population of cancer cells, particularly those with 401 

ecDNA. Notably, the isogenic pair of live -cell imaging cell lines highlights functional cell biology 402 

that is unique to ecDNA (+) cancer cells, including increased variation in the ability to divide and 403 

time to next division. These observations motivate future studies that combine ecDNA reporters 404 

with additional functional sensors, such as DNA damage or replication stress reporters, to directly 405 

assess the causal impact of damaged ecDNA in shaping cancer cell fitness .406 

Our findings further demonstrated that ecDNA(+) cancer cell fitness is strongly coupled to 407 

copy number, and optimal copy number is a common feature of several ecDNA(+) cell lines, 408 

including COLO 320DM, PC3 DM, and GBM39 -EC. Although the precise numerical optima differ 409 

across measurement modalities , we consistently observe a middle optimal range associated with 410 

faster division and increased proliferation at baseline across single -cell multiomics analyses, live -411 

cell imaging, protein -based sorting, and multiplexed IF -FISH. We find that elevated ecDNA copy 412 

number amplifications are associated with a trade -off , as DNA damage signaling may limit the 413 

extent to which ecDNA -driven cancer cells can enhance oncogenic signaling. For instance, a 414

By combining single -cell multiomics sequencing and multiplexed imaging across multiple cancer 418

damage , which curbs ecDNA abundance from continuously increasing without limit. Additionally, 421 

our study makes increasingly clear that copy number shifts are critical for ecDNA(+) cancer cell 422 

adaptation to different environments with different optimal copy number ranges .423 

This framework provides a mechanistic explanation for several prior associations between 424 

ecDNA copy number shifts and therapeutic resistance, revealing that intrinsic ecDNA copy 425 

number heterogeneity gives rise to subpopulations with specific copy numbers that are resistant 426 

to treatment 35 . In glioblastoma, ecDNA (+) GBM39 -EC cells tolerate erlotinib more effectively than 427 

cells with chromosomally integrated EGFR amplifications, in part because the surviving ecDNA(+) 428 

population shifts to a lower EGFR copy number under treatment pressure 14 . Other recent work 429 

similarly revealed that MYCN copy number decreases upon treatment with genotoxic stressors in 430 

neuroblastoma cells 17 . In contrast, SNU16 gastric cancer cells harboring FGFR2 and MYC on 431 

distinct ecDNAs increase FGFR2 copy number upon infigratinib exposure 18 . These observations 432 

suggest that ecDNA copy number modulation is drug -specific and depends on how a specific 433 

treatment perturbs the balance between oncogenic signaling and cellular stress. Our findings 434 

therefore support that a combinatorial approach with therapies that synergistically target diverse 435 

copy number ranges, such as coupling a replication stress enhancer like a CHK1 -inhibitor with an 436 

oncogene suppressor like infigratinib in SNU16 cells 18 , as an effective strategy against ecDNA(+) 437 

cancers. 438 

Other strategies that may hold promise against ecDNA (+) cancers include therapies that 439 

can target or silence retention elements and factors critical for the tethering of ecDNA to mitotic 440 

chromatin to decrease ecDNA copy number 36 . We believe that the TG19 ec and TG19 HSR 441 

isogenic live -cell imaging pair of cell lines will serve enormous utility for identifying ecDNA copy 442 

number -dependent sensitivities from high -throughput screens. The fluorescent -based, real -time 443 

copy number readout eliminates the need for performing additional experimentation to measure 444 

ecDNA copy number after treatment. Being able to efficiently collect information about changes 445 

in ecDNA copy number or tethering in response to varied drug treatments will be essential for 446 

determining and tailoring therapeutic combinations that will combat resistance arising from shifts 447 

in ecDNA abundance. 448

Beyond drug response , ecDNA copy number plasticity also contributes to tumour 449 

progression in vivo. We found that e cDNA(+) cells exhibit greater tumourigenicity in vivo than 450 

HSR (+) cells , and this increased tumourigenicity strongly correlates with an increase in ecDNA 451 

copy number to a new optimal level. It is unclear why the in vivo tumour microenvironment selects 452 

for increased copy number. Given that MYC detected by single -cell RNA sequencing shows high 453 

expression in COLO 320DM xenograft cells but its expression is massively decreased in 454 

COLO320 HSR xenograft cells 37 , our hypothesis is that MYC was downregulated in vivo and 455 

subsequently, cancer cells with ecDNA compensate for decreased MYC expression by increasing 456 

their copy number. The mechanistic explanations behind inhibitory MYC feedback in vivo warrant 457 

further study. Other work has shown how copy number influences subclonal expansion and 458 

metastatic potential in lung cancer 41 . These findings suggest that metastatic niches may require 459 

a different optimal copy than the primary tumour site, and ecDNA(+) cells can more effectively 460 

seed at these secondary sites by shifting their copy number. Future studies to track ecDNA levels 461 

with varying cargoes over space and time, including at metastatic sites in treated patients will be 462 

critical for determining how the dynamic nature of ecDNA contributes to metastases. While we 463 

establish oncogenic copy number shifts as a major contributor to ecDNA(+) cell adaptation, other 464 

factors, including previously reported associations between ecDNA and lower immunogenicity 1,42 –465

or prevalent ecDNA -borne oncogene fusions 37 , may account for increased tumourigenicity of 466 

ecDNA(+) cells in vivo. Oncogene cargo itself could also potentially play a role, including in which 467 

oncogenes and/or immunomodulatory genes and regulatory elements are amplified. Future 468 

studies will be needed to elucidate how immunomodulatory 1 and regulatory 5,46 ecDNA molecules 469 

play unique roles in the tumour microenvironment to potentially contribute to tumour progression 470 

and metastasis .471 

In summary, our results establish ecDNA copy number plasticity as a fundamental 472 

mechanism by which cancer cells generate phenotypic heterogeneity, adapt to therapeutic and 473 

microenvironmental pressures, and evolve toward increased malignancy. These findings 474 

underscore the importance of early ecDNA detection in patients and highlight dynamic ecDNA 475 

copy number regulation as a promising vulnerability for the development of next -generation 476 

combination therapies. 477

Paired scATAC -seq and scRNA -seq analysis 925

Amplicon copy numbers were inferred by calculating fold changes between large genomic 929 

intervals and their local background using our previously developed and validated method 19,48 . To 930

sliding steps. Intervals overlapping ENCODE hg38 blacklist regions were removed due to their 932 

tendency to exhibit artificially high ATAC -seq signal. Focal amplicon regions identified by the 933

regions by high -copy loci. For each window, we computed the average log2(fold change) relative 935 

to the 100 nearest background intervals; assuming a diploid baseline for most of the genome, 936 

copy number was calculated as 2 × 2^log2(fold change). The oncogene amplicon region was 937 

defined as the window containing the gene of interest with the highest inferred copy number and 938 

was added to the Seurat object for copy number –related analyses. 939
