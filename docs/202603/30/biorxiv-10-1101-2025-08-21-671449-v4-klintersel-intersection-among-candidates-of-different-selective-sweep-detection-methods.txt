Abstract 

Detecting signals of natural selection in genomic data often involves applying several methods in parallel. Regions identified by multiple approaches are usually considered strong candidates, as agreement among methods is often taken as supporting evidence. However, the extent to which such overlaps exceed random expectations is rarely evaluated formally. When genomic elements are not independent, coincident candidate sites may arise from the underlying structure of the data rather than from genuine methodological concordance. To address this problem, we introduce two complementary statistical tests designed to evaluate whether the observed overlap among candidate sites detected by different methods exceeds what would be expected by chance. The first is a fast parametric test based on a sequentially conditioned hypergeometric framework that evaluates k-way intersections among candidate sets across genomic windows. The second relies on Monte Carlo simulations and compares the observed inter-method distance profiles with those expected under random association, taking into account the empirical distribution of SNPs along the genome. Introduction 

At the genomic level, natural selection impacts not only alleles with beneficial mutations but also neutral alleles of linked loci, resulting in selective sweeps and a loss of diversity around selected sites [1]. Numerous methods are designed to detect traces of selective sweeps in genomes [2,3]. However, these methods face challenges because similar genomic patterns can arise from different scenarios [3–5]. Some authors [4] argue that the best way to validate candidates would be to simulate data capturing the spectrum of biological processes that could underlie those patterns. These data should then be analyzed with various statistical approaches to evaluate which offers the most reliable results. However, it is not always possible to model the complexity of scenarios (often unknown) that could have given rise to the data. Moreover, there will always be doubts whether enough scenarios have been explored and if the missing ones could yield spurious results. Therefore, a common strategy is to use two or more methods to select the most robust candidates [6]. The problem with this approach is that there is often little overlap between candidates [7], which has led to questioning the reliability of the results obtained through these methods. Additionally, it can be argued that the mere coincidence of results from different statistical analyses does not necessarily validate the analyses due to the patterns of non-independence underlying the biological datasets themselves [8,9]. In this paper, we introduce the software KLinterSel, which implements two complementary statistical tests: the hypergeometric k-intersection (HGkI) and the Kullback-Leibler-like Monte Carlo test (T KL ). These tests evaluate whether the spatial coincidence among candidate SNPs detected by different methods exceeds what would be expected by chance

The HGkI test is a fast parametric approach based on a sequentially conditioned hypergeometric framework that evaluates k-way intersections among candidate sets across genomic windows. By contrast, T KL is a Monte Carlo-based method that operates on inter-method distance patterns: it computes all pairwise distances between candidate SNPs detected by different methods and estimates the expected distance profile under a null model by repeatedly permuting the original SNP positions. The observed distance profile is then compared against this expectation. It should be noted that the tests proposed are not intended to detect selective sweeps directly, nor to validate the biological correctness of candidate loci. Instead, they evaluate whether the spatial coincidence among candidate SNPs detected by different methods exceeds what would be expected by chance given the genomic configuration of the data. Consequently, the approaches are complementary to existing sweep-detection methods rather than a replacement for them. For both tests, KLinterSel computes p-values that quantify the probability of observing the detected level of spatial coincidence under a null hypothesis of random overlap, thereby providing a statistical framework to evaluate whether the observed concordance among candidate sets is compatible with random expectations. In addition, the software computes, for any user-specified distance threshold D, the size of the intersection among candidate lists restricted to SNPs lying within distance D of one another. Both the statistical tests and the intersection analysis can be applied to any number of candidate lists produced by different detection methods. Materials and Methods 

Hypergeometric k-way intersection (HGkI) test 

Parametric methods provide an explicit probabilistic framework to evaluate whether the overlap among candidate sets exceeds that expected under random sampling. The hypergeometric distribution offers a natural model for this purpose when subsets are drawn without replacement from a finite universe. In genome scans for selection, multiple methods are often applied to the same dataset, each producing a list of candidate loci or genomic regions. Even in the absence of a shared selective signal, some overlap among these candidate sets is expected by chance, particularly when the number of tested loci is large or when individual methods report many candidates. The hypergeometric distribution provides a formal way to assess whether the observed overlap exceeds these expectations. Building on this rationale, we introduce the Hypergeometric k-way Intersection (HGkI) test, which quantifies the probability of the observed overlap among candidates detected by k methods under a null model of random association. As a parametric approach based on a known probability distribution, HGkI is computationally efficient and does not require resampling-based simulations.

Defining disjoint windows 

HGkI operates on a discretized representation of the genome. For each chromosome, the genomic coordinate range under analysis is partitioned into non-overlapping windows of fixed width W (bp), covering the portion of the chromosome represented in the input data. Two cases are distinguished: 

(i) SNP-level units (W = 1) 

When W = 1, the test is performed at the SNP level. The universe consists of all SNPs available for analysis in the chromosome, and overlap is defined as exact coincidence of SNP positions across methods. This corresponds to the classical hypergeometric overlap test. 

(ii) Window-level units (W > 1) 

When W > 1, chromosomes are partitioned into disjoint genomic windows of size W bp. The window size is user-defined and applied consistently across methods and chromosomes. Only windows containing at least one SNP are retained, as empty windows cannot be selected and would violate the exchangeability assumption of the hypergeometric null model. Kullback-Leibler-like (T KL ) Monte Carlo test 

Monte Carlo approaches derive null expectations through empirical resampling rather than from an explicit probabilistic model of overlap. Instead of counting coincident SNPs or windows, T KL characterizes concordance among methods through the distribution of

genomic distances between their reported candidates. The observed distance profile is defined as the set of pairwise distances between candidate loci identified by different methods. For each chromosome, all pairwise distances between candidate sets from different methods are computed, yielding a vector of observed distances, denoted Do. This vector summarizes spatial proximity among candidates without requiring exact positional coincidence. To estimate the distance pattern expected by chance, candidate positions are resampled under a null model based on the original SNP dataset. For each method and chromosome, the same number of candidate positions is sampled without replacement from the available SNP positions, preserving both chromosome-specific structure and differences in list sizes among methods. Pairwise distances are then computed for each resampled dataset, producing a vector of simulated distances. This procedure is repeated R times (default R =10,000, adjustable via the --perm argument). For each replicate, distances are sorted in ascending order, and the expected distance profile, denoted De, is obtained by averaging these ordered vectors across replicates. Specifically, the mean of each order statistic (smallest to largest distance) is computed across the R replicates. The entire procedure is performed independently for each chromosome. The computational complexity of estimating De is O(Rk 2n2), where k is the number of methods and n the maximum number of candidate positions per method. To manage computational demands, the program checks available memory and processes permutations sequentially or in parallel as needed. In practice, when n is large, reducing the number of permutations to 1,000 may be necessary; benchmark tests show that results remain virtually unchanged relative to 10,000 permutations.

Statistical significance is assessed by Monte Carlo resampling, using the same procedure. Specifically, the statistic is evaluated only when the median of the observed distances ( Mo) is less than or equal to that of the expected profile ( Me); otherwise, the result is classified as non-significant. Input data and software usage 

Input files 

Both HGkI and T KL tests are implemented in the software KLinterSel. When running the program, the user must specify the input files to be analyzed. The first file contains the genomic positions of all analyzed SNPs, whereas the remaining files contain the candidate sites identified by different selection-detection methods. One file is required per method, with a minimum of two methods; thus, at least three input files are needed: one with all SNP positions and two with the candidate SNPs to be compared. The basic format for both genomic positions and candidate files is a simple text file (.csv, .tsv, or .txt) with a header row and two columns: chromosome identifier and genomic position. The program also accepts genomic position files in .map format (without header) and candidate files in .norm format. The number of input files is not limited, except by the available computational resources. 

Intersection lists 

The results of the HGkI or T KL tests identify chromosomes where the overlap or distance patterns between candidates from different methods are significantly smaller than expected by chance, given the SNP locations in the original dataset. Additionally, we can be interested in identifying groups of candidates from different methods at a specific physical distance ≤

D (default D = 10 kb, can be changed with the --dist argument). We refer to these sites detected by different methods and lying at a distance ≤ D, as intersections. Note that if we have k methods and we focus on their intersections, we will have a maximum distance of ( k -

1) D within the k-tuple of each intersection. Therefore, to maintain a maximum distance D

within the k-tuples, we must define the --dist parameter as D / ( k - 1).

Software usage 

Precompiled binaries are provided for Windows, Linux, and macOS (arm64) and are expected to run on most versions of these operating systems. Alternatively, the program can be installed from the source files available in the GitHub repository. By default, the Monte Carlo T KL test is executed; to run the hypergeometric test, the --HG flag can be specified. Detailed information on installation, usage, program options, and file formats, as well as an example dataset (test_dataset.zip), is provided in the program manual and on the project’s GitHub repository.

KLinterSel analysis options 

For the HGkI test, rather than selecting a single window size, we interpret results across multiple spatial scales. In addition to exact SNP-level coincidences, which are preserved across all window sizes and are inherently informative when the test indicates a non-random signal, the most robust candidates are those showing consistency across several window sizes, particularly when significance at smaller scales is retained. Accordingly, the HGkI test is applied across a range of window sizes, including windows of size 1 (individual SNPs), 3 × 10 3,3 × 10 4, 10 5, 3 × 10⁵,10 6, 1.5 × 10⁶ and 2 × 10⁶ base pairs. For the T KL test, we use 10,000 permutations (the default setting) to estimate the expected distance profile. The program provides a --paint option to generate histograms of the observed and expected-by-chance distance profiles. To define intersections between the candidates, we use a maximum pairwise distance of 1 kb (--dist 1000).

For a given chromosome length and total number of SNPs, we considered several spatial distributions: (i) uniformly distributed SNPs, (ii) SNPs clustered toward one or both chromosomal extremes, (iii) SNPs clustered toward the center of the chromosome, and (iv) SNPs following the empirical distribution observed in the original dataset. Simulation framework 

For each generated SNP universe, candidate positions were randomly sampled in numbers matching those reported by each method, under the null hypothesis of random association among methods. Importantly, the null model is constructed independently for each chromosome using its empirical SNP distribution, both in real-data analyses and in simulations, so chromosome-specific genomic features (e.g., SNP density or spatial clustering) are inherently incorporated into the test, thereby preserving chromosome-specific genomic structure. In all simulations, explicit genomic positions were retained. For the T KL test, these positions were used directly to compute inter-method distance profiles, whereas for HGkI only the number and genomic allocation of candidates within windows were required. For the HGkI test, false-positive behavior was evaluated independently for each SNP distribution scenario. For a given scenario, R = 100 independent realizations of the SNP universe were generated. For each universe realization, S = 100 independent sets of candidate loci were sampled for the k methods under the null hypothesis. This yielded a total of 10,000 replicates per scenario, from which the distribution of HGkI p-values was obtained and used to assess calibration and false-positive rates. Estimation modes 

False-positive rates were estimated using two complementary modes, referred to as per-chromosome and any-chromosome , which capture different aspects of statistical behavior in multi-chromosome analyses. 

Per-chromosome estimation mode 

In the per-chromosome mode, the false-positive rate (FPR) was estimated independently for each chromosome: a replicate was counted as a false positive for a given chromosome if the corresponding test statistic yielded a p-value below the chosen significance threshold. This mode measures the probability that a single chromosome produces a significant result under the null hypothesis and therefore provides a chromosome-wise assessment of calibration. The significance threshold was set to α = 0.05, independently of the test and spatial scenario. 

Any-chromosome estimation mode 

In contrast, the any-chromosome mode evaluates false positives at the experiment-wide

Power Analysis Under Controlled Alternative Scenarios 

To evaluate the power of the HGkI and T KL statistics under controlled alternative scenarios with non-random spatial concordance among methods, we performed a power analysis using the same simulation architecture adopted for false-positive rate estimation ( R

independent SNP universes × S replicate candidate sets per universe). In contrast to the false-positive study, which evaluates calibration under the null hypothesis, the present analysis introduces controlled alternative models that generate structured spatial concordance among methods. These alternative models are applied on top of the different underlying SNP distributions considered in the null scenarios (e.g. uniform, empirical, and related variants). Consequently, it is expected to increase window-based intersection counts (HGkI) and, to a lesser extent, the frequency of short inter-method distances (T KL ). In the present study, we set f = 0.6, meaning that 60% of candidates per method were drawn from the shared hotspot, representing a moderate degree of spatial concordance. Because candidates are always drawn from the empirical SNP universe, the genomic SNP density structure is preserved under both null and alternative models. Thus, detected signals reflect concordance among methods rather than variation in underlying SNP density. 

(B) Distance compression model (global distance shift) 

For each chromosome, several shared clusters were defined by randomly selecting real SNPs as cluster centers. Around each center, a local pool of SNPs was defined within ±δ base pairs. For each method i with ni candidates, a fraction φ (= 0.9) of candidates was sampled from the union of these local pools, whereas the remaining candidates were drawn from the chromosomal background. If the local pools contained insufficient SNPs to satisfy the shared fraction, additional candidates were sampled from the chromosome to complete the set. This model increases spatial clustering and reduces inter-method distances without enforcing exact overlaps among methods. In the present study we used 2 and 8 clusters with δ = 0.25 Mb and δ = 1.8 Mb, respectively.

In contrast, the distance compression model defines one or more clusters centered on real SNPs and samples candidates from local SNP pools around these centers, with the remaining candidates drawn from the chromosomal background. This approach increases spatial clustering and reduces inter-method distances without enforcing exact overlaps or restricting candidates to a single interval. Because the two models introduce spatial structure through different mechanisms, their parameters are not directly comparable but instead reflect the strength of spatial concentration within each framework. The hotspot model modifies the spatial structure of the genomic background, whereas the compression model acts directly on candidate sets by placing them around shared cluster centers. As a result, HGkI is expected to be more sensitive to hotspot-like alternatives that generate concentrated overlap patterns, while T KL may be more sensitive to compression-like alternatives that shift the distribution of inter-method distances. Together, these models capture distinct forms of spatial concordance among candidate sets and are intended to generate different dependence structures rather than to represent specific biological scenarios. 

Simulation framework 

For each chromosome scenario, we generated R independent SNP universes with predefined spatial distributions (uniform, extreme clustering, central clustering, and empirical), as previously described. For each universe, S independent replicate candidate datasets were generated for k methods, matching the observed per-method candidate counts for that chromosome. Because the simulation framework used to estimate statistical power is computationally demanding, R = 20 realizations of the SNP universe were used. For each replicate dataset, the statistical test (HGkI or T KL ) was applied independently to every chromosome (per-chromosome mode), producing a p-value for each chromosome. A chromosome was counted as successfully detected if its p-value was less than or equal to the significance level (α = 0.05). Power was then computed for each chromosome as the proportion of significant chromosome-level tests across all replicates. In this way, power represents the probability that a chromosome under the alternative model is detected as significant in a single chromosome-level test.

Discussion 

It is common practice to apply several selection-detection methods in parallel when searching for genomic signatures of natural selection, based on the assumption that agreement among methods increases confidence in the inferred candidates [6]. In practice, however, different approaches often show only limited overlap [7], which has raised questions about the reliability of results obtained either from individual methods or from their combined use. Ideally, the methods applied should rely on distinct methodological principles. Yet even conceptually similar approaches frequently yield results that differ substantially, both in the number of candidates detected and in their genomic location. In this context, concordance among SNPs identified by different selection scans, particularly when they occur in nearby genomic regions, is often interpreted as supporting evidence when prioritizing candidate loci for further investigation. Assessing such concordance, however, is not always straightforward when hundreds of candidates are involved and exact matches are rare. This is not surprising, as different methods may detect signals within the same genomic region or gene without necessarily identifying the same SNP, owing to methodological differences and linkage-drag effects. To address this issue, we introduced two complementary statistical methods to quantify agreement among SNPs identified by different selection-scan methods: a parametric test 

36 

> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted March 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.08.21.671449 doi: bioRxiv preprint

based on the hypergeometric distribution (HGkI) and a non-parametric, permutation-based test (T KL ). The HGkI test identifies coincident SNPs across methods and evaluates whether the observed number of overlaps exceeds that expected by chance. Besides exact SNP-level coincidences, the test can also be applied using genomic windows, within which all SNPs are collapsed into a single spatial unit. Under this construction, the test evaluates whether the number of coincident windows among methods is greater than expected under the null model. Choosing an appropriate window size is not straightforward. Instead of fixing a single spatial scale, we applied the HGkI test across a range of increasing window sizes and considered chromosomes significant when they showed consistent signals across multiple scales. Increasing window size can improve power when true signals are spatially close but not identical, because nearby candidates are collapsed into the same window. However, larger windows also reduce the number of independent spatial units and increase the probability of random overlaps under the null hypothesis. If windows become too large given the SNP density, spatial resolution is lost and the test becomes uninformative. Like its parametric counterpart, the T KL test evaluates whether the degree of overlap among candidates identified by different methods can be explained by chance given the genomic distribution of SNPs, or whether the observed co-occurrence is unlikely under neutrality and therefore potentially indicative of a genuine selective signal. Unlike HGkI, however, T KL does not focus on discrete overlaps but instead considers the full distribution of inter-method distances between candidate SNPs. SNP positions used in selective sweep analyses are rarely uniformly distributed across the genome. Clustering commonly arises due to linkage disequilibrium, genotyping design, or filtering procedures. Assuming a uniform null model 

37 

> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted March 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.08.21.671449 doi: bioRxiv preprint

may therefore produce expected distances larger than those typically observed in empirical SNP sets. T KL addresses this issue by generating expected distance profiles directly from the empirical distribution of SNP positions. This strategy is inherently more conservative, as it provides null expectations that better reflect the underlying genomic structure. Nevertheless, the program also allows expected distance profiles to be generated under a uniform assumption when this is required for comparison with other methods or theoretical expectations. When analyzing data, a potential pitfall emerges if two methods that are essentially the same, or operate on the same core principles, are inadvertently or deliberately included. In these cases, the identified candidate regions and their locations will likely be very similar. Consequently, the observed overlap might stem from this methodological overlap rather than a true biological signal of selection. To mitigate this, KLinterSel employs a cautious default setting: if two input files contain a comparable number of candidates and at least 95% of the candidates in one file are also present in the other, the subset file is excluded from the analysis. Two points are worth noting. First, using nearly identical methodologies to confirm selective candidates provides little additional information and is generally not standard practice. Second, even when similar methods are included, applying the test still represents a more conservative strategy than ignoring the issue altogether. We illustrated the use of KLinterSel by analysing concordance among four selection scans applied to detect candidates for divergent selection in Cerastoderma edule . Although XP-EHH and XP-nSL are expected to produce partially similar results, their overlap remained

Analyses were performed on both DEG-associated SNPs and RAD-seq SNPs, restricting attention to chromosomes showing significant patterns in both datasets. For more than half of the chromosomes, both HGkI and T KL indicated that the observed agreement could be explained by chance. For the remaining chromosomes, however, concordance patterns were unlikely under the null model, suggesting non-random agreement among methods. After filtering, a region of ~0.3 Mb on chromosome 18 was identified, although this result should be considered a proof of concept. The use of a stringent coincidence threshold (≤1 kb) is conservative and likely underestimates the number of relevant regions; larger thresholds would increase overlap detection without affecting T KL , which is based on full distance distributions. HGkI and T KL provide complementary statistical evaluations of concordance. HGkI is a fast parametric test focused on regional overlap counts, whereas T KL is a Monte Carlo approach based on full distance profiles. As a result, T KL can fail to detect chromosomes where local clustering is offset by long-range dispersion, while HGkI remains sensitive to local overlap signals. 

False-Positive rate and power 

False-positive analyses show that HGkI is consistently conservative, whereas T KL is well calibrated to the nominal significance level. As a result, HGkI maintains strong control of family-wise error rates across chromosomes, while T KL behaves as expected under the nominal level and may require multiple-testing correction in genome-wide analyses.

Simulation scenarios further illustrate that HGkI and T KL respond differently to spatial configurations: HGkI is more sensitive to localized clustering, whereas T KL captures deviations in full distance distributions, making the two approaches complementary for detecting concordance among candidate sets. Statistical power in both tests increases with the number of SNPs per chromosome, reflecting the greater spatial information available, and remains robust across typical RAD-seq and transcriptomic datasets. Reduced power is mainly observed in cases of very low SNP density or few candidate loci, where spatial information is limited, although this does not affect type-I error due to chromosome-specific null models. Importantly, lack of significant overlap or distance deviation should not be interpreted as absence of selection, but rather as lack of evidence for non-random concordance. In this context, KLinterSel is not intended to detect selection directly, but to provide a complementary framework to assess whether agreement among candidate SNPs exceeds random expectations. 

Conclusions 

This work illustrates the utility of KLinterSel for assessing statistical agreement among candidate loci identified by different selection-detection methods, here applied to genomic data from Cerastoderma edule . The analysis shows that candidates detected by the four methods are significantly closer than expected by chance in several chromosomes, whereas no such pattern is observed in others. Taken together, our results show that HGkI and T KL 

capture complementary aspects of spatial agreement and respond to different configurations of candidate distribution. This allows concordance to be evaluated across multiple spatial scales without relying on a single, arbitrarily chosen window size.
