Abstract 41 A central but poorly resolved question regards how genome architecture shapes the selection response and 42 repeatability of polygenic adaptation. We addressed this question using Evolve-and-Resequence 43 experiments under rapid salinity decline in two sibling species (clades) of the invasive copepod 44 

Eurytemora affinis complex that differ strikingly in chromosome number (15 versus 4). The 4-45 chromosome genome arose from chromosomal fusions of the ancestral 15-chromosome genome, bringing 46 together coadapted alleles at fusion sites. Across 10–20 generations of selection, both clades adapted to 47 low salinity but followed divergent evolutionary trajectories. The selection lines of the 15-chromosome 48 clade exhibited highly parallel genomic responses, whereas the 4-chromosome clade lines showed 49 delayed and less repeatable responses. Forward genetic simulations revealed that strong synergistic 50 epistasis among beneficial alleles best explained elevated parallelism in the 15-chromosome clade. 51 Additional simulations varying chromosome number and epistasis revealed that strong positive epistasis, 52 combined with high chromosome numbers, enhances parallelism by enabling recombination to assemble 53 coadapted allelic combinations. In contrast, the high starting frequency of beneficial alleles in the 4-54 chromosome clade lines indicated selection on standing variation, likely acting on alternative linked 55 haplotypes. These findings demonstrate that genome architecture and gene–gene interactions jointly 56 determine the dynamics and predictability of polygenic adaptation. 57 58

Introduction 59 

A fundamental question in evolutionary biology regards how selection acts on complex traits and the 60 mechanisms and repeatability of polygenic adaptation 1-9 . When faced with sudden environmental change, 61 populations can adapt surprisingly quickly, even when selection acts on polygenic traits encoded by many 62 genes 4,10-15 . Polygenic adaptation is crucially important in response to environmental change, as selection 63 in response to abiotic variables often acts on physiological traits, which are typically polygenic in nature. 64 Despite its importance, until recently mechanisms of polygenic adaptation had remained poorly 65 understood, as dissecting polygenic responses requires information on genome architecture and genome-66 wide selection responses of populations. 67 Independent populations, or experimental replicates, may involve similar genetic solutions that 68 reflect parallel evolutionary responses 4,13 . Alternatively, populations may experience divergent genetic 69 paths that nevertheless produce comparable adaptive outcomes at the phenotypic level 3,4,9,16-18 . Dissecting 70 the specific mechanisms of parallel evolution of complex traits is crucial for understanding how natural 71 selection acts on populations and which factors constrain or promote repeatability 3,17 . In addition, 72 determining the repeatability of selection responses is crucial for understanding the speed and extent to 73 which populations could evolve in response to rapid environmental stress and avoid extinctions in the 74 face of rapid global change 19-21 .75 The roles of genome architecture underlying polygenic traits in impacting selection responses and 76 adaptive repeatability have remained poorly explored. While the number of loci contributing to a trait is 77 an important factor influencing constraints on natural selection (e.g., pleiotropy) and the extent of 78 parallelism among replicate evolutionary events 5,8,17,22 , the localization and arrangement of these loci 79 within the genome are also likely to be critically important 23,24 . In particular, genomic features such as the 80 positions and arrangement of genes, chromosome number, and recombination rate influence gene–gene 81 interactions and determine the extent to which loci are co-inherited and co-regulated 25-31 .82 Among genomic features, the number of chromosomes, for a given genome size, could have 83 profound impacts on how selection acts on coadapted gene complexes. On the one hand, genomes with 84 fewer chromosomes and lower recombination rates may preserve combinations of coadapted alleles, 85 making independent populations more likely to reuse the same multi-locus linked haplotypes, thereby 86 increasing parallelism. However, low recombination can also lead to the maintenance of distinct 87 beneficial haplotypes within a population, enabling different replicate lines or populations to fix 88 alternative haplotypes and thereby reducing parallelism 32,33 . In addition, linked selection would likely 89 reduce parallelism in genomes with longer chromosomes, as beneficial allelic combinations would be 90 linked to long stretches of neutral and potentially segregating loci, leading to selection favoring different 91 allelic combinations 34-37 . To explore how genome architecture impacts the selection response and repeatability of 110 polygenic adaptation, we examined the selection responses of sibling species with differing chromosome 111 numbers in the copepod Eurytemora affinis species complex 29,42 . Populations from this species complex 112 are native to saline habitats, but have repeatedly invaded freshwater habitats within the past 80 years, 113 likely due to shipping and ballast water transport 43 (Figure 1a). This species complex is composed of 114 sibling species (clades) that differ drastically in haploid chromosome number (4–15 chromosomes; Figure 115 1b) but possess similar gene content and genome size 29,42 . Despite the large differences in genome 116 architecture, many of the same loci show signatures of natural selection during salinity transitions across 117 the different clades 4,13,14,44 . Most notably, our prior study revealed that the 4-chromosome genome 118 (Atlantic clade of the E. affinis complex, E. carolleeae ) arose from multiple chromosomal fusions of the 119 ancestral 15-chromosome genome (Europe clade, E. affinis proper), bringing together coadapted alleles at 120 the fusion sites (Figure 1c) 29 . As such, this contrast of pre- and post-fusion genomes provides an 121 unprecedented natural model to test how genome architecture impacts selection response in populations 122 with these genomes. 123 

Our prior results suggest that different mechanisms of adaptation might be operating depending 124 on chromosome number. For the Europe clade, with a high number of chromosomes (15), in our prior 125 Evolve-and-Resequence (E&R) study, we had found strikingly high levels of parallelism among selection 126 lines adapting to salinity decline in the laboratory 4. Based on theoretical simulations, this unexpected high 127 level of parallelism was found to be consistent with the action of positive synergistic epistasis among 128 adaptive alleles, bringing coadapted alleles together within genomes 4. We also observed an exceptionally 129 high degree of parallel evolution during saline-to-freshwater invasions by wild populations from the 130 Atlantic (4 chromosomes) and Gulf (7 chromosomes) clades, with selection often favoring the same SNPs 131 during independent invasions 13 . In this prior study, we found that a large proportion of SNPs under 132 directional selection during invasions were at intermediate frequency in the native populations, suggesting 133 the maintenance of genetic variation in the native range through balancing selection 13 . This balanced 134 polymorphism in the native range could then be favored by natural selection during invasions and could 135 help promote parallel evolution 12,32 .136 These prior results above suggest that positive synergistic epistasis and selection on standing 137 genetic variation could both play important roles in driving parallel evolution 1,4,13,46 . However, we do not 138 fully understand the conditions under which one mechanism might prevail over the other. Might genome 139 architecture, particularly chromosome number, impact the selection response of a population? And might 140 the selection regime of a population influence the particular genome architecture that might be favorable 141 in facilitating adaptation to environmental change? 142 143

158 Thus, to address these questions, we determined the impacts of genome architecture, particularly 159 chromosome number, on the repeatability of selection response using Evolve-and-Resequence (E&R) 160 experiments 5,47 under rapid salinity decline in two clades that differ in chromosome number (15 versus 4). 161 We also performed simulations to explore the selective mechanisms that might be involved in driving 162 parallel evolution. Our specific goals were to (1) determine how genome architecture affects the 163 trajectories of beneficial alleles and the number and effect sizes of alleles contributing to adaptation, (2) 164 examine the impacts of genome architecture on the extent of parallelism among replicate selection lines in 165 the E&R experiments, and (3) explore the selective mechanisms underlying the parallel responses, such as 166 the potential roles of positive epistasis, linkage of beneficial alleles, and selection on standing genetic 167 variation. 168

To achieve these goals, we imposed laboratory selection on replicate selection lines from the two 169 clades with different karyotypes (15 versus 4 chromosomes; Figure 1b) under declining salinity for 10–20 170 generations (Figure 2a, b). We then performed time-resolved whole-genome pooled sequencing (E&R) to 171 monitor beneficial allele frequency changes in the replicate selection lines across generations (Figure 2a, 172 b). In addition, we employed forward genetic simulations tailored to the genomic parameters of each 173 clade (effective population size, number of selected loci, and chromosome number) to evaluate which 174 evolutionary processes best explained the observed extent of parallelism. In particular, we examined how 175 chromosome number influenced the relative importance of positive synergistic epistasis versus linkage 176 among beneficial alleles in shaping parallel adaptation. 177 

Given the sharp difference in genome architecture between the Europe and Atlantic clades and 178 the fact that the four chromosomes of the Atlantic clade arose from fusions of the fifteen Europe clade 179 chromosomes, we hypothesized that the two clades would exhibit divergent selection responses. 180 Specifically, we hypothesized that chromosome number, the resulting recombination rate, and the 181 positions of adaptive alleles on the chromosomes would result in varying responses to selection and 182 differing degrees of parallel evolution. We predicted that greater chromosome number and higher 183 recombination rate, such as those of the Europe clade, would enable positive epistatic interactions among 184 many unlinked loci to drive highly parallel responses across replicate lines, as we observed in our 185 previous study 4. In contrast, we hypothesized that selection on the Atlantic clade lines would act on large 186 haplotypes with linked loci, often brought together by chromosomal fusions at regions of reduced 187 recombination 29 . In addition, given our prior results of signatures of balancing selection in ancestral saline 188 populations 13 , we predicted that haplotypes with linked beneficial alleles would already be present in the 189 native range. These pre-existing haplotypes on different genomic backgrounds could be favored during 190 habitat change, consistent with soft selective sweeps that produce weaker selection signals and lower 191 levels of parallelism. This effect would be magnified in genomes with longer chromosomes due to greater 192 linkage with neutral polymorphic sites 12,32 .193 Parallel evolution has long been a central topic of fundamental importance in evolutionary 194 biology 6,48,49 . The degree of parallel evolution during replicate events provides insights into the extent to 195 which evolutionary pathways are labile or constrained. This study is noteworthy for examining the 196 impacts of genome architecture on the extent of parallel evolution and exploring the mechanisms of 197 selection involved. By combining laboratory experimental evolution with whole-genome time-series data, 198 as well as forward simulations of our data, our study directly links chromosome number to the 199 mechanisms and predictability of polygenic adaptation. Previous experimental evolution studies have 200 focused on species or populations with similar genome architectures, especially Drosophila 16-18,50,51 . Results and Discussion 208 

Rapid polygenic selection response to declining salinity in two copepod clades 209 

We subjected laboratory selection lines from two E. affinis complex clades that differ in haploid 210 chromosome number (15 in E. affinis proper versus 4 in E. carolleeae ) to rapid salinity decline across 10– 211 20 generations (Figure 2). Our experimental design introduced salinity reductions by gradually 212 transitioning individuals from brackish water (15 practical salinity units, PSU) to fresh water (0 PSU, 213 Lake Michigan water, ~300 μS/cm). Multiple replicate lines (7–10 per clade; Figure 2a, b) were 214 established from outbred wild populations, from Kiel, Germany, for the Europe clade ( E. affinis proper) 215 and from St. Lawrence marsh (Baie de L’Isle Verte), Quebec, Canada, for the Atlantic clade ( E. 216 

carolleeae ) (Figure 1a). Salinity was gradually reduced each generation from 15 PSU to 0 PSU over six 217 generations (Figure 2a, b; see Methods), imposing strong selection for freshwater tolerance while 218 avoiding immediate extinction. Thereafter, populations were maintained under freshwater conditions for 219 up to 10 generations in the Europe clade and 20 generations in the Atlantic clade selection lines. Replicate 220 control lines for each clade were maintained at constant salinity (15 PSU) to account for potential 221 laboratory drift or beaker effects. 222 Selection lines from both the Europe and Atlantic clades showed rapid genome-wide selection 223 responses to declining salinity (Figure 2). We identified allele frequency changes in replicate selection 224 lines by sequencing pooled genomic DNA from each replicate at multiple time points. To identify 225 candidate SNPs under selection, we applied Cochran–Mantel–Haenszel (CMH) and Chi-square tests, as 226 well as linear mixed models (LMMs), to detect SNPs that deviated from neutral expectations in the 227 selection lines and SNPs that diverged significantly between selection and control lines. These methods 228 yielded 43,792 single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) (out of 474,041 SNPs) in the Europe clade and 229 9,417 SNPs (out of 195,136 SNPs) in the Atlantic clade (Supplementary Table S1). 230 To account for genetic linkage among the SNPs, we grouped candidate SNPs with signatures of 231 selection into “haplotype blocks” 17,54 . These haplotype blocks under selection, treated as “selected alleles” 232 in this study, consisted of proximate SNPs with correlated allele frequency shifts. We tracked the 233 trajectory of each selected allele (haplotype block) via the median SNP frequency within that allele. In 234 total, the Europe clade selection lines showed significant allele frequency changes at 10,224 SNPs, 235 clustered into 261 selected alleles (Supplementary Table S2; Supplementary Figures 1–15). Contrasting selection responses of clades with high- versus low-chromosome number 279 

While both the Europe and Atlantic clade selection lines exhibited rapid evolutionary responses to 280 laboratory salinity decline, the evolutionary trajectories underlying their selection responses were 281 strikingly different (Figure 3). In the Europe clade selection lines, we observed an immediate and 282 pronounced rise in the frequencies of beneficial alleles (haplotype blocks) (Figure 3a), which typically 283 started at low frequency (Figure 3c). In contrast, the selection response of the Atlantic clade lines 284 appeared muted at the early generations in the experiment (Figure 3b), acting on standing genetic 285 variation of intermediate frequency alleles (Figure 3d). These contrasting selection responses were 286 influenced by differences in the starting frequency of the beneficial alleles (Figure 3c, d). 287 The profoundly different selection responses between the clades suggest that differences in 288 genome architecture between the clades led to contrasting mechanisms of selection. Specifically, the 289 selection lines of the pre-fusion 15-chromosome Europe clade exhibited a rapid and strong selection 290 response with a high mean selection coefficient of s = 0.06 ± 0.001 SE (with individual loci reaching up 291 to s = 0.17 during Generations 0–10; Figure 3e). The selected alleles (haplotype blocks) had low starting 292 frequencies at Generation 0 (mean = 0.33 ± 0.01 SE across the base experimental lines; Figure 3a, c) and 293 then increased dramatically in frequency by Generation 10 (Figure 3a). Across the 261 selected alleles 294 (haplotype blocks) in these selection lines, allele frequencies rose by an average of 28.7% (mean increase 295 in absolute frequency of 0.13 ± 0.002 SE) in the selection lines by Generation 10, indicating a robust 296 selection response. 297 In contrast, the selection lines of the post-fusion 4-chromosome Atlantic clade showed a minimal 298 selection response up to Generation 10 but then ultimately exhibited a substantial rise in beneficial allele 299 frequency by Generation 20. Specifically, within the first 10 generations of selection, the selection 300 response of the 66 selected alleles (haplotype blocks) was minimal, showing only a 6.3% average 301 frequency increase by Generation 10 (mean increase in absolute frequency of 0.035 ± 0.005 SE; Figure 302 3b). Adaptive architecture of different clades: genomic distribution of selected loci 354 

The greater difficulty and more time required for recombination to break up linkages between alleles in 355 the Atlantic clade versus the Europe clade is consistent with differences in the genomic distribution of 356 loci under selection. Here, we refer to “adaptive architecture” as the genetic composition and organization 357 underlying an adaptive trait 8.358 As discussed in the previous section, chromosomal fusion events might have important 359 consequences for how populations from different clades respond to natural selection. A notable feature of 360 these genomes is that the 15 chromosomes in the Europe clade genome fused to form 4 chromosomes in 361 the Atlantic clade genome 29,42 . Such fusion events have reshaped genome architecture by bringing 362 together key ion transport-related genes from telomeric regions to the fusion sites 29 . Contrasting extent of parallel selection response in different clades 461 

The repeatability of genomic responses to selection (parallel evolution) differed dramatically between the 462 selection lines of the Europe and Atlantic clades, despite both clades showing a highly polygenic response 463 to salinity decline (Figure 3). The pre-fusion 15-chromosome Europe clade showed a highly parallel 464 genomic response to salinity decline across independent replicates, with many of the same alleles 465 consistently favored across the selection lines (Figure 4a, see details below). In contrast, the post-fusion 466 4-chromosome Atlantic clade showed a much lower extent of parallelism among the selection lines than 467 the Europe clade, particularly during the first 10 generations of selection (Figure 4a), consistent with the 468 muted selection response in the first 10 generations (Figure 3b, previous section). Here, we define 469 “parallel selection” as the case in which the same allele significantly increases in frequency across 470 multiple replicate selection lines. Greater “parallelism” would refer to the same allele increasing in 471 frequency in a greater number of selection lines. 472 To quantify the extent of parallelism of selection response between selection lines from the two 473 clades, we applied three complementary statistical approaches: (1) the Jaccard index, to compare pairwise 474 overlap of selected alleles between replicate lines relative to null simulations of our data, (2) linear 475 model-based slope analysis, to test whether allele frequency changes were consistent in direction and 476 magnitude across replicates, and (3) principal component analysis (PCA), to assess the clustering of 477 replicate lines based on their genome-wide allele frequency changes. 478 To determine the degree of parallelism among replicate selection lines, we first compared the 479 empirical pairwise Jaccard index to the distribution of Jaccard indices generated under a null expectation 480 of independent allele-frequency changes 57 . For both clades, we found that the empirical Jaccard index at a 481 given generation (Figure 4a, solid lines) was significantly greater than the null expectation (Figure 4a, 482 dashed lines) (Mann–Whitney test, all pairwise contrasts two-tailed P < 0.05; Supplementary Table S9). 483 Thus, even the Atlantic clade selection lines exhibited significantly greater parallel evolution than the null 484 expectation, despite much lower parallelism than the Europe clade (see next paragraph) (Supplementary 485 Table S9). 486 Using the Jaccard index, we found pronounced differences in the extent of parallelism between 487 selection lines of the two clades. Role of positive epistasis in driving parallel evolution 598 

The divergent extent of parallel evolution between the clades (previous section) raises questions regarding 599 the mechanisms that drive parallel evolution in these clades. As mentioned above, our prior experimental 600 results 4 had revealed that the high degree of parallelism among selection lines in the Europe clade was 601 consistent with positive epistasis among the alleles favored by selection 62,63 . In contrast, in the Atlantic 602 clade, low recombination and large linkage blocks would make it less likely that the repeated assembly of 603 the same beneficial allelic combinations across replicates would drive parallelism among the selection 604 lines (see previous section). Significant differences 701 were apparent only between a fully linked genome (one chromosome) and the multi-chromosome 702 genomes (Mann–Whitney test, two-tailed P < 0.0001; Supplementary Table S11) (Figure 6a, b). Conclusions 737 

Our study demonstrates that rapid selection responses to a shared environmental stress can proceed 738 through radically different genomic mechanisms due to different adaptive architectures, with contrasting 739 consequences for evolutionary trajectories and repeatability. In particular, in the pre-fusion 15-740 chromosome Europe clade, the higher recombination, resulting from greater numbers of chromosomes, 741 allowed synergistic positive epistasis among genomically dispersed adaptive alleles to generate highly 742 parallel genomic responses across replicate lines. These adaptive alleles tended to be concentrated closer 743 to the edges of the chromosomes in the Europe clade, relative to the Atlantic clade, further enhancing the 744 effects of recombination. In contrast, in the post-fusion 4-chromosome Atlantic clade, adaptation occurred 745 primarily through selection on alternative pre-existing haplotypes of linked adaptive alleles, producing 746 less parallel outcomes in the short run. 747 In selection lines from both clades, laboratory selection acted on standing genetic variation rather 748 than de novo mutations during the short timeframe of this study. However, the extent to which standing 749 variation contributed to the selection response depended jointly on the starting frequency of beneficial 750 alleles and the extent of linkage among the alleles within each genome. In the Europe clade, beneficial 751 alleles generally started at lower frequencies and were distributed across many chromosomes with weaker 752 linkage among loci. In such a case, higher recombination allowed alleles from different genomic 753 backgrounds to repeatedly assemble into the same sets of allelic combinations across replicate lines, 754 promoting the consistent rise of the same alleles and leading to strong parallelism. By contrast, in the 755 Atlantic clade, beneficial alleles were already at intermediate to high frequencies and were embedded 756 within large haplotype blocks due to extensive linkage. Consequently, selection acted largely on a 757 multitude of pre-existing haplotypes. Thus, for the Atlantic clade lines, this soft selective sweep favored 758 different linked allelic combinations in different lines and reduced the likelihood that the same alleles 759 would respond to selection in parallel. 760 Together, these findings provide direct empirical evidence that genome architecture, particularly 761 chromosome number and the recombination landscape, can shape the mode and mechanism of polygenic 762 adaptation. Our study bridges population genomics and evolutionary theory by supporting a central role 763 for synergistic epistasis and its interaction with recombination in driving parallel adaptation. Moreover, 764 our results reveal how chromosomal fusions can constrain recombination and promote soft selective 765 sweeps, thereby redirecting evolutionary trajectories. Given the accelerating pace of climate-driven 766 environmental change, genome architecture may represent a key determinant of both the capacity for 767 adaptation and its predictability. More broadly, this work emphasizes that the repeatability of evolution 768 depends not only on the selective environment but also on the genetic and structural genomic substrate on 769 which selection acts. 770

Experimental populations and Evolve-and-Resequence design 773 

Evolve-and-Resequence (E&R) experiments were performed using populations from two clades of the 774 

Eurytemora affinis species complex, from the Europe clade ( E. affinis proper, 15 chromosomes) and the 775 Atlantic clade ( E. carolleeae, 4 chromosomes). Wild copepods from the Europe clade were collected 776 from Kiel, Germany (54°20′N, 10°09′E), from a salinity of 15 PSU, in 2017 and 2018 4. Copepod samples 777 from the Atlantic clade were collected from a brackish marsh at Baie de L’Isle Verte in the St. Lawrence 778 estuary, Quebec, Canada (48°00′16″N, 69°25′01″W), where salinity fluctuates between 5 and 30 PSU, in 779 2020 and 2021. For each clade, approximately 1,000 individuals were combined to maximize genetic 780 diversity and subsequently maintained in the laboratory incubators at 15 PSU (practical salinity units) and 781 12 °C for 3–4 generations to acclimate to laboratory conditions and promote random mating and 782 recombination. 783 After acclimation, each clade’s population was subdivided into replicate lines in separate 784 containers (beakers). For the Europe clade, two Generation 0 pooled samples (25 males and 25 females 785 per pool) were collected before subdivision to characterize starting allele frequencies. The mixed culture 786 was then divided into 14 beakers, with 10 designated as selection lines and 4 as control lines 4. For the 787 Atlantic clade, 13 beakers were subdivided, comprising 7 selection lines and 6 control lines. One 788 generation after this subdivision (to minimize potential founder effects), Generation 0 samples were 789 collected from each line by pooling 50 adults (25 males and 25 females per pool). For both clades, each 790 beaker initially contained ~500 mixed-stage copepods derived from the acclimated population. 791 Selection lines in each clade were subjected to a stepwise salinity decline to impose strong natural 792 selection for freshwater tolerance (Figure 2). Starting from 15 PSU, salinity was gradually reduced in 793 each generation until reaching freshwater conditions (0 PSU) over six generations. Specifically, in the 794 selection lines of both Europe and Atlantic clades, salinity was lowered from 15 PSU to 10 PSU in 795 Generation 1, then to 5, 1, 0.1, 0.01, and finally to 0 PSU by Generation 6. This gradual protocol 796 prevented acute osmotic shock, allowing the populations to adapt over multiple generations. After 797 reaching 0 PSU, selection lines were maintained in fresh water for additional generations of selection. 798 Specifically, the Europe clade lines were maintained until Generation 10, whereas the Atlantic clade lines 799 were continued until Generation 20. Throughout the experiment, replicate control lines for each clade 800 were kept at a constant 15 PSU to serve as controls for laboratory drift and beaker effects. 801 In this study, generations were not propagated by discrete transfers. Location-based permutation tests of chromosomal localization 922 

To test whether SNPs under selection were non-randomly distributed along chromosomes and spatially 923 biased toward the telomeric regions in either the Europe and Atlantic clades, or towards chromosomal 924 fusion sites in the Atlantic clade, location-based permutation tests were implemented using R. For each 925 clade, the corresponding chromosome-level reference genome assembly and chromosome length were 926 used 29,42 . Chromosomes were partitioned into non-overlapping genomic bins of both 1 Mb and 2 Mb, 927 generating two independent spatial intervals. For telomere analyses, telomeric regions were defined as the 928 terminal bins at each chromosome end in both clades. For fusion-site analyses in the Atlantic clade, bins 929 were defined as the non-overlapping genomic bin containing the midpoint of each chromosomal fusion 930 site (see Du et al. 29 ). 931 For each telomeric or fusion-site bin, the observed test statistic was the number of selected SNPs 932 located within that bin. To generate a null distribution under the hypothesis that selected loci are not 933 spatially biased along chromosomes, chromosome-specific permutation tests were performed using R. 934 Specifically, bins of the same size were randomly sampled from all bins on the same chromosome 935 (including telomeric or fusion bins), and their SNP counts were recorded. This procedure was repeated 936 10 6 times to generate empirical null distributions. P-values were calculated as the right-tail probability 937 that a randomly sampled bin contained at least as many selected SNPs as the observed focal bin. 938
