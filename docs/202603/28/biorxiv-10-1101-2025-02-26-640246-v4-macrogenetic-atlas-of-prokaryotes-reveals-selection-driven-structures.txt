Abstract 18 Macrogenetics investigates patterns and predictors of intraspecific genetic variation across diverse 19 taxa, offering a framework for understanding species diversity and addressing evolutionary 20 hypotheses. Here, we present a macrogenetic atlas of prokaryotes (MAP), integrating genomic 21 data (30 parameters in 12 categories) from 15,235 prokaryotic species and population genetic data 22 (22 parameters in 7 categories) from 786 species with phylogenetic, phenotypic, and ecological 23 information. MAP enables quantitative species characterization, straightforward cross-species 24 comparisons, and easy generation, testing, and refinement of evolutionary hypotheses. For 25 example, our analyses show that long- and short-range genetic linkage capture distinct 26 evolutionary dynamics and independently shape genetic diversity. Further, we propose that as 27 within-species diversity increases, epistatic selection becomes an increasingly strong force 28 structuring diversity, exemplified by convergent ecospecies structures in Streptococcus mitis and S. 29 

oralis . Overall, MAP represents a widely applicable resource (www.genomap.cn) and offers novel 30 insights into the drivers of macroevolution and the life cycle of prokaryotic species. 31 32

Introduction 33 Life is diverse, and its understanding requires macro-scale frameworks that capture species 34 diversity, akin to maps guiding exploration. Since Linnaeus, taxonomy has provided this 35 foundation based on phenotypic classification, and is now being systematically updated by the 36 addition of genomic data, for example in the Tree of Life project 1. The integration of genotype, 37 phenotype, and ecological data remains the major unresolved challenge and is essential for 38 characterizing species and identifying the forces driving diversification and speciation. Within 39 microbiology, classical resources such as Bergey’s Manual , which integrates taxonomy with 40 phenotypic and ecological information 2, have long served this purpose. 41 42 Incorporating within-species genomic variation data is the next critical step. Advances in 43 high-throughput sequencing have produced extensive genomic resources across many taxa 3,4 . With 44 their rapid accumulation, the time is now ripe for systematic integration under a new framework, 45 macrogenetics, which embraces broad taxonomic, spatial and temporal scales across dozens to 46 thousands of species 5,6 . Macrogenetics provides a framework for exploring species diversity and 47 testing long-standing evolutionary hypotheses. For example, community-level species richness has 48 been shown to correlate positively with within-population genetic diversity in fish and mammals 7,8 ,49 supporting the species-genetic diversity correlation (SGDC) concept 9. Other studies have linked 50 genetic diversity to life-history traits such as fecundity and propagule size 10 , and revealed 51 latitudinal gradients in genetic diversity 11 .52 53 Prokaryotes make up more biomass than all organisms except plants 12 . Furthermore, a single 54 species often contains lineages adapted to multiple niches, differing in host range, pathogenicity 55 and nutrient use, as exemplified by Escherichia coli 13 . This contrasts with eukaryotes, where 56 within-species differentiation and specialization are typically limited. Such pervasive intraspecific 57 diversity in prokaryotes raises fundamental questions: how are ecological niches partitioned, and 58 how do phenotypic and niche diversity shape genomic and population genetic features? These 59 differences also imply that differentiation and speciation in prokaryotes are driven by forces 60 distinct from those shaping eukaryotes 14 , but these remain poorly understood. 61 62 Here we present the Macrogenetic Atlas of Prokaryotes (MAP), an open resource 63 (www.genomap.cn) that integrates large-scale genomic and population genetic data with 64 phylogenetic, phenotypic and ecological information. MAP assigns each species a quantitative 65 “position” within prokaryotic diversity, analogous to latitude and longitude on a map, providing a 66 framework for exploring and understanding species diversity. 67

Species-level genomic parameters (GPs) were defined as the median across Rep. genomes, 76 whereas population genetic parameters (PGPs) were estimated from random sampling of ten Rep. 77 genomes, from CG datasets, or from all Rep. genomes (Methods). To ensure robustness, only 78 PGPs with low variability (standard deviation/median < 0.5) across random-sampling replicates 79 were retained, yielding estimates highly consistent with full datasets (correlation coefficient r >80 0.8 for all but one parameter; h/m , r = 0.68) and independent of sample size (Supplementary Figs. 81 2-4). By contrast, all GP and CG-based PGP estimates were retained across species to maximize 82 cross-species comparability, with high variability reflecting substantial within-species 83 heterogeneity. 84 85 In total, we obtained 30 GPs across 12 categories for all 15,235 species, and 22 PGPs across seven 86 categories for 786 species. These were integrated with phylogenetic, phenotypic and ecological 87 data (Supplementary Table 3-6). Metadata and key process outputs, including whole-genome 88 alignments and variation profiles, are available as community resources. We illustrate the utility of 89 MAP across five distinct aspects. 90 91 

Quantitative characterization of prokaryotic species 92 First, MAP translates qualitative descriptions into quantitative, comparable measures ( Fig. 1b,c ). 93 For example, it confirms that the human pathogen Helicobacter pylori is highly recombinogenic 15 ,94 with recombination-derived diversity (‘recombination coverage’, Rec. c 16 ) in the top 1% and 95 short-range (10 bp) linkage disequilibrium (LD, inversely correlated with recombination) in the 96 bottom 1%. This quantitative characterization also revises prevailing views. For example, the 97 widely distributed E. coli , naturally presumed to have a large effective population size ( Ne) and 98 high diversity 13 , instead shows moderately high but not extreme Ne (>71% species; estimated from 99 

dN/dS , a negative proxy for Ne and selection efficacy in many microbial studies 17 ) and 100 intermediate genetic diversity (>39% species). 101 102 

Defining range of prokaryotic diversity 103 Second, MAP delineates the range of genomic parameters across prokaryotes ( Fig. 1b-d ). For 104 example, prokaryotes are generally small and densely coded 18 , with MAP revealing a median 105 genome size of 4.0 Mb (interquartile range, IQR: 2.9–5.3 Mb) and coding proportion of 88% (IQR: 106 86–90%). Genomes larger than 7.3 Mb (top 10%), such as Burkholderia cenocepacia with 107 multipartite chromosomes 19 , define the upper extremes. 108 109 This quantitative framework enables systematic identification of “extreme” species. Overall patterns of prokaryotic genomes 122 Third, MAP reveals overall patterns of prokaryotic genomes ( Fig. 1d ). Archaea and bacteria show 123 broadly similar GP distributions, except that archaeal genes are generally shorter. Most parameters 124 follow roughly normal or right-skewed distributions. In contrast, GC content and codon bias index 125 (CBI) are distinctly bimodal, with GC likely reflecting ancient adaptive legacies 24 , and CBI 126 strongly influenced by GC 25 . Bacterial genome size approximates a normal distribution, whereas 127 core- and pan-gene counts are bimodal. The distribution of core-genes likely reflects different 128 outcomes of an evolutionary tradeoff but with the constraint that a minimum number of genes is 129 needed for cellular function. 130 131 

Easy testing and refining evolutionary hypotheses 132 Fourth, MAP systematically quantified correlations among parameters ( r for quantitative traits, η133 for categorical traits) and assessed their relationships with phylogeny (Pagel’s λ), phenotype, and 134 ecology ( Fig. 1e, Fig. 2 ). We next show that the uniformly derived correlation matrices constitute 135 an empirical resource for testing and refining evolutionary hypotheses. 136 137 

Determinants of genetic diversity 138 Our understanding about the determinants of genetic diversity remains limited. Ne, mutation, and 139 recombination have been proposed as major drivers 26 . While partly tested in eukaryotes, no 140 equivalent dataset existed for prokaryotes because it is challenging to directly measure these 141 factors. The MAP parameters that are most closely related to these proposed drivers are dN/dS 142 (r-adj = -0.76, inversely reflecting N e), substitution rate u (r-adj = 0.13), which measures mutation 143 filtered by selection, and recombination proxies such as the recombination-to-mutation ratio ( r/m 27 ,144 

r-adj = 0.32). All of these correlations with synonymous diversity πs are in the expected direction, 145 providing preliminary empirical data supporting their role as drivers in prokaryotes ( Fig. 2a ). 146 147 MAP confirmed ecological influences: plant-root-associated species exhibited the highest πs,148 consistent with nutrient-rich conditions sustaining large populations, whereas fermented-food 149 species showed the lowest, reflecting artificial selection and bottlenecks. Diversity also positively 150 correlated with temperature ( r-adj = 0.24), supporting warmer environments as drivers of genetic 151 diversity and aligning with latitudinal gradients reported in both bacteria 28,29 and eukaryotes 11 .152 153 

Underexplored correlations 154 MAP also revealed previously overlooked patterns ( Fig. 2b ). Consistent with the established role 155 of selection in shaping prokaryotic genome size 17 , supported by its negative correlation with 156 

dN/dS (r-adj = -0.22), a proxy for selection efficacy, gene length shows an even stronger negative 157 correlation with dN/dS (r-adj = -0.31). This pattern suggests that, analogous to genome size, 158 stronger selection favors longer genes. Gene length also correlated positively with coding density 159 (r-adj = 0.48), possibly because regulatory non-coding regions remain relatively constant 160 regardless of gene size. 161

Overview of prokaryotic genetic diversity 169 Finally, we illustrate how MAP can refine theoretical frameworks and generate new hypotheses. 170 We focused on the relationship between LD and genetic diversity, summarized in a 171 two-dimensional triangular plot, since short-range (10 bp) LD is constrained to be higher than 172 long-range (10 kb) LD ( Fig. 3a ). First, nearly the whole triangle is occupied, implying short- and 173 long-range LD vary largely independently. Second, πs showed a strong negative correlation with 174 short-range LD ( r-adj = -0.58) but no significant correlation with long-range LD. Instead, for 175 species with similar short-range LD, there is a pronounced positive correlation between long-range 176 LD and πs (Fig. 3a ). Therefore, short- and long-range LD reflect different processes and have 177 different drivers. 178 179 These qualitative patterns can be explained by the joint effects of “species age”, reflected in 180 genetic diversity, and barriers to gene flow (with non-recombining clonal species representing a 181 complete barrier), reflected in long-range LD ( Fig. 3b ). As species accumulate age (indicated by 182 increased πs) in the absence of barriers, long-range LD decays rapidly, whereas short-range LD 183 equilibrates more gradually, ultimately toward panmixia with high diversity and little LD at any 184 distance ( Fig. 3b , top arrows). By contrast, barriers to recombination slow the decay of long-range 185 LD, raising its equilibrium level ( Fig. 3b , middle and bottom arrows). Because LD decays more 186 slowly in the presence of gene flow barriers, species positioned along a given vertical line in Fig. 187 

3a tend to be “older” for higher long-range LD values ( Fig. 3b ), thereby explaining the positive 188 association with πs along each vertical line. 189 190 Although simplified, for example because species-wide bottlenecks can reduce diversity and 191 elevate LD, thereby making species appear “younger”, this two-dimensional framework provides a 192 useful lens for interpreting species-specific LD and genetic diversity configurations. For example, 193 a species with low barriers to genetic exchange is Vibrio parahaemolyticus (Fig. 3a,c ), which 194 exhibits low long-range LD but moderate diversity and correspondingly moderate short-range LD. 195 Previous analyses suggested that its diversity remains moderate because population size expanded 196 only recently, and if maintained over longer timescales, diversity would increase considerably, 197 driving the species toward panmixia 32 .198 199 A species with well-recognized barriers to genetic exchange is E. coli (Fig. 3a,c ), where 200 recombination between phylogroups is constrained by homology-dependent mechanisms 33 ,201 resulting in higher long-range LD than V. parahaemolyticus . Another example is H. pylori , a 202 species notorious for its exceptionally high rates of recombination during mixed infection 15 , which 203 have led to very low short-range LD. Epistatic selection as a driver of population structure 223 The paradoxical absence of true panmictic species can be explained if species with low barriers to 224 exchange systematically evolve stronger barriers as species age and accumulate genetic diversity. 225 

V. parahaemolyticus illustrates this process: in addition to weak geographic barriers generating 226 oceanic gene pools 36 , further barriers arise from epistatic selection. Specifically, two major 227 ecospecies are highly differentiated at ~1% of the core genome, reflecting strong selection against 228 gene flow in these regions 37,38 . If functional diversification expands over time, epistatic selection 229 could progressively create barriers to exchange across larger genomic regions. V. 230 

parahaemolyticus would then not evolve into a panmictic species but instead come to resemble the 231 structured, high-diversity species that we have observed. 232 233 We therefore hypothesize that panmictic bacteria are absent because, in species maintaining large 234 

N e, inevitable functional diversification driven by epistatic selection progressively generates 235 long-range LD. This predicts stronger signatures of selection in the genetic regions responsible for 236 population structure in “older” species with higher genetic diversity. To test this, we performed 237 principal component analysis (PCA) for each species with >100 genomes and identified SNPs 238 most strongly associated with the first principal component (top 1% by loading values; 239 PC1-related SNPs), representing the genetic basis of major population structure 39 . As a proxy for 240 selection, we quantified the enrichment of non-synonymous sites among these SNPs relative to the 241 genomic background, using the N/S (non-synonymous to synonymous site) ratio. 242 243 As predicted by the hypothesis, enrichment of non-synonymous sites was positively correlated 244 with πs, indicating that selection is most evident in highly diverse species ( Fig. 3e ). For example, 245 

Pseudomonas fluorescens , which exhibits very high genetic diversity, showed strong enrichment 246 of non-synonymous sites (N/S ratio = 2.5) and a highly structured phylogeny ( Fig. 3c ). Together, 247 these results support a model in which epistatic selection progressively shapes population structure 248

Convergent ecospecies 252 Given the evidence that selection has molded genetic structure, we asked whether we could 253 identify species further along the road to functional differentiation than V. parahaemolyticus . To 254 this end, we examined species with higher diversity and many genomes (>100 genomes). Two 255 such species are Streptococcus mitis and S. oralis . The PCA loading distribution for both 256 resembled that seen for previous ecospecies 37,40 , implying high differentiation in specific regions 257 and high gene flow elsewhere ( Fig. 4a,b ). Furthermore, there is strong enrichment of genes of 258 particular categories with extreme PC1 loadings. For both species, COG categories D (cell cycle 259 control, cell division, chromosome partitioning) and M (cell wall/membrane/envelope biogenesis), 260 and KEGG category ko03036 (chromosome and associated proteins) were involved, suggesting 261 functional divergence in cell division and biogenesis. The convergence of the differentiation signal 262 is further supported by a correlation of 0.41 between PC1 gene loadings in the two species, 263 together with substantial overlap among genes with extreme PC1 loadings in the D, M and 264 ko03036 categories ( Fig. 4c ). 265 266 To characterize these putative ecospecies, we constructed phylogenetic trees based on SNPs with 267 extreme loadings. We found two putative ecospecies for S. mitis and three for S. oralis , which also 268 correspond to distinct clusters in strain PCA loadings ( Fig. 4a,b ). To determine whether they had 269 evolved independently, we constructed a joint tree for the shared high loading genes, which 270 implies independent origin of ecospecies in the two species ( Fig. 4c ). Finally, we constructed 271 phylogenetic trees based on remaining SNPs and all SNPs to examine the pattern of differentiation 272 in the rest of the genome. Distinct ecospecies clusters are visible, implying modest genome-wide 273 differentiation, which contrasts with the absence of differentiation in most of the genome seen in V. 274 

parahaemolyticus and H. pylori 37,40 . This result is consistent with the Streptococcus ecospecies 275 being older and further along the path of functional differentiation and helps to explain the higher 276 long-range LD seen in these two species. 277 278 

Discussion 279 Exploring and understanding the diversity of life requires a framework akin to a geographical map. 280 For prokaryotes, classical resources such as Bergey’s Manual have long served as the initial “map”. 281 MAP extends this foundation with a genome-based atlas, capturing rich genomic and population 282 genetic features across prokaryotic species. It is accessible online, enabling straightforward 283 exploration and use. 284 285 MAP serves both focused and broad research needs. For researchers interested in particular 286 microbes, it provides concise summaries of genomic, population genetic, phenotypic, and 287 ecological features. Beyond single species, it facilitates straightforward comparisons across all 288 available prokaryotes and serves as a resource for macrogenomic and population genetic studies, 289 including both raw and curated non-redundant datasets. The current MAP is primarily based on 290 genomes from isolated strains, and future versions will incorporate metagenome-assembled 291 genomes to expand species coverage and genomic diversity. 292

293 Our analysis of MAP has generated a number of interesting observations that deserve further study. 294 For example, while bacterial genome size approximates a normal distribution, core- and pan-gene 295 counts are unexpectedly bimodal. Gut-dominant Bacteroides , such as B. uniformis and B. fragilis, 296 have exceptionally high genome fluidity. Moreover, we confirm that selection favors bigger 297 genomes, and also find a negative correlation between gene length and dN/dS , suggesting that 298 selection also favors long genes. 299 300 Our analyses and resources are available based on two taxonomic classifications, namely NCBI 301 and Genome Taxonomy Database (GTDB) 41,42 . The problem of bacterial species definitions 302 remains unsolved and large databases inevitably contain errors 43,44 . NCBI is based on user 303 inputted classifications, while GTDB uses an automatic algorithm, based on a universal average 304 nucleotide identity cutoff of 0.95. While GTDB has advantages of consistency and quality control 305 over NCBI, it often splits taxonomically diverse named species into multiple putative species 306 (Supplementary Fig. 6). In many cases, these species do not correspond to coherent evolutionary 307 units. In particular, highly diverse species such as P. fluorescens have very low short-range LD, 308 indicating that gene flow is effective in distributing diversity within the species, and accordingly 309 they are among the best examples of biological species, despite being split into >10 species by 310 GTDB. Therefore, GTDB is not especially well-suited for macrogenomic analysis, especially for 311 highly diverse species, and we presented our main results based on NCBI classification. We 312 nevertheless provide an alternative version of parameter calculation and correlation analysis 313 results based on GTDB (Supplementary Tables 7-9), and show a strong correlation between 314 parameter relationships derived from two taxonomies ( r = 0.96, Supplementary Fig. 7), indicating 315 that the great majority of our results are robust to species definitions. 316 317 The correlation matrix integrating genomic, population genetic, phenotypic, and ecological 318 parameters provides a resource-level overview that facilitates hypothesis generation and testing, 319 thereby helping to refine existing theoretical frameworks. These correlations, however, do not 320 necessarily imply causal relationships and may include false positives arising from multiple 321 testing or sensitivity to outliers; they should therefore be interpreted with caution. Nonetheless, 322 they serve as a valuable starting point for hypothesis-driven and statistically rigorous follow-up 323 analyses. 324 325 As an illustration, motivated by the observed lack of correlation between short- and long-range 326 genetic linkage, we propose and provide evidence that these two linkage scales capture distinct 327 aspects of evolutionary dynamics and independently shape genetic diversity. Classical population 328 genetic theory predicts a negative correlation between genetic diversity and genetic linkage level 26 .329 We indeed find that short-range LD is strongly inversely correlated with genetic diversity. 330 However, there is a pronounced positive correlation in MAP between long-range LD and πs for 331 species exhibiting similar levels of short-range LD ( Fig. 3a ). This arises because long-range LD 332 principally reflects barriers to recombination, which leads to slower LD decay at all genetic 333 distances ( Fig. 3b ). Therefore, for species with similar short-range LD values, species with higher 334 long-range LD tend to be older and more diverse. 335 336

There is one zone in the triangle which is surprisingly empty, corresponding to panmictic species 337 with low LD at all distances ( Fig. 3a ). Many bacteria occupy niches favorable to global dispersal 338 and the maintenance of vast, stable populations over evolutionary timescales and this should 339 generate panmictic, i.e., freely mixed species 32 . Our resolution of this paradox is that as 340 within-species diversity increases, epistatic selection becomes an increasingly strong force. 341 Consistent with this, we identified stronger signatures of selection structuring diversity in older 342 species ( Fig. 3e ). Thus, while there may be species that start on the journey to panmixia, with 343 large population sizes, weak geographic barriers and free recombination (as exemplified by V. 344 

parahaemolyticus ), all such species acquire barriers to recombination due to epistatic selection as 345 they age, and end up with intermediate long-range LD. 346 347 An important implication of our findings is that we can gain insight into the ecological pressures 348 acting on genetically diverse prokaryotic species, simply by characterizing how their diversity is 349 structured. One example of structure generated by selection is bacterial ecospecies, which are 350 characterized by fixed differences in a small fraction of the genome, but free genetic exchange 351 elsewhere. We have previously described the first two bacterial ecospecies in V. parahaemolyticus 352 and H. pylori. In V. parahaemolyticus the structure reflects diversification at motility-related genes, 353 while in H. pylori differentiation has occurred in genes related to immune escape and nutrient 354 uptake 37,40 . Both species have moderate diversity and ecospecies structure is restricted to a small 355 fraction of the genome, representing an early stage of diversification. 356 357 Here we have identified two additional species with ecospecies-like structure, namely S. mitis and 358 

S. oralis . These two sister taxa both inhabit the mouths of humans and other animals and there is a 359 substantial convergence in the genetic basis of the ecospecies structure, with the same cell division 360 and biogenesis associated genome categories and genes involved. These suggest similar ecological 361 driving forces and/or adaptive potentials in both species, which might reflect the diverse niches 362 within complex multispecies biofilms in the oral cavity, that could be exploited by evolving cell 363 shape and cell wall properties. The ecospecies appear to be older than those identified previously 364 and show evidence for some level of differentiation genome-wide, suggesting a later stage of 365 diversification and is likely to be a precursor to full speciation. 366 367 Extrapolating from early to late stage ecospecies divergence and beyond, we can begin to 368 reconstruct the life-cycle of many prokaryotic species. However, ecospecies is only one type of 369 structure caused by selection. We have previously hypothesized the existence of bacterial 370 ecoclines, which exhibit continuous variation driven by a quantitative trait 39 and the genetic 371 structure in the most diverse species such as P. fluorescens resists any immediate classification. 372 Characterizing the full range of prokaryotic genetic structures should provide a unique new 373 window into the forces shaping the most diverse and adaptable organisms on our planet. 374

Representative datasets and clonal groups 398 While robust estimation of population genetic parameters requires randomly sampled 399 representative datasets, biased repeat sampling ( e.g ., strains from disease outbreaks) is prevalent in 400 public genomic datasets, especially for pathogenic species. To reduce the effects of repeat 401 sampling, we generated representative genome datasets as previously described 47 . Briefly, for each 402 species, core-genome (regions present in >95% strains) single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) 403 were identified using SNP-sites v2.5.1 48 . SNPs located in repetitive regions, as identified by TRF 404 v4.07b 49 or BLASTN self-alignment, were excluded from subsequent analyses. Pairwise SNP 405 distances between strains were calculated using snp-dists v0.8.2 406 (https://github.com/tseemann/snp-dists). Genomes of a species were grouped into 407 complete-linkage clusters (CLCs) based on pairwise SNP distances using the R package ape 408 v5.7.1 50 . For the representative dataset of each species, we used a “relative” threshold, defined as 409 1% of the median pairwise SNP distance, as the cutoff, and selected a single genome with the 410 highest N50 from each CLC or from singleton genomes as representative genomes. 411 412 Estimation of substitution rates and selected recombination parameters rely on clonal groups (CGs) 413 consisting of closely related genomes. We used an “absolute” SNP-distance threshold of 5,000 414 SNPs (corresponding to the total SNP cutoff per branch for reliable recombination detection 51 ) as 415 the cutoff to identify CLCs corresponding to CGs. The assignments of the representative and CG 416 datasets are provided in Supplementary Table 1. 417 418

Correlation analysis 534 We used the Python packages pingouin and scipy.stats to calculate correlations between different 535 parameter types. Associations between 540 quantitative and categorical variables were measured with Cramér’s coefficient ( V).
